<h1>Emotional Regulation</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Emotional regulation encompasses the processes by which individuals influence which emotions they have, when they have them, and how they experience and express them. James Gross's process model identifies five families of regulation strategies that intervene at different points in the emotion-generation timeline. The neural basis involves a circuit where the prefrontal cortex (especially ventromedial and dorsolateral regions) modulates amygdala reactivity — a balance disrupted in virtually every mood and anxiety disorder. Effective emotional regulation is one of the strongest predictors of psychological well-being and resilience.</p>
<h2>Regulation Strategies (Gross Process Model)</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Situation selection</strong> — Choosing to approach or avoid emotion-eliciting situations (preventive)</li>
<li><strong>Situation modification</strong> — Actively changing a situation to alter its emotional impact</li>
<li><strong>Attentional deployment</strong> — Directing attention toward or away from emotional stimuli (distraction, rumination)</li>
<li><strong>Cognitive reappraisal</strong> — Reinterpreting the meaning of a situation to change its emotional impact (most adaptive strategy)</li>
<li><strong>Response modulation</strong> — Directly influencing physiological, experiential, or behavioral responses (suppression, exercise, breathing)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Neurotransmitter Modulation</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Serotonin</strong> — Primary modulator of emotional tone; 5-HT deficiency linked to impulsivity, aggression, and mood instability</li>
<li><strong>GABA</strong> — Anxiolytic inhibition of amygdala hyperactivity; target of benzodiazepines</li>
<li><strong>Norepinephrine</strong> — Arousal intensity; excessive NE drives hypervigilance and panic</li>
<li><strong>Dopamine</strong> — Reward-related emotional responses; anhedonia when depleted</li>
<li><strong>Oxytocin</strong> — Social buffering of stress; enhances trust and reduces amygdala threat response</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Relevance</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Anxiety disorders</strong> — Amygdala hyperreactivity with insufficient prefrontal regulation</li>
<li><strong>Major depression</strong> — Impaired cognitive reappraisal, excessive rumination</li>
<li><strong>PTSD</strong> — Fear extinction failure (amygdala-prefrontal disconnection)</li>
<li><strong>Borderline personality disorder</strong> — Pervasive emotion dysregulation across all strategies</li>
<li><strong>Bipolar disorder</strong> — Oscillation between states of extreme emotional dysregulation</li>
</ul>
