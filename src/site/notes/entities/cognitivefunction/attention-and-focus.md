<h1>Attention and Focus</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Attention is the cognitive mechanism that selects relevant information for processing while filtering out irrelevant stimuli. It is not a single unitary process but a family of related functions supported by distinct neural networks. Michael Posner's influential model identifies three attention networks: alerting (maintaining readiness), orienting (directing attention to sensory events), and executive control (resolving conflict between competing stimuli). Deficits in these networks underlie many neuropsychiatric conditions, most notably ADHD, and attention is one of the cognitive functions most sensitive to sleep deprivation, stress, and aging.</p>
<h2>Attention Subtypes</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Sustained attention (vigilance)</strong> — Maintaining focus over extended periods (right frontal and parietal cortex)</li>
<li><strong>Selective attention</strong> — Focusing on one stimulus while ignoring others (visual cortex, parietal cortex)</li>
<li><strong>Divided attention</strong> — Processing multiple information streams simultaneously (prefrontal cortex)</li>
<li><strong>Executive attention</strong> — Resolving conflict and inhibiting prepotent responses (anterior cingulate cortex, dorsolateral PFC)</li>
<li><strong>Orienting attention</strong> — Shifting focus to new stimuli (superior parietal lobule, frontal eye fields)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Neurotransmitter Modulation</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Norepinephrine</strong> — Primary arousal/alerting signal from locus coeruleus; inverted-U dose-response</li>
<li><strong>Dopamine</strong> — Prefrontal dopamine enables sustained focus and working memory; too little (ADHD) or too much impairs</li>
<li><strong>Acetylcholine</strong> — Basal forebrain cholinergic system enhances signal-to-noise ratio in sensory cortex</li>
<li><strong>Glutamate</strong> — NMDA receptor activation required for sustained attention and cortical arousal</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Relevance</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>ADHD</strong> — Dysregulated prefrontal dopamine and norepinephrine; treated with stimulants (methylphenidate, amphetamine)</li>
<li><strong>Traumatic brain injury</strong> — Diffuse axonal injury disrupts attention networks</li>
<li><strong>Sleep deprivation</strong> — Profoundly impairs sustained attention (locus coeruleus fatigue)</li>
<li><strong>Aging</strong> — Age-related decline in selective and divided attention (prefrontal atrophy)</li>
<li><strong>Schizophrenia</strong> — Impaired prepulse inhibition and sensory gating</li>
</ul>
