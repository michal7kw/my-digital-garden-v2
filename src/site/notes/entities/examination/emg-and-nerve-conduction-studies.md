<h1>EMG and Nerve Conduction Studies</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Electromyography (EMG) and Nerve Conduction Studies (NCS) are electrodiagnostic techniques that assess the function of peripheral nerves and muscles. In neurodegenerative disease, particularly ALS, EMG/NCS is essential for confirming lower motor neuron involvement, assessing disease extent, and excluding mimicking conditions. The combination of clinical upper motor neuron signs and EMG-confirmed lower motor neuron involvement is diagnostic for ALS.</p>
<h2>Components</h2>
<h3>Nerve Conduction Studies (NCS)</h3>
<h4>Motor NCS</h4>
<ul>
<li>Stimulate nerve, record from muscle</li>
<li>Measures: Amplitude (axon number), velocity (myelin), latency</li>
<li>Normal motor velocities: &gt;50 m/s (arms), &gt;40 m/s (legs)</li>
</ul>
<h4>Sensory NCS</h4>
<ul>
<li>Stimulate and record from same nerve</li>
<li><strong>Critical in ALS</strong>: Sensory responses are NORMAL</li>
<li>Abnormal sensory = not pure motor neuron disease</li>
</ul>
<h4>F-waves and H-reflexes</h4>
<ul>
<li>Assess proximal nerve segments</li>
<li>F-wave: Motor neuron excitability</li>
<li>H-reflex: Sensory-motor loop (S1)</li>
</ul>

<h2>ALS Electrodiagnosis</h2>
<h3>El Escorial/Awaji Criteria</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Definite ALS</strong>: UMN + LMN signs in 3 regions</li>
<li><strong>Probable ALS</strong>: UMN + LMN in 2 regions</li>
<li>EMG can substitute for clinical LMN signs (Awaji modification)</li>
</ul>

<h3>Regions Examined</h3>
<ol>
<li><strong>Bulbar</strong>: Tongue, face, sternocleidomastoid</li>
<li><strong>Cervical</strong>: C5-T1 myotomes</li>
<li><strong>Thoracic</strong>: Paraspinal muscles (important!)</li>
<li><strong>Lumbosacral</strong>: L2-S1 myotomes</li>
</ol>

<h2>Other Applications</h2>
<h3>Peripheral Neuropathy</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Axonal</strong>: Reduced amplitudes, normal velocities</li>
<li><strong>Demyelinating</strong>: Slowed velocities, conduction block</li>
<li>Distribution: Length-dependent vs multifocal</li>
</ul>
<h3>Myopathy</h3>
<ul>
<li>Small, polyphasic, early-recruiting MUAPs</li>
<li>Spontaneous activity in inflammatory myopathy</li>
<li>Normal NCS</li>
</ul>
<h3>Radiculopathy</h3>
<ul>
<li>Denervation limited to single root distribution</li>
<li>Paraspinal involvement confirms root level</li>
<li>Normal NCS (unless very severe)</li>
</ul>

<h2>Interpretation Principles</h2>
<h3>For ALS Diagnosis</h3>
<ol>
<li>Evidence of LMN degeneration (EMG)</li>
<li>Evidence of UMN degeneration (clinical)</li>
<li>Progressive spread</li>
<li>Exclusion of other causes</li>
</ol>
<h3>EMG Report Components</h3>
<ul>
<li>NCS summary (motor, sensory, F-waves)</li>
<li>Spontaneous activity by muscle</li>
<li>MUAP characteristics</li>
<li>Recruitment pattern</li>
<li>Summary and clinical correlation</li>
</ul>
<h3>Limitations</h3>
<ul>
<li>Operator-dependent</li>
<li>Patient discomfort (needle)</li>
<li>Snapshot in time (may need repeat)</li>
<li>Cannot detect UMN involvement</li>
</ul>
<h2>Practical Considerations</h2>
<h3>Patient Preparation</h3>
<ul>
<li>Explain procedure (surface stimulation, needle insertion)</li>
<li>Avoid lotions/creams (electrode contact)</li>
<li>Warm limbs (cold slows conduction)</li>
<li>Anticoagulation not contraindication</li>
</ul>
<h3>Sampling Strategy</h3>
<ul>
<li>Proximal and distal muscles per limb</li>
<li>Multiple nerve territories</li>
<li>Include thoracic paraspinals (often diagnostic)</li>
<li>Balance thoroughness with patient tolerance</li>
</ul>
<h3>Safety</h3>
<ul>
<li>Minimal risk from needle EMG</li>
<li>Avoid anticoagulated patients near vital structures</li>
<li>Infection risk extremely low</li>
<li>No lasting effects</li>
</ul>
