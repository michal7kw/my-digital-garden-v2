<h1>Nucleolus</h1>
<h2>Description</h2>
<p>The nucleolus is the largest and most prominent sub-nuclear structure, visible under light microscopy as a dense body within the nucleus. It is the site of ribosomal RNA (rRNA) transcription by RNA Polymerase I, rRNA processing, and ribosome subunit assembly. Cells can have one or more nucleoli, typically forming around nucleolar organizer regions (NORs) on acrocentric chromosomes. Beyond its classical role in ribosome production, the nucleolus has emerged as a critical stress sensor and aging regulator. Nucleolar size correlates with rRNA transcription rate and inversely with lifespan — long-lived organisms and caloric-restricted animals have smaller, more compact nucleoli.</p>
<h2>Key Functions</h2>
<ul>
<li>Ribosomal RNA (rRNA) transcription (RNA Pol I)</li>
<li>rRNA processing and modification (snoRNPs)</li>
<li>Ribosome subunit assembly (40S and 60S)</li>
<li>Stress sensing (nucleolar stress response → p53 activation)</li>
<li>Cell cycle regulation (sequestration of cell cycle regulators)</li>
<li>Aging regulation (nucleolar size as longevity predictor)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Nucleolar Stress Response</h2>
<p>When ribosome biogenesis is disrupted (by DNA damage, nutrient deprivation, or drug treatment), ribosomal proteins are released into the nucleoplasm. Free RPL5 and RPL11 bind and inhibit MDM2, stabilizing p53 and triggering cell cycle arrest or apoptosis. This &quot;nucleolar stress response&quot; is a key tumor suppressor mechanism.</p>
