<h1>Wbc</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>White blood cell (WBC) count, also known as leukocyte count, measures the total number of white blood cells in a volume of blood. White blood cells are the cellular effectors of the immune system, responsible for defending the body against infections, foreign substances, and abnormal cells. The WBC count encompasses five major cell types: neutrophils (50-70%), lymphocytes (20-40%), monocytes (2-8%), eosinophils (1-4%), and basophils (0.5-1%), each serving distinct immunological functions.</p>
<p>The WBC count is one of the most commonly ordered laboratory tests and is a core component of the complete blood count (CBC). It provides a rapid assessment of immune system activity and is essential in the evaluation of infections, inflammatory conditions, allergic reactions, immunodeficiency states, and hematologic malignancies. While the total WBC count gives an overview, the differential count (the proportion of each cell type) is often more diagnostically informative, as specific patterns point to different underlying conditions.</p>
<p>Normal WBC values vary by age (neonates and children have higher counts), race (individuals of African descent often have lower baseline counts, termed &quot;benign ethnic neutropenia&quot;), time of day (physiological afternoon peak), stress level, and exercise status. Medications -- particularly corticosteroids, lithium, and beta-agonists -- can significantly alter the WBC count. Interpretation should always consider these contextual factors alongside the clinical presentation.</p>

<h2>Clinical Interpretation</h2>
<p><strong>Elevated WBC (Leukocytosis, &gt;11,000 cells/uL):</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Infections:</strong> Bacterial infections (neutrophilia), viral infections (lymphocytosis), parasitic infections (eosinophilia)</li>
<li><strong>Inflammation:</strong> Rheumatoid arthritis, IBD, vasculitis, tissue necrosis</li>
<li><strong>Stress response:</strong> Physical or emotional stress, surgery, trauma, burns</li>
<li><strong>Medications:</strong> Corticosteroids (demargination), lithium, beta-agonists, epinephrine, G-CSF</li>
<li><strong>Hematologic malignancies:</strong> Leukemia (acute and chronic), lymphoma, myeloproliferative disorders</li>
<li><strong>Smoking:</strong> Chronic mild leukocytosis</li>
<li><strong>Pregnancy:</strong> Physiological leukocytosis (up to 15,000 cells/uL; up to 25,000 during labor)</li>
<li><strong>Asplenia:</strong> Post-splenectomy leukocytosis</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Low WBC (Leukopenia, &lt;4,500 cells/uL):</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Viral infections:</strong> HIV, hepatitis, EBV, influenza (transient suppression)</li>
<li><strong>Bone marrow suppression:</strong> Chemotherapy, radiation therapy, aplastic anemia</li>
<li><strong>Medications:</strong> Clozapine, carbamazepine, methimazole, methotrexate, sulfasalazine</li>
<li><strong>Autoimmune:</strong> Systemic lupus erythematosus, Felty syndrome</li>
<li><strong>Nutritional deficiencies:</strong> B12, folate, copper deficiency</li>
<li><strong>Sepsis:</strong> Late-stage or overwhelming sepsis (poor prognosis)</li>
<li><strong>Hypersplenism:</strong> Sequestration and destruction in enlarged spleen</li>
<li><strong>Benign ethnic neutropenia:</strong> Common in individuals of African descent (constitutional, not pathological)</li>
</ul>
