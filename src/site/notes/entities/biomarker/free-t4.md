<h1>Free T4</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Free thyroxine (Free T4, FT4) is the unbound, biologically available form of the thyroid hormone T4, representing approximately 0.03% of total T4 in the bloodstream. T4 is the predominant hormone secreted by the thyroid gland (accounting for ~90% of thyroid hormone output), and while it has some intrinsic metabolic activity, it primarily serves as a prohormone that is converted to the more active T3 in peripheral tissues by deiodinase enzymes. The thyroid gland produces T4 by iodinating tyrosine residues on thyroglobulin, requiring adequate iodine intake.</p>
<p>Free T4 is the most commonly measured thyroid hormone in clinical practice and, together with TSH, forms the foundation of thyroid function assessment. In the hypothalamic-pituitary-thyroid (HPT) axis, TSH stimulates T4 production, and circulating FT4 feeds back to suppress TSH release. This inverse log-linear relationship means that small changes in FT4 produce large changes in TSH, making TSH the more sensitive screening test. However, FT4 measurement is essential for confirming thyroid dysfunction, differentiating primary from secondary/central causes, and monitoring thyroid hormone replacement therapy.</p>
<p>Free T4 is more stable than FT3 in non-thyroidal illness, making it the preferred hormone measurement in acutely ill patients. However, FT4 can be affected by protein-binding abnormalities (pregnancy, estrogen therapy, genetic TBG abnormalities), and severe illness can suppress FT4 in the late stages of euthyroid sick syndrome. Biotin supplementation can interfere with immunoassays, producing falsely elevated FT4 results.</p>

<h2>Clinical Interpretation</h2>
<p><strong>Elevated Free T4 (&gt;1.8 ng/dL):</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Graves disease</li>
<li>Toxic multinodular goiter</li>
<li>Toxic adenoma</li>
<li>Thyroiditis (subacute, postpartum, painless -- transient elevation from gland destruction)</li>
<li>Excessive levothyroxine dosing</li>
<li>TSH-secreting pituitary adenoma (rare -- elevated FT4 with non-suppressed TSH)</li>
<li>Thyroid hormone resistance (elevated FT4 with normal/elevated TSH)</li>
<li>Biotin interference (artifactual elevation in streptavidin-biotin immunoassays)</li>
<li>Acute psychiatric illness (transient)</li>
<li>Amiodarone-induced thyrotoxicosis</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Low Free T4 (&lt;0.8 ng/dL):</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Primary hypothyroidism (Hashimoto thyroiditis, post-thyroidectomy, post-radioiodine)</li>
<li>Secondary/central hypothyroidism (pituitary or hypothalamic disease -- low FT4 with low/normal TSH)</li>
<li>Iodine deficiency</li>
<li>Medications: lithium, amiodarone (type 1 and 2), carbamazepine, phenytoin</li>
<li>Severe non-thyroidal illness (late stage euthyroid sick syndrome)</li>
<li>Inadequate levothyroxine replacement dosing</li>
</ul>
