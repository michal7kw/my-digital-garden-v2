<h1>Cystatin C</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Cystatin C is a small protein (13 kDa) produced at a constant rate by all nucleated cells in the body. It is freely filtered by the glomeruli in the kidneys and almost completely reabsorbed and catabolized by the proximal tubular cells. Because of these properties, serum cystatin C concentration serves as an excellent endogenous marker of glomerular filtration rate (GFR), often considered superior to creatinine for certain populations.</p>
<p>Unlike creatinine, cystatin C is not significantly affected by muscle mass, sex, age, race, or diet, making it particularly valuable for estimating kidney function in elderly patients, individuals with reduced muscle mass, vegetarians, and patients with liver disease. The CKD-EPI cystatin C equation and the combined creatinine-cystatin C equation provide more accurate GFR estimates than creatinine alone, especially in the near-normal GFR range where early kidney disease detection matters most.</p>
<p>Emerging research also links cystatin C to cardiovascular risk prediction independent of its role as a kidney function marker. Elevated cystatin C levels have been associated with increased risk of heart failure, stroke, and all-cause mortality, suggesting it may reflect vascular endothelial dysfunction or systemic inflammation beyond renal function alone.</p>

<h2>Clinical Interpretation</h2>
<p><strong>Elevated Cystatin C (&gt;0.95 mg/L):</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Decreased glomerular filtration rate / chronic kidney disease</li>
<li>Acute kidney injury</li>
<li>Diabetic nephropathy</li>
<li>Cardiovascular disease risk (independent predictor)</li>
<li>Hyperthyroidism (increases production)</li>
<li>Corticosteroid therapy (can increase levels)</li>
<li>Systemic inflammation</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Low Cystatin C (&lt;0.53 mg/L):</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Hypothyroidism (decreases production)</li>
<li>Generally not clinically concerning</li>
<li>May indicate hyperfiltration in early diabetic nephropathy</li>
</ul>
