<h1>Omega3 Index</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>The Omega-3 Index is defined as the percentage of EPA (eicosapentaenoic acid) plus DHA (docosahexaenoic acid) in red blood cell (RBC) membranes, expressed as a proportion of total RBC fatty acids. Developed by Dr. William Harris and Dr. Clemens von Schacky, the Omega-3 Index is considered the most clinically validated biomarker of long-term omega-3 status. Because red blood cells have a lifespan of approximately 120 days, the index reflects average omega-3 intake over the preceding 3-4 months, making it far more stable and clinically meaningful than plasma omega-3 levels which fluctuate with recent meals.</p>
<p>The Omega-3 Index has been extensively studied as an independent risk factor for cardiovascular disease. An index of 8% or higher is associated with a 90% reduction in the risk of sudden cardiac death compared to an index below 4%. Beyond cardiovascular protection, higher Omega-3 Index values are associated with reduced all-cause mortality, lower rates of cognitive decline, improved mood and reduced depression risk, better inflammatory profiles, and reduced risk of age-related macular degeneration. The Omega-3 Index is increasingly recognized as a modifiable risk factor akin to cholesterol or blood pressure.</p>
<p>This test is ordered to assess the adequacy of omega-3 fatty acid intake, guide fish oil or algal oil supplementation dosing, evaluate cardiovascular risk in at-risk populations, and monitor response to dietary interventions. It is particularly valuable because the response to omega-3 supplementation is highly individual, influenced by genetics, absorption, metabolism, and background diet.</p>

<h2>Clinical Interpretation</h2>
<p><strong>Low Omega-3 Index (&lt;4%):</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Insufficient dietary omega-3 intake (low fish consumption, no supplementation)</li>
<li>Highest risk zone for sudden cardiac death and cardiovascular events</li>
<li>Associated with increased systemic inflammation (elevated hs-CRP, IL-6)</li>
<li>Greater risk of cognitive decline, depression, and dry eye disease</li>
<li>Common in Western diets high in omega-6 fatty acids</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Intermediate Omega-3 Index (4-8%):</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Suboptimal but not critically low</li>
<li>Supplementation of 1-2g EPA+DHA daily typically raises index by 4-5 percentage points over 3-4 months</li>
<li>Dietary increases (2-3 servings fatty fish/week) also effective</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Optimal Omega-3 Index (&gt;8%):</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Cardioprotective zone with lowest risk of sudden cardiac death</li>
<li>Associated with better brain health, mood stability, and lower inflammation</li>
<li>Reflects adequate long-term omega-3 intake</li>
<li>Extremely high values (&gt;16%) are rare and not associated with adverse effects in clinical studies</li>
</ul>
