<h1>S100B</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>S100B is a 21 kDa calcium-binding protein predominantly expressed by astrocytes in the central nervous system. It is released into the bloodstream when brain tissue is damaged or when the blood-brain barrier (BBB) is compromised. S100B serves as an acute biomarker for brain injury and is used clinically to help rule out significant intracranial pathology after head trauma.</p>
<h2>Biological Roles</h2>
<h3>Normal Physiology</h3>
<ul>
<li>Calcium homeostasis in astrocytes</li>
<li>Regulation of cell growth and differentiation</li>
<li>Cytoskeletal dynamics</li>
<li>Paracrine signaling to neurons</li>
</ul>
<h3>Pathological Elevation</h3>
<ul>
<li>Released during astrocyte damage or death</li>
<li>Indicates BBB disruption</li>
<li>At high concentrations, may contribute to neuroinflammation</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Applications</h2>
<h3>Traumatic Brain Injury (TBI)</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Screening tool</strong>: Low S100B (&lt;0.10 ug/L within 3h) suggests low risk of intracranial lesion</li>
<li><strong>CT decision rule</strong>: Can reduce unnecessary CT scans</li>
<li><strong>Scandinavian Guidelines</strong>: Incorporated into TBI management</li>
<li><strong>Limitations</strong>: Sensitivity ~95%, specificity ~30%</li>
</ul>
<h3>Stroke</h3>
<ul>
<li>Elevated within hours of ischemic stroke</li>
<li>Correlates with infarct volume</li>
<li>Prognostic for functional outcome</li>
</ul>
<h3>Cardiac Surgery</h3>
<ul>
<li>Marker of brain injury post-cardiopulmonary bypass</li>
<li>Correlates with neurocognitive decline</li>
</ul>
<h2>Reference Values</h2>
<table>
<thead>
<tr>
<th>Level (ug/L)</th>
<th>Interpretation</th>
</tr>
</thead>
<tbody>
<tr>
<td>&lt;0.10</td>
<td>Normal; low risk of significant brain injury</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>0.10-0.15</td>
<td>Borderline; clinical correlation required</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>&gt;0.15</td>
<td>Elevated; suggests brain injury</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>&gt;1.0</td>
<td>Markedly elevated; significant injury</td>
</tr>
</tbody>
</table>
<p><em>Cutoffs vary by assay and clinical context</em></p>
<h2>Timing Considerations</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Peak</strong>: 2-4 hours after injury</li>
<li><strong>Half-life</strong>: ~2 hours in serum</li>
<li><strong>Sampling window</strong>: Optimal within 3-6 hours of injury</li>
<li><strong>Late samples</strong>: May miss acute elevation</li>
</ul>
<h2>Extracranial Sources (False Positives)</h2>
<ul>
<li>Trauma to extracranial tissues (fractures)</li>
<li>Melanoma (S100B is tumor marker)</li>
<li>Cardiac contusion</li>
<li>Renal impairment (reduced clearance)</li>
<li>Adipose tissue damage</li>
</ul>

<h2>Clinical Decision Support</h2>
<h3>TBI CT Decision Rule (Scandinavian)</h3>
<p>If all criteria met, CT may be avoided:</p>
<ol>
<li>S100B &lt;0.10 ug/L (sampled within 3 hours)</li>
<li>GCS 14-15</li>
<li>No risk factors (anticoagulation, age &gt;65, etc.)</li>
</ol>
<h3>Limitations</h3>
<ul>
<li>Extracranial injuries common in trauma</li>
<li>Requires sampling within optimal window</li>
<li>Not standalone diagnostic test</li>
</ul>
