<h1>Free T3</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Free triiodothyronine (Free T3, FT3) is the unbound, biologically active form of the thyroid hormone T3, representing approximately 0.3% of total T3 in circulation. T3 is the most metabolically potent thyroid hormone, being 3-5 times more active than T4 (thyroxine) at the cellular level. Although a small amount of T3 is secreted directly by the thyroid gland, approximately 80% is produced by peripheral deiodination of T4, primarily in the liver, kidneys, and skeletal muscle via the type 1 and type 2 deiodinase enzymes.</p>
<p>Free T3 exerts its effects by binding to nuclear thyroid hormone receptors, regulating the transcription of genes involved in basal metabolic rate, thermogenesis, heart rate, protein synthesis, and neurodevelopment. Measurement of free T3 is clinically important in several scenarios: confirming hyperthyroidism (especially T3 thyrotoxicosis, where FT3 is elevated with normal FT4), assessing the adequacy of T4-to-T3 conversion in patients on levothyroxine therapy, and evaluating the &quot;low T3 syndrome&quot; (euthyroid sick syndrome) seen in critical illness and caloric restriction.</p>
<p>It is important to note that free T3 is the most sensitive thyroid hormone to non-thyroidal factors. Caloric restriction, acute illness, chronic disease, selenium deficiency, and certain medications (amiodarone, beta-blockers, corticosteroids) can reduce T4-to-T3 conversion, lowering FT3 without indicating primary thyroid disease. Selenium is a cofactor for deiodinase enzymes and plays a key role in T3 production.</p>

<h2>Clinical Interpretation</h2>
<p><strong>Elevated Free T3 (&gt;4.2 pg/mL):</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Graves disease (most common cause of hyperthyroidism)</li>
<li>Toxic multinodular goiter</li>
<li>Toxic adenoma</li>
<li>T3 thyrotoxicosis (elevated FT3 with normal FT4 -- can be the earliest sign of Graves disease relapse)</li>
<li>Exogenous T3 supplementation (liothyronine, desiccated thyroid)</li>
<li>Early or mild hyperthyroidism</li>
<li>Thyroiditis (transient, due to hormone release from damaged gland)</li>
<li>Iodine-induced hyperthyroidism</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Low Free T3 (&lt;2.3 pg/mL):</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Hypothyroidism (primary or secondary)</li>
<li>Euthyroid sick syndrome / non-thyroidal illness (most common cause of low FT3 in hospitalized patients)</li>
<li>Caloric restriction, fasting, eating disorders</li>
<li>Selenium deficiency (impaired T4-to-T3 conversion)</li>
<li>Medications: amiodarone, propranolol, corticosteroids, propylthiouracil</li>
<li>Chronic kidney disease, liver cirrhosis</li>
<li>Aging (mild physiological decline)</li>
<li>Poor T4-to-T3 conversion despite adequate levothyroxine dosing</li>
</ul>
