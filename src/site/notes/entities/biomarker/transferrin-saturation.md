<h1>Transferrin Saturation</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Transferrin saturation (TSAT) is a calculated value that represents the percentage of transferrin (the primary iron transport protein in blood) that is bound to iron. It is derived from the formula: (Serum Iron / Total Iron-Binding Capacity) x 100. This ratio provides a snapshot of iron availability for erythropoiesis and tissue needs, reflecting the balance between iron supply and iron-binding capacity in the circulation.</p>
<p>TSAT is a critical component of the iron panel and is considered one of the most useful tests for evaluating iron status. It is particularly valuable in differentiating iron deficiency anemia from anemia of chronic disease, two conditions that often coexist and can be challenging to distinguish. In iron deficiency, TSAT is characteristically low (&lt;20%), while in iron overload conditions like hereditary hemochromatosis, TSAT is elevated (&gt;45%), often serving as the earliest biochemical abnormality before ferritin rises.</p>
<p>It is important to note that TSAT has significant diurnal variation (iron levels are highest in the morning) and can be transiently affected by recent iron intake, inflammation, and infection. Fasting morning samples provide the most reliable results. In the setting of acute inflammation, serum iron drops rapidly due to hepcidin-mediated iron sequestration, causing a falsely low TSAT that does not necessarily reflect true iron deficiency.</p>

<h2>Clinical Interpretation</h2>
<p><strong>Elevated TSAT (&gt;45%):</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Hereditary hemochromatosis (often &gt;60%, earliest marker)</li>
<li>Iron overload from transfusions or excessive supplementation</li>
<li>Hemolytic anemia (increased iron release from RBC destruction)</li>
<li>Sideroblastic anemia</li>
<li>Acute hepatitis / hepatocellular necrosis (iron release from damaged hepatocytes)</li>
<li>Ineffective erythropoiesis (thalassemia, myelodysplastic syndromes)</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Low TSAT (&lt;20%):</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Iron deficiency anemia (most common cause)</li>
<li>Anemia of chronic disease / inflammation (hepcidin-mediated)</li>
<li>Chronic blood loss (GI bleeding, heavy menstruation)</li>
<li>Malabsorption (celiac disease, gastric bypass)</li>
<li>Inadequate dietary iron intake</li>
<li>Pregnancy (physiological increase in iron demand)</li>
<li>Functional iron deficiency in chronic kidney disease patients on EPO</li>
</ul>
