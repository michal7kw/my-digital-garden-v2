<h1>Co2</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>CO2 (carbon dioxide), measured on a standard metabolic panel, represents the total carbon dioxide content in the blood, which is predominantly in the form of bicarbonate (HCO3-). This measurement reflects the body's acid-base buffering capacity and is a critical component of the bicarbonate buffer system, which maintains blood pH within the narrow physiological range of 7.35-7.45. It is distinct from arterial blood gas CO2 measurements (pCO2), which measure dissolved carbon dioxide gas and reflect respiratory function.</p>
<p>The kidneys and lungs work in concert to regulate blood CO2/bicarbonate levels. The kidneys control bicarbonate reabsorption and regeneration, while the lungs regulate the excretion of volatile CO2 through ventilation. Abnormalities in CO2 levels therefore point to dysfunction in one or both of these organ systems, or to metabolic disturbances that overwhelm the body's buffering capacity. CO2 is a fundamental component of the comprehensive metabolic panel (CMP) and basic metabolic panel (BMP).</p>
<p>CO2 is routinely measured as part of electrolyte panels and metabolic panels during routine health screening, hospital admissions, and monitoring of patients with kidney disease, lung disease, diabetes, or those on medications that affect acid-base balance (e.g., diuretics, metformin, acetazolamide). It is also essential in evaluating unexplained symptoms such as fatigue, confusion, shortness of breath, or muscle weakness.</p>

<li>Ranges are generally consistent across sexes and adult age groups.</li>
<li>Venous CO2 (metabolic panel) is typically 1-2 mEq/L higher than arterial bicarbonate.</li>
<li>Mild variations may occur with altitude (chronic respiratory alkalosis at high altitude lowers bicarbonate).</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Interpretation</h2>
<p><strong>Elevated CO2 (&gt;29 mEq/L) -- Metabolic Alkalosis:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Prolonged vomiting or nasogastric suction (loss of HCl)</li>
<li>Diuretic use (thiazides, loop diuretics)</li>
<li>Excessive bicarbonate or antacid ingestion</li>
<li>Cushing syndrome or corticosteroid use</li>
<li>Severe hypokalemia (drives renal bicarbonate retention)</li>
<li>Chronic respiratory acidosis with renal compensation (COPD)</li>
<li>Primary hyperaldosteronism</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Low CO2 (&lt;23 mEq/L) -- Metabolic Acidosis:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA)</li>
<li>Lactic acidosis (sepsis, shock, intense exercise)</li>
<li>Renal tubular acidosis</li>
<li>Chronic kidney disease (impaired bicarbonate regeneration)</li>
<li>Diarrhea (bicarbonate loss via GI tract)</li>
<li>Methanol, ethylene glycol, or salicylate poisoning</li>
<li>Chronic respiratory alkalosis with renal compensation (hyperventilation syndromes, anxiety)</li>
<li>Metformin use (rare lactic acidosis)</li>
</ul>
