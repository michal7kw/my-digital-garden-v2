<h1>Fibrinogen</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Fibrinogen (Factor I) is a soluble glycoprotein synthesized by the liver that plays a dual role as both a key component of the coagulation cascade and an acute-phase reactant. In hemostasis, thrombin cleaves fibrinogen into fibrin monomers, which polymerize to form the structural mesh of blood clots. As an acute-phase protein, fibrinogen levels rise significantly during inflammation, infection, tissue injury, and malignancy, making it a useful marker of systemic inflammatory status.</p>
<p>Fibrinogen occupies a unique position at the intersection of coagulation and inflammation, contributing to cardiovascular risk through multiple mechanisms. Elevated fibrinogen increases blood viscosity, promotes platelet aggregation, enhances fibrin formation, and stimulates smooth muscle cell proliferation -- all of which accelerate atherosclerosis. Large epidemiological studies (including the Framingham Heart Study) have consistently identified elevated fibrinogen as an independent risk factor for myocardial infarction, stroke, and peripheral arterial disease, with a magnitude of risk comparable to traditional factors like cholesterol and blood pressure.</p>
<p>Beyond cardiovascular disease, fibrinogen serves as a sensitive (though non-specific) marker of the acute-phase response. It rises within 24-48 hours of acute inflammation and can remain elevated for weeks. Serial measurements can help monitor response to treatment in inflammatory conditions and predict outcomes in critical illness.</p>

<h2>Clinical Interpretation</h2>
<p><strong>Elevated Fibrinogen (&gt;400 mg/dL):</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Acute and chronic infections</li>
<li>Chronic inflammatory conditions (rheumatoid arthritis, IBD)</li>
<li>Acute tissue injury, trauma, surgery, burns</li>
<li>Malignancy</li>
<li>Cardiovascular disease risk / active atherosclerosis</li>
<li>Stroke and cerebrovascular disease</li>
<li>Nephrotic syndrome (compensatory hepatic synthesis)</li>
<li>Pregnancy (physiological elevation, especially third trimester)</li>
<li>Smoking (chronic elevation)</li>
<li>Obesity and metabolic syndrome</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Low Fibrinogen (&lt;200 mg/dL):</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC) -- consumption</li>
<li>Severe liver disease / hepatic failure (decreased synthesis)</li>
<li>Primary or secondary fibrinolysis</li>
<li>Congenital hypofibrinogenemia or afibrinogenemia</li>
<li>Massive transfusion (dilutional)</li>
<li>L-asparaginase therapy</li>
<li>Increased bleeding risk when &lt;100 mg/dL</li>
</ul>
