<h1>Dha</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Docosahexaenoic acid (DHA) is a 22-carbon, long-chain omega-3 polyunsaturated fatty acid that is the most abundant omega-3 fatty acid in the brain and retina, comprising approximately 40% of the polyunsaturated fatty acids in the brain and 60% in the retinal photoreceptors. DHA is obtained primarily from fatty fish, shellfish, and algal oil supplements, and can be endogenously synthesized from alpha-linolenic acid (ALA), though this conversion is extremely inefficient (&lt;5%) in humans. DHA is a structural component of neuronal cell membranes and is essential for brain development, cognitive function, and visual acuity throughout the lifespan.</p>
<p>While EPA is predominantly anti-inflammatory, DHA's primary roles are structural and neuroprotective. DHA maintains membrane fluidity, supports synaptic plasticity, promotes neurogenesis, and is the precursor to specialized pro-resolving mediators (SPMs) called protectins and maresins, which actively resolve neuroinflammation. DHA is critical during the third trimester of pregnancy and the first two years of life when rapid brain growth occurs, and maternal DHA status directly influences infant neurocognitive outcomes. In adults, low DHA levels are associated with accelerated brain aging, cognitive decline, Alzheimer's disease risk, and visual impairment.</p>
<p>DHA blood levels are measured as part of an omega-3 fatty acid panel, either as a plasma/serum concentration (reflecting recent 2-4 week intake) or within the Omega-3 Index (EPA+DHA in RBC membranes, reflecting 3-4 months of intake). Testing is ordered to assess omega-3 adequacy, guide supplementation (particularly for brain health and pregnancy), evaluate cognitive decline risk factors, and monitor dietary interventions.</p>

<h2>Clinical Interpretation</h2>
<p><strong>Low DHA:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Insufficient dietary intake of fatty fish or DHA-containing supplements (most common)</li>
<li>Associated with increased risk of cognitive decline and Alzheimer's disease</li>
<li>Poor visual acuity and retinal function</li>
<li>Increased risk of perinatal and postpartum depression</li>
<li>Impaired infant neurodevelopment if maternal DHA is low during pregnancy/lactation</li>
<li>Common in vegan/vegetarian diets without algal DHA supplementation</li>
<li>Associated with ADHD symptom severity in children</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Adequate/High DHA:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Reflects sufficient omega-3 intake from diet or supplementation</li>
<li>Associated with better cognitive performance, memory, and processing speed</li>
<li>Reduced risk of age-related macular degeneration</li>
<li>Better mood stability and lower depression risk</li>
<li>Supports healthy pregnancy outcomes and infant brain development</li>
<li>Very high levels are generally well-tolerated; mild GI effects or fishy aftertaste possible with high-dose supplementation</li>
</ul>
