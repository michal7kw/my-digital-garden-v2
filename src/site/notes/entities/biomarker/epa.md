<h1>Epa</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Eicosapentaenoic acid (EPA) is a 20-carbon omega-3 polyunsaturated fatty acid primarily obtained from fatty fish (salmon, mackerel, sardines, anchovies) and marine-derived supplements such as fish oil and algal oil. EPA is one of the two principal omega-3 fatty acids measured clinically (alongside DHA) and plays a central role in modulating inflammation, cardiovascular function, and mood regulation. It is the direct precursor to anti-inflammatory eicosanoids (resolvins, protectins) that actively resolve tissue inflammation.</p>
<p>EPA's clinical significance is strongest in cardiovascular medicine and psychiatry. The REDUCE-IT trial demonstrated that high-dose EPA (icosapent ethyl 4g/day) reduced major cardiovascular events by 25% in statin-treated patients with elevated triglycerides. In mental health, EPA has shown particular efficacy for depression, with meta-analyses supporting supplements containing greater than or equal to 60% EPA as effective adjuncts to antidepressant therapy. EPA also competes with arachidonic acid (AA) for cyclooxygenase and lipoxygenase enzymes, shifting eicosanoid production away from pro-inflammatory prostaglandins and leukotrienes.</p>
<p>EPA blood levels are typically measured as part of an omega-3 fatty acid panel, either as a plasma/serum level (reflecting recent dietary intake over 2-4 weeks) or as part of the Omega-3 Index (EPA+DHA in red blood cell membranes, reflecting intake over 3-4 months). Testing is ordered to assess omega-3 adequacy, guide supplementation, evaluate cardiovascular risk, and monitor anti-inflammatory dietary interventions.</p>

<h2>Clinical Interpretation</h2>
<p><strong>Low EPA:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Insufficient dietary intake of fatty fish or omega-3 supplements (most common)</li>
<li>Increased cardiovascular and inflammatory risk</li>
<li>Associated with higher rates of depression, anxiety, and cognitive decline</li>
<li>Common in Western diets dominated by omega-6 fatty acids</li>
<li>Higher AA/EPA ratio indicates pro-inflammatory balance</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Elevated EPA:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Reflects adequate omega-3 intake or supplementation</li>
<li>Very high levels (&gt;200 mcg/mL) may slightly increase bleeding time, though clinically significant bleeding is rare</li>
<li>Associated with reduced cardiovascular events, improved triglyceride profiles, and lower inflammatory markers (hs-CRP)</li>
</ul>
