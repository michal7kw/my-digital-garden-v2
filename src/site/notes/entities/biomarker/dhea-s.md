<h1>Dhea S</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Dehydroepiandrosterone sulfate (DHEA-S) is the sulfated form of DHEA, the most abundant circulating steroid hormone in the human body. Produced primarily by the zona reticularis of the adrenal cortex (with small contributions from the gonads), DHEA-S serves as a precursor hormone that can be converted into both androgens (testosterone, dihydrotestosterone) and estrogens (estradiol, estrone) in peripheral tissues. Its sulfated form has a much longer half-life (7-10 hours vs. 30 minutes for DHEA), making serum DHEA-S a more stable and reliable clinical measurement.</p>
<p>DHEA-S levels follow a distinctive age-related pattern: they peak in the mid-20s and then decline steadily at a rate of approximately 2-3% per year, such that by age 70-80, levels are only 10-20% of peak values. This age-related decline, termed &quot;adrenopause,&quot; has been the subject of considerable research regarding its role in aging, immune function, cognitive decline, and cardiovascular health. DHEA-S is often considered a biomarker of biological aging and adrenal reserve.</p>
<p>Clinically, DHEA-S is most frequently measured in the evaluation of androgen excess (hirsutism, acne, virilization) in women and adrenal insufficiency in both sexes. Because it is produced almost exclusively by the adrenal glands, elevated DHEA-S specifically points to an adrenal source of excess androgen production, helping distinguish adrenal from ovarian causes of hyperandrogenism.</p>

<h2>Clinical Interpretation</h2>
<p><strong>Elevated DHEA-S:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Adrenal hyperandrogenism</li>
<li>Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) -- modest elevation</li>
<li>Congenital adrenal hyperplasia (21-hydroxylase deficiency)</li>
<li>Adrenal tumors (virilizing adenoma or carcinoma -- markedly elevated &gt;700 ug/dL)</li>
<li>Cushing disease (ACTH-dependent)</li>
<li>Exogenous DHEA supplementation</li>
<li>Premature adrenarche (in children)</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Low DHEA-S:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Adrenal insufficiency (primary or secondary)</li>
<li>Hypopituitarism</li>
<li>Normal aging (adrenopause)</li>
<li>Chronic glucocorticoid therapy (adrenal suppression)</li>
<li>Anorexia nervosa</li>
<li>Chronic illness / critical illness</li>
<li>Associated with increased all-cause mortality in some studies</li>
</ul>
