<h1>Anti-dsDNA Antibody</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Anti-dsDNA antibodies bind native double-stranded DNA and are a hallmark of systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE). They are part of the ACR/EULAR 2019 classification criteria for SLE. Three main assay types are used clinically: the Farr assay (gold standard, measures high-avidity binding), ELISA (most common, varies by manufacturer), and Crithidia luciliae immunofluorescence (most specific but less sensitive).</p>
<p>Rising titers often precede clinical flares by weeks to months, making anti-dsDNA one of the most useful serological markers for disease monitoring. Anti-dsDNA antibodies can deposit in glomeruli as immune complexes, driving lupus nephritis through complement activation. Monitoring anti-dsDNA alongside complement C3/C4 provides the best serological assessment of SLE disease activity.</p>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Diagnosis</strong>: Highly specific for SLE (&gt;95% specificity); part of ACR/EULAR 2019 classification criteria</li>
<li><strong>Disease monitoring</strong>: Titers fluctuate with disease activity; rising titers precede flares</li>
<li><strong>Lupus nephritis</strong>: High titers (&gt;200 IU/mL) correlate with renal involvement</li>
<li><strong>Complement consumption</strong>: Anti-dsDNA immune complexes activate complement cascade</li>
<li><strong>Assay selection</strong>: Farr assay (gold standard), ELISA (most common), Crithidia (most specific)</li>
</ul>
