<h1>Tsh</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Thyroid Stimulating Hormone (TSH, thyrotropin) is a glycoprotein hormone produced by the anterior pituitary gland that serves as the primary regulator of thyroid function. TSH stimulates the thyroid gland to produce and release thyroxine (T4) and triiodothyronine (T3), the metabolic hormones that regulate virtually every organ system in the body. TSH operates via a classic negative feedback loop: when circulating T4/T3 levels fall, TSH rises to stimulate thyroid hormone production; when T4/T3 levels are adequate, TSH is suppressed.</p>
<p>TSH is considered the single most sensitive and specific screening test for thyroid dysfunction. Because of the logarithmic relationship between TSH and free T4, small changes in thyroid hormone levels produce amplified changes in TSH. For example, a 50% reduction in free T4 can cause a 90-fold increase in TSH. This amplification makes TSH the earliest indicator of both hypothyroidism (elevated TSH) and hyperthyroidism (suppressed TSH), often detecting dysfunction before the patient becomes symptomatic or before free T4/T3 levels move outside their reference ranges (subclinical disease).</p>
<p>TSH is ordered as a first-line screening test for thyroid disease, which affects approximately 5-10% of the population (more common in women and with advancing age). It is also used to monitor levothyroxine dosing in hypothyroid patients, evaluate the cause of symptoms such as fatigue, weight changes, temperature intolerance, hair loss, anxiety, depression, and menstrual irregularities, and to screen high-risk populations (postpartum women, patients with autoimmune conditions, those with family history of thyroid disease, patients on amiodarone or lithium).</p>

<ul>
<li>There is ongoing debate about the upper limit of normal; some endocrinologists advocate narrowing it to 2.5-3.0 mIU/L based on epidemiological data.</li>
<li>TSH exhibits a circadian rhythm, peaking between 2-4 AM and reaching its nadir in the late afternoon; morning draws are recommended for consistency.</li>
<li>TSH increases slightly with age; adults over 70 may have a physiologically higher upper limit (up to 6-7 mIU/L).</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Interpretation</h2>
<p><strong>Elevated TSH (&gt;4.5 mIU/L) -- Hypothyroidism:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Hashimoto's thyroiditis (autoimmune; most common cause in iodine-sufficient areas)</li>
<li>Iodine deficiency (most common cause worldwide)</li>
<li>Post-thyroidectomy or post-radioactive iodine treatment</li>
<li>Medications: lithium, amiodarone, interferon-alpha, checkpoint inhibitors</li>
<li>Subclinical hypothyroidism (TSH elevated, free T4 normal): fatigue, mild weight gain, cold intolerance, constipation, dry skin, depression, elevated cholesterol</li>
<li>Overt hypothyroidism (TSH elevated, free T4 low): profound fatigue, significant weight gain, myxedema, bradycardia, cognitive impairment</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Suppressed TSH (&lt;0.45 mIU/L) -- Hyperthyroidism:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Graves' disease (autoimmune; most common cause)</li>
<li>Toxic multinodular goiter or toxic adenoma</li>
<li>Thyroiditis (subacute, postpartum, silent) -- transient hyperthyroidism from hormone release</li>
<li>Excessive levothyroxine or exogenous thyroid hormone intake</li>
<li>Subclinical hyperthyroidism (TSH low, free T4 normal): increased atrial fibrillation risk, accelerated bone loss</li>
<li>Overt hyperthyroidism (TSH suppressed, free T4/T3 elevated): weight loss, tremor, tachycardia, heat intolerance, anxiety, diarrhea, lid lag</li>
</ul>
