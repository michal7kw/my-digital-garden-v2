<h1>Esr</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Erythrocyte Sedimentation Rate (ESR) is a non-specific blood test that measures the rate at which red blood cells settle to the bottom of a test tube over one hour. The test indirectly measures the degree of inflammation present in the body. When inflammation is present, proteins such as fibrinogen and immunoglobulins coat red blood cells, causing them to clump together (rouleaux formation) and settle more rapidly.</p>
<p>ESR has been used clinically for over a century and remains a valuable screening tool for inflammatory conditions, infections, and autoimmune diseases. While it lacks specificity for any single condition, it is particularly useful for monitoring disease activity in temporal arteritis (giant cell arteritis), polymyalgia rheumatica, and rheumatoid arthritis. ESR tends to change more slowly than C-reactive protein (CRP), making it better suited for tracking chronic inflammatory conditions rather than acute changes.</p>
<p>It is important to note that ESR naturally increases with age and tends to be higher in women than in men. Anemia, pregnancy, and obesity can also elevate ESR independent of inflammation, while polycythemia, sickle cell disease, and extreme leukocytosis can falsely lower the result.</p>

<h2>Clinical Interpretation</h2>
<p><strong>Elevated ESR (&gt;30 mm/hr):</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Acute or chronic infections (bacterial, viral, fungal)</li>
<li>Autoimmune diseases (rheumatoid arthritis, lupus, vasculitis)</li>
<li>Temporal arteritis / polymyalgia rheumatica (ESR often &gt;50 mm/hr)</li>
<li>Malignancies (multiple myeloma, lymphoma)</li>
<li>Chronic kidney disease</li>
<li>Anemia</li>
<li>Pregnancy (physiological elevation)</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Very High ESR (&gt;100 mm/hr):</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Strongly suggests serious underlying pathology: infection, malignancy, or autoimmune disease</li>
<li>Multiple myeloma and other paraproteinemias are classic causes</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Low ESR (&lt;1 mm/hr):</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Polycythemia vera</li>
<li>Sickle cell disease</li>
<li>Severe leukocytosis</li>
<li>Congestive heart failure</li>
<li>Hypofibrinogenemia</li>
<li>Generally not clinically significant as an isolated finding</li>
</ul>
