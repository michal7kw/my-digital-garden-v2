<h1>Complement C4</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Complement C4 is an essential component of the classical and lectin complement activation pathways. It is cleaved during pathway activation, making its serum level a useful indicator of complement consumption in immune complex-mediated diseases.</p>
<p>C4 is often the first complement component to decrease during SLE flares because it sits upstream in the classical pathway, closer to the point of immune complex activation. Genetically low C4 (C4 null alleles, particularly C4A deficiency) is itself an independent risk factor for SLE development, complicating interpretation in some patients. Serial monitoring alongside C3 and anti-dsDNA is standard for SLE disease activity assessment.</p>
<p>The pattern of C4 levels relative to C3 provides important diagnostic information. Low C4 with normal C3 suggests early or mild classical pathway activation, or may indicate hereditary angioedema (C1-INH deficiency). Low C4 with low C3 indicates more extensive complement consumption, typically seen in active lupus nephritis or severe immune complex disease. C4 levels are also reduced in cryoglobulinemia.</p>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>SLE monitoring</strong>: Often the earliest complement component to fall during flares</li>
<li><strong>Hereditary angioedema</strong>: Persistently low C4 is a screening marker for HAE (C1-INH deficiency)</li>
<li><strong>Genetic considerations</strong>: C4A null alleles are a risk factor for SLE; baseline C4 may be constitutively low</li>
<li><strong>Pattern interpretation</strong>: Low C4 + normal C3 = early classical activation or HAE; low C4 + low C3 = extensive consumption</li>
<li><strong>Cryoglobulinemia</strong>: C4 is characteristically very low in mixed cryoglobulinemia</li>
</ul>
