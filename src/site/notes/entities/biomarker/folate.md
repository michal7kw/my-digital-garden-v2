<h1>Folate</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Folate (vitamin B9) is a water-soluble B-vitamin that is essential for one-carbon metabolism, DNA synthesis, DNA repair, and methylation reactions throughout the body. The term &quot;folate&quot; refers to the naturally occurring forms found in food (primarily as polyglutamate derivatives in dark leafy greens, legumes, and liver), while &quot;folic acid&quot; is the synthetic oxidized form used in supplements and food fortification. Active folate in the body exists primarily as 5-methyltetrahydrofolate (5-MTHF), which serves as the methyl donor for the conversion of homocysteine to methionine.</p>
<p>Folate status is critically important during periods of rapid cell division and growth, making it essential during pregnancy (prevention of neural tube defects), infancy, and adolescence. Folate deficiency leads to impaired DNA synthesis, resulting in megaloblastic anemia characterized by large, immature red blood cells. Beyond hematologic effects, inadequate folate status contributes to elevated homocysteine levels (a cardiovascular risk factor), impaired neurotransmitter synthesis, and increased cancer risk through aberrant DNA methylation and impaired DNA repair mechanisms.</p>
<p>Serum folate reflects recent dietary intake (days), while red blood cell (RBC) folate provides a longer-term view of folate status (months). In clinical practice, serum folate is most commonly measured, but RBC folate may be preferred when recent dietary changes could affect serum levels. Folate metabolism is closely interlinked with vitamin B12 (the &quot;methyl trap&quot; hypothesis), and B12 deficiency should always be excluded before treating folate deficiency to avoid masking B12-related neurological damage.</p>

<h2>Clinical Interpretation</h2>
<p><strong>Elevated Folate (&gt;20 ng/mL serum):</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Excessive supplementation (generally not harmful but may mask B12 deficiency)</li>
<li>High folic acid intake from fortified foods</li>
<li>May accelerate progression of pre-existing colorectal adenomas (controversial)</li>
<li>Possible association with unmetabolized folic acid and immune dysfunction at very high synthetic intakes</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Low Folate (&lt;3.0 ng/mL serum):</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Megaloblastic / macrocytic anemia</li>
<li>Neural tube defects risk (in pregnancy)</li>
<li>Elevated homocysteine and increased cardiovascular risk</li>
<li>Impaired DNA methylation</li>
<li>Increased risk of colorectal and other cancers</li>
<li>Depression and cognitive impairment</li>
<li>Causes: inadequate dietary intake, malabsorption (celiac disease, IBD, gastric bypass), alcoholism, medications (methotrexate, phenytoin, trimethoprim, sulfasalazine), increased demand (pregnancy, hemolytic anemia)</li>
</ul>
