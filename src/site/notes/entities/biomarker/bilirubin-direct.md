<h1>Bilirubin Direct</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Direct bilirubin (conjugated bilirubin) is the water-soluble form of bilirubin that has been processed by the liver. After unconjugated bilirubin is taken up by hepatocytes, the enzyme UDP-glucuronosyltransferase (UGT1A1) conjugates it with one or two glucuronic acid molecules, producing bilirubin mono- and diglucuronide. This conjugated form is then actively secreted into the bile canaliculi and ultimately excreted via the biliary system into the intestine.</p>
<p>Measurement of direct bilirubin is essential for differentiating the causes of hyperbilirubinemia. When direct bilirubin constitutes more than 50% of total bilirubin, this indicates either hepatocellular damage (impaired excretion of conjugated bilirubin) or biliary obstruction (physical blockage of bile flow). Direct hyperbilirubinemia is always pathological and requires clinical investigation, unlike isolated indirect hyperbilirubinemia which can be benign (as in Gilbert syndrome).</p>
<p>A particularly important clinical distinction is that direct bilirubin, being water-soluble, can be excreted by the kidneys, causing dark-colored urine (bilirubinuria) -- a sign that is never present in purely hemolytic (pre-hepatic) jaundice. The presence of conjugated bilirubin in urine is an early and sensitive indicator of hepatobiliary disease, sometimes appearing before clinical jaundice is evident.</p>

<h2>Clinical Interpretation</h2>
<p><strong>Elevated Direct Bilirubin (&gt;0.3 mg/dL):</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Hepatocellular disease:</strong> Viral hepatitis, alcoholic hepatitis, drug-induced liver injury, cirrhosis</li>
<li><strong>Biliary obstruction:</strong> Choledocholithiasis (gallstones in common bile duct), pancreatic head carcinoma, cholangiocarcinoma</li>
<li><strong>Intrahepatic cholestasis:</strong> Primary biliary cholangitis, primary sclerosing cholangitis, drug-induced cholestasis, intrahepatic cholestasis of pregnancy</li>
<li><strong>Inherited disorders:</strong> Dubin-Johnson syndrome, Rotor syndrome</li>
<li><strong>Sepsis:</strong> Cholestasis of sepsis / critical illness</li>
<li><strong>Infiltrative diseases:</strong> Hepatic metastases, amyloidosis, sarcoidosis</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Low Direct Bilirubin:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Not clinically significant; direct bilirubin is normally near zero in healthy individuals</li>
</ul>
