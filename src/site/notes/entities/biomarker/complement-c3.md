<h1>Complement C3</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Complement C3 is the most abundant complement protein in the blood and the convergence point of all three complement activation pathways: classical, lectin, and alternative. It plays a central role in innate immunity, opsonization, and inflammatory signaling.</p>
<p>In autoimmune diseases, especially systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE), immune complexes activate the classical pathway, consuming C3 and C4. Serial monitoring of C3 alongside C4 and anti-dsDNA antibodies is standard practice for tracking SLE disease activity. Falling C3 levels often precede clinical flares by weeks, making it a valuable early warning biomarker.</p>
<p>The pattern of complement consumption provides diagnostic clues. Low C3 with low C4 strongly suggests immune complex-mediated classical pathway activation. Low C3 with normal C4 may indicate alternative pathway activation, as seen in C3 glomerulopathy. C3 is also relevant in membranoproliferative glomerulonephritis and cryoglobulinemia.</p>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>SLE monitoring</strong>: Serial C3 measurement tracks disease activity; falling levels precede flares</li>
<li><strong>Lupus nephritis</strong>: Low C3 is a hallmark of active renal involvement</li>
<li><strong>Pathway differentiation</strong>: Low C3 + low C4 = classical pathway; low C3 + normal C4 = alternative pathway</li>
<li><strong>Prognostic value</strong>: Persistently low C3 in SLE is associated with worse renal outcomes</li>
<li><strong>Other conditions</strong>: Relevant in MPGN, cryoglobulinemia, and post-infectious glomerulonephritis</li>
</ul>
