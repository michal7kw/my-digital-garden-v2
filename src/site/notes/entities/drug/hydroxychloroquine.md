<h1>Hydroxychloroquine</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Hydroxychloroquine (HCQ) is an antimalarial aminoquinoline that has been repurposed as a disease-modifying antirheumatic drug. It is a cornerstone therapy for systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE), where it reduces flares, organ damage accrual, and mortality, and is recommended for virtually all SLE patients unless contraindicated. In rheumatoid arthritis, it is often used in combination DMARD regimens (particularly triple therapy with methotrexate and sulfasalazine).</p>
<p>HCQ has an exceptionally long half-life of 40-50 days, meaning therapeutic steady-state levels may take several months to achieve. Clinical benefit in RA and SLE typically becomes apparent after 2-6 months of continuous use. The drug is generally well tolerated, with the most significant long-term concern being retinal toxicity, which is cumulative dose-dependent. Current guidelines recommend baseline ophthalmologic screening and annual screening after 5 years of use (or sooner with risk factors).</p>
<p>Beyond its immunomodulatory effects, hydroxychloroquine has favorable metabolic properties including improved lipid profiles and reduced insulin resistance, and it has antithrombotic effects that are particularly beneficial in SLE patients at risk for antiphospholipid-related events.</p>
<h2>Mechanism of Action</h2>
<p>Hydroxychloroquine is a weak base that accumulates in acidic intracellular compartments, particularly lysosomes and endosomes, raising their pH. This alkalinization disrupts antigen processing and presentation by major histocompatibility complex (MHC) class II molecules, reducing the activation of CD4+ T cells and downstream adaptive immune responses.</p>
<p>A key mechanism involves inhibition of Toll-like receptor (TLR) 7 and TLR9 signaling in plasmacytoid dendritic cells. These endosomal TLRs normally recognize nucleic acid ligands (single-stranded RNA and unmethylated CpG DNA, respectively) and trigger type I interferon production, a central pathogenic pathway in SLE. By raising endosomal pH, HCQ prevents nucleic acid ligands from binding their receptors, suppressing the interferon signature. Additionally, HCQ inhibits cyclic GMP-AMP synthase (cGAS) and the STING pathway, further reducing innate immune activation. It also suppresses pro-inflammatory cytokine release (TNF-alpha, IL-1, IL-6) from monocytes and macrophages.</p>
