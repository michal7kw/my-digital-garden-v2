<h1>Infliximab</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Infliximab (Remicade) is a chimeric (approximately 75% human, 25% murine) IgG1 monoclonal antibody directed against tumor necrosis factor alpha (TNF-alpha). It was the first anti-TNF biologic approved for rheumatoid arthritis and remains a cornerstone therapy for inflammatory bowel disease. The chimeric nature of the antibody means it contains murine variable regions responsible for TNF-alpha binding grafted onto a human IgG1 constant region framework.</p>
<p>A key distinguishing feature of infliximab is its ability to bind both soluble and transmembrane TNF-alpha with high avidity, enabling it to induce complement-dependent cytotoxicity (CDC) and antibody-dependent cell-mediated cytotoxicity (ADCC) against TNF-expressing cells. This property makes infliximab particularly effective in granulomatous diseases such as Crohn's disease, where transmembrane TNF on activated macrophages in granulomas is a primary driver of tissue inflammation. This contrasts with etanercept, which cannot lyse such cells and is consequently ineffective in Crohn's disease.</p>
<p>Infliximab is administered intravenously, typically at 3-5 mg/kg at weeks 0, 2, and 6 as induction, followed by maintenance infusions every 8 weeks. Co-administration with methotrexate is strongly recommended in RA to reduce anti-drug antibody formation and maintain drug levels. The chimeric structure makes infliximab more immunogenic than fully human antibodies, and anti-drug antibodies can reduce efficacy and increase infusion reaction risk over time.</p>
