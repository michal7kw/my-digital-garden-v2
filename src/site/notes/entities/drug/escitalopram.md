<h1>Escitalopram</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Escitalopram (Lexapro/Cipralex) is the therapeutically active S-enantiomer of citalopram. By removing the pharmacologically inert (and potentially counterproductive) R-enantiomer, escitalopram achieves the same SERT inhibition at half the milligram dose, with improved tolerability and a better cardiac safety profile.</p>
<p>Escitalopram is widely considered the best-tolerated SSRI and is first-line in many treatment guidelines for both depression and generalized anxiety disorder. In the landmark CAN-BIND and other comparative studies, escitalopram consistently shows comparable or superior efficacy to other SSRIs with fewer discontinuations due to side effects.</p>
<h2>Mechanism of Action</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>SERT inhibition</strong>: Ki 1.1 nM — most selective SERT inhibitor available</li>
<li><strong>Allosteric binding site</strong>: Escitalopram may bind to both the primary (orthosteric) and a secondary (allosteric) site on SERT, potentially enhancing binding duration and selectivity</li>
<li><strong>No off-target activity</strong>: Essentially zero affinity for histamine, muscarinic, adrenergic, or dopamine receptors at therapeutic doses</li>
<li><strong>Reduced hERG blockade</strong>: Lower QT prolongation risk compared to racemic citalopram (R-enantiomer has higher hERG affinity)</li>
<li><strong>Minimal CYP inhibition</strong>: Very low inhibition of CYP1A2, CYP2D6, CYP3A4 → few drug-drug interactions</li>
</ul>

<h2>Pharmacogenomics</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>CYP2C19</strong>: Primary metabolic pathway. Poor metabolizers have ~2x plasma levels — consider dose reduction</li>
<li><strong>CYP3A4</strong>: Secondary pathway</li>
<li><strong>No significant CYP2D6 involvement</strong>: A key advantage — CYP2D6 poor metabolizers handle escitalopram normally</li>
<li><strong>Clinical testing</strong>: CPIC guidelines recommend dose adjustment for CYP2C19 poor/ultra-rapid metabolizers</li>
</ul>
