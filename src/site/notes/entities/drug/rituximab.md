<h1>Rituximab</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Rituximab (Rituxan/MabThera) is a chimeric mouse-human monoclonal antibody targeting the CD20 antigen expressed on the surface of pre-B and mature B lymphocytes. Originally developed for B-cell non-Hodgkin lymphoma, its demonstrated efficacy in autoimmune diseases transformed the treatment landscape for rheumatoid arthritis, ANCA-associated vasculitis, and numerous other conditions. It was the first B-cell depleting therapy approved for RA and remains a cornerstone treatment after TNF-inhibitor failure.</p>
<p>In rheumatoid arthritis, rituximab is administered as two 1000 mg intravenous infusions separated by two weeks, given in combination with methotrexate. Retreatment courses are typically administered every 6 months based on clinical response, though some patients maintain remission for longer intervals. The REFLEX trial established efficacy in TNF-inadequate responders, and subsequent studies (IMAGE, SERENE) explored its use earlier in the treatment algorithm. Seropositive patients (RF+ and/or anti-CCP+) show the strongest clinical responses.</p>
<p>Rituximab's safety profile includes infusion-related reactions (most common during the first infusion), increased susceptibility to infections particularly with prolonged use, and rare but serious risks including progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy (PML) and hepatitis B reactivation. Screening for hepatitis B is mandatory before treatment initiation, and immunoglobulin levels should be monitored during repeated courses, as hypogammaglobulinemia can develop and increases infection risk.</p>
<h2>Mechanism of Action</h2>
<p>Rituximab binds the CD20 transmembrane phosphoprotein expressed on B lymphocytes from the pre-B cell stage through mature B cells, but absent on hematopoietic stem cells, pro-B cells, and terminally differentiated plasma cells. B-cell depletion occurs through three complementary mechanisms: antibody-dependent cell-mediated cytotoxicity (ADCC) mediated by NK cells and macrophages recognizing the Fc region; complement-dependent cytotoxicity (CDC) through C1q binding and membrane attack complex formation; and direct signaling-induced apoptosis via CD20 cross-linking. The resulting B-cell depletion reduces autoantibody production (RF, anti-CCP), diminishes B-cell antigen presentation to T cells, and decreases pro-inflammatory cytokine release (TNF-alpha, IL-6, lymphotoxin) from B cells in the rheumatoid synovium.</p>
