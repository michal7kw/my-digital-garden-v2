<h1>Low Dose Naltrexone</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Low Dose Naltrexone (LDN) uses naltrexone at 1.5-4.5 mg (compared to the standard 50 mg dose for opioid/alcohol addiction under brand names ReVia/Vivitrol) to achieve immunomodulatory and anti-inflammatory effects through a fundamentally different mechanism. At these ultra-low doses, naltrexone briefly blocks opioid receptors for 4-6 hours, triggering a compensatory upregulation of endogenous opioid peptides (endorphins, enkephalins, OGF) and opioid receptor density. LDN also modulates Toll-like receptor 4 (TLR4) on microglia and macrophages, reducing neuroinflammation and systemic inflammatory signaling. It is prescribed off-label by compounding pharmacies for autoimmune conditions (MS, Crohn's, Hashimoto's), fibromyalgia, chronic fatigue syndrome, and as adjunctive cancer support. While clinical trial evidence is growing, most evidence comes from small trials and case series.</p>
<h2>Mechanism of Action</h2>
<p>At low doses (1.5-4.5 mg taken at bedtime), naltrexone produces a transient, partial opioid receptor blockade lasting approximately 4-6 hours. This brief blockade triggers a rebound upregulation of endogenous opioid production (beta-endorphin, met-enkephalin, opioid growth factor/OGF) and opioid receptor expression, resulting in enhanced endogenous opioid signaling for the remaining 18-20 hours of the day. The immunomodulatory effect is mediated through a separate mechanism: antagonism of Toll-like receptor 4 (TLR4) on microglia and immune cells, reducing the production of pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-6, TNF-alpha, NF-kB signaling). The OGF-OGFr axis modulation may also contribute to anti-proliferative effects relevant to cancer research. This dual mechanism (endorphin upregulation + TLR4 modulation) distinguishes LDN pharmacologically from standard-dose naltrexone.</p>
<h2>Clinical Uses</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Fibromyalgia</strong>: 4.5 mg at bedtime; most studied LDN indication (multiple small RCTs showing 30% pain reduction)</li>
<li><strong>Multiple sclerosis</strong>: 4.5 mg/day; preliminary evidence for quality of life improvement and fatigue reduction</li>
<li><strong>Crohn's disease</strong>: 4.5 mg/day; small RCT showed clinical remission in 89% vs 40% placebo</li>
<li><strong>Hashimoto's thyroiditis</strong>: 1.5-4.5 mg/day; case reports of reduced thyroid antibodies and improved symptoms</li>
<li><strong>Chronic fatigue syndrome</strong>: Growing off-label use for fatigue and immune dysregulation</li>
<li><strong>Cancer support (adjunctive)</strong>: Investigational; OGF-OGFr pathway modulation may have anti-proliferative effects</li>
<li><strong>Complex regional pain syndrome</strong>: Off-label use for neuroinflammatory pain conditions</li>
<li><strong>Note</strong>: Must be compounded; not available as a commercial LDN product</li>
</ul>
