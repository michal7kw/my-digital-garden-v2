<h1>Metoprolol</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Metoprolol is a cardioselective beta-1 blocker available in two formulations: metoprolol tartrate (Lopressor, immediate-release, twice-daily) and metoprolol succinate (Toprol-XL, extended-release, once-daily). It is one of the most prescribed cardiovascular medications worldwide, with indications including hypertension, angina, heart failure (succinate only), post-MI protection, and arrhythmias. The succinate formulation is specifically FDA-approved for heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF) based on the MERIT-HF trial, which demonstrated a 34% reduction in mortality. Metoprolol is metabolized by CYP2D6, and poor metabolizers may experience exaggerated effects.</p>
<h2>Mechanism of Action</h2>
<p>Metoprolol selectively blocks beta-1 adrenergic receptors in cardiac tissue, reducing the effects of catecholamines (epinephrine and norepinephrine) on heart rate, contractility, and conduction velocity. This decreases myocardial oxygen demand and lowers blood pressure through reduced cardiac output. At therapeutic doses, metoprolol is relatively cardioselective, sparing beta-2 receptors in bronchial and vascular smooth muscle, though selectivity diminishes at higher doses. In heart failure, chronic beta-blockade reverses pathologic catecholamine-driven remodeling, improving left ventricular ejection fraction and reducing mortality.</p>
<h2>Clinical Uses</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Hypertension</strong>: Tartrate 50-200 mg BID or succinate 25-400 mg once daily</li>
<li><strong>Heart failure (HFrEF)</strong>: Succinate 12.5-200 mg/day; titrate slowly over weeks (MERIT-HF)</li>
<li><strong>Post-MI</strong>: Tartrate 25-100 mg BID; reduces reinfarction and mortality</li>
<li><strong>Angina pectoris</strong>: Reduces frequency and severity of angina episodes</li>
<li><strong>Supraventricular arrhythmias</strong>: Rate control in atrial fibrillation/flutter</li>
<li><strong>Migraine prophylaxis</strong>: Off-label; evidence-supported for migraine prevention</li>
</ul>
