<h1>Sulfasalazine</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Sulfasalazine (SSZ) is a disease-modifying antirheumatic drug originally developed in the 1940s by Swedish physician Nana Svartz, who combined sulfapyridine (an antibiotic) with 5-aminosalicylic acid (an anti-inflammatory) linked by a diazo bond. It was designed to treat rheumatoid arthritis based on the then-prevalent theory that RA had an infectious etiology. While that hypothesis proved incorrect, the drug was found to be effective in both RA and inflammatory bowel disease through distinct mechanisms.</p>
<p>In rheumatoid arthritis, sulfasalazine is particularly useful for seronegative disease and is a component of the widely used triple DMARD therapy (methotrexate + sulfasalazine + hydroxychloroquine). It is also a first-line DMARD for ankylosing spondylitis and psoriatic arthritis with peripheral joint involvement. The drug is considered relatively safe, with GI side effects being the most common reason for discontinuation. Enteric-coated formulations and gradual dose escalation (starting at 500 mg/day and increasing to 2-3 g/day over weeks) improve tolerability.</p>
<p>Sulfasalazine is unique among DMARDs in that it is considered safe in pregnancy (Category B), making it an important option for women of childbearing age with RA. However, it causes reversible oligospermia in men, which should be discussed before initiating therapy.</p>
<h2>Mechanism of Action</h2>
<p>Sulfasalazine is a prodrug that passes largely intact through the stomach and small intestine. In the colon, bacterial azoreductases cleave the diazo bond, releasing sulfapyridine and 5-aminosalicylic acid (5-ASA, mesalamine). For inflammatory bowel disease, 5-ASA is the primary active component, acting topically on the colonic mucosa. For rheumatic diseases, sulfapyridine is the systemically absorbed active moiety.</p>
<p>Sulfapyridine exerts its immunomodulatory effects through multiple mechanisms: it inhibits NF-kB activation by preventing IkB-alpha degradation, thereby suppressing transcription of pro-inflammatory cytokines (TNF-alpha, IL-1, IL-6, IL-8). It reduces B-cell function and immunoglobulin production, impairs natural killer cell activity, and inhibits folate-dependent enzymes involved in lymphocyte proliferation. Sulfasalazine also inhibits the release of inflammatory mediators from neutrophils and scavenges reactive oxygen species. The net effect is broad suppression of both innate and adaptive immune responses in the joint.</p>
