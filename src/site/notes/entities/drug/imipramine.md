<h1>Imipramine</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Imipramine (Tofranil) was the first tricyclic antidepressant discovered (1957) and established the TCA class as a major treatment for depression. Beyond depression, imipramine remains the only TCA FDA-approved for nocturnal enuresis (bedwetting) in children aged 6 and older, likely through its anticholinergic effects on bladder musculature and alteration of sleep architecture. It is also effective for panic disorder, making it historically significant as the first drug shown to block panic attacks. Imipramine is metabolized to desipramine, a more norepinephrine-selective active metabolite.</p>
<h2>Mechanism of Action</h2>
<p>Imipramine inhibits the reuptake of both norepinephrine (NET) and serotonin (SERT) at presynaptic nerve terminals, with slightly greater noradrenergic than serotonergic activity. Its active metabolite desipramine is a potent and selective norepinephrine reuptake inhibitor. Like other TCAs, imipramine also antagonizes histamine H1 receptors (sedation), muscarinic acetylcholine receptors (anticholinergic effects), and alpha-1 adrenergic receptors (orthostatic hypotension). The anti-enuresis effect involves multiple mechanisms: anticholinergic-mediated increase in bladder capacity, alteration of sleep architecture reducing enuresis events, and direct stimulatory effect on the internal urethral sphincter via alpha-adrenergic activity.</p>
<h2>Clinical Uses</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Depression</strong>: 75-200 mg/day; historically first-line, now reserved for SSRI/SNRI failures</li>
<li><strong>Nocturnal enuresis</strong>: 25-75 mg at bedtime in children &gt;=6 years; FDA-approved</li>
<li><strong>Panic disorder</strong>: Effective at antidepressant doses; one of the first drugs proven for panic</li>
<li><strong>Neuropathic pain</strong>: Used similarly to amitriptyline for chronic pain conditions</li>
<li><strong>ADHD</strong>: Historical use, largely replaced by stimulants and atomoxetine</li>
</ul>
