<h1>Caffeine</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Caffeine is the most widely consumed psychoactive substance on Earth, with approximately 80% of the world's population consuming it daily through coffee, tea, energy drinks, and chocolate. It is a methylxanthine alkaloid that exerts its primary effects through competitive antagonism of adenosine receptors — particularly A1 (widespread cortical/hippocampal) and A2A (concentrated in the striatum). By blocking adenosine's inhibitory tone, caffeine indirectly enhances dopaminergic, noradrenergic, and cholinergic neurotransmission.</p>
<p>Caffeine is notable in the context of this knowledge graph for its extensive interactions with the other core entities. It synergizes with L-Theanine (the classic &quot;calm alertness&quot; stack), with L-Tyrosine (enhanced catecholamine support under stress), and with Nicotine (complementary alerting mechanisms via adenosine vs. nAChR pathways). Its interaction with Creatine is debated — some studies suggest caffeine attenuates creatine's ergogenic effects on muscle performance, though neuroprotective benefits appear preserved. Caffeine also shares Nicotine's association with reduced Parkinson's disease risk, likely through A2A receptor antagonism that protects dopaminergic neurons.</p>
<p><strong>Evidence Level:</strong> 5/5</p>
<h2>Mechanism of Action</h2>
<h3>Adenosine Receptor Antagonism (Primary)</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>A1 receptors</strong> (cortex, hippocampus, cerebellum): Blockade removes adenosine's presynaptic inhibition of excitatory neurotransmitter release, increasing acetylcholine, glutamate, and dopamine release. This is the primary mechanism for cognitive enhancement and wakefulness.</li>
<li><strong>A2A receptors</strong> (striatum, nucleus accumbens): Blockade disrupts the A2A-D2 receptor heterodimer, disinhibiting dopamine D2 signaling. This enhances motivation, reward processing, and motor function. Also the primary mechanism for Parkinson's neuroprotection.</li>
<li>Adenosine normally accumulates during wakefulness and promotes sleep — caffeine blocks this sleep pressure signal</li>
</ul>
<h3>Phosphodiesterase Inhibition (Secondary)</h3>
<ul>
<li>At concentrations above typical dietary intake (~100 uM), caffeine inhibits PDE3 and PDE4</li>
<li>PDE inhibition increases intracellular cAMP and cGMP</li>
<li>Contributes to bronchodilation, cardiac stimulation, and enhanced neurotransmitter signaling</li>
<li>This mechanism becomes more relevant at higher doses (&gt;400 mg)</li>
</ul>
<h3>Downstream Neurotransmitter Effects</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Dopamine</strong>: Increased via A2A antagonism in striatum and A1 antagonism in cortex</li>
<li><strong>Norepinephrine</strong>: Increased via disinhibition of locus coeruleus firing</li>
<li><strong>Acetylcholine</strong>: Increased via A1 antagonism in basal forebrain</li>
<li><strong>Cortisol/Epinephrine</strong>: Stimulates HPA axis and sympathetic adrenal-medullary system (acute stress response)</li>
</ul>
<h3>Tolerance</h3>
<ul>
<li>Chronic caffeine consumption upregulates adenosine receptors (primarily A1)</li>
<li>This is the basis of physical dependence and withdrawal symptoms (headache, fatigue, irritability)</li>
<li>Full tolerance to most effects develops over 1-2 weeks of daily use at fixed doses</li>
<li>Partial tolerance: alerting effects persist partly even with chronic use</li>
</ul>
<h2>Dosage</h2>
<h3>Cognitive Enhancement</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Low dose</strong>: 40-100 mg — subtle alerting, improved reaction time</li>
<li><strong>Standard</strong>: 100-200 mg — peak cognitive enhancement for most people</li>
<li><strong>High</strong>: 200-400 mg — may increase anxiety; individual variation is large</li>
</ul>
<h3>Ergogenic (Athletic Performance)</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Standard protocol</strong>: 3-6 mg/kg body weight, 30-60 minutes pre-exercise</li>
<li><strong>Peak performance</strong>: Appears at ~3 mg/kg for most individuals; higher doses increase side effects without proportional benefit</li>
</ul>
<h3>Genetic Variation (CYP1A2)</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Fast metabolizers</strong> (CYP1A2 *1A/*1A): Half-life 2-3 hours; benefit more from caffeine, tolerate higher doses</li>
<li><strong>Slow metabolizers</strong> (CYP1A2 *1F carriers): Half-life 6-10 hours; increased anxiety, insomnia, and cardiovascular risk at same doses</li>
<li>Approximately 50% of the population are slow metabolizers</li>
</ul>
<h3>Synergistic Stacks</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Caffeine + L-Theanine</strong> (1:2 ratio, e.g., 100mg + 200mg): L-Theanine reduces caffeine's jitteriness while preserving alerting effects</li>
<li><strong>Caffeine + L-Tyrosine</strong>: Enhanced catecholamine support; useful for sustained cognitive performance under stress</li>
<li><strong>Caffeine + Nicotine</strong>: Complementary mechanisms (adenosine vs. nAChR); additive alerting without cross-tolerance</li>
</ul>
<h3>Interaction with Creatine</h3>
<ul>
<li>Some studies show caffeine attenuates creatine's ergogenic effects on high-intensity exercise (possibly via opposing effects on muscle relaxation time)</li>
<li>Cognitive and neuroprotective benefits of creatine appear unaffected by caffeine</li>
<li>Debated and study-dependent; likely dose and timing dependent</li>
</ul>
<h2>Key Research</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Mechanisms of action in the brain</strong>: Comprehensive review of caffeine's adenosine receptor antagonism and downstream neurotransmitter effects (PMID:20164566)</li>
<li><strong>Adenosine receptor pharmacology</strong>: Detailed analysis of A1 and A2A receptor signaling and caffeine's competitive antagonism (PMID:20182035)</li>
<li><strong>Caffeine + L-Theanine synergy</strong>: Combined supplementation improves attention and reduces susceptibility to mind wandering, with L-Theanine attenuating caffeine's anxiogenic effects (PMID:18088379)</li>
<li><strong>Caffeine-creatine interaction</strong>: Meta-analysis examining whether caffeine attenuates creatine's ergogenic effects; concludes interaction is dose-dependent and task-specific (PMID:27612937)</li>
<li><strong>Caffeine and Parkinson's</strong>: Prospective cohort studies showing dose-dependent reduction in Parkinson's disease risk with caffeine consumption, mediated by A2A receptor antagonism (PMID:26899133)</li>
</ul>
