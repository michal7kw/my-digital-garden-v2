<h1>Dulaglutide</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>GLP-1 receptor agonist offering once-weekly subcutaneous injection for the treatment of type 2 diabetes mellitus. Marketed as Trulicity (Eli Lilly), dulaglutide employs a unique dual-chain Fc-fusion architecture that distinguishes it from other GLP-1 agonists. Two identical GLP-1 analog chains are covalently linked to a modified human IgG4 Fc fragment, extending the half-life to approximately 5 days and enabling convenient weekly dosing. The AWARD clinical trial program demonstrated robust glycemic control with HbA1c reductions of 0.8-1.6%, while the landmark REWIND trial (Lancet 2019) established cardiovascular benefit with a 12% reduction in major adverse cardiovascular events (MACE) in patients with type 2 diabetes. Unlike semaglutide, dulaglutide is not approved for obesity management but remains a cornerstone GLP-1 agonist valued for its tolerability and simplicity of once-weekly administration.</p>
<h2>Mechanism of Action</h2>
<p>Dulaglutide is a long-acting GLP-1 receptor agonist with a distinctive dual-chain molecular architecture. It consists of two identical GLP-1 analog peptide chains (~90% homology to native human GLP-1, with amino acid substitutions at positions 8, 22, and 36 to resist DPP-4 degradation) covalently linked via flexible linkers to a modified human IgG4 Fc fragment. The Fc fusion exploits neonatal Fc receptor (FcRn)-mediated recycling to dramatically extend serum half-life to ~5 days, enabling once-weekly subcutaneous dosing. Upon binding GLP-1 receptors on pancreatic beta cells, dulaglutide enhances glucose-dependent insulin secretion, minimizing hypoglycemia risk. It simultaneously suppresses glucagon release from alpha cells in a glucose-dependent manner. Peripherally, dulaglutide delays gastric emptying, contributing to postprandial glucose control and satiety. Centrally, it activates GLP-1 receptors in the hypothalamus and brainstem nucleus tractus solitarius, reducing appetite and caloric intake. Emerging evidence from the REWIND trial suggests direct anti-inflammatory and cardioprotective effects on vascular endothelium, independent of glycemic control.</p>
<h2>Key Clinical Trials</h2>
<h3>AWARD Program (Assessment of Weekly AdministRation of LY2189265 in Diabetes)</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>AWARD-1</strong> (Diabetes Care 2014; PMID: 24879836): Superior HbA1c reduction vs exenatide twice daily at 26 and 52 weeks (1.5mg weekly) - Evidence Level 1b</li>
<li><strong>AWARD-2</strong> (Lancet 2014; PMID: 26089386): Superior glycemic control vs insulin glargine at 52 weeks - Evidence Level 1b</li>
<li><strong>AWARD-3</strong> (Diabetes Care 2014): Non-inferior to metformin in HbA1c reduction at 52 weeks - Evidence Level 1b</li>
<li><strong>AWARD-4</strong> (Diabetes Care 2014): Effective in combination with insulin lispro - Evidence Level 1b</li>
<li><strong>AWARD-5</strong> (Lancet 2014): Superior glycemic control vs sitagliptin at 104 weeks - Evidence Level 1b</li>
<li><strong>AWARD-6</strong> (Lancet 2014; PMID: 25061110): Non-inferior to liraglutide 1.8mg daily in glycemic control with fewer GI side effects - Evidence Level 1b</li>
<li><strong>AWARD-7</strong> (Diabetes Care 2018; PMID: 29118023): Comparable kidney outcomes to insulin glargine in T2DM with moderate-to-severe chronic kidney disease (CKD stages 3-4) - Evidence Level 1b</li>
<li><strong>AWARD-8</strong> (Diabetes Obes Metab 2019; PMID: 30614079): Effective as monotherapy in early T2DM - Evidence Level 1b</li>
<li><strong>AWARD-9</strong> (Diabetes Obes Metab 2020; PMID: 31778667): Superior glycemic control vs insulin glargine with SGLT2 inhibitor background - Evidence Level 1b</li>
<li><strong>AWARD-10</strong> (Diabetes Obes Metab 2020; PMID: 32198164): Superior glycemic control vs dulaglutide 1.5mg with SGLT2 inhibitor - Evidence Level 1b</li>
<li><strong>AWARD-11</strong> (Lancet Diabetes Endocrinol 2022; PMID: 35219920): 3.0mg and 4.5mg weekly doses provided additional HbA1c and weight reduction vs 1.5mg - Evidence Level 1b</li>
</ul>
<h3>REWIND Trial (Researching Cardiovascular Events With a Weekly Incretin in Diabetes)</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>REWIND</strong> (Lancet 2019; PMID: 31189526): 12% reduction in MACE (HR 0.88, 95% CI 0.79-0.99) in T2DM with broad cardiovascular risk profile (1.5mg weekly) - Evidence Level 1a</li>
</ul>
<h3>AWARD-Kidney (Emerging Renal Data)</h3>
<ul>
<li>Prespecified renal composite outcomes from REWIND showed favorable trends in macroalbuminuria reduction and eGFR preservation - Evidence Level 2a</li>
</ul>
<h2>Pharmacokinetics</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Absorption</strong>: Subcutaneous bioavailability ~65-80%, peak concentration at 24-72 hours post-injection</li>
<li><strong>Distribution</strong>: Extensive protein binding via Fc-mediated albumin binding, wide tissue distribution</li>
<li><strong>Metabolism</strong>: Proteolytic degradation by endogenous peptidases, not CYP-mediated</li>
<li><strong>Elimination</strong>: Excreted via urine and feces as proteolytic fragments and intact drug</li>
<li><strong>Half-life</strong>: ~5 days, enabling steady-state after 2-4 weeks of weekly dosing</li>
</ul>
<h2>Dosing and Administration</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Initial dose</strong>: 0.75mg subcutaneously once weekly</li>
<li><strong>Titration</strong>: May increase to 1.5mg weekly for additional glycemic control</li>
<li><strong>Higher doses</strong>: 3.0mg and 4.5mg weekly approved in some regions (US, EU) based on AWARD-11 data for patients requiring additional HbA1c and weight reduction</li>
<li><strong>Renal impairment</strong>: No dose adjustment required for mild-to-severe renal impairment; use caution in ESRD</li>
<li><strong>Hepatic impairment</strong>: No dose adjustment required for mild-to-moderate hepatic impairment</li>
<li><strong>Administration</strong>: Subcutaneous injection in abdomen, thigh, or upper arm; available as single-dose pen or prefilled syringe; can be taken with or without food at any time of day</li>
</ul>
<h2>Adverse Effects Profile</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Common (≥10%)</strong>: Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, constipation, abdominal pain, fatigue, decreased appetite</li>
<li><strong>Less Common (1-10%)</strong>: Hypoglycemia (with concomitant insulin/sulfonylureas), dyspepsia, dizziness, injection site reactions</li>
<li><strong>Rare (&lt;1%)</strong>: Acute pancreatitis, cholelithiasis, hypersensitivity reactions</li>
<li><strong>Serious Warnings</strong>: Thyroid C-cell tumor risk (rodent studies), contraindicated in MEN2 and personal/family history of medullary thyroid carcinoma, acute kidney injury (secondary to dehydration from GI effects)</li>
</ul>
