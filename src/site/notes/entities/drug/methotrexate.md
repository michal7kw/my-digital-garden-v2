<h1>Methotrexate</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Methotrexate (MTX) is the anchor disease-modifying antirheumatic drug (DMARD) and the first-line treatment for rheumatoid arthritis worldwide. Originally developed as a folate antimetabolite for cancer chemotherapy in the 1940s, its anti-inflammatory properties at low doses were recognized decades later, and it has since become the cornerstone of RA management.</p>
<p>At the doses used for RA (typically 7.5-25 mg once weekly, oral or subcutaneous), methotrexate is well tolerated by most patients. Folic acid supplementation (1 mg daily or 5 mg weekly) is standard co-therapy to reduce side effects such as nausea, stomatitis, and hepatotoxicity. Regular monitoring of complete blood counts and liver function tests is required throughout treatment.</p>
<p>Methotrexate is also widely used in psoriasis, psoriatic arthritis, juvenile idiopathic arthritis, and at much higher doses in oncology (acute lymphoblastic leukemia, osteosarcoma, lymphomas). Its efficacy as monotherapy and as a backbone for combination DMARD regimens (including with biologic agents) is supported by extensive clinical evidence spanning several decades.</p>
<h2>Mechanism of Action</h2>
<p>Methotrexate is a folate analogue that competitively inhibits dihydrofolate reductase (DHFR), the enzyme responsible for converting dihydrofolate to tetrahydrofolate, an essential cofactor for purine and pyrimidine synthesis. It also inhibits aminoimidazole carboxamide ribonucleotide (AICAR) transformylase, leading to accumulation of AICAR and subsequent increase in extracellular adenosine release.</p>
<p>At the low doses used in rheumatic diseases, the primary anti-inflammatory mechanism is now understood to be the adenosine-mediated pathway rather than direct antifolate effects. Increased extracellular adenosine acts on adenosine A2A and A2B receptors on immune cells, suppressing pro-inflammatory cytokine production (TNF-alpha, IL-1, IL-6), inhibiting NF-kB signaling, reducing neutrophil adhesion, and promoting an anti-inflammatory macrophage phenotype. Intracellularly, methotrexate is polyglutamated, which prolongs its retention in cells (weeks) and enhances its inhibitory effects on folate-dependent enzymes.</p>
