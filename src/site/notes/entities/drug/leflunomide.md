<h1>Leflunomide</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Leflunomide is a disease-modifying antirheumatic drug that inhibits pyrimidine synthesis, serving as an important alternative to methotrexate for patients with rheumatoid arthritis who cannot tolerate or have contraindications to MTX. Approved for RA in 1998, it has demonstrated efficacy comparable to methotrexate in clinical trials, with a distinct side effect profile.</p>
<p>The drug is administered orally, typically with a loading dose of 100 mg daily for 3 days followed by a maintenance dose of 20 mg daily (reduced to 10 mg daily if not tolerated). Its active metabolite, teriflunomide (A77 1726), has a very long half-life of 14-18 days, which has important clinical implications: steady state takes approximately 2 months to achieve without loading doses, and the drug persists in the body for extended periods after discontinuation. A cholestyramine washout procedure (8 g three times daily for 11 days) can accelerate elimination when rapid clearance is needed, such as before conception or in cases of serious toxicity.</p>
<p>Leflunomide is also used off-label for psoriatic arthritis and, less commonly, in combination with methotrexate for refractory RA (though this combination requires careful hepatic monitoring). Like methotrexate, it is absolutely contraindicated in pregnancy (Category X) and requires reliable contraception during treatment and for an extended period after discontinuation.</p>
<h2>Mechanism of Action</h2>
<p>Leflunomide is an isoxazole derivative that is rapidly and almost completely converted in the gut wall and liver to its active open-ring metabolite, teriflunomide (A77 1726). Teriflunomide reversibly inhibits dihydroorotate dehydrogenase (DHODH), a mitochondrial enzyme that catalyzes the fourth step in the de novo pyrimidine biosynthesis pathway, specifically the oxidation of dihydroorotate to orotate.</p>
<p>This mechanism exploits a fundamental difference between resting and activated lymphocytes. Resting cells can meet their pyrimidine requirements through the salvage pathway, recycling preformed nucleotides. However, activated T and B lymphocytes undergoing clonal expansion require an approximately 8-fold increase in pyrimidine pools and become critically dependent on the de novo synthesis pathway. By blocking DHODH, teriflunomide arrests activated lymphocytes in the S phase of the cell cycle, suppressing their proliferation without significantly affecting resting immune cells or other rapidly dividing cell types that have sufficient salvage pathway capacity. Secondary effects include inhibition of tyrosine kinase activity and reduced expression of adhesion molecules on endothelial cells.</p>
