<h1>Tofacitinib</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Tofacitinib (Xeljanz) is the first-in-class oral Janus kinase (JAK) inhibitor approved for the treatment of rheumatoid arthritis. As a small molecule, it represented a paradigm shift in RA treatment by offering an oral alternative to injectable biologic DMARDs. Tofacitinib preferentially inhibits JAK1 and JAK3, with functional selectivity over JAK2, thereby blocking intracellular signaling from multiple cytokine receptors that drive inflammatory and autoimmune pathology.</p>
<p>The drug is approved for moderate-to-severe rheumatoid arthritis (after methotrexate failure), psoriatic arthritis, ulcerative colitis, and juvenile idiopathic arthritis (polyarticular course). It is administered orally as either immediate-release (5 mg twice daily) or extended-release (11 mg once daily) formulations. Post-marketing surveillance identified increased cardiovascular and malignancy risks in older patients with cardiovascular risk factors, leading to an FDA boxed warning and restriction to patients who have failed TNF inhibitor therapy.</p>
<h2>Mechanism of Action</h2>
<p>Tofacitinib inhibits JAK1 and JAK3 preferentially, with lesser activity against JAK2 and minimal effect on TYK2. JAK1/JAK3 heterodimerize at gamma-chain cytokine receptors (shared by IL-2, IL-4, IL-7, IL-9, IL-15, and IL-21), making tofacitinib particularly effective at suppressing T-cell and NK cell activation. Additionally, JAK1 inhibition blocks IL-6 signaling through gp130 receptors, reducing acute phase reactant production and systemic inflammation.</p>
<p>By simultaneously dampening multiple cytokine signaling axes, tofacitinib reduces Th1 and Th17 differentiation, inhibits NK cell cytotoxicity, and decreases B-cell immunoglobulin production. This broad immunomodulatory profile explains both its therapeutic efficacy across multiple autoimmune conditions and its side effect profile, including increased susceptibility to herpes zoster reactivation (linked to impaired NK cell surveillance).</p>
