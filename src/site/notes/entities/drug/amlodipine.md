<h1>Amlodipine</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Amlodipine (Norvasc) is a long-acting dihydropyridine calcium channel blocker and one of the most prescribed antihypertensives worldwide. It relaxes vascular smooth muscle to reduce blood pressure and is used for hypertension, chronic stable angina, and vasospastic (Prinzmetal) angina. Its uniquely long half-life (30-50 hours) enables once-daily dosing with smooth 24-hour blood pressure control and minimal reflex tachycardia. Amlodipine is well-tolerated with predictable, dose-dependent side effects, the most common being peripheral edema (ankle swelling). It is frequently combined with other antihypertensives in fixed-dose combinations (e.g., amlodipine/benazepril, amlodipine/atorvastatin).</p>
<h2>Mechanism of Action</h2>
<p>Amlodipine selectively blocks L-type voltage-gated calcium channels in vascular smooth muscle cells, preventing calcium influx required for muscle contraction. This results in arterial vasodilation and reduced peripheral vascular resistance, lowering blood pressure without significant effects on cardiac conduction. Unlike non-dihydropyridine calcium channel blockers such as verapamil and diltiazem, amlodipine has minimal direct effects on cardiac contractility and atrioventricular node conduction. Its long duration of action stems from slow association with and dissociation from the calcium channel binding site, enabling once-daily dosing with stable 24-hour blood pressure control.</p>
<h2>Clinical Uses</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Hypertension</strong>: 2.5-10 mg once daily; effective as monotherapy or in combination</li>
<li><strong>Chronic stable angina</strong>: 5-10 mg/day; reduces frequency of angina episodes</li>
<li><strong>Vasospastic angina (Prinzmetal)</strong>: Particularly effective due to coronary vasodilation</li>
<li><strong>Raynaud phenomenon</strong>: Off-label use for vasospasm-related conditions</li>
<li><strong>Combination therapy</strong>: Available as Caduet (with atorvastatin), Lotrel (with benazepril)</li>
</ul>
