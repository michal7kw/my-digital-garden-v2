<h1>Levothyroxine</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Levothyroxine (Synthroid/Levoxyl/Tirosint) is a synthetic thyroxine (T4) and the standard of care for hypothyroidism, making it the most prescribed medication for thyroid disorders and one of the top 3 most prescribed drugs overall. It replaces the thyroid hormone that the body cannot produce sufficiently, with dose titration guided by TSH levels checked every 6-8 weeks until stable. Absorption is significantly affected by food and co-administered medications (calcium, iron, PPIs), requiring consistent empty-stomach administration 30-60 minutes before breakfast. Tirosint (liquid gel capsule) has improved absorption in patients with GI malabsorption. Narrow therapeutic index necessitates using the same brand/manufacturer consistently.</p>
<h2>Mechanism of Action</h2>
<p>Levothyroxine is a synthetic form of the endogenous thyroid hormone thyroxine (T4). After absorption, it is converted peripherally to the more active triiodothyronine (T3) by deiodinase enzymes, primarily in the liver and kidneys. T3 enters target cells and binds to nuclear thyroid hormone receptors, forming a complex that modulates transcription of genes regulating basal metabolic rate, protein synthesis, thermogenesis, and development. The long half-life of 6-7 days provides stable plasma levels with once-daily dosing, and dose adjustments are guided by TSH feedback through the hypothalamic-pituitary-thyroid axis.</p>
<h2>Clinical Uses</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Primary hypothyroidism</strong>: 1.6 mcg/kg/day (full replacement); most common indication</li>
<li><strong>Hashimoto's thyroiditis</strong>: Lifelong replacement as thyroid function progressively declines</li>
<li><strong>Post-thyroidectomy</strong>: Full replacement dose required after total thyroidectomy</li>
<li><strong>TSH suppression</strong>: Higher doses for differentiated thyroid cancer to suppress TSH-driven tumor growth</li>
<li><strong>Myxedema coma</strong>: IV levothyroxine for this medical emergency (200-500 mcg loading dose)</li>
<li><strong>Subclinical hypothyroidism</strong>: Treatment considered when TSH &gt; 10 mIU/L or with symptoms</li>
</ul>
