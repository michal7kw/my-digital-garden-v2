<h1>Sarilumab</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Sarilumab (Kevzara) is a fully human IgG1 monoclonal antibody targeting the alpha subunit of the interleukin-6 receptor (IL-6Ralpha). As the second IL-6R inhibitor approved for rheumatoid arthritis, it shares the same mechanistic class as tocilizumab but differs in key pharmacological properties. Its fully human composition (versus tocilizumab's humanized framework) may contribute to lower immunogenicity, and it demonstrates approximately 20-fold higher binding affinity for IL-6Ralpha.</p>
<p>Sarilumab is administered subcutaneously at 200 mg every two weeks, with dose reduction to 150 mg available for management of neutropenia, thrombocytopenia, or elevated liver enzymes. The MONARCH trial demonstrated superiority of sarilumab monotherapy over adalimumab monotherapy in RA, establishing its role for patients who cannot use methotrexate. The TARGET and MOBILITY trials confirmed efficacy in combination with conventional DMARDs after inadequate response to TNF inhibitors or methotrexate, respectively.</p>
<p>The safety profile is consistent with IL-6R blockade class effects, including neutropenia, elevated transaminases, and lipid elevations. As with tocilizumab, CRP suppression can mask signs of infection, requiring clinical vigilance. Regular laboratory monitoring (neutrophils, platelets, liver enzymes, lipids) is required, particularly during the first months of therapy.</p>
<h2>Mechanism of Action</h2>
<p>Sarilumab binds with high affinity to both soluble and membrane-bound forms of IL-6Ralpha, competitively inhibiting IL-6 from engaging its receptor. This prevents assembly of the IL-6/IL-6R/gp130 signaling complex and blocks both classical signaling (direct receptor activation on cells expressing membrane IL-6R) and trans-signaling (activation mediated by soluble IL-6R/IL-6 complexes binding gp130 on any cell). The downstream effect is inhibition of JAK-STAT3 pathway activation, leading to reduced hepatic acute phase protein synthesis, decreased Th17 polarization, and suppression of synovial inflammation.</p>
