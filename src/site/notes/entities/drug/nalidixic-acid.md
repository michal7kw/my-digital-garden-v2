<h1>Nalidixic Acid</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Nalidixic acid is the first synthetic quinolone antibiotic, introduced in 1962. It was discovered as a byproduct of chloroquine synthesis and became the prototype for the entire quinolone/fluoroquinolone antibiotic class. Nalidixic acid has a narrow antibacterial spectrum limited to gram-negative bacteria, particularly Enterobacteriaceae, and achieves therapeutic concentrations only in the urinary tract, restricting its clinical use to uncomplicated urinary tract infections (UTIs).</p>
<p>While nalidixic acid itself has been largely superseded by newer fluoroquinolones (norfloxacin, ciprofloxacin, levofloxacin) with broader spectra and better tissue penetration, its historical significance as the founding member of the quinolone class and its role in understanding quinolone resistance mechanisms remain important.</p>
<h2>Mechanism of Action</h2>
<p>Nalidixic acid inhibits the A subunit (GyrA) of bacterial DNA gyrase (topoisomerase II), an enzyme essential for DNA replication, transcription, and repair. DNA gyrase introduces negative supercoils into DNA ahead of the replication fork and is required for the decatenation of newly replicated chromosomes. By trapping gyrase-DNA complexes in a cleaved state, nalidixic acid creates double-strand DNA breaks that are lethal to the bacterium. Unlike later fluoroquinolones, nalidixic acid does not significantly inhibit topoisomerase IV and lacks a fluorine atom at position 6, which limits its potency and spectrum. Resistance develops rapidly through point mutations in gyrA (particularly at codons Ser83 and Asp87), making nalidixic acid resistance a sentinel marker for emerging fluoroquinolone resistance in gram-negative pathogens.</p>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<p>Nalidixic acid was historically used for uncomplicated lower UTIs caused by E. coli, Proteus, Klebsiella, and Enterobacter species. Its use has declined substantially due to the availability of more potent fluoroquinolones and rising resistance rates. Nalidixic acid is concentrated in urine and does not achieve adequate systemic tissue levels, precluding its use for systemic infections. Adverse effects include gastrointestinal disturbance, CNS effects (headache, dizziness, rarely seizures), phototoxicity, and rare hemolytic anemia in G6PD-deficient patients. Nalidixic acid resistance testing in Salmonella and other Enterobacteriaceae is used as a screening tool for reduced fluoroquinolone susceptibility, as gyrA mutations conferring nalidixic acid resistance often precede full fluoroquinolone resistance.</p>
