<h1>Clomipramine</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Clomipramine (Anafranil) is a tricyclic antidepressant distinguished by its exceptionally potent serotonin reuptake inhibition, making it the gold standard pharmacotherapy for Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD). It was the first drug demonstrated to be effective for OCD in controlled trials and remains the most effective single agent for this condition, with response rates of 40-60% compared to 30-40% for SSRIs. It is also used for depression, panic disorder, and cataplexy. Its active metabolite desmethylclomipramine provides additional norepinephrine reuptake inhibition.</p>
<h2>Mechanism of Action</h2>
<p>Clomipramine is the most serotonin-selective of the TCAs, with potent inhibition of the serotonin reuptake transporter (SERT). Its primary metabolite, desmethylclomipramine, is a potent norepinephrine reuptake inhibitor (NET), giving the parent-metabolite combination a dual mechanism. Like other TCAs, clomipramine also antagonizes H1 histamine receptors (causing sedation), muscarinic acetylcholine receptors (anticholinergic effects), and alpha-1 adrenergic receptors (orthostatic hypotension). The anti-obsessional effect is thought to be specifically mediated by serotonergic mechanisms, as noradrenergic-selective TCAs like desipramine are ineffective for OCD.</p>
<h2>Clinical Uses</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>OCD</strong>: 25-250 mg/day; gold standard treatment, often used after SSRI failure</li>
<li><strong>Depression</strong>: 100-250 mg/day; effective but side effect profile limits first-line use</li>
<li><strong>Panic disorder</strong>: Effective at doses similar to depression treatment</li>
<li><strong>Cataplexy</strong>: Used in narcolepsy for cataplexy symptom management</li>
<li><strong>Chronic pain</strong>: Neuropathic pain management similar to other TCAs</li>
</ul>
