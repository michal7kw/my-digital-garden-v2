<h1>Abatacept</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Abatacept (Orencia) is a selective T-cell costimulation modulator composed of the extracellular domain of human CTLA-4 fused to the modified Fc portion of human IgG1. It represents a fundamentally different approach to RA treatment by targeting the upstream adaptive immune response rather than blocking downstream inflammatory cytokines like TNF-alpha or IL-6. By interrupting the costimulatory signal required for full T-cell activation, abatacept modulates the immune cascade at its initiation point.</p>
<p>Clinical data from the AMPLE trial demonstrated comparable efficacy to adalimumab in methotrexate-inadequate responders, while the ATTEST trial showed similar responses to infliximab. Abatacept is particularly favored in anti-CCP antibody positive patients, where several analyses suggest enhanced efficacy correlating with autoantibody titers. The AVERT trial further demonstrated that early intervention with abatacept plus methotrexate in ACPA-positive early RA can induce sustained drug-free remission in a subset of patients.</p>
<p>Abatacept is available in both intravenous (weight-based dosing monthly) and subcutaneous (125 mg weekly) formulations. Its safety profile is generally favorable, with lower rates of serious infections compared to some TNF inhibitors. It may be preferred in patients with recurrent infections, COPD (though caution is advised), or a history of demyelinating disease where TNF inhibitors are contraindicated.</p>
<h2>Mechanism of Action</h2>
<p>Full T-cell activation requires two signals: Signal 1 from T-cell receptor recognition of antigen-MHC complex, and Signal 2 from costimulatory molecule interaction, primarily CD28 on T cells binding CD80 (B7-1) or CD86 (B7-2) on antigen-presenting cells. Abatacept, as a CTLA-4-Ig fusion protein, competes with CD28 for binding to CD80/CD86 with higher avidity than CD28 itself. By blocking this costimulatory interaction, T cells that encounter antigen fail to become fully activated, instead entering a state of anergy or undergoing apoptosis. This selectively dampens antigen-driven T-cell responses while preserving baseline immune function to a greater degree than broad cytokine blockade.</p>
