<h1>Liraglutide</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>First commercial GLP-1 receptor agonist, approved by the FDA in 2010 for type 2 diabetes (Victoza) and in 2014 for obesity (Saxenda). Liraglutide mimics the incretin hormone GLP-1 with 97% structural homology to the native peptide, modified with a C16 fatty acid side chain that enables non-covalent albumin binding and extends its half-life to approximately 13 hours, permitting once-daily subcutaneous dosing. The LEAD program established efficacy in type 2 diabetes with HbA1c reductions of 1.0-1.5%, while the SCALE program demonstrated 5-8% body weight loss at the 3mg obesity dose. The landmark LEADER trial (Lancet 2016) showed 13% reduction in major adverse cardiovascular events (MACE) in patients with T2DM, establishing the cardiovascular benefit class effect for GLP-1 receptor agonists.</p>
<h2>Mechanism of Action</h2>
<p>Liraglutide is a once-daily GLP-1 receptor agonist with 97% structural homology to native human GLP-1. A C16 palmitic acid chain is attached via a glutamoyl spacer at lysine 26, enabling reversible non-covalent binding to serum albumin. This albumin binding shields the peptide from rapid renal clearance and DPP-4 degradation, extending the half-life to approximately 13 hours. Liraglutide activates GLP-1 receptors on pancreatic beta cells, enhancing glucose-dependent insulin secretion (with minimal hypoglycemia risk since the effect is glucose-dependent). It simultaneously suppresses glucagon secretion from alpha cells during hyperglycemia. Peripherally, liraglutide delays gastric emptying, reducing postprandial glucose excursions and contributing to early satiety. Centrally, it acts on GLP-1 receptors in the hypothalamus (particularly the arcuate nucleus) and brainstem (nucleus tractus solitarius and area postrema), reducing appetite and caloric intake. The appetite-suppressing effect is dose-dependent and more pronounced at the 3mg Saxenda dose compared to the 1.2-1.8mg Victoza doses.</p>
<h2>Key Clinical Trials</h2>
<h3>LEAD Program (Liraglutide Effect and Action in Diabetes)</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>LEAD-1</strong> (Diabetes Care 2009; PMID: 19244089): Liraglutide 0.6-1.8mg + glimepiride vs rosiglitazone + glimepiride; HbA1c reduction up to 1.1% - Evidence Level 1b</li>
<li><strong>LEAD-2</strong> (Diabetes Care 2009; PMID: 19244090): Liraglutide 0.6-1.8mg + metformin vs glimepiride + metformin; 1.0% HbA1c reduction at 1.8mg, similar efficacy to glimepiride with weight loss benefit - Evidence Level 1b</li>
<li><strong>LEAD-3</strong> (Lancet 2009; PMID: 19665278): Liraglutide 1.2-1.8mg monotherapy vs glimepiride 8mg; superior HbA1c reduction (1.1-1.2% vs 0.5%) with weight loss instead of weight gain - Evidence Level 1b</li>
<li><strong>LEAD-4</strong> (Diabetes Care 2009; PMID: 19244091): Liraglutide 0.6-1.8mg + metformin + rosiglitazone; HbA1c reduction up to 1.5% - Evidence Level 1b</li>
<li><strong>LEAD-5</strong> (Diabetes Care 2009; PMID: 19244092): Liraglutide 1.8mg + metformin + glimepiride vs insulin glargine; HbA1c reduction 1.3% with weight loss benefit - Evidence Level 1b</li>
<li><strong>LEAD-6</strong> (Lancet 2009; PMID: 19854321): Liraglutide 1.8mg daily vs exenatide 10mcg BID; superior HbA1c reduction (1.12% vs 0.79%), better tolerability and convenience - Evidence Level 1b</li>
</ul>
<h3>LEADER Trial (Liraglutide Effect and Action in Diabetes: Evaluation of Cardiovascular Outcome Results)</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>LEADER</strong> (N Engl J Med 2016; PMID: 27295427): 9,340 patients with T2DM and high CV risk, median follow-up 3.8 years. 13% reduction in composite MACE (CV death, nonfatal MI, nonfatal stroke): HR 0.87, 95% CI 0.78-0.97, p=0.01 for superiority. 22% reduction in CV death (HR 0.78). Significant reduction in all-cause mortality (HR 0.85). Established cardiovascular benefit as a class effect for GLP-1 receptor agonists - Evidence Level 1a</li>
</ul>
<h3>SCALE Program (Satiety and Clinical Adiposity - Liraglutide Evidence)</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>SCALE Obesity and Pre-diabetes</strong> (N Engl J Med 2015; PMID: 25715380): 3.7% weight loss (8.0% vs 2.6% placebo) at 56 weeks with liraglutide 3mg daily in obese non-diabetic patients. 63% of prediabetic patients achieved normoglycemia - Evidence Level 1a</li>
<li><strong>SCALE Maintenance</strong> (JAMA 2015; PMID: 25671406): Liraglutide 3mg for weight maintenance after low-calorie diet-induced weight loss; 6.2% additional weight loss vs 0.2% regain on placebo at 56 weeks - Evidence Level 1a</li>
<li><strong>SCALE Sleep Apnea</strong> (presented at EASD 2016): Liraglutide 3mg reduced obstructive sleep apnea severity in obese patients, with improvements in AHI proportional to weight loss - Evidence Level 2a</li>
<li><strong>SCALE Diabetes</strong> (Diabetes Care 2015; PMID: 25715380): Liraglutide 3mg in overweight/obese patients with T2DM; 5.4% weight loss (6.0% vs 2.0% placebo) with HbA1c reduction of 1.1% - Evidence Level 1a</li>
</ul>
<h3>Emerging Indications</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>NAFLD/NASH</strong>: Reduction in liver fat content and ALT levels (Phase 2 trials, LEAN trial) - Evidence Level 2b</li>
<li><strong>PCOS</strong>: Improved menstrual regularity and metabolic parameters - Evidence Level 2b</li>
<li><strong>Neurodegenerative disease</strong>: Neuroprotective properties in preclinical models - Evidence Level 3</li>
<li><strong>Addiction</strong>: Reduced alcohol intake in preclinical models - Evidence Level 3</li>
</ul>
<h2>Pharmacokinetics</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Absorption</strong>: Subcutaneous bioavailability approximately 55%. Peak plasma concentration reached in 8-12 hours post-injection. Absorption is not significantly affected by injection site (abdomen, thigh, or upper arm)</li>
<li><strong>Distribution</strong>: &gt;98% protein binding to serum albumin via the C16 fatty acid chain. Apparent volume of distribution ~12.5 L/kg, indicating limited tissue distribution beyond the vascular compartment</li>
<li><strong>Metabolism</strong>: Not metabolized by CYP enzymes. Degraded by endogenous DPP-4 and general proteolytic cleavage to smaller peptide fragments. The albumin binding provides protection from rapid enzymatic degradation</li>
<li><strong>Elimination</strong>: Cleared via both renal and hepatic pathways. Urinary and fecal excretion of metabolites. No clinically significant accumulation with once-daily dosing at steady state (achieved after 3-4 days)</li>
<li><strong>Half-life</strong>: Approximately 13 hours after subcutaneous administration, enabling convenient once-daily dosing</li>
</ul>
<h2>Dosing and Administration</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Victoza (T2DM)</strong>: Initiate at 0.6mg daily for 1 week, increase to 1.2mg daily; may further increase to 1.8mg daily based on glycemic response and tolerability. Administer once daily at any time, independent of meals</li>
<li><strong>Saxenda (obesity)</strong>: Initiate at 0.6mg daily, titrate by 0.6mg increments weekly to target dose of 3.0mg daily. Gradual titration minimizes gastrointestinal side effects. Discontinue if 5% weight loss not achieved by 16 weeks</li>
<li><strong>Administration</strong>: Subcutaneous injection in abdomen, thigh, or upper arm using a prefilled multi-dose pen. Rotate injection sites. Can be taken at any time of day, with or without food</li>
<li><strong>Dose adjustments</strong>: No dose adjustment needed for mild-to-moderate renal impairment; use caution in severe renal impairment (CrCl &lt;30 mL/min). No adjustment for hepatic impairment</li>
<li><strong>Missed doses</strong>: If a dose is missed by more than 12 hours, skip and take next dose at the regular time; do not double dose</li>
</ul>
<h2>Adverse Effects Profile</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Common (≥10%)</strong>: Nausea (most frequent, 25-40%, dose-dependent and typically transient over 2-4 weeks), vomiting (10-15%), diarrhea (10-15%), constipation (5-10%), headache (5-10%), dyspepsia</li>
<li><strong>Less Common (1-10%)</strong>: Hypoglycemia (primarily when combined with insulin or sulfonylureas), injection site reactions (erythema, pruritus), dizziness, fatigue, abdominal distension</li>
<li><strong>Rare (&lt;1%)</strong>: Acute pancreatitis (incidence &lt;0.3%, discontinue if suspected), cholelithiasis/cholecystitis, acute kidney injury (usually secondary to dehydration from GI effects), hypersensitivity reactions (urticaria, angioedema)</li>
<li><strong>Serious Warnings</strong>: Thyroid C-cell tumor risk (observed in rodent studies at clinically relevant exposures; contraindicated in MEN2 and personal/family history of MTC), pancreatitis risk, suicidal ideation and behavior (post-marketing reports in obesity indication)</li>
</ul>
