<h1>Clozapine</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Clozapine (Clozaril) is the prototypical atypical antipsychotic and the only antipsychotic with demonstrated superiority in treatment-resistant schizophrenia, where approximately 30-60% of patients who fail other antipsychotics will respond. It is also the only antipsychotic FDA-approved for reducing suicidal behavior in schizophrenia. Despite its unique efficacy, clozapine carries a 1-2% risk of agranulocytosis, requiring mandatory blood monitoring through the Clozapine REMS program. Other serious risks include myocarditis, metabolic syndrome, and seizures (dose-dependent). Its pharmacologic profile is uniquely broad, with weak D2 binding but high affinity for D4, 5-HT2A, muscarinic, and histaminergic receptors.</p>
<h2>Mechanism of Action</h2>
<p>Clozapine has a unique receptor binding profile that distinguishes it from all other antipsychotics. It is a relatively weak dopamine D2 receptor antagonist but has high affinity for D4 receptors and potent 5-HT2A serotonin receptor antagonism. The high 5-HT2A/D2 binding ratio is thought to account for its low incidence of extrapyramidal symptoms and tardive dyskinesia. Clozapine also has significant antagonism at alpha-1 and alpha-2 adrenergic, muscarinic M1/M4, and histamine H1 receptors. The anti-suicidal effect may be related to its serotonergic activity. Its propensity for metabolic syndrome (weight gain, dyslipidemia, hyperglycemia) is linked to H1 and 5-HT2C receptor antagonism.</p>
<h2>Clinical Uses</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Treatment-resistant schizophrenia</strong>: 300-450 mg/day (target), up to 900 mg/day; used after failure of at least 2 adequate antipsychotic trials</li>
<li><strong>Suicidality in schizophrenia/schizoaffective disorder</strong>: Only antipsychotic with this FDA indication</li>
<li><strong>Parkinson's disease psychosis</strong>: Low doses (6.25-50 mg) due to minimal D2 blockade</li>
<li><strong>Refractory bipolar disorder</strong>: Off-label for treatment-resistant mania or mixed states</li>
</ul>
