<h1>Tocilizumab</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Tocilizumab (Actemra) is a humanized monoclonal antibody directed against the interleukin-6 receptor (IL-6R). It was the first IL-6R inhibitor approved for clinical use and remains one of the most versatile biologic DMARDs, with indications spanning rheumatoid arthritis, giant cell arteritis, juvenile idiopathic arthritis, and cytokine release syndrome. Its mechanism of blocking IL-6 signaling distinguishes it from TNF inhibitors and other biologic classes.</p>
<p>A key clinical advantage of tocilizumab is its demonstrated efficacy as monotherapy without methotrexate, making it particularly suitable for patients who cannot tolerate conventional DMARDs. The AMBITION trial established non-inferiority of tocilizumab monotherapy to methotrexate, and subsequent studies (ACT-RAY, ADACTA) confirmed its unique position among biologics in this regard. It is available in both intravenous (4-8 mg/kg every 4 weeks) and subcutaneous (162 mg weekly or every 2 weeks) formulations.</p>
<p>Because tocilizumab profoundly suppresses the acute phase response, it masks classical infection markers such as CRP elevation and fever. Clinicians must maintain heightened vigilance for infections, as patients may present with minimal systemic signs despite serious underlying infection. Regular monitoring of neutrophil counts, liver enzymes, and lipid panels is essential throughout treatment.</p>
<h2>Mechanism of Action</h2>
<p>Tocilizumab binds to both soluble IL-6R (sIL-6R) and membrane-bound IL-6R (mIL-6R), preventing formation of the IL-6/IL-6R/gp130 hexameric signaling complex. This blocks two distinct signaling pathways: classical signaling through membrane-bound IL-6R on hepatocytes, neutrophils, and certain lymphocytes; and trans-signaling through soluble IL-6R that activates endothelial cells, fibroblasts, and other cells lacking membrane IL-6R. By inhibiting gp130-mediated activation of JAK1/JAK2 and downstream STAT3 phosphorylation, tocilizumab suppresses hepatic CRP synthesis, Th17 cell differentiation, hepcidin production (improving anemia of chronic disease), and synovial inflammation.</p>
