<h1>Prednisone</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Prednisone (Deltasone/Rayos) is a synthetic corticosteroid and one of the most commonly prescribed anti-inflammatory and immunosuppressive medications. It is a prodrug converted to active prednisolone in the liver. Prednisone is used across virtually every medical specialty for conditions ranging from asthma exacerbations and COPD to autoimmune diseases (lupus, rheumatoid arthritis, inflammatory bowel disease), allergic reactions, transplant rejection prevention, and certain cancers. While highly effective for acute inflammation, chronic use produces a predictable cascade of adverse effects including Cushing's syndrome, osteoporosis, adrenal suppression, hyperglycemia, immunosuppression, and mood disturbances. Doses must be tapered gradually after prolonged use to prevent adrenal crisis.</p>
<h2>Mechanism of Action</h2>
<p>Prednisone is a prodrug that is hepatically converted to its active form, prednisolone, by 11-beta-hydroxysteroid dehydrogenase type 1. Prednisolone binds to intracellular glucocorticoid receptors, and the activated receptor-ligand complex translocates to the nucleus where it modulates gene transcription. It upregulates anti-inflammatory proteins (e.g., lipocortin-1, which inhibits phospholipase A2) and suppresses pro-inflammatory mediators including NF-kB-driven cytokines (TNF-alpha, IL-1, IL-6), prostaglandins, and leukotrienes. The immunosuppressive effects result from reduced T-cell proliferation, impaired antigen presentation, and redistribution of lymphocytes from the circulation to lymphoid tissue.</p>
<h2>Clinical Uses</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Asthma/COPD exacerbations</strong>: 40-60 mg/day for 5-7 days; rapid anti-inflammatory burst</li>
<li><strong>Autoimmune diseases</strong>: Variable doses for lupus, RA, vasculitis, inflammatory myopathies</li>
<li><strong>Inflammatory bowel disease</strong>: 40-60 mg/day for Crohn's/UC flares, then taper</li>
<li><strong>Allergic reactions</strong>: 40-60 mg for severe allergic reactions, anaphylaxis adjunct</li>
<li><strong>Organ transplant</strong>: Part of immunosuppressive regimens to prevent rejection</li>
<li><strong>Hematologic malignancies</strong>: Component of chemotherapy regimens (e.g., R-CHOP for lymphoma)</li>
<li><strong>Adrenal insufficiency</strong>: Physiologic replacement at 5 mg/day (with hydrocortisone preferred)</li>
<li><strong>Temporal arteritis/PMR</strong>: 40-60 mg/day for GCA; 15-20 mg/day for PMR</li>
</ul>
