<h1>Rapamycin</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Rapamycin (Sirolimus/Rapamune) is an mTOR inhibitor originally discovered as an antifungal from Easter Island soil bacteria (Streptomyces hygroscopicus) and later developed as an immunosuppressant for organ transplant rejection. It has become one of the most studied longevity interventions after demonstrating lifespan extension across yeast, worms, flies, and mice (including when started in middle age). The PEARL trial and other human studies are investigating intermittent low-dose rapamycin for anti-aging effects. FDA-approved indications include renal transplant rejection, lymphangioleiomyomatosis (LAM), and tuberous sclerosis complex (TSC). Key side effects at immunosuppressive doses include mouth ulcers, hyperlipidemia, impaired wound healing, and thrombocytopenia. The longevity community uses intermittent low-dose protocols (e.g., 5-6 mg weekly) to minimize mTORC2 inhibition while preserving mTORC1-mediated autophagy activation.</p>
<h2>Mechanism of Action</h2>
<p>Rapamycin (sirolimus) binds to the intracellular protein FKBP12, and the resulting complex specifically inhibits mechanistic target of rapamycin complex 1 (mTORC1), a serine/threonine kinase that integrates nutrient, energy, and growth factor signals to regulate cell growth and metabolism. Inhibition of mTORC1 suppresses downstream effectors including S6K1 and 4E-BP1, reducing protein synthesis and cell proliferation while potently activating autophagy, the cellular self-recycling process. The longevity effects observed across multiple model organisms are attributed to this mimicry of caloric restriction signaling, improved proteostasis, and reduced senescent cell accumulation. At higher doses or with chronic exposure, rapamycin can also inhibit mTORC2, which mediates metabolic side effects including insulin resistance and hyperlipidemia.</p>
<h2>Clinical Uses</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Renal transplant rejection</strong>: 2-5 mg/day with trough monitoring; FDA-approved</li>
<li><strong>Lymphangioleiomyomatosis (LAM)</strong>: FDA-approved; stabilizes lung function</li>
<li><strong>Tuberous sclerosis complex</strong>: FDA-approved for renal angiomyolipomas and SEGA</li>
<li><strong>Coronary stent coating</strong>: Drug-eluting stents to prevent restenosis</li>
<li><strong>Longevity/anti-aging (off-label)</strong>: Intermittent dosing (e.g., 5-6 mg weekly) being studied in clinical trials</li>
<li><strong>Immune rejuvenation</strong>: Low-dose rapamycin improved vaccine response in elderly (PEARL-related research)</li>
<li><strong>Cancer</strong>: mTOR pathway inhibition investigated across multiple cancer types (everolimus is a rapamycin analog used in cancer)</li>
</ul>
