<h1>Carbamazepine</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Carbamazepine (Tegretol) is an anticonvulsant and mood stabilizer that has been in clinical use since the 1960s. It is a first-line treatment for focal (partial) epilepsy and the gold standard drug for trigeminal neuralgia. It is also FDA-approved for bipolar disorder (acute mania) under the brand name Equetro. Carbamazepine has the notable property of auto-induction: it induces its own metabolism via CYP3A4, causing its half-life to decrease from 25-65 hours initially to 12-17 hours after 2-4 weeks. This potent CYP3A4 induction also reduces the efficacy of many co-administered drugs, including oral contraceptives, warfarin, and other anticonvulsants. HLA-B*1502 screening is recommended before starting carbamazepine in patients of Southeast Asian ancestry due to risk of Stevens-Johnson syndrome.</p>
<h2>Mechanism of Action</h2>
<p>Carbamazepine stabilizes the inactivated state of voltage-gated sodium channels, preventing the rapid repetitive firing of neurons that underlies seizure propagation while preserving normal single action potential generation. This use-dependent sodium channel blockade means the drug preferentially affects neurons that are firing at high frequency. Additionally, carbamazepine has minor effects on calcium channels and may modulate glutamate release. The mood-stabilizing effect in bipolar disorder may involve additional mechanisms including modulation of second-messenger systems and neuroprotective effects.</p>
<h2>Clinical Uses</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Focal epilepsy</strong>: 400-1200 mg/day; first-line for partial seizures with or without secondary generalization</li>
<li><strong>Trigeminal neuralgia</strong>: 200-1200 mg/day; gold standard treatment for this condition</li>
<li><strong>Bipolar disorder (mania)</strong>: 400-1600 mg/day; FDA-approved (Equetro)</li>
<li><strong>Neuropathic pain</strong>: Effective for various neuropathic pain syndromes</li>
<li><strong>Glossopharyngeal neuralgia</strong>: Off-label but effective for this related cranial neuralgia</li>
</ul>
