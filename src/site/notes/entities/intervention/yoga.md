<h1>Yoga</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Yoga is a mind-body practice originating from ancient India that integrates physical postures (asana), breathwork (pranayama), and meditation/mindfulness. Modern yoga encompasses diverse styles ranging from gentle (Yin, Restorative, Hatha) to vigorous (Ashtanga, Power, Vinyasa). It is one of the most widely practiced complementary health approaches, with an estimated 300 million practitioners worldwide. Evidence from over 300 RCTs supports benefits across multiple health domains.</p>
<p>Clinical evidence is strongest for stress reduction, anxiety, chronic low back pain, and balance/fall prevention. A 2020 Cochrane review found yoga effective for chronic non-specific low back pain (moderate certainty). Meta-analyses demonstrate significant reductions in cortisol, blood pressure, resting heart rate, and inflammatory markers (CRP, IL-6). Yoga is unique among exercise modalities in simultaneously improving physical (flexibility, strength, balance), autonomic (HRV, vagal tone), and psychological (anxiety, depression, well-being) outcomes. The combination of physical practice with breathwork and mindfulness creates synergistic effects not seen with exercise alone.</p>
<p><strong>Type</strong>: mind_body</p>
<h2>Mechanism</h2>
<p>Yoga exerts multi-system effects through three integrated pathways. Physical postures (asana) create sustained muscular activation, improving flexibility, proprioception, and balance through prolonged muscle-tendon stretch and neuromuscular adaptation. Pranayama breathing techniques activate vagal afferents, shifting autonomic balance toward parasympathetic dominance and increasing heart rate variability. Mindfulness/meditation components reduce default mode network rumination and enhance prefrontal cortex regulation of the amygdala. Collectively, these pathways reduce HPA axis activation, lowering cortisol and ACTH. Brain imaging studies show yoga practitioners have increased GABA levels (27% increase after a single session), enhanced gray matter volume in hippocampus and prefrontal cortex, and reduced amygdala reactivity. Anti-inflammatory effects operate through reduced NF-kB activation and lower circulating IL-6, TNF-alpha, and CRP.</p>
<h2>Evidence Summary</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Chronic low back pain</strong>: Effective for pain and function improvement (Cochrane review, moderate certainty, Evidence Level 1a)</li>
<li><strong>Anxiety</strong>: Moderate-large effect size (SMD=-0.76) across RCTs (meta-analysis, Evidence Level 1a)</li>
<li><strong>Depression</strong>: Small-moderate antidepressant effect (SMD=-0.40), particularly in clinical populations (Evidence Level 1a)</li>
<li><strong>Blood pressure</strong>: Reduces systolic BP 4-5 mmHg, diastolic 3 mmHg (meta-analysis, Evidence Level 1a)</li>
<li><strong>Cortisol reduction</strong>: Significant cortisol reduction across diverse populations (Evidence Level 1b)</li>
<li><strong>GABA levels</strong>: 27% increase in brain GABA after single yoga session (Streeter et al., 2007, Evidence Level 2b)</li>
<li><strong>HRV improvement</strong>: Enhanced vagal tone and parasympathetic modulation (Evidence Level 2a)</li>
<li><strong>Balance/falls</strong>: Reduces fall risk in elderly by 29% (meta-analysis, Evidence Level 1a)</li>
<li><strong>Inflammation</strong>: Reduced CRP, IL-6, TNF-alpha with regular practice (Evidence Level 2a)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Protocol</h2>
<h3>Dosing Information</h3>
<p>2-5 sessions per week, 30-60 minutes per session. Style varies by goal: Hatha for general health, Yin for flexibility and connective tissue, Vinyasa/Power for cardiovascular fitness, Restorative for stress and recovery.</p>
<h2>Safety Information</h2>
<h3>Contraindications</h3>
<ul>
<li>Acute musculoskeletal injury (modify poses)</li>
<li>Uncontrolled hypertension (avoid inversions)</li>
<li>Severe osteoporosis (avoid extreme flexion/extension)</li>
<li>Glaucoma (avoid prolonged inversions)</li>
<li>Pregnancy (avoid hot yoga, deep twists, prone positions after first trimester)</li>
</ul>
