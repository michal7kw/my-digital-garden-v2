<h1>Time-Restricted Eating</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Time-restricted eating (TRE) is a specific form of intermittent fasting that restricts daily food consumption to a consistent window (typically 8-12 hours), with the primary emphasis on aligning eating patterns with circadian rhythms rather than caloric restriction. Pioneered by Dr. Satchin Panda (Salk Institute), TRE leverages the insight that metabolic efficiency varies dramatically across the 24-hour cycle -- insulin sensitivity, gastric motility, and nutrient processing peak during daytime hours and decline in the evening.</p>
<p>Research demonstrates that TRE improves metabolic health markers even without caloric restriction. Panda's mouse studies showed that mice fed a high-fat diet within a 9-12 hour window gained 28% less weight than ad libitum-fed controls eating identical calories. Human RCTs confirm improvements in fasting glucose, insulin sensitivity, blood pressure, and oxidative stress markers with 8-10 hour eating windows. Early TRE (eTRE, eating window ending by mid-afternoon) shows greater metabolic benefits than late TRE, likely due to circadian alignment. TRE is one of the most practical and sustainable dietary interventions, requiring no calorie counting or food restriction.</p>
<p><strong>Type</strong>: dietary</p>
<h2>Mechanism</h2>
<p>TRE aligns nutrient intake with peripheral clock gene expression cycles (BMAL1, CLOCK, Per, Cry) in liver, pancreas, and adipose tissue. Eating during the biological daytime, when insulin sensitivity and metabolic enzyme activity peak, optimizes nutrient processing. Late-night eating disrupts peripheral clocks, creating circadian misalignment between central (SCN) and peripheral oscillators, which impairs glucose tolerance and lipid metabolism. The extended overnight fast (14-16 hours) depletes hepatic glycogen, activates AMPK, promotes autophagy, enhances fat oxidation via increased lipolysis, and allows insulin levels to drop to baseline, restoring receptor sensitivity. TRE also reduces the total duration of postprandial metabolic stress (oxidative stress, inflammation) that accompanies each meal.</p>
<h2>Evidence Summary</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Weight management</strong>: TRE without caloric restriction reduces body weight 2-4% over 8-12 weeks; greater effect with caloric deficit (Evidence Level 1b)</li>
<li><strong>Circadian metabolic optimization</strong>: Early TRE (eTRE) improves insulin sensitivity, beta-cell responsiveness, and blood pressure more than late TRE (Sutton et al., 2018, Cell Metabolism, Evidence Level 2b)</li>
<li><strong>Glycemic control</strong>: Reduced fasting glucose and insulin in overweight/obese adults (Evidence Level 1b)</li>
<li><strong>Blood pressure</strong>: 4-7 mmHg systolic reduction with 10-hour TRE (Evidence Level 2a)</li>
<li><strong>Oxidative stress</strong>: Reduced 8-isoprostane and other oxidative markers (Evidence Level 2b)</li>
<li><strong>Adherence</strong>: Higher adherence than calorie-counting diets; 86% compliance in 12-week studies (Evidence Level 2a)</li>
<li><strong>Firefighter study</strong>: 10-hour TRE improved cardiometabolic health in shift workers (Panda lab, Evidence Level 2b)</li>
<li><strong>No calorie counting</strong>: Benefits occur independent of caloric restriction when eating window is maintained (Evidence Level 2b)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Protocol</h2>
<h3>Dosing Information</h3>
<p>Eating within 8-12 hour daily window, ideally starting 1-2 hours after waking and stopping 2-3 hours before sleep. 16:8 (16h fast, 8h eat) most studied. Early TRE (e.g., 7am-3pm) metabolically superior to late TRE. Consistency of window more important than exact duration.</p>
<h2>Safety Information</h2>
<h3>Contraindications</h3>
<ul>
<li>Pregnancy and breastfeeding</li>
<li>History of eating disorders</li>
<li>Type 1 diabetes (without medical supervision)</li>
<li>Children and adolescents</li>
<li>Severe underweight (BMI &lt;18.5)</li>
<li>Certain medications requiring food at specific times</li>
<li>Shift workers (requires modified approach)</li>
</ul>
