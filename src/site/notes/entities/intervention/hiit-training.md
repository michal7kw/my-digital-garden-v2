<h1>HIIT Training</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>High-Intensity Interval Training (HIIT) is an exercise protocol alternating short bursts (10-60 seconds) of near-maximal or supramaximal effort with recovery periods of low-intensity activity or rest. It is the most time-efficient method for improving cardiovascular fitness (VO2max), insulin sensitivity, mitochondrial function, and metabolic health, achieving comparable or superior adaptations to moderate-intensity continuous training in 40-60% less time. Common protocols include Tabata (20s on/10s off x 8), Norwegian 4x4 (4 min at 90-95% HRmax), and sprint intervals (30s all-out Wingate sprints).</p>
<p>Meta-analyses consistently show HIIT improves VO2max by 5-8% more than moderate-intensity continuous exercise. The HIIT-RT study demonstrated that HIIT reversed age-related decline in mitochondrial function in older adults, with proteomic analysis showing enhanced mitochondrial protein synthesis. HIIT also produces significant improvements in HbA1c (-0.5-0.8%) and insulin sensitivity in type 2 diabetes populations. However, HIIT should be balanced with Zone 2 training and strength training for optimal health outcomes, and 48 hours between sessions is recommended for adequate recovery.</p>
<h2>Mechanism</h2>
<p>Repeated high-intensity efforts create metabolic stress that drives potent cardiovascular and cellular adaptations. Rapid glycogen depletion activates AMPK, stimulating mitochondrial biogenesis via PGC-1alpha and increasing GLUT4 translocation for enhanced glucose uptake. Elevated post-exercise oxygen consumption (EPOC) persists 12-24 hours, increasing total caloric expenditure by 6-15% beyond the session itself. The intense sympathetic activation triggers acute growth hormone release (up to 450% increase) and testosterone elevation. Cardiac output adaptations include increased stroke volume and left ventricular compliance. At the cellular level, HIIT upregulates antioxidant enzyme systems (SOD, catalase) and improves mitochondrial respiratory chain efficiency.</p>
<h2>Evidence Summary</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>VO2max</strong>: HIIT improves VO2max 5-8% more than moderate-intensity continuous training (meta-analysis of 28 RCTs, Evidence Level 1a)</li>
<li><strong>Insulin sensitivity</strong>: Improvements in HbA1c of 0.5-0.8% in type 2 diabetes, comparable to metformin (Evidence Level 1a)</li>
<li><strong>Mitochondrial function</strong>: Reversed age-related mitochondrial decline in older adults (Robinson et al., 2017, Cell Metabolism, Evidence Level 2b)</li>
<li><strong>Cardiovascular mortality</strong>: Associated with 15-20% reduction in all-cause mortality (observational, Evidence Level 2a)</li>
<li><strong>Time efficiency</strong>: 10-20 min HIIT equivalent to 40-60 min moderate continuous exercise for cardiometabolic outcomes (Evidence Level 1b)</li>
<li><strong>Body composition</strong>: Effective for visceral fat reduction despite shorter duration (Evidence Level 1a)</li>
<li><strong>Cognitive function</strong>: Acute HIIT improves executive function and BDNF release (Evidence Level 2a)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Protocol</h2>
<h3>Dosing Information</h3>
<p>2-3 sessions per week with 48h between sessions. Protocols: Tabata (4 min), Norwegian 4x4 (16 min), or 30-second sprints x 4-6. Always include 5-minute warm-up and cool-down. Intensity: 85-95% HRmax during work intervals.</p>
<h2>Safety Information</h2>
<h3>Contraindications</h3>
<ul>
<li>Uncontrolled hypertension (&gt;180/110 mmHg)</li>
<li>Recent cardiac event (&lt;6 months)</li>
<li>Severe aortic stenosis</li>
<li>Uncontrolled arrhythmias</li>
<li>Acute illness or infection</li>
<li>Decompensated heart failure</li>
<li>Severe COPD exacerbation</li>
<li>Orthopedic limitations preventing high-intensity movement</li>
</ul>
<h3>Side Effects</h3>
<ul>
<li>Extreme fatigue during/after session (expected)</li>
<li>Nausea if intensity too high or inadequate warm-up</li>
<li>Muscle soreness (DOMS) 24-48 hours post-workout</li>
<li>Increased injury risk without proper form</li>
<li>Overtraining if inadequate recovery</li>
<li>Potential for rhabdomyolysis (rare, extreme overexertion)</li>
</ul>
