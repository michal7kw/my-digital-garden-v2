<h1>Cognitive Training</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Cognitive training encompasses structured mental exercises designed to maintain or improve specific cognitive domains including memory, processing speed, attention, and executive function. Programs range from computerized brain training platforms (BrainHQ, Lumosity, CogMed) to traditional approaches such as learning a musical instrument, studying a new language, or engaging in strategic games. The field distinguishes between &quot;near transfer&quot; (improvement on trained tasks) and &quot;far transfer&quot; (generalization to untrained real-world abilities).</p>
<p>The landmark ACTIVE trial (2,832 participants, 10-year follow-up) demonstrated that processing speed training reduced dementia risk by 29% and preserved instrumental activities of daily living. Near-transfer effects are well-established across multiple domains, while far-transfer remains debated. Cognitive training is most effective when combined with physical exercise, adequate sleep, and nutritional support (Mediterranean diet, omega-3s), forming a multimodal neuroprotective strategy.</p>
<h2>Mechanism</h2>
<p>Cognitive training drives experience-dependent neuroplasticity through repeated activation of neural circuits. Processing speed training enhances white matter integrity and myelination in frontoparietal networks. Working memory training increases prefrontal cortex activation and strengthens dorsolateral prefrontal-parietal connectivity. Training stimulates BDNF release, which supports synaptic plasticity and hippocampal neurogenesis. The &quot;cognitive reserve&quot; hypothesis suggests that accumulated neural efficiency and compensatory network recruitment delays onset of clinical dementia despite underlying pathology.</p>
<h2>Evidence Summary</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Dementia prevention</strong>: ACTIVE trial showed 29% reduced dementia risk with processing speed training over 10 years (Edwards et al., 2017, Evidence Level 1b)</li>
<li><strong>Near-transfer</strong>: Robust improvements on trained cognitive domains across 52 RCTs (meta-analysis, Evidence Level 1a)</li>
<li><strong>Far-transfer</strong>: Limited evidence for generalization to daily function; results mixed (Evidence Level 2b)</li>
<li><strong>MCI populations</strong>: Moderate improvements in memory and executive function in mild cognitive impairment (Evidence Level 2a)</li>
<li><strong>Dose-response</strong>: 10-20 hours over 5-10 weeks is the minimum effective dose; booster sessions extend benefits (Evidence Level 2a)</li>
<li><strong>Synergy with exercise</strong>: Combined cognitive-physical training shows larger effects than either alone (Evidence Level 1b)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Protocol</h2>
<h3>Dosing Information</h3>
<p>10-20 hours over 5-10 weeks. Processing speed training (BrainHQ) 3-5 sessions per week, 30-60 min each. Booster sessions every 1-3 years maintain benefits.</p>
<h2>Safety Information</h2>
<h3>Contraindications</h3>
<ul>
<li>Active psychosis or severe psychiatric instability</li>
<li>Unrealistic expectations may cause frustration in dementia patients</li>
</ul>
