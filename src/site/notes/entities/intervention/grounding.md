<h1>Grounding</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Grounding (also called earthing) is the practice of direct physical contact with the Earth's surface through barefoot walking, lying on the ground, or using conductive indoor systems (grounding mats, sheets, patches) connected to earth ground. The proposed mechanism involves transfer of free electrons from the Earth's negatively charged surface into the body, where they act as mobile antioxidants that neutralize reactive oxygen species. Evidence is preliminary but intriguing, with pilot studies reporting improved sleep, reduced pain, normalized cortisol rhythm, and decreased blood viscosity.</p>
<p>While the theoretical framework is plausible (the Earth maintains a ~-500kV ionospheric potential with a constant supply of free electrons at the surface), the evidence base remains limited to small studies with methodological constraints. Most published research comes from a relatively small group of investigators, and large-scale RCTs are lacking. Grounding should be considered a low-risk complementary practice rather than a primary intervention. The strongest signal appears to be for cortisol normalization and sleep improvement, which may be partially mediated by time spent outdoors and relaxation.</p>
<p><strong>Type</strong>: environmental</p>
<h2>Mechanism</h2>
<p>Proposed electron transfer from Earth's surface provides antioxidant effects by neutralizing reactive oxygen species in tissues. The Earth's surface maintains a negative charge, and direct contact may allow electron flow into the body. Pilot studies suggest grounding normalizes the diurnal cortisol rhythm (reducing nocturnal cortisol and restoring the morning peak), reduces blood viscosity by increasing erythrocyte zeta potential (surface charge), and shifts autonomic balance toward parasympathetic dominance. Thermal infrared imaging shows reduced inflammation at injury sites during grounding. The mechanism remains under investigation and has not been fully validated.</p>
<h2>Evidence Summary</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Cortisol normalization</strong>: Pilot study (n=12) showed normalized diurnal cortisol profile with overnight grounding, with reduced nighttime levels (Ghaly &amp; Teplitz, 2004, Evidence Level 4)</li>
<li><strong>Sleep improvement</strong>: Subjective sleep quality improved in grounded subjects, with reduced sleep onset latency and night waking (Evidence Level 4)</li>
<li><strong>Blood viscosity</strong>: Grounding for 2 hours reduced blood viscosity by increasing erythrocyte zeta potential (Chevalier et al., 2013, Evidence Level 4)</li>
<li><strong>Pain reduction</strong>: Delayed-onset muscle soreness (DOMS) reduced with grounding in pilot RCT (Evidence Level 3b)</li>
<li><strong>Inflammation</strong>: Thermal imaging showed reduced inflammation markers at wound sites during grounding (Evidence Level 4)</li>
<li><strong>Limitations</strong>: Small sample sizes, limited independent replication, publication bias concerns; large RCTs needed</li>
</ul>
<h2>Protocol</h2>
<h3>Dosing Information</h3>
<p>30-60 minutes barefoot contact with earth daily. Indoor grounding mats/sheets for overnight use. Conductive surfaces include grass, soil, sand, concrete (not asphalt or wood).</p>
<h2>Safety Information</h2>
<h3>Contraindications</h3>
<ul>
<li>Parasitic infections risk in tropical areas (barefoot outdoors)</li>
<li>Open wounds on feet</li>
<li>Electrical grounding pads may interfere with certain medical devices</li>
<li>Caution with blood-thinning medications (theoretical blood viscosity effects)</li>
</ul>
