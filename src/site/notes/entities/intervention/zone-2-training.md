<h1>Zone 2 Training</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Zone 2 training is moderate-intensity aerobic exercise performed at a pace that maximizes fat oxidation while staying below the first lactate threshold (LT1, typically 1.5-2.0 mmol/L blood lactate). At this intensity, the exerciser can maintain a conversation but feels a sustained effort. Zone 2 represents the &quot;sweet spot&quot; for mitochondrial adaptation -- the highest intensity at which Type I slow-twitch muscle fibers are primarily recruited and mitochondria can fully oxidize fat for fuel. It is the foundation of Dr. Peter Attia's and Dr. Inigo San Millan's longevity exercise framework.</p>
<p>Zone 2 training is arguably the most important exercise modality for metabolic health and longevity. Dr. San Millan's research at the University of Colorado demonstrates that Zone 2 capacity (fat oxidation rate, mitochondrial function) is the strongest predictor of metabolic health and differentiates metabolically healthy from unhealthy individuals better than any other measure. Professional athletes dedicate 75-80% of total training volume to Zone 2 (polarized training model). For non-athletes, 3-4 sessions of 45-90 minutes per week builds the aerobic base that supports all other exercise modalities and daily energy levels.</p>
<p><strong>Type</strong>: exercise</p>
<h2>Mechanism</h2>
<p>Zone 2 intensity preferentially recruits Type I (slow-twitch) muscle fibers with high mitochondrial density, maximizing mitochondrial biogenesis through PGC-1alpha activation without the excessive lactate accumulation that occurs at higher intensities. At this intensity, pyruvate enters mitochondria for complete oxidation via the TCA cycle and electron transport chain, rather than being converted to lactate in the cytoplasm. This maximizes fat oxidation (peak fat oxidation occurs at ~60-65% VO2max). Chronic Zone 2 training increases mitochondrial density and volume (up to 50-100% increase), enhances capillary density in muscle, improves lipid oxidation enzyme activity, and increases stroke volume. The metabolic flexibility gained (ability to switch between fat and glucose oxidation) is a hallmark of metabolic health and is impaired in insulin resistance, type 2 diabetes, and metabolic syndrome.</p>
<h2>Evidence Summary</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Mitochondrial biogenesis</strong>: Zone 2 training increases mitochondrial volume and density by 50-100% over 6-12 months (Evidence Level 2a)</li>
<li><strong>Metabolic health</strong>: Zone 2 capacity (fat oxidation rate) is the strongest differentiator between metabolically healthy and unhealthy individuals (San Millan &amp; Brooks, 2018, Evidence Level 2b)</li>
<li><strong>Cardiovascular fitness</strong>: VO2max improvement of 10-20% over 12-24 weeks with consistent Zone 2 training (Evidence Level 1b)</li>
<li><strong>Fat oxidation</strong>: Peak fat oxidation occurs at Zone 2 intensity (~60-65% VO2max); impaired in metabolic disease (Evidence Level 2a)</li>
<li><strong>Insulin sensitivity</strong>: Improved HOMA-IR and glucose disposal with regular aerobic training at Zone 2 (Evidence Level 1a)</li>
<li><strong>All-cause mortality</strong>: Higher cardiorespiratory fitness (VO2max) associated with 60-70% reduced mortality (meta-analysis, Evidence Level 1a)</li>
<li><strong>Polarized training</strong>: Elite athletes train 75-80% in Zone 2; this model also benefits recreational athletes (Evidence Level 2a)</li>
<li><strong>Cancer risk</strong>: Higher CRF associated with reduced cancer incidence and mortality (Evidence Level 2a)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Protocol</h2>
<h3>Dosing Information</h3>
<p>3-4 sessions per week, 45-90 minutes per session. Intensity: conversational pace, RPE 3-4/10, 60-70% max heart rate. Modalities include cycling, jogging, rowing, swimming, or fast walking. Heart rate monitor or lactate meter recommended for accurate Zone 2 identification. MAF method: 180 minus age = target heart rate ceiling.</p>
<h3>Administration</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Timing</strong>: Anytime; morning fasted sessions may enhance fat oxidation adaptation</li>
<li><strong>Frequency</strong>: 3-4 sessions per week (minimum 150 minutes/week total)</li>
<li><strong>Duration</strong>: 45-90 minutes per session</li>
</ul>
<h2>Safety Information</h2>
<h3>Contraindications</h3>
<ul>
<li>Acute cardiovascular event (physician clearance required)</li>
<li>Severe decompensated heart failure</li>
<li>Active musculoskeletal injury (modify modality)</li>
</ul>
