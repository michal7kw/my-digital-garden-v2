<h1>Sleep Optimization</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Sleep optimization encompasses evidence-based strategies to improve sleep duration, quality, efficiency, and architecture (NREM/REM distribution) for health and cognitive performance. Adequate sleep (7-9 hours for adults) is essential for glymphatic clearance of neurotoxins (including amyloid-beta and tau), memory consolidation (hippocampal replay during slow-wave sleep), immune function, hormonal regulation, and metabolic health. Sleep is now recognized as one of the most impactful modifiable risk factors for dementia, cardiovascular disease, and premature mortality.</p>
<p>Interventions include sleep hygiene practices (consistent schedule, dark/cool environment, limiting blue light), cognitive behavioral therapy for insomnia (CBT-I, first-line treatment for chronic insomnia), targeted supplementation (magnesium, glycine, L-theanine, melatonin for circadian misalignment), and environmental optimization (temperature 18-20C, darkness &lt;1 lux). Short sleep (&lt;6 hours) is associated with 12% increased all-cause mortality, 48% increased coronary heart disease risk, and 2x accelerated cognitive decline. CBT-I is superior to pharmacotherapy for chronic insomnia with sustained benefits and no dependency risk.</p>
<h2>Mechanism</h2>
<p>Sleep serves multiple critical functions organized across sleep stages. During NREM slow-wave sleep (stages 3-4), the glymphatic system activates -- interstitial space expands by 60%, and cerebrospinal fluid flow increases dramatically, clearing metabolic waste including amyloid-beta (40-60% clearance occurs during sleep). Memory consolidation occurs through hippocampal-cortical replay during slow-wave oscillations, transferring episodic memories to neocortical long-term storage. REM sleep supports emotional memory processing, synaptic pruning, and creative problem-solving. Sleep regulates the HPA axis (cortisol nadir during early sleep, morning surge), growth hormone release (80% secreted during deep sleep), and melatonin-driven circadian rhythms. Sleep deprivation increases inflammatory markers (IL-6, CRP, TNF-alpha), impairs insulin sensitivity within one night, and reduces natural killer cell activity by 70%.</p>
<h2>Evidence Summary</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Mortality</strong>: Short sleep (&lt;6h) associated with 12% increased all-cause mortality (meta-analysis of 1.3M participants, Evidence Level 1a)</li>
<li><strong>Dementia</strong>: Sleep disturbances increase Alzheimer's risk 1.5-2x; glymphatic clearance is sleep-dependent (Evidence Level 2a)</li>
<li><strong>CBT-I</strong>: First-line for chronic insomnia; superior to pharmacotherapy with sustained 6-12 month benefits (Evidence Level 1a)</li>
<li><strong>Cardiovascular disease</strong>: Short sleep associated with 48% increased CHD risk (meta-analysis, Evidence Level 1a)</li>
<li><strong>Immune function</strong>: One night of poor sleep reduces NK cell activity by 70% (Evidence Level 2b)</li>
<li><strong>Glucose metabolism</strong>: Even one night of sleep restriction impairs insulin sensitivity by 25% (Evidence Level 2a)</li>
<li><strong>Temperature</strong>: Room temperature 18-20C optimal for sleep onset and SWS duration (Evidence Level 2b)</li>
<li><strong>Light exposure</strong>: Evening blue light delays melatonin onset by 90 minutes (Evidence Level 2a)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Protocol</h2>
<h3>Dosing Information</h3>
<p>Target 7-9 hours/night for adults (7-8h for &gt;65). Consistent sleep/wake schedule (+/-30 min). Room temperature 18-20C. Darkness &lt;1 lux. No screens 1h before bed (or use blue-light blocking). Caffeine cutoff 8-10h before sleep. Morning sunlight exposure (10+ min) to anchor circadian rhythm.</p>
<h2>Safety Information</h2>
<h3>Contraindications</h3>
<ul>
<li>Melatonin supplementation: caution with autoimmune conditions, concurrent immunosuppression</li>
<li>Sleep restriction therapy (component of CBT-I): avoid in epilepsy, bipolar disorder, severe sleep apnea</li>
</ul>
