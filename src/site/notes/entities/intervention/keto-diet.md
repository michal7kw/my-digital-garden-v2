<h1>Keto Diet</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>The ketogenic diet is a very low-carbohydrate (&lt;20-50g/day), high-fat (70-80% of calories), moderate-protein dietary pattern that shifts the body's primary fuel source from glucose to ketone bodies (beta-hydroxybutyrate, acetoacetate, acetone). Originally developed in the 1920s for pediatric epilepsy, it has gained widespread attention for weight loss, metabolic health, and potential neuroprotective applications. Nutritional ketosis (blood BHB 0.5-3.0 mmol/L) is distinct from diabetic ketoacidosis.</p>
<p>Evidence supports the ketogenic diet for drug-resistant epilepsy (50% seizure reduction in ~50% of patients), short-term weight loss (superior to low-fat diets at 6 months, equivalent at 12 months), and type 2 diabetes management (HbA1c reduction, medication reduction). Emerging research explores applications in neurodegenerative diseases, cancer adjunct therapy, and PCOS. However, long-term cardiovascular safety remains debated due to elevated LDL cholesterol in some individuals, and adherence is challenging. The diet requires careful supplementation (electrolytes, fiber, micronutrients) and medical supervision for metabolic conditions.</p>
<h2>Mechanism</h2>
<p>Carbohydrate restriction depletes hepatic glycogen within 24-72 hours, triggering hepatic ketogenesis from fatty acid beta-oxidation. Ketone bodies (primarily beta-hydroxybutyrate) cross the blood-brain barrier and serve as an efficient alternative fuel for neurons and cardiac muscle. BHB also acts as a signaling molecule: it inhibits HDAC histone deacetylases (epigenetic modulation), activates AMPK, reduces oxidative stress via upregulation of Nrf2 antioxidant pathways, and suppresses NLRP3 inflammasome activation (anti-inflammatory). In the brain, ketones bypass impaired glucose metabolism seen in Alzheimer's disease, providing an alternative energy substrate. Reduced insulin and mTOR signaling may activate autophagy.</p>
<h2>Evidence Summary</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Epilepsy</strong>: 50% seizure reduction in ~50% of drug-resistant epilepsy patients; established therapy since 1920s (Evidence Level 1a)</li>
<li><strong>Weight loss</strong>: Superior to low-fat diets at 6 months; equivalent at 12 months (meta-analysis, Evidence Level 1a)</li>
<li><strong>Type 2 diabetes</strong>: HbA1c reduction of 0.5-1.5%, medication reduction or elimination in many patients (Evidence Level 1b)</li>
<li><strong>Cardiovascular risk</strong>: LDL elevation in some individuals; improved triglycerides and HDL; long-term CV outcomes uncertain (Evidence Level 2b)</li>
<li><strong>Neurodegeneration</strong>: Preliminary evidence for cognitive improvement in MCI and early Alzheimer's via ketone metabolism (Evidence Level 3a)</li>
<li><strong>Cancer</strong>: Adjunct therapy under investigation; reduced tumor glucose availability; human RCTs limited (Evidence Level 3b)</li>
<li><strong>Adherence</strong>: 50-60% dropout rate in long-term studies; Mediterranean keto and modified versions improve compliance (Evidence Level 2b)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Protocol</h2>
<h3>Dosing Information</h3>
<p>&lt;20-50g net carbs/day, 70-80% fat, 15-20% protein. Monitor blood BHB (target 0.5-3.0 mmol/L). Supplement electrolytes (sodium 3-5g, potassium 3-4g, magnesium 300-400mg daily). 2-4 week adaptation period (&quot;keto flu&quot;).</p>
<h2>Safety Information</h2>
<h3>Contraindications</h3>
<ul>
<li>Type 1 diabetes (ketoacidosis risk without supervision)</li>
<li>Pancreatic insufficiency</li>
<li>Liver failure</li>
<li>Fat-malabsorption disorders (carnitine or fatty acid oxidation defects)</li>
<li>Porphyria</li>
<li>Pregnancy and breastfeeding (insufficient data)</li>
</ul>
<h3>Side Effects</h3>
<ul>
<li>Keto flu during adaptation (fatigue, headache, nausea, 1-2 weeks)</li>
<li>Constipation (low fiber intake)</li>
<li>Nutrient deficiencies (B vitamins, vitamin C, fiber, potassium)</li>
<li>Elevated LDL cholesterol in some individuals</li>
<li>Kidney stone risk (increased uric acid)</li>
<li>Halitosis (acetone breath)</li>
</ul>
