<h1>Intermittent Fasting</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Intermittent fasting (IF) is a dietary pattern cycling between defined periods of eating and voluntary fasting. Major protocols include 16:8 (16 hours fasting, 8 hours eating), 5:2 (5 normal days, 2 days at 500-600 kcal), alternate-day fasting (ADF), and extended fasts (24-72 hours). IF is distinguished from caloric restriction by its emphasis on meal timing rather than total calorie reduction, though caloric deficits often occur naturally due to compressed eating windows.</p>
<p>Evidence from both animal and human studies demonstrates benefits for metabolic health markers including fasting insulin, insulin sensitivity (HOMA-IR), body weight (-3-8% over 3-12 months), visceral fat, blood lipids, and inflammatory markers. The metabolic switch from glucose to fatty acid/ketone oxidation typically occurs at 12-16 hours of fasting, activating autophagy, AMPK, and sirtuin longevity pathways. However, recent large-scale data (AHA 2024 epidemiological analysis) raised concerns about cardiovascular mortality with extreme time-restricted eating (&lt;8h window), highlighting the importance of protocol selection and individual suitability. IF is most effective when the eating window aligns with circadian rhythms (earlier in the day).</p>
<h2>Mechanism</h2>
<p>Extending the fasting period beyond 12-16 hours triggers the &quot;metabolic switch&quot; from hepatic glycogen utilization to fatty acid mobilization and ketogenesis. This shift activates multiple longevity and cellular maintenance pathways: AMPK (energy sensor) upregulates autophagy and mitochondrial biogenesis; sirtuins (SIRT1, SIRT3) enhance DNA repair and metabolic efficiency; mTOR suppression reduces anabolic signaling and promotes cellular cleanup via autophagy-lysosomal degradation of damaged proteins and organelles. Fasting reduces circulating insulin levels by 20-50%, restoring insulin receptor sensitivity. Growth hormone secretion increases 200-300% during extended fasts, preserving lean mass. BDNF production increases, supporting neuroplasticity and neuroprotection.</p>
<h2>Evidence Summary</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Weight loss</strong>: 3-8% body weight reduction over 3-12 months across protocols (meta-analysis of 27 RCTs, Evidence Level 1a)</li>
<li><strong>Insulin sensitivity</strong>: Fasting insulin reduced 20-31%, HOMA-IR improved significantly (Evidence Level 1a)</li>
<li><strong>Autophagy</strong>: Activated at 16-24h of fasting; peak at 48-72h in humans (Evidence Level 2b)</li>
<li><strong>Cardiovascular markers</strong>: Improved LDL, triglycerides, blood pressure in metabolically unhealthy populations (Evidence Level 1b)</li>
<li><strong>Inflammation</strong>: Reduced IL-6, TNF-alpha, and CRP with regular IF (Evidence Level 2a)</li>
<li><strong>Cognitive function</strong>: Increased BDNF and improved memory in animal models; human data limited (Evidence Level 3a)</li>
<li><strong>Longevity</strong>: Caloric restriction/IF extends lifespan in animal models; human longevity data pending (Evidence Level 2b)</li>
<li><strong>Safety concern</strong>: AHA 2024 observational data suggested cardiovascular risk with &lt;8h eating windows; causality not established (Evidence Level 3b)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Protocol</h2>
<h3>Dosing Information</h3>
<p>Begin with 12:12 and gradually extend to 16:8 over 2-4 weeks. Eating window ideally 8am-4pm (early TRE) for circadian alignment. Hydration critical during fasting (water, black coffee, tea). Electrolytes (sodium, potassium, magnesium) for fasts &gt;24 hours.</p>
<h2>Safety Information</h2>
<h3>Contraindications</h3>
<ul>
<li>Pregnancy and breastfeeding</li>
<li>History of eating disorders</li>
<li>Type 1 diabetes (without medical supervision)</li>
<li>Children and adolescents</li>
<li>Severe underweight (BMI &lt;18.5)</li>
<li>Active gallstone disease</li>
<li>Advanced chronic kidney disease</li>
<li>Certain medications requiring food</li>
</ul>
<h3>Side Effects</h3>
<ul>
<li>Initial hunger (adapts within 1-2 weeks)</li>
<li>Headaches (often electrolyte-related)</li>
<li>Fatigue during adaptation (1-2 weeks)</li>
<li>Irritability (&quot;hangry&quot;)</li>
<li>Difficulty concentrating initially</li>
<li>Constipation (inadequate hydration/fiber)</li>
<li>Potential overeating during eating window</li>
</ul>
