<h1>Sauna</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Sauna bathing is a form of passive heat therapy using dry heat (Finnish sauna, 80-100C) or infrared radiation (infrared sauna, 40-60C). The practice has deep roots in Finnish culture and is now studied extensively for cardiovascular and longevity benefits. The landmark Kuopio Ischemic Heart Disease (KIHD) cohort (2,315 Finnish men, 20-year follow-up) demonstrated a dose-response relationship between sauna frequency and cardiovascular mortality, with 4-7 sessions per week associated with a 63% reduction in sudden cardiac death compared to 1 session per week.</p>
<p>Sauna use triggers a hormetic stress response with physiological adaptations similar to moderate-intensity exercise: heart rate increases to 100-150 bpm, cardiac output rises, and peripheral vasodilation occurs. Regular sauna use improves endothelial function, reduces blood pressure, enhances heat shock protein expression, and may reduce the risk of dementia, respiratory diseases, and all-cause mortality. The combination of sauna and cold exposure (contrast therapy) is increasingly popular and may amplify hormetic benefits.</p>
<p><strong>Type</strong>: heat_therapy</p>
<h2>Mechanism</h2>
<p>Thermal stress activates heat shock proteins (HSP70, HSP90, HSP27) that function as molecular chaperones, preventing protein misfolding and enhancing cellular stress resistance. Core temperature elevation to 38.5-39C increases heart rate to 100-150 bpm, mimicking moderate cardiovascular exercise. Enhanced nitric oxide (NO) production via eNOS activation improves endothelial function and reduces arterial stiffness. Acute sympathetic nervous system activation transitions to parasympathetic dominance post-session. Growth hormone release increases up to 200-300% during prolonged sessions (especially with multiple rounds). Sweating facilitates elimination of heavy metals (arsenic, cadmium, lead, mercury) and BPA. Infrared sauna specifically penetrates tissue 3-4cm deep, directly heating muscle and subcutaneous tissue.</p>
<h2>Evidence Summary</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Cardiovascular mortality</strong>: 63% reduction in sudden cardiac death with 4-7 sessions/week vs 1/week (KIHD cohort, 20-year follow-up, Laukkanen et al., 2015, JAMA Internal Medicine, Evidence Level 2a)</li>
<li><strong>All-cause mortality</strong>: 40% reduction with frequent sauna use (KIHD cohort, Evidence Level 2a)</li>
<li><strong>Blood pressure</strong>: 3-7 mmHg reduction in systolic BP with regular use (Evidence Level 2a)</li>
<li><strong>Dementia risk</strong>: 65% reduced risk of dementia and 66% reduced Alzheimer's risk with 4-7 sessions/week (KIHD, Evidence Level 2b)</li>
<li><strong>Heat shock proteins</strong>: HSP70 and HSP90 upregulated; neuroprotective and cardioprotective (Evidence Level 2b)</li>
<li><strong>Growth hormone</strong>: 200-300% increase, especially with multiple rounds and heat acclimation (Evidence Level 2b)</li>
<li><strong>Respiratory disease</strong>: Reduced risk of pneumonia and common respiratory infections (Evidence Level 2b)</li>
<li><strong>Heavy metal detoxification</strong>: Sweat contains measurable arsenic, cadmium, lead, mercury (Evidence Level 3a)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Protocol</h2>
<h3>Dosing Information</h3>
<p>15-20 minutes per session, 2-7 times per week at 80-100C (Finnish) or 40-60C (infrared). Multiple rounds with cold plunge between sessions amplify hormetic response. Hydrate with 500mL water per session. Avoid alcohol before or during.</p>
<h2>Safety Information</h2>
<h3>Contraindications</h3>
<ul>
<li>Unstable angina or recent myocardial infarction</li>
<li>Severe aortic stenosis</li>
<li>Acute illness with fever</li>
<li>Pregnancy (especially first trimester)</li>
<li>Uncontrolled hypertension</li>
<li>Alcohol intoxication</li>
<li>Recent stroke (&lt;3 months)</li>
</ul>
