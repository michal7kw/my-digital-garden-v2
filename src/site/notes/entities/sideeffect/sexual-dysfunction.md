<h1>Sexual Dysfunction</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>SSRI-induced sexual dysfunction (SSD) is the most clinically significant and common adverse effect of SSRI antidepressants, affecting 30-70% of patients depending on the specific SSRI and assessment method. It encompasses the full sexual response cycle:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Decreased libido</strong> (reduced sexual desire) — most common complaint</li>
<li><strong>Arousal difficulties</strong> (erectile dysfunction in men, reduced lubrication in women)</li>
<li><strong>Delayed orgasm or anorgasmia</strong> (inability to reach orgasm) — often most distressing</li>
<li><strong>Decreased genital sensitivity</strong></li>
</ul>
<p>SSD is the leading cause of SSRI non-adherence, with up to 40% of patients discontinuing medication due to sexual side effects. Importantly, many patients do not spontaneously report these effects — direct, specific questioning is essential.</p>
<h2>Mechanism</h2>
<p>SSRI-induced sexual dysfunction results from increased serotonergic activity at specific receptor subtypes:</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>5-HT2A/2C activation</strong> in mesolimbic pathway → suppresses dopamine release in nucleus accumbens → reduces sexual desire and reward</li>
<li><strong>5-HT2A activation</strong> in spinal cord → inhibits sexual reflexes (ejaculation, orgasm)</li>
<li><strong>5-HT2C activation</strong> → inhibits norepinephrine release → reduced arousal</li>
<li><strong>Nitric oxide reduction</strong> → serotonin-mediated decrease in peripheral NO → erectile/lubrication difficulties</li>
<li><strong>Peripheral SERT blockade</strong> on platelets and smooth muscle → may affect genital blood flow</li>
</ol>

<h2>Management Strategies</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Wait and watch</strong>: Some tolerance develops in 30% of patients over 6-12 months</li>
<li><strong>Dose reduction</strong>: If clinically feasible</li>
<li><strong>Switch SSRI</strong>: To vortioxetine, vilazodone, or bupropion (non-serotonergic)</li>
<li><strong>Augmentation</strong>: Bupropion (adds dopaminergic activity), buspirone (5-HT1A agonist)</li>
<li><strong>Drug holidays</strong>: Sertraline and paroxetine allow brief weekend holidays (controversial)</li>
<li><strong>PDE5 inhibitors</strong>: Sildenafil for SSRI-induced erectile dysfunction (effective)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Post-SSRI Sexual Dysfunction (PSSD)</h2>
<p>A controversial but increasingly recognized condition where sexual dysfunction persists for months or years after SSRI discontinuation. Prevalence and mechanism are poorly understood. EMA (European Medicines Agency) updated SSRI labeling in 2019 to include PSSD warnings.</p>
