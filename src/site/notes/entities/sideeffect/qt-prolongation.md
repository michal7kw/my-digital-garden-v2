<h1>QT Prolongation</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>QT prolongation refers to the abnormal lengthening of the QT interval on the electrocardiogram (ECG), reflecting delayed ventricular repolarization. The QT interval spans from the onset of the QRS complex (ventricular depolarization) to the end of the T wave (ventricular repolarization) and is corrected for heart rate (QTc) using Bazett's or Fridericia's formula. A QTc exceeding 450 ms in males or 460 ms in females is generally considered prolonged, with values above 500 ms carrying substantially increased risk of life-threatening arrhythmias.</p>
<p>The primary danger of QT prolongation is torsades de pointes (TdP), a polymorphic ventricular tachycardia that can degenerate into ventricular fibrillation and sudden cardiac death. TdP arises from early afterdepolarizations triggered by the prolonged action potential, creating a vulnerable substrate for re-entrant arrhythmias. At the molecular level, most drug-induced QT prolongation results from blockade of the hERG (human ether-a-go-go-related gene) potassium channel, which conducts the rapid component of the delayed rectifier potassium current (IKr) essential for cardiac repolarization. The hERG channel has an unusually promiscuous drug-binding site that accommodates structurally diverse compounds, explaining why QT prolongation is observed across many drug classes.</p>
<p>Electrolyte imbalances significantly modify the risk of drug-induced TdP. Hypokalemia is the most important predisposing factor, as low extracellular potassium reduces IKr current and synergistically prolongs repolarization. <a href="/entities/labtest/potassium/" class="internal-link">Potassium</a> replacement to maintain levels above 4.0 mEq/L is a cornerstone of risk mitigation. Hypomagnesemia similarly increases TdP risk, and intravenous <a href="/entities/supplement/magnesium/" class="internal-link">Magnesium</a> sulfate is the first-line treatment for acute TdP regardless of serum magnesium level, as magnesium suppresses early afterdepolarizations and stabilizes the cardiac action potential. The <a href="/entities/organ/heart/" class="internal-link">Heart</a> is uniquely sensitive to these electrolyte perturbations because of the narrow safety margin between normal repolarization and arrhythmogenic threshold.</p>
<p>Drug-induced QT prolongation has been a leading cause of drug withdrawal and regulatory action. Cisapride, terfenadine, and grepafloxacin were all removed from markets primarily due to TdP risk. Current FDA guidance (ICH E14) requires thorough QT studies for virtually all new drugs. Risk factors for TdP beyond drug exposure include female sex (women have inherently longer QTc), congenital long QT syndrome, bradycardia, structural heart disease, hepatic impairment (altered drug metabolism), and concomitant use of multiple QT-prolonging drugs or CYP3A4 inhibitors.</p>
<h2>Key Mechanisms</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>hERG channel blockade</strong>: Drug binding to the IKr channel pore reduces repolarizing potassium current</li>
<li><strong>Action potential prolongation</strong>: Extended plateau phase increases window for early afterdepolarizations</li>
<li><strong>Torsades de pointes initiation</strong>: Early afterdepolarizations trigger re-entrant circuits in heterogeneous repolarization</li>
<li><strong>Electrolyte modulation</strong>: Hypokalemia and hypomagnesemia reduce repolarization reserve</li>
<li><strong>Transmural dispersion</strong>: Differential prolongation across epicardial, midmyocardial (M-cells), and endocardial layers</li>
<li><strong>CYP3A4 interactions</strong>: Inhibition of drug metabolism increases plasma levels and QT-prolonging effect</li>
</ul>
