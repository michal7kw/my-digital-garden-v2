<h1>Gastrointestinal Bleeding</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Gastrointestinal (GI) bleeding is one of the most clinically significant drug-induced adverse effects, encompassing a spectrum from asymptomatic occult blood loss and iron deficiency anemia to acute upper GI hemorrhage requiring emergency intervention. The annual incidence of upper GI bleeding is approximately 50-150 per 100,000 population, and drug-induced causes account for a substantial proportion. NSAIDs (non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs) and anticoagulants/antiplatelet agents are the primary pharmacological culprits, often acting through distinct but sometimes overlapping mechanisms.</p>
<p>NSAIDs, including <a href="/entities/drug/aspirin/" class="internal-link">Aspirin</a> and <a href="/entities/drug/ibuprofen/" class="internal-link">Ibuprofen</a>, cause GI bleeding primarily through inhibition of cyclooxygenase-1 (COX-1), which catalyzes the synthesis of prostaglandins PGE2 and PGI2 in the gastric mucosa. These prostaglandins play critical cytoprotective roles: stimulating mucus and bicarbonate secretion, maintaining mucosal blood flow, and promoting epithelial cell renewal. COX-1 inhibition removes this prostaglandin-dependent mucosal defense, rendering the <a href="/entities/organ/stomach/" class="internal-link">Stomach</a> and duodenum vulnerable to acid-peptic injury. Aspirin irreversibly acetylates COX-1 in platelets (which lack nuclei and cannot resynthesize the enzyme), producing a dual insult: mucosal damage plus impaired platelet-dependent hemostasis. Even low-dose aspirin (75-100 mg/day) used for cardiovascular prophylaxis doubles the risk of GI bleeding.</p>
<p><a href="/entities/supplement/omega-3-fatty-acids/" class="internal-link">Omega-3 Fatty Acids</a> at high doses (&gt;3 g/day of EPA+DHA) may also increase bleeding tendency through multiple mechanisms: competition with arachidonic acid for COX-mediated thromboxane synthesis, altered platelet membrane fluidity, and production of less potent thromboxane A3. While the clinical significance of omega-3-related bleeding is debated (large trials have not shown major hemorrhagic events), caution is warranted in patients on concurrent anticoagulant or antiplatelet therapy, and perioperative guidelines often recommend cessation of high-dose fish oil before surgery.</p>
<p>Risk stratification for drug-induced GI bleeding involves patient factors (age &gt;65, prior GI bleed, H. pylori infection, concurrent corticosteroids or SSRIs) and drug factors (NSAID type, dose, duration, concurrent anticoagulation). Gastroprotective strategies include co-prescription of proton pump inhibitors (PPIs), use of COX-2-selective NSAIDs (celecoxib), H. pylori eradication, and minimizing NSAID dose and duration. For patients requiring long-term antiplatelet therapy, PPI co-therapy reduces the risk of upper GI bleeding by approximately 60-80%.</p>
<h2>Key Mechanisms</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>COX-1 inhibition</strong>: Loss of cytoprotective prostaglandins (PGE2, PGI2) in gastric mucosa</li>
<li><strong>Platelet inhibition</strong>: Irreversible COX-1 acetylation by aspirin impairs thromboxane A2-dependent hemostasis</li>
<li><strong>Direct mucosal toxicity</strong>: Acidic NSAIDs cause topical epithelial damage via ion trapping in mucosal cells</li>
<li><strong>Anticoagulant-related</strong>: Warfarin, DOACs impair coagulation cascade without mucosal injury</li>
<li><strong>Dual pathway risk</strong>: Combined antiplatelet + anticoagulant therapy dramatically increases hemorrhagic risk</li>
<li><strong>Omega-3 effects</strong>: Thromboxane A3 production, altered platelet aggregation at high doses</li>
</ul>
