<h1>Hepatotoxicity</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Hepatotoxicity, or drug-induced liver injury (DILI), refers to liver damage caused by medications, supplements, or other xenobiotics. It is the single most common reason for post-marketing withdrawal of approved drugs and accounts for approximately 50% of cases of acute liver failure in the United States. The <a href="/entities/organ/liver/" class="internal-link">Liver</a> is uniquely vulnerable to chemical injury because it is the primary site of drug metabolism, receiving the entire portal venous blood supply from the gastrointestinal tract and concentrating both parent compounds and their reactive metabolites.</p>
<p>Two fundamental patterns of hepatotoxicity are recognized. Intrinsic (dose-dependent) hepatotoxicity occurs predictably at a given dose threshold and is exemplified by <a href="/entities/drug/acetaminophen/" class="internal-link">Acetaminophen</a> (paracetamol) overdose -- the leading cause of acute liver failure in Western countries. Acetaminophen is normally conjugated by glucuronidation and sulfation, but at supratherapeutic doses these pathways become saturated and CYP2E1 generates the reactive metabolite NAPQI (N-acetyl-p-benzoquinone imine). NAPQI depletes hepatic glutathione stores and, when glutathione is exhausted, covalently binds to cellular proteins causing mitochondrial dysfunction, oxidative stress, and hepatocellular necrosis. <a href="/entities/supplement/nac/" class="internal-link">NAC</a> (N-acetylcysteine) is the specific antidote for acetaminophen poisoning, replenishing glutathione and scavenging NAPQI when administered within 8-10 hours of ingestion.</p>
<p>Idiosyncratic (dose-independent) hepatotoxicity is unpredictable, occurs in susceptible individuals regardless of dose, and often involves immune-mediated mechanisms (HLA-associated drug hypersensitivity) or metabolic idiosyncrasy (genetic variation in drug-metabolizing enzymes). Examples include isoniazid, amoxicillin-clavulanate, and diclofenac. Herbal and dietary supplements are increasingly recognized as causes of DILI, accounting for approximately 20% of cases in prospective registries. Green tea extract (high-dose EGCG), kava, black cohosh, and pyrrolizidine alkaloid-containing herbs are among the most commonly implicated supplements.</p>
<p>Monitoring for hepatotoxicity relies on serum liver biochemistry, particularly <a href="/entities/biomarker/alanine-transaminase/" class="internal-link">Alanine Transaminase</a> (ALT), which is relatively liver-specific and rises early in hepatocellular injury. Patterns of injury are classified as hepatocellular (ALT-predominant), cholestatic (ALP-predominant), or mixed, and the R-ratio (ALT/ALP, each expressed as multiples of ULN) guides classification. Hy's Law -- a combination of hepatocellular injury (ALT &gt;3x ULN) with jaundice (bilirubin &gt;2x ULN) in the absence of biliary obstruction -- identifies patients at approximately 10% risk of fatal outcome and is the key regulatory signal for drug development programs.</p>
<h2>Key Mechanisms</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Reactive metabolite formation</strong>: CYP450-mediated bioactivation generates electrophilic metabolites that bind cellular proteins</li>
<li><strong>Glutathione depletion</strong>: Reactive metabolites consume GSH; NAC restores GSH and directly scavenges reactive species</li>
<li><strong>Mitochondrial dysfunction</strong>: Impaired electron transport chain, uncoupled OXPHOS, MPT pore opening</li>
<li><strong>Immune-mediated injury</strong>: Drug-protein adducts act as neoantigens triggering adaptive immune attack (HLA-restricted)</li>
<li><strong>Bile salt export pump inhibition</strong>: BSEP inhibition causes intracellular bile acid accumulation and cholestatic injury</li>
<li><strong>Oxidative stress amplification</strong>: ROS generation, lipid peroxidation, and inflammatory cytokine release</li>
</ul>
