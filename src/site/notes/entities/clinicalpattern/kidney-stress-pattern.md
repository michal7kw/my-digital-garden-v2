<h1>Kidney Stress Pattern</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Impaired kidney function indicated by elevated creatinine, BUN, and reduced eGFR. Early detection enables intervention to slow progression.</p>
<h2>Symptom Cluster</h2>
<p>Kidney stress is frequently asymptomatic in early stages (CKD stages 1-3a), which is why laboratory screening is critical. As kidney function declines, the following symptom cluster emerges:</p>
<ul>
<li>Fatigue and reduced exercise tolerance (accumulation of uremic toxins, anemia from reduced erythropoietin production, and metabolic acidosis)</li>
<li>Nocturia (increased nighttime urination) -- often the earliest symptom, reflecting impaired urine concentrating ability</li>
<li>Foamy urine (indicates proteinuria from glomerular damage)</li>
<li>Peripheral edema (ankles, feet, periorbital swelling) from sodium and fluid retention</li>
<li>Changes in urination: decreased urine output, or paradoxically increased frequency in early CKD</li>
<li>Loss of appetite, nausea, metallic taste (uremic toxin accumulation affecting GI tract)</li>
<li>Pruritus (itching) -- from phosphate retention, calcium-phosphate deposition in skin, and elevated parathyroid hormone</li>
<li>Muscle cramps (electrolyte imbalances: hyperkalemia, hypocalcemia, hyperphosphatemia)</li>
<li>Cognitive changes: difficulty concentrating, confusion (uremic encephalopathy in advanced disease)</li>
<li>Hypertension (both cause and consequence of kidney disease; present in 80-90% of CKD patients)</li>
<li>Bone pain (renal osteodystrophy from secondary hyperparathyroidism in advanced CKD)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Diagnostic Criteria</h2>
<p>Based on KDIGO (Kidney Disease: Improving Global Outcomes) 2012 Clinical Practice Guidelines:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>eGFR (estimated glomerular filtration rate)</strong>: Primary staging biomarker; calculated from serum creatinine using CKD-EPI equation (2021 race-free equation preferred); eGFR &lt;60 mL/min/1.73m2 sustained for &gt;3 months defines CKD stage 3+; staging: G1 (&gt;90, normal), G2 (60-89, mild decrease), G3a (45-59, mild-moderate), G3b (30-44, moderate-severe), G4 (15-29, severe), G5 (&lt;15, kidney failure)</li>
<li><strong>Serum creatinine</strong>: &gt;1.2 mg/dL (male) or &gt;1.0 mg/dL (female) suggests impaired filtration; however, creatinine is influenced by muscle mass, diet, and hydration, making it an imperfect marker alone</li>
<li><strong>BUN (blood urea nitrogen)</strong>: &gt;20 mg/dL; BUN:creatinine ratio &gt;20:1 suggests prerenal cause (dehydration, heart failure), while a proportional rise suggests intrinsic renal disease</li>
<li><strong>Cystatin C</strong>: More accurate than creatinine for eGFR estimation in patients with extremes of muscle mass (sarcopenic elderly, bodybuilders); CKD-EPI creatinine-cystatin C equation is most accurate</li>
<li><strong>Albuminuria staging</strong>: Urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio (UACR): A1 (&lt;30 mg/g, normal), A2 (30-300 mg/g, moderately increased / &quot;microalbuminuria&quot;), A3 (&gt;300 mg/g, severely increased / &quot;macroalbuminuria&quot;); albuminuria is an independent predictor of CKD progression and cardiovascular risk</li>
<li><strong>Pattern trigger in this KG</strong>: 2 of 3 criteria met: eGFR &lt;60, creatinine &gt;1.2, BUN &gt;20</li>
<li><strong>Supplementary workup</strong>: Urinalysis with microscopy (casts, dysmorphic RBCs), electrolyte panel (potassium, phosphate, bicarbonate), intact PTH, 25-OH vitamin D, hemoglobin (anemia screening), renal ultrasound (kidney size, obstruction, cysts)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Differential Diagnosis</h2>
<p>Elevated creatinine or reduced eGFR does not always indicate chronic kidney disease. Key differentials:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Prerenal azotemia (most common acute cause)</strong>: Dehydration, heart failure, hepatorenal syndrome, NSAID-induced renal vasoconstriction, ACE-I/ARB initiation -- all reduce renal perfusion without intrinsic kidney damage; BUN:creatinine ratio &gt;20:1; reversible with volume repletion or removal of offending agent; fractional excretion of sodium (FENa) &lt;1%</li>
<li><strong>Acute kidney injury (AKI)</strong>: Rapid decline in kidney function over hours to days; distinguished from CKD by baseline creatinine comparison, lack of small echogenic kidneys on ultrasound, and often reversible; causes include sepsis, nephrotoxins (aminoglycosides, contrast dye, NSAIDs), rhabdomyolysis, urinary obstruction</li>
<li><strong>Falsely elevated creatinine</strong>: High muscle mass (bodybuilders, athletes), creatine supplementation, high dietary meat intake, and certain medications (trimethoprim, cimetidine) can elevate creatinine without impaired kidney function; cystatin C resolves the discrepancy</li>
<li><strong>Diabetic nephropathy</strong>: The most common cause of CKD in developed countries; progressive albuminuria (microalbuminuria to macroalbuminuria over years), declining eGFR, and often concurrent diabetic retinopathy; requires strict glycemic and blood pressure control</li>
<li><strong>Hypertensive nephrosclerosis</strong>: Second most common cause; benign nephrosclerosis from long-standing hypertension; typically modest proteinuria (&lt;1 g/day) with gradual eGFR decline</li>
<li><strong>Glomerulonephritis</strong>: IgA nephropathy, membranous nephropathy, focal segmental glomerulosclerosis (FSGS), lupus nephritis -- distinguished by active urine sediment (RBC casts, dysmorphic RBCs), nephrotic-range proteinuria, and often requires renal biopsy for definitive diagnosis</li>
<li><strong>Polycystic kidney disease (PKD)</strong>: Genetic (autosomal dominant); enlarged kidneys with multiple cysts on ultrasound; family history; distinct management including tolvaptan</li>
</ul>
<h2>Management Approach</h2>
<p>Management goals are to slow CKD progression, prevent complications, and protect cardiovascular health (cardiovascular disease is the leading cause of death in CKD patients):</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Blood pressure control (most important modifiable factor)</strong>: Target &lt;130/80 mmHg (KDIGO 2021); ACE-I or ARB preferred (reduce intraglomerular pressure and proteinuria); do not combine ACE-I + ARB; tolerate up to 30% creatinine rise after initiation (expected hemodynamic effect); SGLT2 inhibitors (dapagliflozin, empagliflozin) now recommended for CKD with eGFR &gt;20 regardless of diabetes status (DAPA-CKD and EMPA-KIDNEY trials)</li>
<li><strong>Glycemic control (if diabetic)</strong>: HbA1c target 6.5-7.5% (avoid tight control if eGFR &lt;30 due to hypoglycemia risk); metformin dose-reduce at eGFR 30-45, discontinue at eGFR &lt;30; SGLT2 inhibitors provide dual glycemic and renoprotective benefit</li>
<li><strong>Dietary modifications</strong>: Moderate protein intake (0.8 g/kg/day for CKD 3-5, avoid &gt;1.3 g/kg/day which accelerates progression); sodium restriction &lt;2 g/day (reduces proteinuria and blood pressure); potassium restriction if hyperkalemic; phosphorus restriction if elevated (limit processed foods, cola, dairy); adequate hydration (typically 1.5-2 L/day unless fluid-restricted)</li>
<li><strong>Avoid nephrotoxins</strong>: NSAIDs (reduce renal blood flow), aminoglycoside antibiotics, iodinated contrast dye (use with hydration protocol if essential), herbal supplements with unknown renal effects, excessive protein supplements</li>
<li><strong>Supplement considerations</strong>: Vitamin D (most CKD patients are deficient; cholecalciferol to maintain 25-OH D 30-50 ng/mL; active vitamin D/calcitriol if eGFR &lt;30), omega-3 fatty acids (anti-inflammatory and cardiovascular benefit), sodium bicarbonate (if metabolic acidosis, serum bicarbonate &lt;22), alpha-lipoic acid (antioxidant nephroprotection), CoQ10 (mitochondrial support)</li>
<li><strong>Monitoring</strong>: eGFR and UACR at least annually for CKD 1-2, every 3-6 months for CKD 3-4; electrolytes, phosphate, calcium, PTH, and hemoglobin per KDIGO frequency recommendations; nephrology referral at eGFR &lt;30, rapidly declining eGFR (&gt;5 mL/min/year), or unexplained hematuria/proteinuria</li>
<li><strong>Anemia management</strong>: Screen for iron deficiency (ferritin &lt;100 ng/mL or TSAT &lt;20% in CKD); IV iron if oral fails; erythropoiesis-stimulating agents (ESAs) if Hb &lt;10 g/dL after iron optimization; target Hb 10-11.5 g/dL (avoid &gt;13)</li>
</ul>
