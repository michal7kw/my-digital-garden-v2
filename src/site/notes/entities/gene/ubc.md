<h1>UBC</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Ubiquitin C (UBC) is one of four genes in the human genome that encode ubiquitin, a highly conserved 76-amino acid protein fundamental to virtually all eukaryotic cellular processes. UBC encodes a polyubiquitin precursor consisting of tandem ubiquitin repeats that are cleaved into individual ubiquitin monomers by deubiquitinating enzymes. As a stress-responsive gene, UBC expression is upregulated under conditions of cellular stress including heat shock, oxidative damage, and proteotoxic insults, making it the primary source of ubiquitin during stress conditions.</p>
<p>Ubiquitin functions as a post-translational modifier that is covalently attached to target proteins through an enzymatic cascade involving E1 activating enzymes, E2 conjugating enzymes, and E3 ligase enzymes. The attachment of ubiquitin chains (polyubiquitination) to substrate proteins serves as a molecular signal that can direct proteins for proteasomal degradation, regulate DNA repair, modulate signal transduction, and control endocytic trafficking.</p>
<h2>Function</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Protein degradation</strong>: Polyubiquitin chains linked through lysine 48 (K48) mark proteins for destruction by the 26S proteasome, maintaining protein quality control</li>
<li><strong>DNA damage response</strong>: K63-linked ubiquitin chains recruit DNA repair factors to sites of double-strand breaks and stalled replication forks</li>
<li><strong>Signal transduction</strong>: Monoubiquitination and K63-linked chains regulate NF-kB signaling, receptor internalization, and kinase activation</li>
<li><strong>Autophagy</strong>: Ubiquitin tags damaged organelles and protein aggregates for selective autophagy</li>
<li><strong>Cell cycle regulation</strong>: Ubiquitin-mediated degradation of cyclins and CDK inhibitors drives cell cycle progression</li>
<li><strong>Stress response</strong>: UBC expression increases under heat shock, oxidative stress, and endoplasmic reticulum stress to replenish the free ubiquitin pool</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Neurodegenerative diseases</strong>: Ubiquitin-positive inclusions are hallmarks of Alzheimer's disease, Parkinson's disease, and ALS; impaired ubiquitin-proteasome system (UPS) function contributes to pathological protein accumulation</li>
<li><strong>Cancer</strong>: Dysregulation of ubiquitin-dependent proteolysis affects tumor suppressors (p53, PTEN) and oncoproteins; UBC overexpression is observed in multiple cancer types</li>
<li><strong>Immune disorders</strong>: Ubiquitination regulates innate and adaptive immune signaling; defects cause autoinflammatory conditions</li>
<li><strong>Therapeutic target</strong>: Proteasome inhibitors (bortezomib, carfilzomib) exploit ubiquitin-dependent degradation in multiple myeloma treatment</li>
</ul>
