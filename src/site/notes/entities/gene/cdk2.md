<h1>CDK2</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>CDK2 encodes Cyclin-Dependent Kinase 2, a 298-amino acid serine/threonine protein kinase (33.9 kDa) that serves as a catalytic subunit of the CDK2-cyclin E and CDK2-cyclin A complexes. It is a master regulator of the G1/S phase transition and S-phase progression in the cell cycle, and plays additional roles in DNA damage response, centrosome duplication, and meiosis.</p>
<p>CDK2 activity is tightly controlled through multiple mechanisms: cyclin binding (cyclins E and A), activating phosphorylation by CDK-activating kinase (CAK), inhibitory phosphorylation by Wee1/Myt1 kinases, and binding of CDK inhibitors (p21/CDKN1A, p27/CDKN1B). This multi-layered regulation ensures precise timing of DNA replication initiation and prevents premature or aberrant S-phase entry.</p>
<h2>Function</h2>
<p>At the G1/S transition, CDK2-cyclin E phosphorylates the retinoblastoma protein (Rb), releasing E2F transcription factors to activate genes required for DNA replication. CDK2-cyclin E also phosphorylates its own inhibitor p27 to trigger its degradation, creating a positive feedback loop. During S-phase, CDK2-cyclin A phosphorylates DNA replication factors including CDC6 and MCM proteins to prevent re-replication, and also phosphorylates E2F1 to terminate its transcriptional program. CDK2 contributes to centrosome duplication by phosphorylating nucleophosmin (NPM1) and CP110. In the DNA damage response, CDK2 is inhibited through p53-dependent p21 induction, enforcing cell cycle arrest. CDK2 also crosstalks with the mTOR pathway through phosphorylation of substrates involved in cell growth signaling.</p>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<p>CDK2 is frequently hyperactivated in cancer through multiple mechanisms: cyclin E amplification/overexpression (breast, ovarian cancer), loss of CDK inhibitors p21 or p27 (many tumor types), and upstream oncogenic signaling. While CDK2 was initially considered essential for proliferation, CDK2 knockout mice are viable (though sterile), suggesting compensatory mechanisms by CDK1 in somatic cells. This has complicated drug development, though selective CDK2 inhibitors are now in clinical trials for CCNE1-amplified cancers where cells are uniquely dependent on CDK2 activity. Pan-CDK inhibitors such as palbociclib primarily target CDK4/6, but newer compounds with CDK2 selectivity are showing promise in cyclin E-driven tumors resistant to CDK4/6 inhibition.</p>
