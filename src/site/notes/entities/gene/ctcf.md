<h1>CTCF</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>CTCF (CCCTC-Binding Factor) encodes an 11-zinc-finger transcription factor that functions as a master regulator of chromatin architecture, gene expression, and genome organization. CTCF is one of the most highly conserved transcription factors in bilaterian organisms, reflecting its fundamental importance in genome regulation. It recognizes a 20-bp consensus motif and binds to approximately 40,000-80,000 sites across the human genome, many of which are invariant across cell types and species.</p>
<p>CTCF functions primarily as a chromatin insulator and architectural protein. Together with the cohesin complex (including RAD21, SMC1, SMC3), CTCF defines the boundaries of topologically associating domains (TADs) -- self-interacting genomic regions that constrain enhancer-promoter communication. CTCF-mediated loops are formed through a loop extrusion mechanism where cohesin slides along chromatin until it encounters convergently oriented CTCF binding sites. This three-dimensional genome architecture is essential for proper gene regulation during development, X chromosome inactivation, and genomic imprinting.</p>
<h2>Function</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Chromatin insulator activity</strong>: Blocks enhancer-promoter interactions when positioned between them, preventing inappropriate gene activation across domain boundaries</li>
<li><strong>TAD boundary formation</strong>: Defines the borders of topologically associating domains in partnership with cohesin, compartmentalizing the genome into regulatory neighborhoods</li>
<li><strong>Genomic imprinting</strong>: At the H19/IGF2 locus, methylation-sensitive CTCF binding on the maternal allele controls parent-of-origin-specific gene expression</li>
<li><strong>X chromosome inactivation</strong>: Participates in X inactivation center regulation and choice of which X chromosome to silence</li>
<li><strong>Transcriptional regulation</strong>: Can function as an activator or repressor depending on context; regulates oncogenes (MYC), tumor suppressors (p53 targets), and developmental genes</li>
<li><strong>Recombination regulation</strong>: Influences V(D)J recombination at immunoglobulin and T-cell receptor loci by regulating chromatin accessibility</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Cancer</strong>: CTCF binding site mutations disrupt TAD boundaries, leading to oncogene activation (e.g., aberrant enhancer-hijacking of PDGFRA in glioblastoma, TAL1 in T-ALL); CTCF itself is mutated in endometrial cancer, breast cancer, and Wilms tumor</li>
<li><strong>Developmental disorders</strong>: Heterozygous CTCF mutations cause autosomal dominant intellectual disability with microcephaly and growth retardation (CTCF-related disorder)</li>
<li><strong>Genomic imprinting disorders</strong>: Disruption of CTCF binding at imprinted loci contributes to Beckwith-Wiedemann syndrome and Silver-Russell syndrome</li>
<li><strong>Epigenetic therapy</strong>: Understanding CTCF-mediated genome architecture informs strategies for epigenetic cancer therapy, including HDAC and BET inhibitor mechanisms</li>
</ul>
