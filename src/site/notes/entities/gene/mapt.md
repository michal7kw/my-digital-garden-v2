<h1>MAPT</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>MAPT (Microtubule-Associated Protein Tau) encodes the tau protein, a natively unfolded protein that stabilizes microtubules in axons and plays critical roles in axonal transport, neuronal polarity, and signal transduction. The MAPT gene is located on chromosome 17q21.31 and produces six tau isoforms in the adult human brain through alternative splicing of exons 2, 3, and 10, yielding proteins with three or four microtubule-binding repeats (3R or 4R tau).</p>
<p>While MAPT mutations do not directly cause Alzheimer's disease, tau protein is central to AD pathology as the primary component of neurofibrillary tangles. The MAPT H1 haplotype is a significant genetic risk factor for progressive supranuclear palsy, corticobasal degeneration, and Parkinson's disease.</p>
<h2>Function</h2>
<p>Tau protein binds to and stabilizes microtubules in neuronal axons, promoting microtubule assembly and preventing depolymerization. This stabilization is essential for maintaining axonal structure and enabling efficient anterograde and retrograde transport of organelles, vesicles, and signaling molecules along the axon. Tau's binding to microtubules is regulated by phosphorylation: moderate phosphorylation by kinases such as GSK-3beta and CDK5 reduces microtubule affinity, allowing dynamic remodeling, while hyperphosphorylation causes complete detachment from microtubules. Detached hyperphosphorylated tau misfolds and aggregates into paired helical filaments (PHFs), which accumulate as neurofibrillary tangles (NFTs). Tau also participates in signal transduction, interacts with the plasma membrane, and functions in DNA protection under stress conditions in the nucleus.</p>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<p>Mutations in MAPT cause frontotemporal dementia with parkinsonism linked to chromosome 17 (FTDP-17), a group of autosomal dominant tauopathies. Over 50 pathogenic MAPT mutations have been identified, affecting either tau protein function (missense mutations in microtubule-binding domains) or splicing regulation (intronic mutations altering the 3R/4R tau ratio). In Alzheimer's disease, while MAPT is not mutated, tau pathology (Braak staging of NFT distribution) correlates more closely with cognitive decline than amyloid plaque burden. The prion-like spread of pathological tau along anatomically connected brain regions is now recognized as a key mechanism of neurodegeneration. Anti-tau immunotherapies, tau aggregation inhibitors, and antisense oligonucleotides targeting MAPT expression are in clinical development. The MAPT H1/H2 haplotype structure also influences risk for progressive supranuclear palsy, corticobasal degeneration, and Parkinson's disease.</p>
