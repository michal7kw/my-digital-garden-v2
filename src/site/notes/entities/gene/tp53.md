<h1>TP53</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p><strong>TP53</strong> is arguably the most important tumor suppressor gene in the human genome, often referred to as the <strong>&quot;Guardian of the Genome.&quot;</strong> It encodes the protein <strong>p53</strong>, a transcription factor that regulates the cell cycle and protects genomic stability.</p>
<p>Under normal conditions, p53 levels are kept low by MDM2. However, in response to cellular stress (DNA damage, hypoxia, oncogene activation), p53 is stabilized and activated. It then orchestrates a response that either repairs the damage or, if the damage is irreparable, eliminates the cell.</p>
<h2>Biological Functions</h2>
<h3>1. Cell Cycle Arrest</h3>
<p>p53 induces cell cycle arrest at the G1/S checkpoint to allow time for DNA repair.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Mechanism:</strong> p53 transcriptionally activates <strong>p21 (CDKN1A)</strong>, which inhibits cyclin-dependent kinases (CDK2), halting the cell cycle.</li>
</ul>
<h3>2. DNA Repair</h3>
<p>p53 activates the expression of DNA repair genes (e.g., <em>GADD45</em>) to fix genetic errors before cell division proceeds.</p>
<h3>3. Apoptosis (Programmed Cell Death)</h3>
<p>If DNA damage is severe and unrepairable, p53 triggers apoptosis to prevent the propagation of mutations.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Mechanism:</strong> Upregulates pro-apoptotic proteins like <strong>BAX</strong>, <strong>PUMA</strong>, and <strong>NOXA</strong> while downregulating anti-apoptotic proteins like <strong>BCL-2</strong>.</li>
</ul>
<h3>4. Senescence</h3>
<p>p53 can induce permanent cell cycle arrest (senescence), preventing the proliferation of damaged cells without killing them.</p>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Cancer Mutations:</strong> Somatic mutations in TP53 are found in approximately <strong>50% of all human cancers</strong>, making it the most frequently mutated gene in cancer. Loss of p53 function allows cells with DNA damage to divide, leading to genomic instability and tumor progression.</li>
<li><strong>Li-Fraumeni Syndrome:</strong> A rare hereditary disorder caused by germline mutations in TP53. Carriers have a vastly increased risk of developing multiple types of cancer (sarcomas, breast, brain, adrenal) at a young age.</li>
</ul>
