<h1>SP1</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>SP1 (Specificity Protein 1) is a ubiquitously expressed zinc finger transcription factor that was among the first mammalian transcription factors to be identified and cloned. SP1 belongs to the Sp/KLF (Kruppel-Like Factor) superfamily and contains three Cys2His2-type zinc finger domains at its C-terminus that bind GC-rich sequences (GC boxes, 5'-GGGCGG-3') and GT/CACCC boxes in promoters and enhancers. SP1 activates transcription of thousands of genes, particularly housekeeping genes that lack TATA boxes and instead rely on GC-rich promoter elements.</p>
<p>SP1 is subject to extensive post-translational regulation including phosphorylation, O-GlcNAcylation, SUMOylation, ubiquitination, and acetylation, which modulate its transcriptional activity, stability, and DNA binding. SP1 interacts with components of the basal transcription machinery (TAF1, TBP), other transcription factors (NF-kB, GATA factors, p53), and chromatin modifiers. Its expression and activity change during development, aging, and disease states, making it both a constitutive transcription factor and a regulated effector of signaling pathways.</p>
<h2>Function</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Housekeeping gene regulation</strong>: Drives basal transcription of essential genes involved in cell cycle (p21, cyclin D1), metabolism (DHFR, adenosine deaminase), and growth (VEGF, PDGF)</li>
<li><strong>GC-rich promoter activation</strong>: Essential for transcription from TATA-less, GC-rich promoters that control many constitutively expressed and growth-regulated genes</li>
<li><strong>Chromatin remodeling interaction</strong>: Recruits histone acetyltransferases (p300/CBP) and SWI/SNF complexes to target promoters, facilitating chromatin accessibility</li>
<li><strong>Cell cycle regulation</strong>: Activates expression of cell cycle regulators (cyclin D1, p21, p27) and is itself regulated by cell cycle-dependent phosphorylation</li>
<li><strong>Lipid and glucose metabolism</strong>: Regulates expression of fatty acid synthase, sterol regulatory element-binding proteins, and insulin receptor; integrates metabolic signaling</li>
<li><strong>Apoptosis modulation</strong>: Activates pro-apoptotic genes (Bak, caspase-3) and anti-apoptotic genes (Bcl-2, survivin) in context-dependent fashion</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Cancer</strong>: SP1 is overexpressed in gastric, pancreatic, breast, and thyroid cancers; drives expression of pro-angiogenic (VEGF), pro-invasive (MMP-2, uPA), and anti-apoptotic genes; correlates with poor prognosis</li>
<li><strong>Metabolic disease</strong>: SP1-mediated regulation of insulin receptor, GLUT4 transporter, and lipogenic enzymes links SP1 dysregulation to insulin resistance and type 2 diabetes</li>
<li><strong>Neurodegenerative disease</strong>: SP1 overexpression in Huntington's disease is toxic to neurons; altered SP1-mediated transcription contributes to Alzheimer's disease pathology</li>
<li><strong>Aging</strong>: SP1 protein levels decline with age, contributing to decreased expression of longevity-associated genes and telomerase</li>
<li><strong>Therapeutic targeting</strong>: Mithramycin A and related aureolic acid antibiotics inhibit SP1 binding to GC-rich DNA and are under investigation as anticancer agents</li>
</ul>
