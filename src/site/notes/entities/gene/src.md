<h1>SRC</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>SRC (SRC Proto-Oncogene, Non-Receptor Tyrosine Kinase) encodes the proto-oncogene tyrosine-protein kinase c-Src, a 60 kDa non-receptor tyrosine kinase that was the first retroviral oncogene discovered. The viral form v-Src was identified in Rous sarcoma virus by Peyton Rous, and the subsequent discovery of its cellular counterpart c-Src by Harold Varmus and J. Michael Bishop earned them the 1989 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine. SRC is the founding member of the Src family kinases (SFKs), which includes Fyn, Yes, Lck, Lyn, Hck, Fgr, and Blk.</p>
<p>c-Src contains an SH4 myristoylation domain, a unique region, SH3 and SH2 protein interaction domains, a catalytic tyrosine kinase domain (SH1), and a C-terminal regulatory tail. In its inactive state, intramolecular interactions between phospho-Y527 (in the C-terminal tail) and the SH2 domain, and between the SH3 domain and a polyproline linker, maintain c-Src in a closed, autoinhibited conformation. Dephosphorylation of Y527 or engagement of SH2/SH3 domains by competing ligands activates the kinase. Activated c-Src phosphorylates numerous substrates to regulate cell proliferation, survival, motility, and adhesion.</p>
<h2>Function</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Cell proliferation signaling</strong>: Phosphorylates and activates EGFR, HER2, PDGFR, and other receptor tyrosine kinases, amplifying mitogenic signals through Ras-MAPK and PI3K-Akt pathways</li>
<li><strong>Cell adhesion and migration</strong>: Phosphorylates focal adhesion kinase (FAK), paxillin, and p130Cas at focal adhesions, regulating cell-matrix interactions and cell motility</li>
<li><strong>Cytoskeletal remodeling</strong>: Activates WASP/WAVE family proteins and cortactin to drive actin polymerization, membrane ruffling, and invadopodia formation</li>
<li><strong>Survival signaling</strong>: Activates STAT3 and Akt to promote anti-apoptotic gene expression</li>
<li><strong>Receptor trafficking</strong>: Regulates endocytosis and recycling of growth factor receptors and integrins</li>
<li><strong>Bone remodeling</strong>: Essential for osteoclast function; SRC-knockout mice develop osteopetrosis due to defective bone resorption</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Cancer</strong>: c-Src is activated (rarely mutated) in colorectal, breast, pancreatic, lung, and head/neck cancers; drives invasion, metastasis, angiogenesis, and drug resistance</li>
<li><strong>Therapeutic targeting</strong>: SRC inhibitors dasatinib (also targets BCR-ABL) and bosutinib are approved for CML; SRC inhibitors are in clinical trials for solid tumors, often in combination regimens</li>
<li><strong>Osteoporosis</strong>: SRC kinase activity is essential for osteoclast-mediated bone resorption; SRC inhibition reduces bone loss in preclinical models</li>
<li><strong>Drug resistance</strong>: SRC activation mediates resistance to EGFR inhibitors, HER2-targeted therapies, and anti-hormonal therapy in cancer</li>
<li><strong>Cardiovascular disease</strong>: SRC kinase regulates vascular permeability and endothelial barrier function; contributes to atherosclerotic plaque instability</li>
</ul>
