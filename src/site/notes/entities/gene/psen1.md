<h1>PSEN1</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>The <strong>PSEN1</strong> gene encodes <strong>Presenilin-1</strong>, a multi-pass transmembrane protein that functions as the catalytic core of the <strong>Gamma-Secretase</strong> complex. This protease complex is responsible for the intramembrane cleavage of several proteins, most notably the <strong>Amyloid Precursor Protein (APP)</strong> and <strong>Notch</strong> receptors.</p>
<p>Mutations in PSEN1 are the most common cause of <strong>Familial Alzheimer's Disease (FAD)</strong>, a rare, early-onset form of the disease that is fully penetrant (if you have the mutation, you will develop the disease).</p>
<h2>Biological Functions</h2>
<h3>1. Gamma-Secretase Activity</h3>
<p>Presenilin-1 provides the active site (two aspartate residues) for gamma-secretase. This enzyme cleaves APP to generate Amyloid-Beta (Aβ) peptides.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Normal Function:</strong> Produces a mix of Aβ40 (shorter, less toxic) and Aβ42 (longer, aggregation-prone).</li>
</ul>
<h3>2. Notch Signaling</h3>
<p>Cleaves Notch receptors to release the Notch Intracellular Domain (NICD), which translocates to the nucleus to regulate developmental genes. Crucial for neurogenesis and cell differentiation.</p>
<h3>3. Calcium Homeostasis</h3>
<p>Regulates calcium leak from the endoplasmic reticulum (ER). Mutations can disrupt this, leading to calcium overload and neuronal toxicity.</p>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Familial Alzheimer's Disease (FAD):</strong> Over 200 different mutations in PSEN1 have been identified.
<ul>
<li><strong>Mechanism:</strong> Most mutations shift the cleavage site, increasing the ratio of <strong>Aβ42 to Aβ40</strong>. Aβ42 aggregates much more rapidly into toxic oligomers and plaques.</li>
<li><strong>Onset:</strong> Typically very early, often between ages 30 and 50.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>Acne Inversa:</strong> Rare mutations are linked to this chronic skin condition.</li>
</ul>
