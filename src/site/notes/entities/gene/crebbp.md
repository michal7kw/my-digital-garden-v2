<h1>CREBBP</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>CREBBP (CREB-binding protein, also known as CBP) encodes a large, multi-domain nuclear protein that functions as a histone acetyltransferase (HAT) and transcriptional coactivator. At 265 kDa, CBP is one of the largest transcriptional regulators in the human genome and serves as a critical integrator of numerous signaling pathways converging on gene expression.</p>
<p>CBP acts as a scaffold protein that bridges DNA-binding transcription factors to the basal transcriptional machinery. It interacts with over 400 proteins, including CREB, p53, NF-kB, nuclear hormone receptors, and STAT family members, making it a master regulator of transcriptional programs governing cell growth, differentiation, and DNA repair.</p>
<h2>Function</h2>
<p>CBP possesses intrinsic histone acetyltransferase activity, catalyzing the acetylation of histones H3 and H4 to promote an open chromatin state permissive for transcription. Beyond histones, CBP acetylates non-histone substrates including p53 (activating its tumor suppressor function), STAT1, and FOXO transcription factors. CBP is closely related to p300 (encoded by EP300), and the two proteins share overlapping but non-redundant functions. CBP is essential for embryonic development, hematopoiesis, and long-term memory formation through CREB-dependent transcription.</p>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<p>Heterozygous loss-of-function mutations in CREBBP cause Rubinstein-Taybi Syndrome (RTS), characterized by intellectual disability, broad thumbs and toes, distinctive facial features, and increased cancer susceptibility. Somatic mutations in CREBBP are recurrent in multiple cancers, particularly B-cell lymphomas (diffuse large B-cell lymphoma and follicular lymphoma), where loss of CBP HAT activity disrupts normal differentiation and tumor suppression. CBP is also implicated in acute myeloid leukemia via chromosomal translocations creating CBP fusion proteins (e.g., MOZ-CBP). Small-molecule inhibitors targeting the CBP bromodomain are under investigation as cancer therapeutics.</p>
