<h1>NR3C1</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>NR3C1 (Nuclear Receptor Subfamily 3 Group C Member 1) encodes the glucocorticoid receptor (GR), a ligand-activated transcription factor belonging to the nuclear hormone receptor superfamily. The GR is ubiquitously expressed and mediates the diverse physiological effects of cortisol (and other glucocorticoids) on metabolism, immune function, cognition, and stress response.</p>
<p>In its inactive state, GR resides in the cytoplasm bound to a chaperone complex including HSP90, HSP70, and immunophilins. Upon cortisol binding, GR undergoes conformational change, dissociates from chaperones, dimerizes, and translocates to the nucleus where it regulates transcription of target genes.</p>
<h2>Function</h2>
<p>GR regulates gene expression through multiple mechanisms. As a homodimer, it binds glucocorticoid response elements (GREs) in DNA to activate anti-inflammatory genes such as GILZ and MKP-1 (transactivation). As a monomer, it physically interacts with and inhibits pro-inflammatory transcription factors NF-kB and AP-1 (transrepression), which is the primary mechanism of anti-inflammatory glucocorticoid action. GR also influences gene expression through chromatin remodeling and interaction with coactivators (including <a href="/entities/gene/crebbp/" class="internal-link">CREBBP</a>/CBP) and corepressors. In the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis, GR mediates negative feedback by suppressing CRH in the hypothalamus and ACTH in the pituitary, maintaining cortisol homeostasis. In the hippocampus, GR signaling is essential for memory consolidation, emotional regulation, and stress adaptation.</p>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<p>NR3C1 polymorphisms and epigenetic modifications are associated with altered stress reactivity, psychiatric disorders, and metabolic disease. The BclI and N363S polymorphisms increase glucocorticoid sensitivity and are linked to visceral obesity, insulin resistance, and cardiovascular risk. Epigenetic silencing of NR3C1 through promoter methylation (particularly exon 1F) is observed in individuals exposed to early-life adversity and is associated with increased HPA axis reactivity and susceptibility to depression and PTSD. GR resistance (Chrousos syndrome), caused by inactivating NR3C1 mutations, presents with compensatory HPA axis activation, hypertension, hypokalemia, and androgen excess. Synthetic glucocorticoids (dexamethasone, prednisolone) exert their therapeutic effects through GR, making NR3C1 central to understanding glucocorticoid pharmacology and resistance.</p>
