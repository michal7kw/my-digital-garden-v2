<h1>BPA Exposure</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Bisphenol A (BPA) is a synthetic chemical used in the production of polycarbonate plastics (water bottles, food containers, dental sealants) and epoxy resins (can linings, thermal receipt paper). It is one of the highest-volume chemicals produced globally (&gt;8 million tonnes/year), and biomonitoring studies consistently detect BPA in &gt;90% of population samples, primarily through dietary ingestion (leaching from food packaging), dermal absorption (thermal paper), and inhalation. The widespread human exposure to BPA at levels sufficient to exert biological effects has made it one of the most extensively studied environmental chemicals, generating over 10,000 peer-reviewed publications.</p>
<p>BPA is classified as an endocrine-disrupting chemical (EDC) primarily due to its ability to bind and activate <a href="/entities/hormone/estrogen/" class="internal-link">Estrogen</a> receptors (ERalpha and ERbeta), though with approximately 1,000-10,000 times lower affinity than estradiol. However, several factors amplify its biological impact beyond what binding affinity alone would predict: BPA also activates membrane estrogen receptors (GPER/GPR30), interacts with androgen receptors (as an antagonist), thyroid hormone receptors, and peroxisome proliferator-activated receptors (PPARgamma). At environmentally relevant doses (nanomolar range), BPA activates rapid non-genomic signaling pathways that are not captured by traditional high-dose toxicology studies, supporting the &quot;low-dose hypothesis&quot; that has been central to the regulatory controversy surrounding BPA.</p>
<p>The metabolic effects of BPA are among the most clinically significant. BPA promotes adipogenesis through PPARgamma activation, disrupts <a href="/entities/labtest/insulin/" class="internal-link">Insulin</a> signaling in pancreatic beta cells and hepatocytes, impairs glucose-stimulated insulin secretion, and promotes visceral fat accumulation. Epidemiological studies have consistently associated higher urinary BPA levels with increased prevalence of <a class="internal-link is-unresolved" href="/404">Obesity</a>, type 2 diabetes, and metabolic syndrome. In animal studies, perinatal BPA exposure programs offspring for increased adiposity, insulin resistance, and metabolic dysfunction in adulthood -- an &quot;obesogenic&quot; effect that may involve epigenetic reprogramming (altered DNA methylation at metabolic gene promoters). BPA exposure has also been associated with <a href="/entities/condition/pcos/" class="internal-link">PCOS</a> (polycystic ovary syndrome), with BPA levels significantly higher in PCOS patients compared to controls, likely reflecting both estrogenic effects on ovarian function and insulin-resistant metabolic disruption.</p>
<p>Regulatory responses to BPA have been heterogeneous. France banned BPA in all food contact materials in 2015, and the EFSA dramatically lowered its tolerable daily intake to 0.2 ng/kg bw/day in 2023 -- 20,000 times lower than the previous limit -- based on immune system effects. The FDA banned BPA from infant bottles and sippy cups in 2012 but maintains that current exposure levels are safe for adults. Importantly, many BPA alternatives (BPS, BPF, BPAF) show similar or greater endocrine-disrupting activity, raising concerns about &quot;regrettable substitution.&quot; Reducing exposure involves avoiding polycarbonate plastics (recycling code #7), minimizing canned food consumption, not heating food in plastic containers, and reducing handling of thermal receipt paper.</p>
<h2>Key Mechanisms</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Estrogen receptor activation</strong>: Binds ERalpha, ERbeta, and membrane GPER at low nanomolar concentrations</li>
<li><strong>Androgen receptor antagonism</strong>: Blocks androgen signaling, disrupting male reproductive development</li>
<li><strong>PPARgamma activation</strong>: Promotes adipocyte differentiation and lipid accumulation (obesogenic)</li>
<li><strong>Insulin signaling disruption</strong>: Impairs beta cell function, hepatic insulin sensitivity, and GLUT4 translocation</li>
<li><strong>Epigenetic reprogramming</strong>: Alters DNA methylation at imprinted genes, metabolic gene promoters (Agouti mouse model)</li>
<li><strong>Thyroid hormone interference</strong>: Competitive binding at thyroid receptor; disrupts TH-dependent brain development</li>
</ul>
