<h1>Heavy Metal Exposure</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Heavy metal exposure refers to the accumulation of toxic metallic elements in the body at levels sufficient to cause adverse health effects. The metals of greatest public health concern are lead (Pb), mercury (Hg), cadmium (Cd), and arsenic (As -- technically a metalloid), each with distinct sources, toxicokinetics, and target organ effects. Unlike organic toxins, heavy metals cannot be metabolically degraded and must be either sequestered (in bone, metallothionein-bound complexes) or actively excreted. Their biological half-lives range from weeks (inorganic arsenic) to decades (lead in bone, cadmium in kidneys), making chronic low-level exposure particularly insidious.</p>
<p>The primary toxicological mechanism shared across heavy metals is disruption of thiol-dependent biochemistry. Lead, mercury, cadmium, and arsenic all have high affinity for sulfhydryl (-SH) groups on proteins, displacing essential metals (zinc, iron, copper) from enzyme active sites and structural motifs. This results in widespread enzyme inhibition, protein dysfunction, and depletion of the body's primary antioxidant defense system, <a href="/entities/supplement/glutathione/" class="internal-link">Glutathione</a> (GSH). Glutathione is the most abundant intracellular thiol and plays a central role in heavy metal detoxification through direct conjugation (catalyzed by glutathione S-transferases), facilitating biliary and renal excretion. <a href="/entities/supplement/nac/" class="internal-link">NAC</a> (N-acetylcysteine) supports detoxification by serving as a cysteine donor for glutathione synthesis and directly chelating certain metals. <a href="/entities/supplement/selenium/" class="internal-link">Selenium</a> provides additional protection by forming insoluble mercury-selenium complexes (reducing mercury bioavailability) and supporting selenoprotein antioxidant enzymes (glutathione peroxidases, thioredoxin reductases).</p>
<p>Lead exposure remains widespread despite regulatory progress, with sources including aging paint (pre-1978 housing), contaminated water (lead service lines), occupational exposure (battery manufacturing, smelting), and imported spices/cosmetics. Lead inhibits delta-aminolevulinic acid dehydratase and ferrochelatase in heme biosynthesis (causing anemia), disrupts calcium-dependent neurotransmission (causing neurodevelopmental toxicity in children), and accumulates in bone (half-life 20-30 years). Mercury exists in inorganic (industrial exposure), organic methylmercury (seafood bioaccumulation), and elemental (dental amalgams, artisanal gold mining) forms, each with distinct toxicity profiles. Methylmercury crosses the blood-brain barrier and placenta, causing neurological damage. Cadmium accumulates in the kidneys (half-life 10-30 years) bound to metallothionein, eventually causing <a href="/entities/condition/chronic-kidney-disease/" class="internal-link">Chronic Kidney Disease</a> through proximal tubular damage.</p>
<p>Arsenic exposure through contaminated groundwater affects over 200 million people globally, particularly in Bangladesh, West Bengal, and parts of South America. Chronic arsenic exposure causes a distinctive pattern of skin lesions (keratoses, hyperpigmentation), peripheral neuropathy, and dramatically increased risk of skin, lung, bladder, and liver cancers. Arsenic disrupts cellular signaling, epigenetic regulation (DNA methylation), and DNA repair mechanisms.</p>
<h2>Key Mechanisms</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Sulfhydryl group binding</strong>: Heavy metals bind to -SH groups on enzymes, transporters, and structural proteins</li>
<li><strong>Glutathione depletion</strong>: Direct conjugation with GSH and inhibition of glutathione reductase</li>
<li><strong>Oxidative stress</strong>: Fenton-type reactions (iron/copper displacement), mitochondrial dysfunction, and antioxidant depletion</li>
<li><strong>Enzyme inhibition</strong>: Displacement of essential metals from active sites (e.g., lead displaces zinc from delta-ALAD)</li>
<li><strong>Epigenetic disruption</strong>: Arsenic and cadmium alter DNA methylation, histone modification, and miRNA expression</li>
<li><strong>Ion channel interference</strong>: Lead mimics calcium; cadmium mimics zinc in cellular transport and signaling</li>
</ul>
