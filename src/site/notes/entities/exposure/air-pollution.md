<h1>Air Pollution</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Air pollution is the single largest environmental risk factor for disease and premature death worldwide, responsible for an estimated 6.7 million deaths annually (WHO, 2023). The pollutants of greatest health concern include particulate matter (PM2.5 -- particles with aerodynamic diameter less than 2.5 micrometers, and PM10), ground-level ozone (O3), nitrogen dioxide (NO2), sulfur dioxide (SO2), and carbon monoxide (CO). Among these, PM2.5 carries the most robust epidemiological evidence for adverse health effects, with well-established dose-response relationships for cardiovascular mortality, respiratory disease, lung cancer, and all-cause mortality at levels well below current regulatory standards.</p>
<p>PM2.5 particles are small enough to penetrate deep into the alveolar region of the lungs, where they trigger local inflammatory responses and, critically, can translocate across the alveolar-capillary barrier into the systemic circulation. Once in the bloodstream, ultrafine particles and their surface-adsorbed chemicals (polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons, transition metals, endotoxin) activate circulating monocytes, induce endothelial dysfunction, promote atherosclerotic plaque progression, and trigger pro-thrombotic states. This systemic <a href="/entities/condition/inflammation/" class="internal-link">Inflammation</a> pathway explains why <a href="/entities/condition/cardiovascular-disease/" class="internal-link">Cardiovascular Disease</a> -- not respiratory disease -- accounts for the majority of air pollution-attributable mortality. Long-term PM2.5 exposure is associated with accelerated atherosclerosis, increased coronary artery calcium scores, higher carotid intima-media thickness, and elevated risk of myocardial infarction, stroke, heart failure, and atrial fibrillation.</p>
<p>The mechanistic link between air pollution and systemic disease centers on oxidative stress and NF-kB-mediated inflammatory signaling. PM2.5-associated transition metals (iron, copper, vanadium) catalyze Fenton reactions generating hydroxyl radicals, while organic components activate aryl hydrocarbon receptor (AhR) signaling and induce CYP1A1/1B1 expression. These pathways converge on NF-kB activation, driving expression of pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-6, TNF-alpha), adhesion molecules, and matrix metalloproteinases. Emerging evidence also links PM2.5 exposure to neuroinflammation, accelerated cognitive decline, and increased risk of Alzheimer's and Parkinson's diseases, likely through olfactory nerve translocation and blood-brain barrier disruption.</p>
<p>Several nutritional interventions have shown protective effects against air pollution-induced damage. <a href="/entities/supplement/nac/" class="internal-link">NAC</a> (N-acetylcysteine) replenishes glutathione stores depleted by oxidative stress and has demonstrated protective effects against PM2.5-induced lung inflammation in both animal and human studies. <a href="/entities/supplement/omega-3-fatty-acids/" class="internal-link">Omega-3 Fatty Acids</a> (EPA and DHA) attenuate the cardiovascular inflammatory response to PM2.5 by competing with arachidonic acid metabolism and generating anti-inflammatory resolvins. B vitamins (folate, B6, B12) have shown promise in mitigating PM2.5-induced epigenetic changes (DNA methylation alterations) and cardiovascular effects in controlled exposure studies. Sulforaphane (from broccoli sprouts) enhances Phase II detoxification enzymes (NQO1, GSTs) and has demonstrated enhanced excretion of airborne pollutant metabolites in Chinese clinical trials.</p>
<h2>Key Mechanisms</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Oxidative stress</strong>: Transition metals on PM2.5 catalyze ROS generation; organic components activate AhR signaling</li>
<li><strong>Systemic inflammation</strong>: NF-kB activation drives circulating IL-6, TNF-alpha, CRP elevation</li>
<li><strong>Endothelial dysfunction</strong>: Reduced NO bioavailability, increased adhesion molecule expression</li>
<li><strong>Autonomic imbalance</strong>: PM2.5 activates pulmonary C-fiber reflexes causing sympathetic activation</li>
<li><strong>Epigenetic modification</strong>: PM2.5 alters DNA methylation at CpG sites in inflammatory and coagulation genes</li>
<li><strong>Alveolar translocation</strong>: Ultrafine particles cross the lung-blood barrier into systemic circulation</li>
</ul>
