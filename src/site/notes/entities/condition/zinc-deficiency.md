<h1>Zinc Deficiency</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>A nutritional deficiency state characterized by insufficient levels of zinc, an essential trace element involved in immune function, protein synthesis, and wound healing.</p>
<h2>Symptoms</h2>
<ul>
<li>Loss of appetite</li>
<li>Impaired immune function (frequent infections)</li>
<li>Hair loss (alopecia)</li>
<li>Diarrhea</li>
<li>Delayed wound healing</li>
<li>Impaired taste (hypogeusia) or smell (hyposmia)</li>
<li>Skin lesions (acrodermatitis, eczema-like rashes)</li>
<li>Night blindness</li>
<li>Growth retardation (in children)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Treatment Approaches</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Zinc supplementation</strong>: Zinc picolinate or zinc bisglycinate (best bioavailability), zinc acetate, zinc gluconate. Dose: 15-30 mg elemental zinc/day for mild deficiency, up to 50 mg/day for moderate-severe (short-term). Take away from iron and calcium supplements (competitive absorption)</li>
<li><strong>Dietary sources</strong>: Oysters (highest source), red meat, poultry, crab, beans, nuts, pumpkin seeds, whole grains. Note: phytates in grains/legumes reduce absorption — soaking, sprouting, and fermentation improve bioavailability</li>
<li><strong>Cofactor support</strong>: Vitamin C (enhances zinc absorption), vitamin A (zinc and vitamin A are synergistic for immune function), copper monitoring (long-term zinc &gt;40 mg/day can deplete copper — supplement 1-2 mg copper if needed)</li>
<li><strong>Address underlying causes</strong>: Treat malabsorption conditions (celiac disease, Crohn's, short bowel syndrome), reduce alcohol intake, review medications, ensure adequate protein intake (zinc absorption requires amino acids)</li>
<li><strong>Probiotics</strong>: Support gut health and mineral absorption</li>
<li><strong>Monitoring</strong>: Serum zinc (fasting morning sample), alkaline phosphatase (zinc-dependent enzyme — low ALP can indicate deficiency), clinical response to supplementation</li>
</ul>
