<h1>Bulimia Nervosa</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Bulimia Nervosa (BN) is a serious eating disorder characterized by recurrent episodes of binge eating (consuming objectively large amounts of food with a sense of loss of control) followed by inappropriate compensatory behaviors to prevent weight gain. It affects 1-3% of young women and 0.1-0.5% of young men, with typical onset in late adolescence.</p>
<p>Serotonergic dysfunction is central to bulimia's neurobiology — reduced 5-HT activity in hypothalamic appetite centers contributes to binge eating, while serotonergic deficits in prefrontal regions impair impulse control. Dietary restriction (common in bulimia) depletes tryptophan, further reducing serotonin synthesis and perpetuating the binge-purge cycle.</p>
<h2>Symptoms</h2>
<h3>Binge Eating Episodes</h3>
<ul>
<li>Eating an objectively large amount of food in a discrete period (e.g., 2 hours)</li>
<li>Sense of loss of control during the episode</li>
<li>At least once weekly for 3 months (DSM-5 criteria)</li>
</ul>
<h3>Compensatory Behaviors</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Purging type</strong> (~80%): Self-induced vomiting, laxative or diuretic misuse</li>
<li><strong>Non-purging type</strong> (~20%): Fasting, excessive exercise</li>
</ul>
<h3>Associated Features</h3>
<ul>
<li>Self-evaluation unduly influenced by body weight/shape</li>
<li>Often normal weight or slightly overweight (unlike anorexia nervosa)</li>
<li>Russell's sign (calluses on knuckles from self-induced vomiting)</li>
<li>Dental erosion, parotid gland swelling, metabolic alkalosis</li>
<li>High comorbidity: Depression (50-70%), anxiety, substance use</li>
</ul>
<h2>Pathophysiology</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Serotonin dysfunction</strong>: Reduced CSF 5-HIAA (serotonin metabolite), decreased 5-HT2A binding in medial orbital frontal cortex</li>
<li><strong>Tryptophan depletion cycle</strong>: Dietary restriction → low tryptophan → reduced serotonin → impaired satiety → binge eating</li>
<li><strong>Dopamine</strong>: Altered reward processing in ventral striatum</li>
<li><strong>HPA axis</strong>: Cortisol dysregulation from stress and nutritional instability</li>
<li><strong>Genetics</strong>: ~55-62% heritability; SLC6A4, HTR2A, BDNF Val66Met implicated</li>
</ul>
<h2>Treatment</h2>
<h3>Pharmacotherapy</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Fluoxetine 60mg</strong>: Only FDA-approved medication for bulimia nervosa. Higher dose needed (60mg vs 20mg for depression). Reduces binge/purge frequency by 50-67%</li>
<li><strong>Other SSRIs</strong>: Sertraline, citalopram used off-label with some evidence</li>
<li><strong>Topiramate</strong>: May reduce binge frequency but not first-line</li>
<li><strong>Note</strong>: TCAs and MAOIs have evidence but safety concerns limit use</li>
</ul>
<h3>Psychotherapy (First-Line)</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>CBT-E</strong> (Enhanced Cognitive Behavioral Therapy): Gold standard; targets cognitive distortions about eating, shape, and weight</li>
<li><strong>IPT</strong> (Interpersonal Therapy): Alternative when CBT unavailable</li>
</ul>
<h3>Combination</h3>
<ul>
<li>Fluoxetine + CBT-E superior to either alone</li>
</ul>
