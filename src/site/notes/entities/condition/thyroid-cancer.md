<h1>Thyroid Cancer</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>A type of cancer that starts in the thyroid gland, with papillary thyroid carcinoma being the most common and generally treatable form.</p>
<h2>Symptoms</h2>
<ul>
<li>Painless lump or nodule in the neck (most common presenting sign)</li>
<li>Difficulty swallowing (dysphagia) or breathing</li>
<li>Vocal hoarseness or voice changes (recurrent laryngeal nerve involvement)</li>
<li>Swollen cervical lymph nodes</li>
<li>Neck or throat pain radiating to the ears</li>
<li>Many cases discovered incidentally on imaging</li>
</ul>
<h2>Treatment Approaches</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Surgery (primary)</strong>: Total thyroidectomy for tumors &gt; 4 cm or with extrathyroidal extension; lobectomy for small (&lt; 1 cm) low-risk papillary carcinomas</li>
<li><strong>Radioactive iodine (RAI)</strong>: I-131 ablation post-thyroidectomy for intermediate/high-risk differentiated thyroid cancer -- eliminates residual tissue</li>
<li><strong>TSH suppression</strong>: Levothyroxine at doses to suppress TSH (0.1-0.5 mIU/L) -- reduces recurrence risk in differentiated thyroid cancer</li>
<li><strong>External beam radiation</strong>: For anaplastic thyroid cancer or unresectable disease</li>
<li><strong>Targeted therapy</strong>: Lenvatinib, sorafenib for radioiodine-refractory differentiated thyroid cancer; vandetanib, cabozantinib for medullary thyroid cancer</li>
<li><strong>Active surveillance</strong>: Increasingly accepted for papillary microcarcinomas (&lt; 1 cm) without aggressive features</li>
<li><strong>Supplements</strong>: Selenium (supports thyroid function, may reduce anti-TPO antibodies), vitamin D, zinc<br />
