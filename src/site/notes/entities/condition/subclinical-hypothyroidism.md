<h1>Subclinical Hypothyroidism</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>A mild form of hypothyroidism characterized by elevated TSH (4.5-10 mIU/L) with normal free T4. Prevalence 4-10% in adults, higher in women and elderly. Progresses to overt hypothyroidism at 2-5% per year, especially with positive TPO antibodies. Treatment is debated — guidelines recommend treatment when TSH &gt;10, or with symptoms/TPO antibodies/pregnancy at TSH 4.5-10.</p>
<h2>Symptoms</h2>
<ul>
<li>Often asymptomatic (detected on routine labs)</li>
<li>Mild fatigue, sluggishness</li>
<li>Depressed mood, brain fog</li>
<li>Dry skin, hair changes</li>
<li>Weight gain (modest)</li>
<li>Cold intolerance, constipation</li>
</ul>
<h2>Treatment Approaches</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Watchful waiting</strong>: Monitor TSH every 6-12 months if TSH 4.5-10 and asymptomatic</li>
<li><strong>Levothyroxine</strong>: Start if TSH &gt;10, or TSH 4.5-10 with symptoms, positive TPO antibodies, or pregnancy. Start low (25-50 mcg), titrate to TSH 1.0-2.5</li>
<li><strong>Optimize thyroid cofactors</strong>: Selenium (200 mcg — reduces TPO antibodies in Hashimoto's), zinc, iodine (if deficient, not excessive), iron (required for TPO enzyme)</li>
<li><strong>Avoid thyroid disruptors</strong>: Gluten (if Hashimoto's — molecular mimicry), excessive cruciferous raw intake, soy isoflavones</li>
<li><strong>Supplements</strong>: Selenium (strongest evidence), zinc, vitamin D, iron (if deficient), ashwagandha (may support T4→T3 conversion)</li>
</ul>
