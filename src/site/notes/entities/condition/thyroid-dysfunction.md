<h1>Thyroid Dysfunction</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Thyroid dysfunction encompasses a spectrum of disorders involving abnormal thyroid hormone production, ranging from hypothyroidism (underactive) to hyperthyroidism (overactive). The thyroid gland regulates basal metabolic rate, thermogenesis, cardiac function, bone metabolism, and neurological development. Thyroid disorders affect approximately 12% of the population, with subclinical forms affecting an additional 5-10%.</p>
<h2>Symptom Cluster</h2>
<p>Thyroid dysfunction presents with opposing symptom clusters depending on whether the gland is underactive or overactive:</p>
<p><strong>Hypothyroidism (underactive) -- the more common presentation:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Fatigue, lethargy, and excessive sleepiness (the most common presenting symptom)</li>
<li>Weight gain or difficulty losing weight despite unchanged diet</li>
<li>Cold intolerance (reduced thermogenesis from decreased basal metabolic rate)</li>
<li>Constipation (reduced GI motility)</li>
<li>Dry skin, brittle nails, and hair thinning or loss (especially the lateral third of eyebrows -- a classic sign)</li>
<li>Bradycardia (heart rate &lt;60 bpm) and diastolic hypertension</li>
<li>Myxedema: non-pitting edema of face (periorbital puffiness), hands, and feet</li>
<li>Cognitive slowing: poor concentration, memory impairment, slow speech</li>
<li>Depression, apathy, and reduced motivation</li>
<li>Menstrual irregularities: menorrhagia (heavy periods), oligomenorrhea</li>
<li>Hoarse voice (from myxedematous infiltration of vocal cords)</li>
<li>Delayed deep tendon reflexes (prolonged relaxation phase)</li>
<li>Elevated cholesterol (LDL-C and total cholesterol) due to reduced LDL receptor expression</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Hyperthyroidism (overactive):</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Weight loss despite increased appetite (hypermetabolism)</li>
<li>Heat intolerance, excessive sweating</li>
<li>Tremor (fine, rapid tremor of hands)</li>
<li>Tachycardia, palpitations, atrial fibrillation (in 10-15% of hyperthyroid patients)</li>
<li>Anxiety, irritability, emotional lability, insomnia</li>
<li>Increased bowel frequency (not true diarrhea)</li>
<li>Proximal muscle weakness (thyrotoxic myopathy)</li>
<li>Menstrual irregularities: oligomenorrhea, amenorrhea</li>
<li>Lid lag and stare (sympathetic overactivation); Graves-specific: proptosis (exophthalmos), conjunctival injection</li>
<li>Pretibial myxedema and thyroid acropachy (Graves-specific)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Diagnostic Criteria</h2>
<p>Diagnosis follows a stepwise laboratory approach:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>TSH (thyroid-stimulating hormone)</strong>: The single most important screening test; the pituitary's sensitive feedback signal for thyroid status. Normal range: 0.4-4.0 mIU/L (some experts advocate 0.4-2.5 mIU/L as optimal). Elevated TSH indicates hypothyroidism; suppressed TSH indicates hyperthyroidism.</li>
<li><strong>Free T4 (thyroxine)</strong>: Normal range approximately 0.8-1.8 ng/dL. Low free T4 with elevated TSH = primary hypothyroidism. High free T4 with suppressed TSH = primary hyperthyroidism. Normal free T4 with abnormal TSH = subclinical disease.</li>
<li><strong>Free T3 (triiodothyronine)</strong>: The biologically active hormone (3-5x more potent than T4). Normal range approximately 2.3-4.2 pg/mL. Important in hyperthyroidism (may be elevated when free T4 is normal -- &quot;T3 thyrotoxicosis&quot;). In hypothyroidism, T3 is often preserved until late disease due to peripheral deiodinase upregulation.</li>
<li><strong>Diagnostic patterns</strong>:
<ul>
<li>Overt hypothyroidism: TSH elevated (&gt;10 mIU/L), free T4 low</li>
<li>Subclinical hypothyroidism: TSH mildly elevated (4.0-10 mIU/L), free T4 normal</li>
<li>Overt hyperthyroidism: TSH suppressed (&lt;0.1 mIU/L), free T4 and/or free T3 elevated</li>
<li>Subclinical hyperthyroidism: TSH suppressed (&lt;0.4 mIU/L), free T4 and free T3 normal</li>
<li>Central hypothyroidism (rare): TSH low/normal, free T4 low (pituitary or hypothalamic failure)</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>Antibody testing (for etiology)</strong>: TPO antibodies (Hashimoto thyroiditis -- most common cause of hypothyroidism), TSH receptor antibodies / TRAb (Graves disease -- most common cause of hyperthyroidism), thyroglobulin antibodies</li>
<li><strong>Imaging</strong>: Thyroid ultrasound (nodules, goiter, echogenicity pattern); radioactive iodine uptake (RAIU) and scan to differentiate causes of hyperthyroidism (diffuse uptake = Graves; focal = toxic nodule; low uptake = thyroiditis)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Differential Diagnosis</h2>
<p>Thyroid dysfunction symptoms are non-specific and overlap with many conditions:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Depression/anxiety</strong>: Hypothyroidism mimics major depressive disorder; hyperthyroidism mimics generalized anxiety disorder and panic disorder; TSH should be checked in all new psychiatric presentations</li>
<li><strong>Adrenal insufficiency</strong>: Fatigue, weight changes, and cold intolerance overlap with hypothyroidism; distinguished by hypotension (vs. hypertension in hypothyroidism), hyperpigmentation, salt craving, and low morning cortisol</li>
<li><strong>Iron deficiency anemia</strong>: Fatigue, cold intolerance, hair loss, and cognitive impairment overlap significantly; check ferritin and CBC alongside thyroid panel</li>
<li><strong>Perimenopause/menopause</strong>: Hot flashes, mood changes, weight gain, and menstrual irregularities overlap with both hypo- and hyperthyroidism; often coexist (thyroid disease peaks in perimenopausal women)</li>
<li><strong>Chronic fatigue syndrome/fibromyalgia</strong>: Fatigue and cognitive complaints overlap with hypothyroidism; normal thyroid function tests distinguish these</li>
<li><strong>Diabetes mellitus</strong>: Weight changes, fatigue, and frequent urination overlap; type 1 diabetes and autoimmune thyroid disease frequently coexist (autoimmune polyglandular syndrome)</li>
<li><strong>Cardiac arrhythmia</strong>: Atrial fibrillation from hyperthyroidism must be distinguished from primary cardiac etiologies; TSH should be checked in all new-onset AF</li>
<li><strong>Pheochromocytoma</strong>: Palpitations, hypertension, sweating, and anxiety mimic hyperthyroidism; distinguished by episodic pattern, elevated plasma/urine metanephrines</li>
<li><strong>Non-thyroidal illness (&quot;sick euthyroid&quot; / euthyroid sick syndrome)</strong>: Acute illness suppresses TSH and T3 without true thyroid disease; common in hospitalized patients; repeat testing 6-8 weeks after recovery</li>
</ul>
<h2>Management Approach</h2>
<p>Management depends on whether the dysfunction is hypothyroid or hyperthyroid, and whether it is overt or subclinical:</p>
<p><strong>Hypothyroidism management:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Levothyroxine (T4)</strong>: First-line treatment for overt hypothyroidism; starting dose 1.6 mcg/kg/day for healthy adults (lower starting dose 25-50 mcg/day in elderly or cardiac patients, titrating slowly); take on empty stomach 30-60 minutes before breakfast (or at bedtime, consistently); separate from calcium, iron, PPI, and fiber supplements by 4+ hours</li>
<li><strong>TSH monitoring</strong>: Recheck TSH 6-8 weeks after dose initiation or any dose change; target TSH 0.5-2.5 mIU/L for most adults (slightly higher target 4-6 mIU/L acceptable in elderly &gt;75 years)</li>
<li><strong>Subclinical hypothyroidism</strong>: Treatment recommended if TSH &gt;10 mIU/L; consider treatment if TSH 4.0-10 with symptoms, positive TPO antibodies, dyslipidemia, or fertility goals; observe and retest in 3-6 months if TSH 4.0-7.0 without symptoms</li>
<li><strong>T3 combination therapy</strong>: Controversial; some patients with persistent symptoms on T4 monotherapy may benefit from adding liothyronine (T3) at a ratio of T4:T3 ~13:1; not routinely recommended by major guidelines but recognized in clinical practice</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Hyperthyroidism management:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Antithyroid drugs</strong>: Methimazole (preferred, 10-30 mg/day) or propylthiouracil (PTU, reserved for first trimester pregnancy and thyroid storm); typical treatment course 12-18 months for Graves disease; 30-50% remission rate</li>
<li><strong>Radioactive iodine (RAI)</strong>: Definitive treatment for Graves disease; results in hypothyroidism requiring lifelong levothyroxine in 80-90% of cases</li>
<li><strong>Thyroidectomy</strong>: For large goiters, suspected malignancy, moderate-severe Graves ophthalmopathy, or patient preference</li>
<li><strong>Beta-blockers</strong>: Propranolol (20-40 mg q6-8h) for symptomatic relief of tremor, tachycardia, and anxiety while waiting for definitive treatment to take effect</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Supportive measures (all thyroid dysfunction):</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Nutritional support</strong>: Selenium (200 mcg/day -- reduces TPO antibodies in Hashimoto, may reduce Graves ophthalmopathy progression per EUGOGO trial), zinc (15-30 mg/day -- cofactor for T4 to T3 conversion by deiodinase enzymes), iodine (ensure adequate but not excessive intake: 150 mcg/day for adults; excess iodine can worsen autoimmune thyroiditis), iron (required for thyroid peroxidase activity; correct deficiency)</li>
<li><strong>Avoid goitrogens in excess</strong>: Raw cruciferous vegetables (broccoli, cabbage, kale) contain glucosinolates that can inhibit iodine uptake when consumed in very large quantities; cooking largely inactivates these compounds; normal dietary intake is not a concern</li>
<li><strong>Address autoimmune root causes</strong>: Optimize vitamin D (autoimmune thyroiditis more common in vitamin D deficiency), gut health (intestinal permeability associated with autoimmune thyroid disease), stress management (stress triggers autoimmune flares)</li>
<li><strong>Monitoring frequency</strong>: Every 6-8 weeks during dose adjustment, then every 6-12 months once stable; monitor free T4 and free T3 in addition to TSH if symptoms persist despite normal TSH; annual thyroid ultrasound if nodules present</li>
</ul>
