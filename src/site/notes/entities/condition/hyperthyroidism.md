<h1>Hyperthyroidism</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>A condition where the thyroid gland produces and releases excessive amounts of thyroid hormones (T3 and T4), accelerating the body's metabolism.</p>
<h2>Symptoms</h2>
<ul>
<li>Unintentional weight loss despite increased appetite</li>
<li>Rapid or irregular heartbeat (tachycardia, palpitations)</li>
<li>Heat intolerance and excessive sweating</li>
<li>Anxiety, irritability, and nervousness</li>
<li>Tremor (fine hand tremor)</li>
<li>Fatigue and muscle weakness</li>
<li>Frequent bowel movements or diarrhea</li>
<li>Insomnia</li>
<li>Thinning skin and brittle hair</li>
</ul>
<h2>Treatment Approaches</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Antithyroid medications</strong>: Methimazole (first-line) or propylthiouracil (PTU — preferred in first trimester pregnancy). Block thyroid hormone synthesis. Typically 12-18 months for Graves' disease</li>
<li><strong>Beta-blockers</strong>: Propranolol for rapid symptom relief (controls tachycardia, tremor, anxiety) while antithyroid medications take effect</li>
<li><strong>Radioactive iodine (RAI)</strong>: Definitive treatment for Graves' disease — destroys overactive thyroid tissue. Usually results in hypothyroidism requiring lifelong levothyroxine</li>
<li><strong>Surgery</strong>: Thyroidectomy for large goiters, suspected cancer, or failed medical therapy</li>
<li><strong>Supplements</strong>: Selenium (200 mcg/day — reduces Graves' ophthalmopathy severity), L-carnitine (2-4 g/day — counteracts thyroid hormone effects on muscle), vitamin D, omega-3 fatty acids (anti-inflammatory)</li>
<li><strong>Lifestyle</strong>: Stress reduction (stress can trigger/worsen Graves'), adequate calcium and vitamin D (hyperthyroidism accelerates bone loss), moderate exercise (avoid high-intensity during active disease)</li>
</ul>
