<h1>Metabolic Syndrome</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Cluster of metabolic abnormalities including central obesity, insulin resistance, dyslipidemia, and hypertension that significantly increase risk of cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, and all-cause mortality. Affects approximately 35% of U.S. adults.</p>
<h2>Symptom Cluster</h2>
<p>Metabolic syndrome is often called a &quot;silent killer&quot; because it is frequently asymptomatic until organ damage has occurred. The defining clinical features are measurable signs rather than subjective symptoms:</p>
<ul>
<li>Central/abdominal obesity: increased waist circumference (&gt;40 inches/102 cm men, &gt;35 inches/88 cm women per ATP III; lower cutoffs for Asian populations: &gt;35.5 inches/90 cm men, &gt;31.5 inches/80 cm women)</li>
<li>Hypertension: often asymptomatic; may present with headaches, visual changes, or epistaxis at very high levels</li>
<li>Fatigue and low energy (driven by insulin resistance, impaired mitochondrial function, and sleep apnea)</li>
<li>Difficulty losing weight despite caloric restriction (hyperinsulinemia promotes lipogenesis and prevents lipolysis)</li>
<li>Increased thirst and frequent urination (when glucose intolerance progresses toward diabetes)</li>
<li>Acanthosis nigricans (dark, velvety skin patches in body folds -- marker of insulin resistance)</li>
<li>Skin tags (acrochordons)</li>
<li>Often asymptomatic until complications develop: the syndrome is typically detected through routine laboratory screening revealing the characteristic biomarker pattern rather than through symptom-driven clinical evaluation</li>
<li>When complications emerge: chest pain (coronary artery disease), claudication (peripheral artery disease), visual changes (diabetic or hypertensive retinopathy), numbness/tingling (peripheral neuropathy)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Diagnostic Criteria</h2>
<p>Defined by the harmonized criteria from the IDF/AHA/NHLBI (2009 Joint Interim Statement). Diagnosis requires 3 or more of the following 5 criteria:</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Elevated waist circumference</strong>: Population- and country-specific cutoffs; for US: &gt;=102 cm (40 in) men, &gt;=88 cm (35 in) women; for Asian populations: &gt;=90 cm men, &gt;=80 cm women</li>
<li><strong>Elevated triglycerides</strong>: &gt;=150 mg/dL (1.7 mmol/L), or on drug treatment for elevated triglycerides</li>
<li><strong>Reduced HDL cholesterol</strong>: &lt;40 mg/dL (1.0 mmol/L) men, &lt;50 mg/dL (1.3 mmol/L) women, or on drug treatment for reduced HDL</li>
<li><strong>Elevated blood pressure</strong>: Systolic &gt;=130 mmHg and/or diastolic &gt;=85 mmHg, or on antihypertensive drug treatment</li>
<li><strong>Elevated fasting glucose</strong>: &gt;=100 mg/dL (5.6 mmol/L), or on drug treatment for elevated glucose</li>
</ol>
<p>Additional biomarkers that strengthen the clinical picture (not part of formal criteria but useful for risk stratification):</p>
<ul>
<li>Fasting insulin &gt;10-15 mIU/L and/or HOMA-IR &gt;2.5 (confirming underlying insulin resistance)</li>
<li>hs-CRP &gt;2 mg/L (inflammatory component)</li>
<li>Elevated uric acid &gt;7 mg/dL (associated with metabolic syndrome and independently predicts cardiovascular events)</li>
<li>Small dense LDL pattern (LDL pattern B) and elevated ApoB (atherogenic dyslipidemia characteristic of metabolic syndrome)</li>
<li>Elevated ALT (suggesting NAFLD, present in 60-80% of metabolic syndrome patients)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Differential Diagnosis</h2>
<p>Metabolic syndrome is a clinical diagnosis based on defined criteria, but individual components have important differentials:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Cushing syndrome</strong>: Presents with central obesity, hypertension, glucose intolerance, and dyslipidemia -- virtually identical to metabolic syndrome; distinguished by moon facies, buffalo hump, purple striae &gt;1 cm, proximal muscle weakness, easy bruising; screen with overnight 1 mg dexamethasone suppression test or 24-hour urinary free cortisol</li>
<li><strong>Hypothyroidism</strong>: Causes weight gain, fatigue, elevated cholesterol, and mild glucose intolerance; screen with TSH; easily treated and should not be missed as a reversible contributor</li>
<li><strong>Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS)</strong>: 40-80% of PCOS patients meet metabolic syndrome criteria; PCOS adds hyperandrogenism and oligo-anovulation to the metabolic picture; metabolic syndrome management is a core component of PCOS treatment</li>
<li><strong>Medication-induced metabolic syndrome</strong>: Atypical antipsychotics (olanzapine, clozapine -- most potent metabolic effects), corticosteroids, some anticonvulsants (valproic acid), beta-blockers (particularly atenolol -- increase TG, decrease HDL, worsen glucose), protease inhibitors (HIV treatment); drug substitution should be considered when feasible</li>
<li><strong>Lipodystrophy</strong>: Genetic or acquired (HIV-associated) loss of adipose tissue causing severe insulin resistance and atherogenic dyslipidemia despite thin body habitus; absence of subcutaneous fat on examination is the key distinguishing feature</li>
<li><strong>Growth hormone deficiency (adult-onset)</strong>: Causes increased visceral adiposity, dyslipidemia, and insulin resistance; may be suspected in patients with history of pituitary disease, head trauma, or radiation; screen with IGF-1</li>
<li><strong>Obstructive sleep apnea</strong>: Present in 40-60% of metabolic syndrome patients; independently worsens insulin resistance and hypertension through intermittent hypoxia and sympathetic activation; screen with STOP-BANG questionnaire; polysomnography for diagnosis</li>
</ul>
<h2>Management Approach</h2>
<p>Metabolic syndrome is highly responsive to lifestyle intervention, and treatment of the syndrome reduces cardiovascular risk beyond treating any individual component:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Weight loss (highest priority)</strong>: 5-10% body weight loss improves all 5 criteria; 10-15% can normalize metabolic parameters in many patients; waist circumference reduction is more important than scale weight; caloric deficit of 500-750 kcal/day; combination of dietary change and exercise is superior to either alone</li>
<li><strong>Dietary intervention</strong>: Mediterranean diet (strongest evidence: PREDIMED trial showed 30% CVD risk reduction); low glycemic index/load diet; reduce refined carbohydrates and added sugars (limit to &lt;25 g/day); increase fiber to &gt;30 g/day; healthy fats (olive oil, nuts, avocado, fatty fish) replacing saturated and trans fats; time-restricted eating (12-16 hour overnight fast) improves insulin sensitivity and triglycerides</li>
<li><strong>Exercise</strong>: 150-300 minutes/week moderate-intensity aerobic exercise (brisk walking, cycling, swimming) AND resistance training 2-3x/week (critical for improving insulin sensitivity via increased muscle GLUT4 expression); high-intensity interval training (HIIT) may be superior for visceral fat reduction; even without weight loss, exercise independently improves insulin sensitivity and lipid profile</li>
<li><strong>Sleep and stress management</strong>: 7-9 hours quality sleep (sleep restriction worsens all metabolic parameters); treat sleep apnea aggressively (CPAP reduces nocturnal blood pressure by 5-10 mmHg); stress reduction (chronic cortisol elevation drives visceral fat accumulation, insulin resistance, and hypertension)</li>
<li><strong>Supplement considerations</strong>: Berberine (500 mg 2-3x/day -- improves glucose, lipids, and waist circumference; comparable to metformin in some trials), omega-3 fatty acids (2-4 g/day EPA+DHA for triglycerides), magnesium (400 mg/day -- cofactor for 300+ enzymes in glucose and lipid metabolism), vitamin D (maintain 40-60 ng/mL), alpha-lipoic acid (600 mg/day for insulin sensitivity), resveratrol (150-500 mg/day -- SIRT1 activation), curcumin (500-1000 mg/day for inflammation), NMN (250-500 mg/day for NAD+/SIRT1 support)</li>
<li><strong>Pharmacotherapy (when lifestyle insufficient)</strong>: Treat each component individually: antihypertensives (ACE-I/ARB preferred given renal/metabolic benefits), statins (for LDL and cardiovascular risk), metformin (for glucose and insulin resistance), GLP-1 receptor agonists (semaglutide -- addresses obesity, glucose, and cardiovascular risk simultaneously; emerging as a cornerstone therapy), SGLT2 inhibitors (cardiovascular and renal benefit regardless of diabetes), fibrates or icosapent ethyl (for residual hypertriglyceridemia)</li>
<li><strong>Monitoring</strong>: Fasting metabolic panel (glucose, insulin, lipids) every 3-6 months during active treatment; blood pressure at each visit; waist circumference; HbA1c if glucose elevated; liver enzymes (NAFLD screening); uric acid; hs-CRP for inflammatory risk assessment</li>
</ul>
