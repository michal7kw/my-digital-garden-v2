<h1>Obstructive Sleep Apnea</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>A sleep disorder where throat muscles intermittently relax and block the airway during sleep, causing breathing to stop and start.</p>
<h2>Symptoms</h2>
<ul>
<li>Loud snoring (often reported by bed partner)</li>
<li>Witnessed episodes of stopped breathing during sleep</li>
<li>Gasping or choking during sleep</li>
<li>Morning headache</li>
<li>Excessive daytime sleepiness (Epworth Sleepiness Scale &gt;10)</li>
<li>Difficulty concentrating and memory problems</li>
<li>Irritability and mood changes</li>
<li>Nocturia (frequent nighttime urination)</li>
<li>Dry mouth or sore throat upon waking</li>
</ul>
<h2>Treatment Approaches</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>CPAP therapy</strong>: Continuous Positive Airway Pressure — gold standard treatment. Pneumatic splinting of the upper airway. Adherence is the main challenge (50-80% compliance)</li>
<li><strong>Oral appliances</strong>: Mandibular advancement devices — second-line for mild-moderate OSA or CPAP intolerance. Custom-fitted by dental sleep specialist</li>
<li><strong>Weight loss</strong>: 10% weight loss can reduce AHI by 26-50%. Bariatric surgery may be considered for morbid obesity with severe OSA</li>
<li><strong>Positional therapy</strong>: Avoid supine sleeping (positional OSA); use positional devices or body pillows</li>
<li><strong>Surgery</strong>: Uvulopalatopharyngoplasty (UPPP), maxillomandibular advancement, hypoglossal nerve stimulation (Inspire device — FDA-approved for moderate-severe OSA)</li>
<li><strong>Supplements</strong>: NAC (reduces oxidative stress from intermittent hypoxia), vitamin D (often deficient in OSA), omega-3 fatty acids, magnesium (muscle relaxation and sleep quality)</li>
<li><strong>Lifestyle</strong>: Alcohol avoidance (especially before bed — increases airway collapsibility), smoking cessation, regular exercise, avoid sedatives/opioids</li>
</ul>
