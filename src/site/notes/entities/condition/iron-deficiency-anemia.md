<h1>Iron Deficiency Anemia</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>The most common nutritional deficiency worldwide, affecting an estimated 1.2 billion people. Iron deficiency progresses through stages: depleted stores (low ferritin) → iron-deficient erythropoiesis (low transferrin saturation) → frank anemia (low hemoglobin). Causes include inadequate dietary intake, malabsorption (celiac, H. pylori, PPI use), chronic blood loss (menstruation, GI bleeding), and increased demands (pregnancy, growth). Symptoms are often attributed to &quot;just being tired&quot; and diagnosis is delayed.</p>
<h2>Symptoms</h2>
<ul>
<li>Fatigue and weakness (most common)</li>
<li>Pallor (conjunctival, palmar)</li>
<li>Shortness of breath on exertion</li>
<li>Dizziness, headaches</li>
<li>Pica (craving ice, dirt — pathognomonic)</li>
<li>Restless leg syndrome</li>
<li>Brittle nails (koilonychia), hair loss</li>
<li>Cold intolerance</li>
</ul>
<h2>Treatment Approaches</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Oral iron</strong>: Ferrous sulfate 325mg (65mg elemental iron) on empty stomach with vitamin C for absorption. Every-other-day dosing may be more effective (hepcidin kinetics)</li>
<li><strong>IV iron</strong>: Ferric carboxymaltose or iron sucrose for malabsorption, intolerance, or severe deficiency</li>
<li><strong>Dietary</strong>: Red meat (heme iron — 15-35% absorption), liver, dark leafy greens, legumes, fortified cereals</li>
<li><strong>Address underlying cause</strong>: GI workup for occult blood loss, celiac screening, H. pylori treatment</li>
<li><strong>Monitoring</strong>: Ferritin (target &gt;50 ng/mL for symptom resolution), hemoglobin, reticulocyte count</li>
<li><strong>Cofactors</strong>: Vitamin C (doubles non-heme iron absorption), avoid calcium/tea/coffee with iron</li>
</ul>
