<h1>Tinnitus</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Tinnitus is the perception of sound without an external acoustic stimulus, most commonly described as ringing, buzzing, hissing, or roaring. Affects 10-15% of adults, with 1-2% experiencing severe, debilitating symptoms. Can be subjective (most common -- neural) or objective (rare -- vascular or muscular origin). Most cases are associated with sensorineural hearing loss. Central auditory pathway reorganization and limbic system involvement (emotional distress amplification) are key pathophysiological mechanisms.</p>
<h2>Symptoms</h2>
<ul>
<li>Phantom auditory perception: ringing, buzzing, clicking, hissing, or roaring</li>
<li>May be unilateral or bilateral, constant or intermittent</li>
<li>Sleep disturbance and insomnia (50% of patients)</li>
<li>Difficulty concentrating, reading, or following conversations</li>
<li>Anxiety, depression, and irritability (significant psychological burden)</li>
<li>Hyperacusis (heightened sensitivity to normal sounds) in 40% of patients</li>
<li>Pulsatile tinnitus (synchronous with heartbeat) suggests vascular cause -- requires workup</li>
</ul>
<h2>Treatment Approaches</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Sound therapy</strong>: Sound masking devices, white noise generators, hearing aids with tinnitus masking features; notched sound therapy (frequency-targeted)</li>
<li><strong>Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT)</strong>: Strongest evidence for reducing tinnitus distress and improving quality of life; addresses catastrophizing and maladaptive coping</li>
<li><strong>Tinnitus retraining therapy (TRT)</strong>: Combines directive counseling with sound generators to achieve habituation over 12-18 months</li>
<li><strong>Hearing aids</strong>: For patients with concomitant hearing loss -- amplification often reduces tinnitus perception by restoring auditory input</li>
<li><strong>Pharmacotherapy</strong>: No FDA-approved drug for tinnitus; SSRIs/SNRIs for comorbid anxiety/depression; low-dose amitriptyline; melatonin for sleep</li>
<li><strong>Lifestyle</strong>: Noise protection (earplugs in loud environments), stress reduction (meditation, yoga), regular exercise, limit caffeine and alcohol, sleep hygiene</li>
<li><strong>Supplements</strong>: Magnesium (cochlear neuroprotection), zinc (if deficient -- associated with tinnitus severity), ginkgo biloba (mixed evidence, may improve cochlear blood flow), NAC, B-complex vitamins</li>
