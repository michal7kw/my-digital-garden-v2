<h1>Iron Deficiency</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Iron deficiency is the most common nutritional deficiency worldwide, affecting an estimated 1-2 billion people. It represents a state of depleted iron stores that, if untreated, progresses through stages from storage depletion to iron-deficient erythropoiesis to frank iron deficiency anemia. Iron is essential for oxygen transport (hemoglobin), cellular energy production (mitochondrial cytochromes), DNA synthesis, and neurotransmitter metabolism.</p>
<h2>Symptom Cluster</h2>
<p>Iron deficiency produces a characteristic symptom cluster that reflects impaired oxygen delivery, mitochondrial dysfunction, and altered neurotransmitter metabolism:</p>
<ul>
<li>Fatigue and weakness disproportionate to activity level (the earliest and most common symptom, present even before anemia develops)</li>
<li>Pallor of skin, mucous membranes, and nail beds (best assessed at conjunctivae and palmar creases)</li>
<li>Exertional dyspnea and reduced exercise tolerance (impaired oxygen delivery to working muscles)</li>
<li>Pagophagia (craving and chewing ice) -- a highly specific form of pica that is nearly pathognomonic for iron deficiency</li>
<li>Restless leg syndrome (RLS): uncomfortable urge to move legs at rest, particularly in the evening; iron deficiency is found in 25-40% of RLS patients</li>
<li>Koilonychia (spoon-shaped nails) -- a late sign reflecting severe, prolonged deficiency</li>
<li>Angular cheilitis (cracking at mouth corners) and glossitis (smooth, swollen tongue)</li>
<li>Hair thinning and increased hair shedding (telogen effluvium)</li>
<li>Cold intolerance (impaired thermoregulation due to reduced thyroid hormone metabolism)</li>
<li>Cognitive impairment: difficulty concentrating, poor memory, reduced work productivity (iron is required for dopamine receptor D2 function and myelin synthesis)</li>
<li>Headaches, particularly with exertion</li>
<li>In children: developmental delay, behavioral changes, impaired school performance</li>
</ul>
<h2>Diagnostic Criteria</h2>
<p>Iron deficiency is staged based on progressive depletion of body iron compartments:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Stage 1 -- Iron depletion (storage iron depleted)</strong>: Ferritin &lt;30 ng/mL (many experts use &lt;45 ng/mL as a functional threshold, particularly for fatigue and hair loss); serum iron, TIBC, and hemoglobin may still be normal</li>
<li><strong>Stage 2 -- Iron-deficient erythropoiesis</strong>: Ferritin &lt;15 ng/mL, transferrin saturation &lt;20%, TIBC &gt;360 mcg/dL, serum iron &lt;60 mcg/dL; hemoglobin may still be normal or borderline</li>
<li><strong>Stage 3 -- Iron deficiency anemia</strong>: All above plus hemoglobin &lt;12 g/dL (women) or &lt;13 g/dL (men), MCV &lt;80 fL (microcytic), MCH &lt;27 pg (hypochromic), RDW &gt;14.5% (increased red cell size variation)</li>
<li><strong>Key biomarker panel</strong>: Ferritin (most sensitive single test for iron stores; note: falsely elevated as acute phase reactant in inflammation, liver disease, malignancy), transferrin saturation (TSAT &lt;20% indicates inadequate iron supply for erythropoiesis), TIBC (elevated &gt;360 in iron deficiency as liver produces more transferrin to scavenge iron), soluble transferrin receptor (sTfR, elevated in iron deficiency but not in inflammation -- useful to distinguish iron deficiency from anemia of chronic disease)</li>
<li><strong>Reticulocyte hemoglobin content (CHr)</strong>: &lt;29 pg is an early marker of functional iron deficiency, available on modern hematology analyzers; useful in inflammatory states where ferritin is unreliable</li>
</ul>
<h2>Differential Diagnosis</h2>
<p>Conditions that can mimic or coexist with iron deficiency:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Anemia of chronic disease (ACD)</strong>: The most important differential; caused by chronic inflammation, infection, or malignancy; distinguished by normal or elevated ferritin (&gt;100 ng/mL), low serum iron AND low TIBC (both reduced in ACD, whereas TIBC is elevated in iron deficiency), and elevated inflammatory markers; sTfR/ferritin index helps differentiate (&gt;2 favors iron deficiency, &lt;1 favors ACD)</li>
<li><strong>Thalassemia trait</strong>: Microcytic anemia with normal or elevated iron stores; distinguished by Mentzer index (MCV/RBC count: &lt;13 suggests thalassemia, &gt;13 suggests iron deficiency), elevated HbA2 on hemoglobin electrophoresis, and normal/high ferritin; very common in Mediterranean, Middle Eastern, and Southeast Asian populations</li>
<li><strong>Sideroblastic anemia</strong>: Defective heme synthesis causing iron accumulation in mitochondria; distinguished by ringed sideroblasts on bone marrow aspirate and elevated ferritin</li>
<li><strong>Chronic blood loss (occult)</strong>: Must always be investigated as the cause of iron deficiency in adult men and postmenopausal women; GI sources (colon polyps, gastric ulcer, celiac disease, inflammatory bowel disease, hookworm) require endoscopic evaluation; menorrhagia in premenopausal women</li>
<li><strong>Copper deficiency</strong>: Rare but mimics iron deficiency with microcytic anemia that is refractory to iron supplementation; check ceruloplasmin and serum copper</li>
<li><strong>Lead poisoning</strong>: Causes microcytic anemia with basophilic stippling; check blood lead level if exposure risk exists</li>
<li><strong>Hypothyroidism</strong>: Can cause a mild normocytic or microcytic anemia and fatigue that overlaps with iron deficiency symptoms</li>
</ul>
<h2>Management Approach</h2>
<p>Management focuses on replenishing iron stores while identifying and treating the underlying cause of iron loss:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Oral iron supplementation (first-line)</strong>: Iron bisglycinate (25-50 mg elemental iron/day -- best tolerated, highest bioavailability), ferrous sulfate (65 mg elemental iron per 325 mg tablet -- cheapest, most studied, but highest GI side effect rate), ferrous fumarate, or ferrous gluconate; take on empty stomach or with vitamin C (250-500 mg) to enhance absorption; avoid taking with calcium, dairy, tea, coffee, or antacids which inhibit absorption; every-other-day dosing may improve fractional absorption by avoiding hepcidin rebound (recent evidence supports this approach)</li>
<li><strong>IV iron (when oral fails or is insufficient)</strong>: Ferric carboxymaltose (Ferinject/Injectafer, 750-1000 mg per infusion), iron sucrose (Venofer, 200-300 mg per session), or ferumoxytol (Feraheme, 510 mg per dose); indicated for oral intolerance, malabsorption (celiac, post-bariatric surgery, IBD), severe deficiency (Hb &lt;8 g/dL), ongoing losses exceeding oral replacement capacity, or need for rapid repletion (e.g., perioperative)</li>
<li><strong>Vitamin C co-supplementation</strong>: 250-500 mg with each iron dose; reduces ferric (Fe3+) to ferrous (Fe2+) iron in the gut, increasing absorption by 2-3 fold; also beneficial as an antioxidant</li>
<li><strong>Identify and treat underlying cause</strong>: Endoscopy (upper and/or lower) for unexplained iron deficiency in men and postmenopausal women; celiac disease screening (tissue transglutaminase IgA); gynecologic evaluation for menorrhagia; stool testing for occult blood and parasites; medication review (PPIs and H2 blockers reduce iron absorption)</li>
<li><strong>Dietary optimization</strong>: Heme iron sources (red meat, organ meats, dark poultry) have 15-35% absorption vs. 2-20% for non-heme plant sources; pair non-heme iron foods with vitamin C; avoid tea/coffee with meals (tannins chelate iron); cast-iron cookware increases dietary iron content</li>
<li><strong>Monitoring</strong>: Recheck ferritin and CBC at 8-12 weeks (ferritin should increase &gt;30 ng/mL; Hb should rise 1-2 g/dL in 4 weeks if iron deficiency was the cause); continue supplementation for 3-6 months after normalization to replenish stores; target ferritin &gt;50 ng/mL (some experts recommend &gt;100 ng/mL for optimal function)</li>
</ul>
