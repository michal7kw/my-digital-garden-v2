<h1>Vascular Dementia</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Vascular Dementia (VaD) results from cerebrovascular pathology that disrupts brain function. It encompasses a spectrum from strategic single strokes to diffuse small vessel disease. Unlike the gradual decline of Alzheimer's disease, VaD often presents with stepwise deterioration following vascular events.</p>
<h2>Subtypes</h2>
<ol>
<li><strong>Multi-Infarct Dementia</strong>: Multiple large vessel strokes</li>
<li><strong>Small Vessel Disease (SVD)</strong>: White matter lesions, lacunar infarcts</li>
<li><strong>Strategic Single-Infarct Dementia</strong>: Critical location (thalamus, hippocampus)</li>
<li><strong>Hypoperfusion Dementia</strong>: Cardiac arrest, severe hypotension</li>
<li><strong>Hemorrhagic Dementia</strong>: Post-hemorrhage cognitive decline</li>
</ol>
<h2>Clinical Features</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Executive Dysfunction</strong>: Prominent early (planning, organization)</li>
<li><strong>Processing Speed</strong>: Significantly slowed</li>
<li><strong>Attention</strong>: Impaired concentration</li>
<li><strong>Memory</strong>: Variable; retrieval often worse than encoding</li>
<li><strong>Gait Disturbance</strong>: Small-stepped, magnetic gait</li>
<li><strong>Urinary Symptoms</strong>: Urgency, incontinence</li>
<li><strong>Mood Changes</strong>: Depression, emotional lability</li>
</ul>
<h2>Vascular Risk Factors</h2>
<ul>
<li>Hypertension (most important modifiable factor)</li>
<li>Diabetes mellitus</li>
<li>Atrial fibrillation</li>
<li>Hyperlipidemia</li>
<li>Smoking</li>
<li>Obesity</li>
<li>Physical inactivity</li>
</ul>
<h2>Neuroimaging Findings</h2>
<ul>
<li>White matter hyperintensities (leukoaraiosis)</li>
<li>Lacunar infarcts</li>
<li>Cortical/subcortical infarcts</li>
<li>Microbleeds (on susceptibility-weighted imaging)</li>
<li>Brain atrophy</li>
</ul>
