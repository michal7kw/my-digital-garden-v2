<h1>Vascular Dementia</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Cognitive impairment caused by cerebrovascular disease, including strokes and small vessel disease. The second most common cause of dementia after Alzheimer's disease, often occurring in combination (mixed dementia).</p>
<h2>Symptoms</h2>
<ul>
<li>Stepwise cognitive decline (often following strokes) rather than gradual progression</li>
<li>Impaired executive function, planning, and processing speed</li>
<li>Memory loss (less prominent than in Alzheimer's), confusion, and disorientation</li>
<li>Gait disturbance, urinary urgency, and pseudobulbar affect</li>
<li>Depression and apathy (more common than in Alzheimer's)</li>
<li>Focal neurological signs depending on location of vascular damage</li>
</ul>
<h2>Treatment Approaches</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Vascular risk management (primary strategy)</strong>: Antihypertensives (target BP &lt; 130/80), statins, antiplatelet therapy (aspirin, clopidogrel), anticoagulation for atrial fibrillation</li>
<li><strong>Cholinesterase inhibitors</strong>: Donepezil, galantamine -- modest benefit for cognitive symptoms (off-label, evidence limited)</li>
<li><strong>Antidepressants</strong>: SSRIs for comorbid depression (sertraline, citalopram)</li>
<li><strong>Lifestyle</strong>: Regular aerobic exercise (30 min/day -- strongest modifiable factor), Mediterranean diet, cognitive stimulation and social engagement, smoking cessation</li>
<li><strong>Supplements</strong>: Omega-3 fatty acids (DHA for vascular health), B-complex vitamins (homocysteine reduction), CoQ10, phosphatidylserine, resveratrol</li>
<li><strong>Rehabilitation</strong>: Occupational therapy, speech therapy, and structured cognitive training<br />
