<h1>PMDD</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Premenstrual Dysphoric Disorder (PMDD) is a severe mood disorder affecting 3-8% of menstruating women. It is characterized by cyclical mood symptoms (irritability, depression, anxiety, affective lability) that emerge during the luteal phase (after ovulation) and resolve within a few days of menstruation onset. PMDD is distinct from the milder premenstrual syndrome (PMS) in severity and functional impairment.</p>
<p>PMDD represents an abnormal CNS sensitivity to normal hormonal fluctuations — specifically, the rise and fall of progesterone-derived neuroactive steroids (allopregnanolone) that modulate GABA-A receptor function. Women with PMDD have normal hormone levels but abnormal neuronal response to allopregnanolone cycling.</p>
<h2>Symptoms</h2>
<h3>Affective (≥1 required for diagnosis)</h3>
<ul>
<li>Marked affective lability (sudden sadness, tearfulness, mood swings)</li>
<li>Marked irritability or anger</li>
<li>Markedly depressed mood, hopelessness</li>
<li>Marked anxiety, tension, feeling &quot;on edge&quot;</li>
</ul>
<h3>Behavioral/Physical</h3>
<ul>
<li>Decreased interest in usual activities (anhedonia)</li>
<li>Difficulty concentrating</li>
<li>Lethargy, fatigue</li>
<li>Appetite changes (cravings, overeating)</li>
<li>Sleep disturbance (hypersomnia or insomnia)</li>
<li>Feeling overwhelmed or out of control</li>
<li>Physical symptoms: breast tenderness, bloating, joint/muscle pain, headache</li>
</ul>
<h3>Timing</h3>
<ul>
<li>Symptoms present in the luteal phase (days 21-28 of cycle)</li>
<li>Resolve within a few days of menstruation onset</li>
<li>Must be confirmed by prospective daily charting for ≥2 symptomatic cycles</li>
</ul>
<h2>Pathophysiology</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Allopregnanolone sensitivity</strong>: Normal progesterone metabolite (allopregnanolone) is a potent GABA-A receptor positive allosteric modulator. In PMDD, altered sensitivity to allopregnanolone cycling causes paradoxical anxiety/dysphoria during luteal phase</li>
<li><strong>Serotonin-steroid interaction</strong>: Estrogen and progesterone modulate serotonin synthesis, receptor expression, and SERT density. Luteal progesterone rise may acutely upregulate SERT → reduced synaptic serotonin</li>
<li><strong>GABA-A receptor plasticity</strong>: Abnormal GABA-A receptor subunit expression in response to allopregnanolone fluctuation</li>
<li><strong>Genetics</strong>: ESR1 (estrogen receptor alpha) variants; 5-HTTLPR S allele may increase susceptibility</li>
</ul>
<h2>Treatment</h2>
<h3>First-Line: SSRIs (Unique Pharmacology)</h3>
<p>SSRIs are uniquely effective in PMDD — they work within 1-2 days (not 2-4 weeks as in depression), suggesting a mechanism beyond standard serotonergic remodeling:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Proposed mechanism</strong>: SSRIs enhance allopregnanolone synthesis from progesterone (3α-HSD enzyme induction), restoring GABA-A modulation</li>
<li><strong>Continuous dosing</strong>: Standard daily SSRI throughout cycle</li>
<li><strong>Intermittent (luteal phase only)</strong>: SSRIs given only during days 14-28 are equally effective — this is unique to PMDD and supports the rapid neuroactive steroid mechanism</li>
</ul>

<h3>Other Options</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>GnRH agonists</strong>: Leuprolide (chemical menopause) — effective but limited by bone density loss</li>
<li><strong>Oral contraceptives</strong>: Drospirenone/ethinyl estradiol (Yaz) — FDA-approved</li>
<li><strong>Brexanolone/Zuranolone</strong>: Neuroactive steroid analogues (approved for postpartum depression, trials in PMDD)</li>
<li><strong>CBT</strong>: Helpful adjunct but less effective than SSRIs for PMDD specifically</li>
</ul>
