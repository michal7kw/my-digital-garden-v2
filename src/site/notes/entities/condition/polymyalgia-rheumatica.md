<h1>Polymyalgia Rheumatica</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Polymyalgia rheumatica (PMR) is an inflammatory condition of unknown etiology that exclusively affects adults over the age of 50, with peak incidence between 70 and 80 years. It is the most common inflammatory rheumatic disease in the elderly population, with a lifetime prevalence of approximately 2.4% in women and 1.7% in men. PMR is characterized by bilateral, symmetric pain and prolonged morning stiffness involving the shoulder girdle, hip girdle, and neck, with dramatic elevations in inflammatory markers (ESR and CRP).</p>
<p>The pathophysiology involves subclinical vasculitis and synovitis of proximal joints and periarticular structures, particularly subdeltoid/subacromial bursitis, biceps tenosynovitis, and trochanteric bursitis detectable on ultrasound and MRI. The condition shares immunopathological features with giant cell arteritis (GCA), involving Th1 and Th17 cell-mediated inflammation with elevated IL-6 as a key cytokine driver. Approximately 15-20% of PMR patients develop GCA, and 40-60% of GCA patients have PMR symptoms, supporting the concept of a shared disease spectrum.</p>
<p>Diagnosis relies on the 2012 ACR/EULAR provisional classification criteria, which include age over 50, bilateral shoulder aching, abnormal ESR or CRP, and morning stiffness exceeding 45 minutes. A hallmark diagnostic feature is the dramatic and rapid response to low-dose glucocorticoids (prednisone 15-20 mg/day), with substantial improvement expected within 24-72 hours. Failure to respond should prompt reconsideration of the diagnosis. Treatment typically requires 12-24 months of slowly tapered glucocorticoids, with approximately 50% of patients experiencing relapse during taper. Tocilizumab (anti-IL-6 receptor) has emerged as an effective steroid-sparing agent for refractory or relapsing disease.</p>
<h2>Symptoms</h2>
<ul>
<li>Bilateral shoulder pain and stiffness</li>
<li>Hip girdle pain and stiffness</li>
<li>Morning stiffness greater than 45 minutes</li>
<li>Difficulty rising from chair or lifting arms</li>
<li>Constitutional symptoms (fatigue, weight loss, low-grade fever)</li>
<li>Rapid onset (days to weeks)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Risk Factors</h2>
<ul>
<li>Age over 50 (peak 70-80)</li>
<li>Female sex (2-3:1 ratio)</li>
<li>Northern European ancestry</li>
<li>HLA-DRB1*04</li>
<li>Environmental triggers (seasonal variation suggests infectious trigger)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Treatment Approaches</h2>
<ul>
<li>Low-dose prednisone 15-20 mg/day (rapid response is nearly diagnostic)</li>
<li>Slow taper over 12-24 months</li>
<li>Tocilizumab for steroid-sparing</li>
<li>Methotrexate as steroid-sparing agent for relapsing disease</li>
<li>Monitor for giant cell arteritis symptoms throughout treatment</li>
</ul>
