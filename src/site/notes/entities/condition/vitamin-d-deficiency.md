<h1>Vitamin D Deficiency</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>A condition characterized by inadequate levels of vitamin D in the body, which is essential for calcium absorption, bone health, and immune function.</p>
<h2>Symptom Cluster</h2>
<p>Vitamin D deficiency often develops insidiously, and many individuals are asymptomatic until deficiency is moderate to severe. The characteristic symptom cluster includes:</p>
<ul>
<li>Bone pain, particularly in the lower back, pelvis, and legs (due to impaired calcium-phosphate mineralization of bone matrix -- osteomalacia)</li>
<li>Muscle weakness, especially proximal muscles (difficulty rising from a chair, climbing stairs); vitamin D receptors (VDR) are expressed in skeletal muscle and regulate calcium-dependent muscle contraction</li>
<li>Muscle spasms and cramps (hypocalcemia from inadequate calcium absorption)</li>
<li>Fatigue and tiredness disproportionate to activity level (one of the most common presenting complaints; vitamin D is involved in mitochondrial function and ATP production)</li>
<li>Frequent illness or infections: recurrent upper respiratory infections, prolonged illness duration (vitamin D activates cathelicidin and defensins in innate immune cells; deficiency impairs first-line antimicrobial defense)</li>
<li>Mood changes, particularly depression and seasonal affective disorder (SAD); vitamin D modulates serotonin synthesis through regulation of tryptophan hydroxylase-2</li>
<li>Slower wound healing (vitamin D is involved in new skin cell growth and the inflammatory phase of wound repair)</li>
<li>Hair loss (telogen effluvium; alopecia areata is associated with vitamin D deficiency)</li>
<li>In children: rickets (bowed legs, widened wrists, craniotabes, delayed fontanelle closure, dental abnormalities, failure to thrive)</li>
<li>In severe deficiency: tetany (involuntary muscle contraction), seizures (severe hypocalcemia), and heart failure (cardiomyopathy)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Diagnostic Criteria</h2>
<p>Diagnosis is based on serum 25-hydroxyvitamin D [25(OH)D], the primary circulating form and best indicator of vitamin D status:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Severe deficiency</strong>: 25(OH)D &lt;10 ng/mL (&lt;25 nmol/L) -- risk of rickets in children, osteomalacia in adults, symptomatic hypocalcemia</li>
<li><strong>Deficiency</strong>: 25(OH)D &lt;20 ng/mL (&lt;50 nmol/L) -- per Endocrine Society and most international guidelines; increased risk of bone loss, fractures, falls, and immune dysfunction</li>
<li><strong>Insufficiency</strong>: 25(OH)D 20-29 ng/mL (50-72 nmol/L) -- suboptimal; may still impair PTH regulation and bone health</li>
<li><strong>Sufficiency</strong>: 25(OH)D 30-50 ng/mL (75-125 nmol/L) -- adequate for bone and general health</li>
<li><strong>Optimal (functional medicine perspective)</strong>: 40-60 ng/mL (100-150 nmol/L) -- associated with lowest all-cause mortality and optimal immune function in observational studies</li>
<li><strong>Potential toxicity</strong>: 25(OH)D &gt;100 ng/mL (&gt;250 nmol/L) -- risk of hypercalcemia; very rare with oral supplementation under 10,000 IU/day</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Supplementary laboratory evaluation:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Serum calcium and phosphate (low or low-normal in deficiency)</li>
<li>Intact PTH (elevated in deficiency -- secondary hyperparathyroidism as parathyroid glands try to maintain calcium homeostasis by mobilizing bone)</li>
<li>Alkaline phosphatase (ALP, elevated in osteomalacia reflecting increased osteoblast activity)</li>
<li>24-hour urine calcium (low in deficiency; useful to monitor during supplementation to avoid hypercalciuria)</li>
<li>Serum magnesium (magnesium is required for vitamin D activation; deficiency impairs the conversion of 25(OH)D to active 1,25(OH)2D and causes vitamin D resistance)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Differential Diagnosis</h2>
<p>Vitamin D deficiency symptoms overlap with several other conditions, and the underlying cause of the deficiency itself requires investigation:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Primary hyperparathyroidism</strong>: Elevated PTH with elevated (not low) calcium distinguishes this from secondary hyperparathyroidism of vitamin D deficiency; both cause bone pain and fatigue; check calcium alongside PTH</li>
<li><strong>Osteoporosis without vitamin D deficiency</strong>: Postmenopausal bone loss, steroid-induced osteoporosis, and other causes of low bone density that are independent of vitamin D; DEXA scan and complete metabolic evaluation needed</li>
<li><strong>Fibromyalgia</strong>: Widespread musculoskeletal pain and fatigue overlap significantly with vitamin D deficiency; some studies suggest 30-40% of fibromyalgia patients are vitamin D deficient; supplementation may improve symptoms in this subset</li>
<li><strong>Hypothyroidism</strong>: Fatigue, depression, muscle weakness, weight gain, and cold intolerance overlap; TSH should be checked concurrently</li>
<li><strong>Iron deficiency</strong>: Fatigue, hair loss, and impaired cognition overlap; often coexists with vitamin D deficiency, especially in women and vegetarians</li>
<li><strong>Chronic kidney disease</strong>: CKD impairs the renal 1-alpha-hydroxylase that converts 25(OH)D to active 1,25(OH)2D; patients may have adequate 25(OH)D but insufficient active vitamin D; requires active vitamin D (calcitriol) supplementation</li>
<li><strong>Malabsorption syndromes</strong>: Celiac disease, Crohn's disease, short bowel syndrome, chronic pancreatitis, bariatric surgery (especially Roux-en-Y gastric bypass) -- all impair fat-soluble vitamin absorption including vitamin D; may require higher oral doses or parenteral supplementation</li>
<li><strong>Medications causing vitamin D depletion</strong>: Anticonvulsants (phenytoin, carbamazepine, phenobarbital -- induce CYP enzymes that catabolize vitamin D), glucocorticoids (impair calcium absorption and vitamin D metabolism), cholestyramine (bile acid sequestrant impairs fat-soluble vitamin absorption), rifampin, antiretrovirals</li>
</ul>
<h2>Management Approach</h2>
<p>Management involves repleting vitamin D stores and then maintaining adequate levels long-term:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Vitamin D3 (cholecalciferol) supplementation (preferred over D2/ergocalciferol)</strong>: Vitamin D3 is more effective at raising and sustaining 25(OH)D levels than D2; D3 has a longer half-life and stronger affinity for vitamin D binding protein
<ul>
<li><strong>Deficiency (&lt;20 ng/mL)</strong>: Loading dose of 50,000 IU weekly for 8-12 weeks, then maintenance of 2,000-5,000 IU daily; alternatively, 6,000-10,000 IU daily for 8 weeks then maintenance</li>
<li><strong>Insufficiency (20-29 ng/mL)</strong>: 2,000-4,000 IU daily for 3 months, then recheck</li>
<li><strong>Maintenance (once sufficient)</strong>: 1,000-4,000 IU daily depending on body weight, skin color, sun exposure, and target level; obese patients typically need 2-3x higher doses due to sequestration in adipose tissue</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>Magnesium co-supplementation</strong>: 400 mg/day (as glycinate, citrate, or taurate for best absorption); critical and often overlooked -- magnesium is required for vitamin D binding protein, 25-hydroxylase, and 1-alpha-hydroxylase; supplementing vitamin D without adequate magnesium may be ineffective and can worsen magnesium depletion</li>
<li><strong>Vitamin K2 co-supplementation (MK-7 form preferred)</strong>: 100-200 mcg/day; vitamin K2 activates matrix Gla protein and osteocalcin, directing calcium mobilized by vitamin D into bones and away from soft tissues (arterial walls, kidneys); prevents potential calcium mishandling from high-dose vitamin D supplementation; especially important with doses &gt;4,000 IU/day</li>
<li><strong>Calcium</strong>: Ensure adequate dietary calcium (1,000-1,200 mg/day from food sources preferred: dairy, sardines, fortified plant milks, leafy greens); supplement only if dietary intake is insufficient; calcium supplements without K2 are associated with increased cardiovascular calcification in some studies</li>
<li><strong>Sun exposure</strong>: 10-30 minutes of midday sun exposure to face, arms, and legs (without sunscreen) 2-3 times per week can produce 10,000-20,000 IU of vitamin D3; darker skin, higher latitudes (&gt;35 degrees), winter months, sunscreen use, and aging all reduce cutaneous vitamin D synthesis; UVB exposure must be balanced against skin cancer risk</li>
<li><strong>Address underlying causes</strong>: Treat malabsorption conditions, review and modify medications causing vitamin D depletion, consider intramuscular vitamin D (ergocalciferol 300,000 IU annually or cholecalciferol 100,000 IU every 3 months) for severe malabsorption</li>
<li><strong>Monitoring</strong>: Recheck 25(OH)D at 8-12 weeks after initiating supplementation; then every 6-12 months during maintenance; also monitor serum calcium, phosphate, and PTH (PTH should normalize as vitamin D levels reach sufficiency); 24-hour urine calcium if using high-dose supplementation long-term (to detect hypercalciuria before symptomatic kidney stones develop)</li>
</ul>
