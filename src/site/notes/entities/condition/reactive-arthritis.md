<h1>Reactive Arthritis</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Reactive arthritis (ReA) is a form of spondyloarthropathy that develops as a sterile inflammatory response following infection at a distant site, most commonly the gastrointestinal or urogenital tract. The classic triggering organisms include Chlamydia trachomatis (urogenital), and Salmonella, Shigella, Yersinia, and Campylobacter species (enteric). The condition was historically known as Reiter syndrome when presenting with the classic triad of arthritis, urethritis, and conjunctivitis, though this complete triad occurs in only a minority of patients.</p>
<p>The pathogenesis involves molecular mimicry and aberrant immune responses to bacterial antigens that persist in joint tissue. HLA-B27 is present in 60-80% of patients with reactive arthritis, compared to 6-8% of the general population, and confers both increased susceptibility and a more severe disease course. The HLA-B27 molecule may facilitate disease through several proposed mechanisms including arthritogenic peptide presentation, heavy chain misfolding triggering the unfolded protein response, and formation of cell-surface homodimers that activate NK and T cell receptors. Bacterial DNA and antigens (but not viable organisms) have been detected in synovial tissue, supporting the concept of immune-mediated rather than directly infectious joint inflammation.</p>
<p>The typical presentation involves acute asymmetric oligoarthritis predominantly affecting the lower extremities, developing 1-4 weeks after the triggering infection. Extra-articular manifestations include enthesitis, dactylitis, mucocutaneous lesions (keratoderma blennorrhagica, circinate balanitis, oral ulcers), and ocular inflammation (conjunctivitis or anterior uveitis). Most cases are self-limited, resolving within 3-12 months, but approximately 15-20% of patients develop chronic or relapsing arthritis. Treatment is primarily symptomatic with NSAIDs; sulfasalazine or TNF inhibitors are reserved for chronic or refractory cases. Antibiotics are only indicated for documented active Chlamydia infection and do not alter the course of established reactive arthritis.</p>
<h2>Symptoms</h2>
<ul>
<li>Asymmetric oligoarthritis (lower limb predominant)</li>
<li>Urethritis</li>
<li>Conjunctivitis or anterior uveitis</li>
<li>Enthesitis</li>
<li>Keratoderma blennorrhagica (skin)</li>
<li>Circinate balanitis</li>
<li>Dactylitis</li>
<li>Low back pain (sacroiliitis)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Risk Factors</h2>
<ul>
<li>HLA-B27 positive</li>
<li>Recent GI infection (Salmonella, Shigella, Yersinia, Campylobacter)</li>
<li>Recent urogenital infection (Chlamydia trachomatis)</li>
<li>Male sex (post-urethritis form)</li>
<li>Age 20-40</li>
</ul>
<h2>Treatment Approaches</h2>
<ul>
<li>NSAIDs (first-line for joint symptoms)</li>
<li>Intra-articular corticosteroid injections</li>
<li>Sulfasalazine for chronic/refractory arthritis</li>
<li>Antibiotics for active Chlamydia infection only</li>
<li>TNF inhibitors for severe refractory cases</li>
<li>Physical therapy for joint mobility</li>
<li>Most cases self-limited (3-12 months)</li>
</ul>
