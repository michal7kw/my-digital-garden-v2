<h1>Tendinitis</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Tendinitis is the inflammation or irritation of a tendon, the thick fibrous cords that attach muscle to bone. It most commonly affects the rotator cuff (shoulder), Achilles tendon, patellar tendon (jumper's knee), and lateral epicondyle (tennis elbow). The condition typically results from repetitive micro-trauma, sudden overload, or age-related degeneration of tendon tissue. Modern understanding recognizes a continuum between acute tendinitis (predominantly inflammatory) and chronic tendinosis (degenerative with failed healing), and the term tendinopathy is increasingly used to encompass both.</p>
<p>Risk factors include repetitive occupational or athletic movements, age over 40 (when tendons lose elasticity and vascularity), sudden increases in training intensity, poor biomechanics, and certain medications -- notably fluoroquinolone antibiotics, which can weaken tendon collagen and increase rupture risk. Systemic conditions such as diabetes and obesity also predispose to tendinopathy through microangiopathy and chronic low-grade inflammation.</p>
<p>Treatment follows a stepwise approach: initial rest, ice, and NSAIDs for the acute inflammatory phase; graduated eccentric exercise programs (the cornerstone of rehabilitation); and interventional options including corticosteroid injection (limited to short-term use due to tendon weakening), platelet-rich plasma (PRP) injections (emerging evidence), and extracorporeal shockwave therapy. Surgery is reserved for chronic refractory cases after at least 6-12 months of conservative management.</p>
