<h1>Insulin Resistance</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>A physiological condition where cells fail to respond normally to the hormone insulin, leading to hyperinsulinemia and metabolic dysfunction.</p>
<h2>Symptom Cluster</h2>
<p>Insulin resistance often develops silently over years before clinical symptoms manifest. The defining symptom and sign cluster includes:</p>
<ul>
<li>Acanthosis nigricans: dark, velvety patches of skin in body folds (neck, axillae, groin) -- the most specific clinical sign of hyperinsulinemia</li>
<li>Central/abdominal obesity: waist circumference &gt;40 inches (men) or &gt;35 inches (women), with a waist-to-hip ratio &gt;0.9 (men) or &gt;0.85 (women)</li>
<li>Post-prandial lethargy and &quot;carb crash&quot;: excessive drowsiness 1-2 hours after carbohydrate-rich meals due to reactive hypoglycemia following exaggerated insulin spikes</li>
<li>Frequent hunger and carbohydrate cravings: driven by impaired glucose uptake by cells despite elevated blood glucose, creating a paradox of &quot;starvation in the midst of plenty&quot;</li>
<li>Difficulty losing weight despite caloric restriction: hyperinsulinemia promotes lipogenesis and inhibits lipolysis, making fat mobilization resistant to caloric deficit</li>
<li>Skin tags (acrochordons): multiple skin tags, especially on the neck and axillae, strongly correlate with insulin resistance</li>
<li>Fatigue and reduced exercise tolerance</li>
<li>Mood instability, irritability, and anxiety (particularly between meals)</li>
<li>In women: irregular menstrual cycles, hirsutism, acne (suggesting PCOS overlap)</li>
<li>In men: reduced libido and erectile dysfunction (insulin resistance lowers SHBG, altering free testosterone)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Diagnostic Criteria</h2>
<p>There is no universally agreed single diagnostic criterion. Diagnosis is established through a combination of clinical features and laboratory markers:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Fasting insulin</strong>: &gt;10 mIU/L suggests insulin resistance; &gt;15 mIU/L is highly suggestive; &gt;25 mIU/L is diagnostic in most clinical contexts (note: reference ranges vary by laboratory)</li>
<li><strong>HOMA-IR (Homeostatic Model Assessment)</strong>: Calculated as (fasting insulin x fasting glucose) / 405 (glucose in mg/dL); values &gt;2.0 suggest insulin resistance; &gt;2.5 is a commonly used clinical cutoff; &gt;3.0 is clearly abnormal</li>
<li><strong>Triglyceride-to-HDL ratio</strong>: &gt;3.0 (using mg/dL) is a simple, accessible surrogate marker with good sensitivity for insulin resistance; often referred to as a &quot;poor man's insulin resistance test&quot;</li>
<li><strong>HbA1c</strong>: 5.7-6.4% defines prediabetes (reflecting chronic hyperglycemia from insulin resistance); however, HbA1c can be normal in early insulin resistance when the pancreas compensates with sufficient insulin production</li>
<li><strong>Oral glucose tolerance test (OGTT) with insulin levels</strong>: 2-hour glucose &gt;140 mg/dL (impaired glucose tolerance) combined with exaggerated insulin response (peak insulin &gt;100 mIU/L) provides the best non-clamp assessment</li>
<li><strong>Gold standard</strong>: Hyperinsulinemic-euglycemic clamp (research settings only); measures the glucose infusion rate needed to maintain euglycemia during a standardized insulin infusion; glucose disposal rate &lt;4.7 mg/kg/min defines insulin resistance</li>
<li><strong>Biomarker patterns</strong>: Elevated triglycerides (&gt;150 mg/dL), reduced HDL (&lt;40 mg/dL men, &lt;50 mg/dL women), elevated fasting glucose (100-125 mg/dL), and elevated uric acid collectively support the diagnosis</li>
</ul>
<h2>Differential Diagnosis</h2>
<p>Insulin resistance is both a condition and a pathophysiological mechanism underlying many diseases. Key differential considerations:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Type 2 diabetes mellitus</strong>: Progression along the insulin resistance spectrum where pancreatic beta-cell compensation fails; distinguished by fasting glucose &gt;126 mg/dL or HbA1c &gt;6.5%; insulin resistance is the precursor, diabetes is the decompensated state</li>
<li><strong>Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS)</strong>: 50-70% of PCOS patients have insulin resistance; hyperinsulinemia drives ovarian androgen production; distinguished by oligo/anovulation, clinical/biochemical hyperandrogenism, and polycystic ovarian morphology on ultrasound</li>
<li><strong>Cushing syndrome</strong>: Cortisol excess causes profound insulin resistance; distinguished by moon facies, buffalo hump, purple striae, proximal myopathy, easy bruising; confirmed by 24-hour urinary free cortisol, late-night salivary cortisol, or dexamethasone suppression test</li>
<li><strong>Acromegaly</strong>: Growth hormone excess causes insulin resistance; distinguished by coarsened facial features, enlarged hands/feet, prognathism; confirmed by elevated IGF-1 and failure to suppress GH on OGTT</li>
<li><strong>Lipodystrophy syndromes</strong>: Genetic or acquired loss of adipose tissue forces ectopic lipid deposition in liver and muscle, causing severe insulin resistance; distinguished by absence of subcutaneous fat despite metabolic syndrome features</li>
<li><strong>Medication-induced</strong>: Corticosteroids, atypical antipsychotics (olanzapine, clozapine), thiazide diuretics, beta-blockers, protease inhibitors, and some immunosuppressants (tacrolimus, sirolimus) can induce or worsen insulin resistance</li>
<li><strong>Sleep apnea</strong>: Intermittent hypoxia and sleep fragmentation independently worsen insulin resistance; screening with Epworth Sleepiness Scale and overnight oximetry/polysomnography is warranted in obese patients</li>
</ul>
<h2>Management Approach</h2>
<p>Insulin resistance is highly responsive to lifestyle intervention, which should be the foundation of all management strategies:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Dietary intervention (most impactful)</strong>: Low glycemic index/load diet reducing refined carbohydrates and added sugars; Mediterranean diet pattern (strongest evidence base); time-restricted eating / intermittent fasting (16:8 or 5:2 patterns improve insulin sensitivity independent of weight loss); adequate protein (1.2-1.6 g/kg/day to support muscle mass and satiety); fiber intake &gt;30 g/day (slows glucose absorption and feeds beneficial gut bacteria)</li>
<li><strong>Exercise (second most impactful)</strong>: Resistance/strength training 2-3x/week (directly increases muscle GLUT4 expression and glycogen storage capacity, making muscles more insulin-sensitive); moderate-intensity aerobic exercise 150+ min/week; combination is superior to either alone; a single exercise session can improve insulin sensitivity for 24-48 hours</li>
<li><strong>Weight management</strong>: 5-10% body weight loss produces clinically significant improvement in insulin sensitivity; visceral fat reduction is more important than total weight loss; waist circumference is a better progress marker than BMI</li>
<li><strong>Sleep optimization</strong>: 7-9 hours per night; treat sleep apnea if present (CPAP improves insulin sensitivity); even 2-3 nights of sleep restriction (4-5 hours) measurably worsens insulin sensitivity in healthy individuals</li>
<li><strong>Supplement considerations</strong>: Berberine (500 mg 2-3x/day -- activates AMPK, comparable to metformin in several trials), alpha-lipoic acid (600 mg/day -- enhances insulin signaling), chromium picolinate (200-1000 mcg/day -- cofactor for insulin receptor), magnesium (400 mg/day -- depleted in insulin resistance, required for insulin signaling), NMN or NR (NAD+ precursors that activate SIRT1/AMPK axis)</li>
<li><strong>Pharmacotherapy (when lifestyle insufficient)</strong>: Metformin (first-line, 500-2000 mg/day, activates AMPK), GLP-1 receptor agonists (semaglutide, liraglutide -- improve insulin sensitivity and promote weight loss), pioglitazone (PPARgamma agonist, directly improves insulin sensitivity but causes weight gain and fluid retention)</li>
<li><strong>Monitoring</strong>: Fasting insulin and HOMA-IR every 3-6 months; HbA1c every 3-6 months; lipid panel (triglyceride:HDL ratio); waist circumference; consider continuous glucose monitoring (CGM) for real-time feedback on glycemic responses to diet</li>
</ul>
