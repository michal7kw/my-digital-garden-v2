<h1>Panic Disorder</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Panic Disorder is characterized by recurrent, unexpected panic attacks — discrete episodes of intense fear or discomfort that reach a peak within minutes. Attacks are accompanied by at least 4 of 13 defined symptoms. Between attacks, patients develop persistent worry about future attacks (anticipatory anxiety) and may develop avoidance behaviors that can progress to agoraphobia.</p>
<p>Lifetime prevalence is 2-3%, with onset typically in late adolescence or early adulthood. Women are affected twice as often as men. The disorder involves dysregulation of the fear network — amygdala hyperreactivity, insufficient prefrontal cortex inhibition, and brainstem autonomic dysregulation. Serotonergic and noradrenergic systems are both implicated.</p>
<h2>Symptoms</h2>
<h3>Panic Attack Criteria (≥4 symptoms)</h3>
<ul>
<li>Palpitations or accelerated heart rate</li>
<li>Sweating</li>
<li>Trembling or shaking</li>
<li>Shortness of breath or smothering sensation</li>
<li>Choking feeling</li>
<li>Chest pain or discomfort</li>
<li>Nausea or abdominal distress</li>
<li>Dizziness, lightheadedness, or faintness</li>
<li>Chills or hot flashes</li>
<li>Paresthesias (numbness or tingling)</li>
<li>Derealization or depersonalization</li>
<li>Fear of losing control or &quot;going crazy&quot;</li>
<li>Fear of dying</li>
</ul>
<h3>Between Attacks</h3>
<ul>
<li>Persistent worry about recurrence (anticipatory anxiety)</li>
<li>Behavioral changes to avoid triggers</li>
<li>Agoraphobia in ~30-50% of cases</li>
</ul>
<h2>Pathophysiology</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Amygdala hyperreactivity</strong>: Exaggerated fear response to interoceptive cues</li>
<li><strong>Prefrontal hypofunction</strong>: Inadequate top-down regulation of amygdala</li>
<li><strong>Locus coeruleus</strong>: Noradrenergic hyperactivation → physical symptoms</li>
<li><strong>Serotonin</strong>: Reduced 5-HT1A receptor binding in raphe and limbic regions</li>
<li><strong>CO2 hypersensitivity</strong>: Panic patients have exaggerated suffocation alarm (35% CO2 challenge)</li>
<li><strong>Genetics</strong>: 40% heritability; SLC6A4, COMT, ADORA2A implicated</li>
</ul>
<h2>Treatment</h2>
<h3>First-Line: SSRIs</h3>
<table>
<thead>
<tr>
<th>SSRI</th>
<th>Evidence</th>
<th>Notes</th>
</tr>
</thead>
<tbody>
<tr>
<td>Sertraline</td>
<td>FDA-approved</td>
<td>25-200 mg; start low (25mg) to avoid initial activation</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Paroxetine</td>
<td>FDA-approved</td>
<td>10-60 mg; potent but discontinuation risk</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Fluoxetine</td>
<td>FDA-approved</td>
<td>10-60 mg; activating — may temporarily worsen panic initially</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Escitalopram</td>
<td>Strong evidence</td>
<td>10-20 mg; off-label but widely used</td>
</tr>
</tbody>
</table>
<p><strong>Important</strong>: Start SSRIs at half the usual depression dose — panic patients are sensitive to initial activation/jitteriness.</p>
<h3>Adjunctive</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Benzodiazepines</strong>: Clonazepam or alprazolam for acute relief; taper once SSRI effective (2-4 weeks)</li>
<li><strong>CBT with interoceptive exposure</strong>: First-line psychotherapy; comparable to SSRIs</li>
<li><strong>Combination</strong>: SSRI + CBT superior to either alone</li>
</ul>
