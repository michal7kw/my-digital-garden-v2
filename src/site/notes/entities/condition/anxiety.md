<h1>Anxiety</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Anxiety disorders are the most prevalent mental health conditions globally, affecting approximately 300 million people. While anxiety is a normal adaptive response to threat, anxiety disorders involve disproportionate or persistent fear and worry that impairs daily functioning. The neural basis involves amygdala hyperreactivity (threat detection set too high), insufficient prefrontal cortical regulation (inability to downregulate fear responses), and dysregulated neurotransmitter systems. Chronic anxiety activates the HPA axis, elevates cortisol, disrupts sleep, impairs immune function, and significantly increases cardiovascular risk.</p>
<h2>Subtypes</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD)</strong> — Chronic, excessive worry about multiple domains (health, work, relationships) lasting 6+ months</li>
<li><strong>Social Anxiety Disorder</strong> — Intense fear of social situations and negative evaluation</li>
<li><strong>Panic Disorder</strong> — Recurrent unexpected panic attacks with anticipatory anxiety</li>
<li><strong>Specific Phobias</strong> — Disproportionate fear of specific objects/situations</li>
<li><strong>Agoraphobia</strong> — Fear of situations where escape might be difficult</li>
</ul>
<h2>Symptoms</h2>
<ul>
<li>Excessive worry and rumination</li>
<li>Restlessness, feeling &quot;on edge&quot;</li>
<li>Muscle tension (especially neck, shoulders, jaw)</li>
<li>Sleep disturbance (difficulty falling asleep, racing thoughts)</li>
<li>Difficulty concentrating</li>
<li>Irritability</li>
<li>GI symptoms (nausea, IBS-like symptoms — gut-brain axis)</li>
<li><strong>Panic attacks</strong>: Palpitations, chest tightness, shortness of breath, derealization, fear of dying</li>
</ul>
<h2>Risk Factors</h2>
<ul>
<li>Family history (30-40% heritable)</li>
<li>Childhood adversity / insecure attachment</li>
<li>Female sex (2x more common in women)</li>
<li>Chronic stress / high-demand environments</li>
<li>Personality traits (neuroticism, perfectionism)</li>
<li>Caffeine sensitivity</li>
<li>Gut dysbiosis (emerging evidence for microbiome-anxiety connection)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Treatment Approaches</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>CBT (first-line)</strong>: Exposure therapy (gold standard for phobias/social anxiety), cognitive restructuring, interoceptive exposure for panic</li>
<li><strong>Medications</strong>: SSRIs (sertraline, escitalopram — first-line), SNRIs (venlafaxine), buspirone (GAD-specific), benzodiazepines (short-term only — addiction risk)</li>
<li><strong>Exercise</strong>: 150+ min/week moderate exercise — anxiolytic effect comparable to medication for mild-moderate anxiety</li>
<li><strong>Mindfulness-based interventions</strong>: MBSR (moderate effect sizes), meditation (10+ min/day)</li>
<li><strong>Breathwork</strong>: Box breathing, 4-7-8 technique, physiological sigh — direct vagal nerve activation reduces sympathetic arousal</li>
<li><strong>Sleep optimization</strong>: Anxiety and insomnia form a bidirectional cycle; treating one improves the other</li>
<li><strong>Supplements</strong>: L-theanine (200mg — promotes alpha waves), ashwagandha (KSM-66, 600mg — cortisol reduction), magnesium glycinate (400mg), GABA precursors</li>
<li><strong>Limit stimulants</strong>: Caffeine sensitivity is significantly higher in anxiety-prone individuals</li>
</ul>
