<h1>Pernicious Anemia</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>An autoimmune condition where antibodies destroy gastric parietal cells or intrinsic factor, preventing vitamin B12 absorption. Leads to megaloblastic anemia and potentially irreversible neurological damage (subacute combined degeneration of the spinal cord). The most common cause of B12 deficiency in developed countries. Often associated with other autoimmune conditions (thyroid, vitiligo, type 1 diabetes). Diagnosis requires low B12, elevated MMA/homocysteine, and anti-intrinsic factor or anti-parietal cell antibodies.</p>
<h2>Symptoms</h2>
<ul>
<li>Fatigue, weakness, pallor (anemia)</li>
<li>Macrocytic red blood cells (MCV &gt;100 fL)</li>
<li>Glossitis (smooth, beefy-red tongue)</li>
<li>Peripheral neuropathy (numbness, tingling in hands/feet)</li>
<li>Cognitive changes (memory loss, confusion, depression)</li>
<li>Subacute combined degeneration (posterior column and lateral corticospinal tract damage — advanced)</li>
<li>Ataxia and balance problems</li>
</ul>
<h2>Treatment Approaches</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>B12 replacement</strong>: IM cyanocobalamin or hydroxocobalamin injections (1000 mcg daily x 1 week, then weekly x 1 month, then monthly for life). High-dose oral B12 (1000-2000 mcg daily) may suffice for some patients</li>
<li><strong>Monitoring</strong>: B12 levels, MMA, homocysteine, CBC (reticulocyte response in 3-5 days, Hb normalizes 6-8 weeks)</li>
<li><strong>Neurological assessment</strong>: Nerve conduction studies if neuropathy suspected — early treatment prevents irreversible damage</li>
<li><strong>Associated condition screening</strong>: Thyroid function, glucose, other autoimmune markers</li>
<li><strong>Folate</strong>: Correct concurrent folate deficiency (but never give folate alone — masks B12 deficiency while neurological damage progresses)</li>
</ul>
