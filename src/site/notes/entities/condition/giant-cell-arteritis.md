<h1>Giant Cell Arteritis</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Giant cell arteritis (GCA) is a large-vessel vasculitis that primarily affects the aorta and its branches, with a predilection for the temporal arteries. It is a medical emergency because untreated disease carries a 15-20% risk of irreversible vision loss from anterior ischemic optic neuropathy. GCA occurs exclusively in adults over 50 years of age, with peak incidence between 70-80 years, and affects women approximately three times more often than men.</p>
<p>GCA has a strong association with polymyalgia rheumatica (PMR), with 40-60% overlap between the two conditions. The hallmark symptoms are new-onset temporal headache, jaw claudication, and visual disturbances. When vision loss is threatened, treatment with IV methylprednisolone should not be delayed for biopsy confirmation. Tocilizumab (IL-6 receptor inhibitor) has emerged as an important steroid-sparing agent, reducing cumulative glucocorticoid exposure during the prolonged taper typically required (1-2 years).</p>
