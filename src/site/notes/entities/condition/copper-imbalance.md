<h1>Copper Imbalance</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>A medical condition characterized by either a deficiency or an excess (toxicity) of copper, an essential trace mineral required for various physiological processes.</p>
<h2>Symptoms</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Deficiency</strong>: Fatigue, weakness, and pallor (copper-deficiency anemia — often microcytic or normocytic)</li>
<li><strong>Deficiency</strong>: Neutropenia (low white blood cells — copper is essential for myeloid cell maturation)</li>
<li><strong>Deficiency</strong>: Peripheral neuropathy and myelopathy mimicking B12 deficiency (subacute combined degeneration)</li>
<li><strong>Deficiency</strong>: Osteoporosis and bone fragility (copper required for lysyl oxidase in collagen cross-linking)</li>
<li><strong>Deficiency</strong>: Impaired wound healing and frequent infections</li>
<li><strong>Toxicity</strong>: Nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, and diarrhea (acute)</li>
<li><strong>Toxicity</strong>: Jaundice, hepatomegaly, and liver failure (chronic — Wilson's disease)</li>
<li><strong>Toxicity</strong>: Kayser-Fleischer rings in eyes (copper deposits in cornea — Wilson's disease)</li>
<li><strong>Toxicity</strong>: Neuropsychiatric symptoms (tremor, dysarthria, personality changes — Wilson's disease)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Treatment Approaches</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Copper deficiency repletion</strong>: Oral copper gluconate or copper sulfate (2-8 mg elemental copper/day). IV copper for severe deficiency or malabsorption (copper chloride). Hematological recovery expected in 4-12 weeks</li>
<li><strong>Zinc-induced deficiency</strong>: Most common iatrogenic cause — reduce or stop excessive zinc supplementation (zinc induces intestinal metallothionein which traps copper). Maintain zinc-to-copper ratio around 8-15:1</li>
<li><strong>Wilson's disease (copper excess)</strong>: Chelation with D-penicillamine or trientine (first-line), zinc acetate for maintenance therapy (blocks intestinal copper absorption), low-copper diet (avoid liver, shellfish, chocolate, mushrooms, nuts), liver transplant for fulminant hepatic failure</li>
<li><strong>Dietary sources of copper</strong>: Organ meats (liver), shellfish (oysters), dark chocolate, nuts and seeds, legumes, whole grains</li>
<li><strong>Monitoring</strong>: Serum copper, ceruloplasmin, 24-hour urine copper, zinc levels (always check together), CBC with differential (neutrophil count), liver function tests</li>
<li><strong>Address underlying causes</strong>: Review zinc supplementation dose, bariatric surgery history (malabsorption), celiac disease, excessive antacid use</li>
</ul>
