<h1>Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Juvenile idiopathic arthritis (JIA) is an umbrella term encompassing chronic arthritis of unknown etiology in children under 16 years of age with symptoms persisting for at least 6 weeks. It is the leading cause of acquired disability in children. JIA comprises several distinct subtypes: oligoarticular (most common), polyarticular (RF-positive or RF-negative), systemic (Still disease), enthesitis-related, and psoriatic arthritis.</p>
<p>Each subtype has distinct clinical features, genetic associations, and treatment responses. Systemic JIA (Still disease) is particularly notable for spiking fevers, salmon-colored evanescent rash, and risk of macrophage activation syndrome -- a life-threatening complication. Oligoarticular JIA carries a high risk of chronic anterior uveitis, often asymptomatic, requiring regular ophthalmologic screening. Modern biologic therapies (TNF inhibitors, IL-6 and IL-1 inhibitors) have transformed outcomes, but early recognition and aggressive treatment remain essential to prevent joint damage and growth disturbance.</p>
