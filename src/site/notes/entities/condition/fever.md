<h1>Fever</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Fever (pyrexia) is an elevation of core body temperature above the normal range, typically defined as an oral temperature exceeding 38.0 degrees C (100.4 degrees F) or a rectal temperature above 38.3 degrees C. Fever represents a coordinated physiological response orchestrated by the hypothalamic thermoregulatory center in response to pyrogenic stimuli, distinct from hyperthermia (unregulated temperature elevation due to heat exposure or metabolic dysfunction).</p>
<p>The febrile response is initiated when exogenous pyrogens (bacterial lipopolysaccharides, viral components, fungal cell wall elements) stimulate immune cells -- primarily monocytes, macrophages, and Kupffer cells -- to release endogenous pyrogens, most notably interleukin-1 (IL-1), interleukin-6 (IL-6), and tumor necrosis factor alpha (TNF-alpha). These cytokines act on the organum vasculosum of the lamina terminalis (OVLT) to induce prostaglandin E2 (PGE2) synthesis via cyclooxygenase-2 (COX-2). PGE2 raises the hypothalamic thermoregulatory set point, triggering heat conservation (vasoconstriction, behavioral bundling) and heat generation (shivering thermogenesis) until the new set point is reached.</p>
<p>Fever is an evolutionarily conserved defense mechanism present across vertebrates for over 600 million years. Moderate fever (38-40 degrees C) enhances immune function by increasing neutrophil migration, T-cell proliferation, antibody production, and interferon activity, while simultaneously impairing replication of many temperature-sensitive pathogens. The metabolic cost of fever is substantial, with each 1 degree C rise increasing basal metabolic rate by approximately 10-13%.</p>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<p>Fever is the most common reason for emergency department visits in children and one of the most frequent presenting symptoms across all age groups. Common etiologies include viral upper respiratory infections, urinary tract infections, pneumonia, and gastroenteritis. Fever of unknown origin (FUO) is defined as fever exceeding 38.3 degrees C on multiple occasions over more than 3 weeks without diagnosis after initial investigation, and may result from infections, malignancies, autoimmune disorders, or drug reactions. Antipyretic medications (acetaminophen, NSAIDs) work by inhibiting COX-mediated PGE2 synthesis, lowering the hypothalamic set point. While fever suppression provides symptomatic relief, evidence suggests it may prolong some infections by blunting the adaptive immune response. Malignant hyperthermia is a distinct pharmacogenetic emergency triggered by volatile anesthetics. Febrile seizures affect 2-5% of children aged 6 months to 5 years and are generally benign. Extreme hyperpyrexia (above 41.5 degrees C) can cause protein denaturation, cerebral edema, and multiorgan failure, requiring emergency cooling.</p>
