<h1>Chronic Inflammation</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Chronic inflammation is a prolonged inflammatory response that can last for weeks, months, or even years. It is characterized by the simultaneous active inflammation, tissue destruction, and attempts at healing.</p>
<h2>Symptom Cluster</h2>
<p>Chronic inflammation presents with a diffuse, non-specific symptom cluster that often develops insidiously and is easily attributed to other causes:</p>
<ul>
<li>Persistent fatigue and malaise disproportionate to activity level (the most common presenting complaint)</li>
<li>Low-grade fever (37.2-38.0 C / 99-100.4 F), often intermittent and fluctuating</li>
<li>Diffuse myalgia (muscle aches) and arthralgia (joint pain) without frank synovitis</li>
<li>Generalized muscle stiffness, particularly morning stiffness lasting &gt;30 minutes</li>
<li>Skin manifestations: rashes, dermatitis, or slow wound healing</li>
<li>Unexplained weight changes (gain from fluid retention and metabolic dysregulation, or loss from catabolic cytokine effects)</li>
<li>Cognitive symptoms: &quot;brain fog,&quot; impaired concentration, memory difficulties (driven by neuroinflammatory cytokine signaling across the blood-brain barrier)</li>
<li>Mood disturbances: irritability, anxiety, depression (inflammatory cytokines directly affect serotonin and dopamine metabolism)</li>
<li>Gastrointestinal symptoms: bloating, altered bowel habits, food sensitivities (intestinal permeability changes)</li>
<li>Frequent or prolonged infections (immune system dysregulation with paradoxical immunosuppression despite chronic activation)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Diagnostic Criteria</h2>
<p>There is no single definitive diagnostic test for chronic inflammation. Diagnosis relies on a pattern of biomarker elevations in the appropriate clinical context:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>hs-CRP (high-sensitivity C-reactive protein)</strong>: &gt;2 mg/L suggests low-grade systemic inflammation; &gt;3 mg/L is associated with elevated cardiovascular risk; values &gt;10 mg/L suggest acute infection or autoimmune flare and should not be attributed to chronic low-grade inflammation without further workup</li>
<li><strong>ESR (erythrocyte sedimentation rate)</strong>: Elevated above age/sex-adjusted norms (approximate upper limit: age/2 for men, (age+10)/2 for women); less specific than hs-CRP but useful for monitoring trends</li>
<li><strong>Fibrinogen</strong>: &gt;400 mg/dL supports inflammatory state; also an acute phase reactant and independent cardiovascular risk factor</li>
<li><strong>Homocysteine</strong>: &gt;12 micromol/L associated with inflammatory vascular damage; reflects B-vitamin status (B6, B12, folate) and methylation capacity</li>
<li><strong>Supplementary markers</strong>: Ferritin (elevated as acute phase reactant even without iron overload), IL-6 (research-grade, direct upstream driver of CRP), TNF-alpha, omega-6:omega-3 ratio (&gt;10:1 is pro-inflammatory), arachidonic acid:EPA ratio (&gt;10 suggests inflammatory prostaglandin dominance)</li>
<li><strong>Complete blood count patterns</strong>: Elevated neutrophil-to-lymphocyte ratio (NLR &gt;3), elevated monocytes, elevated red cell distribution width (RDW &gt;14.5%)</li>
<li><strong>Exclusion of acute causes</strong>: Must rule out active infection, malignancy, autoimmune disease in flare, and recent trauma/surgery before diagnosing chronic low-grade inflammation</li>
</ul>
<h2>Differential Diagnosis</h2>
<p>Chronic low-grade inflammation is both a standalone condition and a feature of many diseases. Key differential considerations:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Autoimmune diseases</strong>: Rheumatoid arthritis, systemic lupus erythematosus, inflammatory bowel disease, psoriasis -- all cause chronic inflammation but with specific organ targeting and autoantibody profiles; often present with hs-CRP &gt;10 and specific autoantibodies (RF, ANA, anti-CCP, ANCA)</li>
<li><strong>Occult infection</strong>: Chronic dental infections (periodontitis), Helicobacter pylori, hepatitis B/C, HIV, tuberculosis -- each can drive persistent systemic inflammation; dental exam, hepatitis panel, and targeted cultures should be considered</li>
<li><strong>Occult malignancy</strong>: Many cancers produce inflammatory cytokines; unexplained weight loss, night sweats, or markedly elevated ferritin/LDH should prompt age-appropriate cancer screening</li>
<li><strong>Metabolic syndrome / visceral adiposity</strong>: Visceral fat is the largest endocrine organ in obese individuals, producing IL-6, TNF-alpha, and leptin; often the primary driver of elevated hs-CRP in the absence of other causes</li>
<li><strong>Chronic stress / HPA axis dysregulation</strong>: Sustained cortisol elevation initially suppresses but eventually dysregulates immune function, leading to chronic inflammatory signaling</li>
<li><strong>Intestinal permeability (&quot;leaky gut&quot;)</strong>: Increased translocation of bacterial lipopolysaccharide (LPS) from gut lumen to systemic circulation activates toll-like receptor 4 (TLR4) and NF-kB; associated with dysbiosis, celiac disease, and food sensitivities</li>
<li><strong>Medication-induced</strong>: Chronic NSAID use (paradoxically can drive rebound inflammation), oral contraceptives (elevate CRP), and some antipsychotics</li>
</ul>
<h2>Management Approach</h2>
<p>Management targets the root cause(s) while reducing systemic inflammatory burden:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Dietary intervention (highest impact)</strong>: Anti-inflammatory diet pattern (Mediterranean diet has strongest evidence); increase omega-3 fatty acids (fatty fish 2-3x/week, or 2-4 g EPA+DHA/day supplementation); reduce omega-6 seed oils (soybean, corn, sunflower); eliminate or reduce ultra-processed foods, refined sugar, and trans fats; increase polyphenol-rich foods (berries, dark chocolate, green tea, turmeric); consider elimination diet if food sensitivities suspected</li>
<li><strong>Supplement considerations</strong>: Fish oil / omega-3 (2-4 g/day EPA+DHA), curcumin (500-1000 mg/day with piperine for absorption), vitamin D (maintain 25-OH D at 40-60 ng/mL), magnesium (400 mg/day), CoQ10 (100-200 mg/day), N-acetyl cysteine (600-1200 mg/day for glutathione support), ashwagandha (300-600 mg/day for stress-mediated inflammation)</li>
<li><strong>Lifestyle modifications</strong>: Regular moderate exercise (150-300 min/week -- anti-inflammatory myokines released during exercise directly suppress TNF-alpha and IL-6); 7-9 hours quality sleep (sleep deprivation elevates CRP by 25-50%); stress management (meditation, yoga, breathwork reduce cortisol-driven inflammation); smoking cessation; moderate alcohol (or abstinence)</li>
<li><strong>Address root causes</strong>: Treat periodontal disease, optimize gut health (probiotics, prebiotics, consider GI workup), manage metabolic syndrome, screen for and treat autoimmune conditions, address chronic stress</li>
<li><strong>Pharmacological options (when indicated)</strong>: Low-dose aspirin (75-100 mg/day for cardiovascular risk), low-dose naltrexone (4.5 mg nightly, off-label for autoimmune-driven inflammation), targeted biologics for specific autoimmune conditions (anti-TNF, anti-IL-6)</li>
<li><strong>Monitoring</strong>: Repeat hs-CRP and ESR every 3-6 months to assess treatment response; omega-6:omega-3 ratio if supplementing; homocysteine if B-vitamin supplementation initiated</li>
</ul>
