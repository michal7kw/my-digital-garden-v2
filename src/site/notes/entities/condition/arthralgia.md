<h1>Arthralgia</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Arthralgia refers to pain originating from one or more joints without the objective inflammatory signs (swelling, warmth, erythema) that define arthritis. It is one of the most common musculoskeletal complaints, affecting an estimated 15-25% of adults at any given time. Arthralgia may involve a single joint (monoarthralgia), 2-4 joints (oligoarthralgia), or 5 or more joints (polyarthralgia), and can be acute (less than 6 weeks) or chronic.</p>
<p>The pain in arthralgia arises from nociceptors in the joint capsule, ligaments, periosteum, and periarticular structures, as articular cartilage itself is aneural. Joint pain signaling involves both A-delta fibers (sharp, well-localized pain) and C-fibers (dull, diffuse, aching pain), with sensitization of these nociceptors by inflammatory mediators (prostaglandins, bradykinin, substance P, nerve growth factor) contributing to chronic pain states. Central sensitization can develop in persistent arthralgia, amplifying pain perception beyond the degree of peripheral tissue pathology.</p>
<p>Arthralgia is distinguished from arthritis by the absence of objective joint inflammation on examination, though it may represent early or subclinical inflammatory joint disease. In rheumatoid arthritis, arthralgia often precedes the development of clinical synovitis by months to years, and the presence of anti-citrullinated protein antibodies (ACPA) in patients with arthralgia significantly predicts progression to RA.</p>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<p>Arthralgia has a wide differential diagnosis spanning mechanical, inflammatory, infectious, metabolic, and neuropathic etiologies. Osteoarthritis-related arthralgia is the most common cause in older adults, driven by cartilage degradation, subchondral bone changes, and low-grade synovial inflammation. Viral arthralgia accompanies infections such as hepatitis B/C, parvovirus B19, chikungunya, and dengue. Drug-induced arthralgia occurs with aromatase inhibitors, statins, fluoroquinolones, and checkpoint inhibitors. Post-infectious reactive arthralgia can follow gastrointestinal or genitourinary infections. Fibromyalgia causes widespread pain that often includes prominent arthralgias. Management depends on the underlying cause and ranges from physical therapy and weight management to anti-inflammatory medications (NSAIDs, corticosteroids), disease-modifying agents for autoimmune etiologies, and supplements such as omega-3 fatty acids, curcumin, and glucosamine that target joint inflammation and cartilage support.</p>
