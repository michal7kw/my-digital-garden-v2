<h1>Gouty Arthritis</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Gout is the most common inflammatory arthritis, caused by deposition of monosodium urate crystals in joints when serum uric acid exceeds the saturation point (~6.8 mg/dL). Acute flares cause excruciating pain (classically in the first MTP joint — &quot;podagra&quot;), while chronic tophaceous gout leads to joint destruction. Prevalence is rising globally (3.9% in US adults), driven by obesity, metabolic syndrome, fructose consumption, and chronic kidney disease.</p>
<h2>Symptoms</h2>
<ul>
<li>Acute attacks: Sudden, severe joint pain (often nocturnal onset), swelling, redness, warmth — first MTP joint in 50% of initial flares</li>
<li>Intercritical periods: Asymptomatic between attacks</li>
<li>Chronic tophaceous gout: Visible urate deposits (tophi) in joints, ears, tendons</li>
<li>Kidney stones (uric acid stones in 10-25%)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Treatment Approaches</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Acute flare</strong>: NSAIDs (indomethacin, naproxen), colchicine (within 12h of onset), corticosteroids</li>
<li><strong>Urate-lowering therapy</strong>: Allopurinol (first-line), febuxostat — target serum urate &lt;6 mg/dL</li>
<li><strong>Dietary</strong>: Limit purines (organ meats, shellfish, red meat), avoid fructose/alcohol (especially beer), increase hydration, cherries (evidence for reduced flare frequency)</li>
<li><strong>Lifestyle</strong>: Weight loss, exercise (reduces uric acid), adequate hydration</li>
<li><strong>Supplements</strong>: Vitamin C (500mg — modest uricosuric effect), tart cherry extract, quercetin</li>
</ul>
