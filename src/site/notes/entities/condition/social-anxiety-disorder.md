<h1>Social Anxiety Disorder</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Social Anxiety Disorder (SAD, formerly Social Phobia) is characterized by intense, persistent fear and avoidance of social situations where the individual might be scrutinized or judged by others. It is the third most common psychiatric disorder globally (after depression and alcohol use disorder), with a lifetime prevalence of 7-12%. Onset is typically in adolescence (median age 13), and untreated SAD follows a chronic, unremitting course.</p>
<p>SAD involves dysfunction in the &quot;social brain&quot; network — amygdala hyperreactivity to social threat cues (angry/contemptuous faces), insufficient prefrontal regulation, and altered insula processing of interoceptive signals (awareness of blushing, sweating, trembling). Serotonergic dysfunction is implicated: reduced 5-HT1A binding in amygdala and raphe, and increased SERT binding in some studies.</p>
<h2>Symptoms</h2>
<h3>Core Features</h3>
<ul>
<li>Intense fear of being negatively evaluated in social situations</li>
<li>Fear of visibly showing anxiety (blushing, sweating, trembling, stumbling over words)</li>
<li>Avoidance of social interactions, public speaking, eating in public, using public restrooms</li>
<li>Anticipatory anxiety (hours to weeks before feared event)</li>
</ul>
<h3>Physical Symptoms During Exposure</h3>
<ul>
<li>Blushing, sweating, trembling</li>
<li>Tachycardia, muscle tension</li>
<li>Nausea, GI distress</li>
<li>Mind going blank, difficulty speaking</li>
</ul>
<h3>Subtypes</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Generalized</strong>: Fear across most social situations (more severe, chronic)</li>
<li><strong>Performance-only</strong>: Fear limited to public speaking/performance (may respond to beta-blockers)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Pathophysiology</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Amygdala hyperreactivity</strong>: Exaggerated response to social threat cues (fMRI studies)</li>
<li><strong>Prefrontal hypofunction</strong>: Reduced medial PFC regulation of amygdala</li>
<li><strong>Anterior insula</strong>: Heightened interoceptive awareness → amplifies somatic anxiety symptoms</li>
<li><strong>Serotonin</strong>: Reduced 5-HT1A binding in amygdala; SSRI treatment normalizes amygdala reactivity</li>
<li><strong>Genetics</strong>: 30-40% heritability; SLC6A4 5-HTTLPR S allele, RGS2, OXTR implicated</li>
</ul>

<h3>Psychotherapy</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>CBT with exposure</strong>: First-line; includes cognitive restructuring + graduated social exposure</li>
<li><strong>Combination</strong>: SSRI + CBT may be superior, especially for generalized subtype</li>
</ul>
<h3>Other</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>SNRIs</strong>: Venlafaxine ER (FDA-approved)</li>
<li><strong>Beta-blockers</strong>: Propranolol for performance-only subtype (off-label, acute use)</li>
</ul>
