<h1>Multiple Sclerosis</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Multiple sclerosis (MS) is a chronic autoimmune disease where autoreactive T cells and B cells attack the myelin sheath of CNS neurons, causing inflammatory demyelination and axonal damage. Lesions (plaques) can occur anywhere in the CNS but preferentially affect the optic nerves, periventricular white matter, brainstem, cerebellum, and spinal cord. Most patients (85%) present with relapsing-remitting MS (RRMS) — discrete episodes of neurological dysfunction followed by partial or complete recovery. Without treatment, most convert to secondary progressive MS (SPMS) within 15-20 years. Vitamin D deficiency and EBV infection are the strongest modifiable risk factors.</p>
<h2>Symptoms</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Visual</strong>: Optic neuritis (painful vision loss, often first symptom), diplopia</li>
<li><strong>Sensory</strong>: Numbness, tingling, Lhermitte's sign (electric shock down spine on neck flexion)</li>
<li><strong>Motor</strong>: Limb weakness, spasticity, gait disturbance</li>
<li><strong>Cerebellar</strong>: Ataxia, intention tremor, dysarthria</li>
<li><strong>Autonomic</strong>: Bladder dysfunction, sexual dysfunction</li>
<li><strong>Cognitive</strong>: Memory impairment, processing speed reduction (in 40-65%)</li>
<li><strong>Fatigue</strong>: Most common and disabling symptom (80% of patients)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Risk Factors</h2>
<ul>
<li>Female sex (3:1 ratio)</li>
<li>EBV infection (32x increased risk if EBV seropositive — strongest environmental factor)</li>
<li>Vitamin D deficiency / low sun exposure (latitude gradient)</li>
<li>Smoking (1.5x risk, worsens progression)</li>
<li>Family history (HLA-DRB1*15:01 allele)</li>
<li>Obesity in adolescence</li>
</ul>
<h2>Treatment Approaches</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Disease-modifying therapies (DMTs)</strong>: Start early, treat aggressively. Platform therapies: interferon-beta, glatiramer acetate. High-efficacy: natalizumab, ocrelizumab (anti-CD20), fingolimod, cladribine. Escalation vs. early high-efficacy strategy debated</li>
<li><strong>Relapse management</strong>: High-dose IV methylprednisolone (1g x 3-5 days), plasma exchange for steroid-refractory relapses</li>
<li><strong>Symptom management</strong>: Baclofen/tizanidine (spasticity), amantadine (fatigue), gabapentin (neuropathic pain), intermittent catheterization (bladder)</li>
<li><strong>Lifestyle</strong>: Exercise (improves fatigue, mood, and possibly neuroprotection), vitamin D optimization (target 40-60 ng/mL), smoking cessation, Mediterranean diet</li>
<li><strong>Supplements</strong>: Vitamin D (2000-5000 IU/day — strongest evidence, multiple RCTs), omega-3, biotin (high-dose for progressive MS — controversial), alpha-lipoic acid</li>
</ul>
