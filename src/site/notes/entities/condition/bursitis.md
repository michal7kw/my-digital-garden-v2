<h1>Bursitis</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Bursitis is the inflammation of a bursa, a small fluid-filled sac that cushions bones, tendons, and muscles near joints. The most commonly affected sites are the subacromial bursa (shoulder), olecranon bursa (elbow), trochanteric bursa (hip), and prepatellar bursa (knee, &quot;housemaid's knee&quot;). Bursitis is classified as either aseptic (mechanical or inflammatory) or septic (infectious), a distinction with critical treatment implications.</p>
<p>Aseptic bursitis typically results from repetitive pressure or friction, overuse, or underlying inflammatory conditions such as rheumatoid arthritis or gout. Crystal deposition (urate or calcium pyrophosphate) can also trigger bursitis. Septic bursitis, most common at the olecranon and prepatellar bursae due to their superficial location, requires prompt antibiotic treatment and is considered a medical emergency if not addressed. Staphylococcus aureus is the most common causative organism.</p>
<p>Diagnosis relies on clinical examination, with aspiration of bursal fluid serving dual diagnostic (cell count, crystals, culture) and therapeutic (decompression) purposes. Treatment of aseptic bursitis includes rest, ice, NSAIDs, and corticosteroid injection for refractory cases. Septic bursitis requires aspiration and targeted antibiotic therapy, with surgical bursectomy reserved for recurrent or refractory cases. Prevention focuses on protective padding, ergonomic modifications, and treatment of underlying inflammatory conditions.</p>
