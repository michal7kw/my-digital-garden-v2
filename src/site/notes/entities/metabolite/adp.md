<h1>ADP</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Adenosine diphosphate (ADP) is a nucleotide composed of adenine, ribose, and two phosphate groups (molecular formula C10H15N5O10P2, molecular weight 427.20 Da). It is the immediate precursor and hydrolysis product of adenosine triphosphate (ATP), the universal energy currency of the cell. The ADP/ATP ratio is a primary indicator of cellular energy status and a key regulatory signal for metabolic pathways.</p>
<p>ADP is present in all living cells and participates in energy metabolism, signal transduction, and platelet aggregation. Intracellular ADP concentrations are tightly regulated, as they directly control the rate of mitochondrial oxidative phosphorylation through a mechanism known as respiratory control.</p>
<h2>Mechanism of Action</h2>
<p>ADP serves as the substrate for ATP synthase (Complex V) in the mitochondrial inner membrane. During oxidative phosphorylation, the proton-motive force generated by the electron transport chain drives protons through ATP synthase, which catalyzes the phosphorylation of ADP to ATP. The availability of ADP is the primary rate-limiting factor for mitochondrial respiration under normal conditions: when ATP consumption increases (e.g., during exercise), ADP rises, stimulating the electron transport chain to increase oxygen consumption and ATP production. When the ADP/ATP ratio rises critically, AMP-activated protein kinase (AMPK) is activated, triggering catabolic pathways (fatty acid oxidation, glucose uptake, autophagy) and inhibiting anabolic processes (lipogenesis, protein synthesis). In the creatine phosphate shuttle, creatine kinase transfers a phosphate group from phosphocreatine to ADP, rapidly regenerating ATP in tissues with high energy demands (skeletal muscle, brain, heart). Extracellular ADP also acts as a signaling molecule by binding P2Y1 and P2Y12 purinergic receptors on platelets, triggering platelet shape change, granule secretion, and aggregation -- a pathway targeted by antiplatelet drugs clopidogrel and ticagrelor.</p>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<p>ADP metabolism is central to understanding bioenergetic disorders, mitochondrial diseases, and metabolic syndrome. Impaired mitochondrial ADP phosphorylation underlies the pathophysiology of mitochondrial myopathies, MELAS, and other oxidative phosphorylation defects. The ADP/ATP ratio is used as a biomarker of mitochondrial function in research settings. In cardiovascular medicine, ADP-mediated platelet activation through P2Y12 receptors is a critical therapeutic target: P2Y12 inhibitors (clopidogrel, prasugrel, ticagrelor, cangrelor) are cornerstone antiplatelet agents for acute coronary syndromes and percutaneous coronary intervention. Creatine supplementation enhances the phosphocreatine/ADP buffering system, improving high-intensity exercise performance and showing neuroprotective potential. Dysregulated ADP/ATP ratios are observed in ischemia-reperfusion injury, sepsis, and cancer cell metabolism (Warburg effect).</p>
