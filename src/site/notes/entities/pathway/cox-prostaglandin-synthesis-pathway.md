<h1>COX-Prostaglandin Synthesis Pathway</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>The cyclooxygenase-prostaglandin synthesis pathway (arachidonic acid cascade) is a central mediator of inflammation, pain, and fever. When cell membranes are disrupted by injury or inflammatory signals, phospholipase A2 (PLA2) cleaves membrane phospholipids to release arachidonic acid. Cyclooxygenase enzymes then convert arachidonic acid to the unstable intermediate PGH2, which is further processed by tissue-specific synthases into biologically active prostanoids: prostaglandin E2 (PGE2), prostacyclin (PGI2), thromboxane A2 (TXA2), prostaglandin D2 (PGD2), and prostaglandin F2alpha (PGF2alpha).</p>
<p>Two COX isoforms have distinct physiological roles. COX-1 (encoded by PTGS1) is constitutively expressed in most tissues and produces prostaglandins essential for gastric mucosal protection, platelet aggregation (via TXA2), and renal blood flow. COX-2 (encoded by PTGS2) is the inducible isoform, upregulated by pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-1beta, TNF-alpha), LPS, growth factors, and mechanical stress through NF-kB and MAPK signaling. COX-2-derived PGE2 is the principal prostanoid mediating inflammatory pain (via sensitization of peripheral nociceptors through EP receptors), fever (via action on the hypothalamic thermoregulatory center), vasodilation, and edema.</p>
<p>This pathway is the primary pharmacological target of NSAIDs. Non-selective NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen) inhibit both COX-1 and COX-2, providing anti-inflammatory and analgesic effects but with gastric and renal side effects from COX-1 inhibition. COX-2 selective inhibitors (celecoxib) spare COX-1, reducing GI toxicity but carrying cardiovascular risk. Aspirin irreversibly acetylates COX, permanently inactivating platelet TXA2 production -- the basis of its antiplatelet effect. Glucocorticoids act upstream by inducing lipocortin, which inhibits PLA2 and thus arachidonic acid release. Omega-3 fatty acids compete with arachidonic acid as COX/LOX substrates, producing less inflammatory eicosanoids.</p>
