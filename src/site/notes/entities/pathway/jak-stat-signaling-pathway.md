<h1>JAK-STAT Signaling Pathway</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>The Janus kinase-signal transducer and activator of transcription (JAK-STAT) pathway is one of the principal intracellular signaling cascades used by cytokines and growth factors to regulate gene expression. When a cytokine binds its receptor, receptor-associated JAK kinases (JAK1, JAK2, JAK3, or TYK2) become activated through transphosphorylation. These activated JAKs then phosphorylate specific tyrosine residues on the receptor, creating docking sites for STAT proteins. Once recruited, STATs are phosphorylated by JAKs, dimerize, and translocate to the nucleus where they drive transcription of target genes involved in immunity, inflammation, cell proliferation, and differentiation.</p>
<p>The pathway is central to signaling by numerous cytokines critical in autoimmune disease: IL-6 signals through JAK1/JAK2/TYK2 and STAT3, driving acute phase responses and Th17 differentiation; IL-12 and IL-23 use JAK2/TYK2 to activate STAT4 and STAT3 respectively, promoting Th1 and Th17 responses; IFN-gamma signals through JAK1/JAK2 and STAT1 to activate macrophages. Endogenous negative regulation is provided by SOCS (suppressor of cytokine signaling) proteins, which form a classical negative feedback loop by binding to phosphorylated JAKs or receptors and targeting them for proteasomal degradation.</p>
<p>The clinical importance of this pathway is demonstrated by the success of JAK inhibitors (jakinibs) in treating rheumatoid arthritis, psoriatic arthritis, ulcerative colitis, and atopic dermatitis. Tofacitinib (JAK1/3), baricitinib (JAK1/2), and upadacitinib (JAK1-selective) represent a new class of targeted synthetic DMARDs that offer oral administration as an alternative to biologic therapies. Safety monitoring for these agents includes cardiovascular risk, venous thromboembolism, and malignancy, reflecting the broad physiological roles of JAK-STAT signaling.</p>
