<h1>TNF Signaling Pathway</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>The tumor necrosis factor (TNF) signaling pathway is one of the most important pro-inflammatory cascades in innate and adaptive immunity. TNF-alpha, produced primarily by activated macrophages, binds two receptors: TNFR1 (ubiquitous, mediates most inflammatory and apoptotic signals) and TNFR2 (restricted to immune and endothelial cells, primarily pro-survival). TNFR1 signaling recruits TRADD, which serves as a platform for two divergent pathways: Complex I (TRADD/RIP1/TRAF2/cIAP1/2) activates NF-kB and MAPK to drive pro-inflammatory gene transcription, while Complex II (TRADD/FADD/Caspase-8) triggers apoptosis when NF-kB-dependent survival signals fail.</p>
<p>In rheumatoid arthritis, TNF-alpha is the dominant cytokine in the inflamed synovium, driving synovial fibroblast proliferation, angiogenesis, cartilage destruction via MMP induction, and bone erosion via RANKL upregulation. The clinical success of five approved TNF inhibitors (adalimumab, etanercept, infliximab, certolizumab, golimumab) -- the most commercially successful class of biologics in history -- validates TNF as the preeminent therapeutic target in inflammatory rheumatic diseases. TNF inhibitors are first-line biologic therapy for RA, psoriatic arthritis, ankylosing spondylitis, and Crohn's disease. Natural modulators include curcumin and omega-3 fatty acids, which suppress NF-kB-dependent TNF transcription.</p>
