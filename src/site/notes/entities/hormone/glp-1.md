<h1>GLP-1</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>GLP-1 (Glucagon-like peptide-1) is a 30- or 31-amino acid peptide hormone produced primarily by intestinal L-cells in the distal ileum and colon in response to nutrient ingestion. As a key incretin hormone, GLP-1 accounts for approximately 50-70% of the postprandial insulin secretion. Its actions are glucose-dependent, meaning it primarily stimulates insulin secretion when blood glucose is elevated, thereby providing glycemic control with minimal hypoglycemia risk. Native GLP-1 has an extremely short half-life (~1-2 minutes) due to rapid degradation by the enzyme dipeptidyl peptidase-4 (DPP-4), which has led to the development of synthetic GLP-1 receptor agonists with extended half-lives.</p>
<h2>Biological Functions</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Insulin secretion</strong>: Potentiates glucose-dependent insulin release from pancreatic beta cells via cAMP-PKA signaling</li>
<li><strong>Glucagon suppression</strong>: Inhibits alpha-cell glucagon secretion during hyperglycemia, reducing hepatic glucose production</li>
<li><strong>Gastric emptying</strong>: Delays gastric emptying rate, contributing to postprandial glucose control and enhanced satiety</li>
<li><strong>Appetite regulation</strong>: Acts on hypothalamic and brainstem GLP-1 receptors to reduce food intake and promote weight loss</li>
<li><strong>Cardiovascular effects</strong>: Improves endothelial function, reduces blood pressure, and provides cardioprotection</li>
<li><strong>Beta-cell preservation</strong>: Promotes beta-cell proliferation and inhibits apoptosis</li>
<li><strong>Neuroprotection</strong>: Potential neuroprotective effects in Alzheimer's and Parkinson's disease</li>
</ul>
<h2>Synthesis and Secretion</h2>
<p>GLP-1 is derived from the preproglucagon gene (GCG), which also encodes glucagon and GLP-2. In intestinal L-cells, post-translational processing yields active GLP-1 (7-36) amide and GLP-1 (7-37). Secretion is stimulated by:</p>
<ul>
<li>Direct nutrient contact with L-cells (glucose, fatty acids, amino acids)</li>
<li>Neural signals from the vagus nerve</li>
<li>Endocrine factors (GIP, somatostatin)</li>
<li>Postprandial nutrient sensing mechanisms</li>
</ul>
<h2>Signaling Pathway</h2>
<p>GLP-1 binds to the GLP-1 receptor (GLP1R), a class B G-protein coupled receptor (GPCR) expressed on:</p>
<ul>
<li>Pancreatic beta cells (primary target)</li>
<li>Pancreatic alpha cells</li>
<li>Gastric mucosa</li>
<li>Hypothalamus (arcuate nucleus, nucleus tractus solitarius)</li>
<li>Cardiovascular system (heart, blood vessels)</li>
<li>Kidneys</li>
<li>Peripheral nervous system</li>
</ul>
<p>Receptor activation triggers:</p>
<ul>
<li>Gs protein activation → increased cAMP → PKA activation</li>
<li>Epac (exchange protein directly activated by cAMP) signaling</li>
<li>PI3K-Akt pathway activation</li>
<li>MAPK/ERK pathway activation</li>
<li>Beta-arrestin recruitment</li>
</ul>
<h2>Pharmacological Targeting</h2>
<h3>Native GLP-1 Limitations</h3>
<ul>
<li>Extremely short half-life (1-2 minutes)</li>
<li>Rapid degradation by DPP-4 (cleaves Ala8-Glu9 bond)</li>
<li>Inadequate for therapeutic use without modification</li>
</ul>
<h3>GLP-1 Receptor Agonists</h3>
<p><strong>Long-acting (once weekly)</strong>:</p>
<ul>
<li>Semaglutide (Ozempic, Wegovy, Rybelsus) - half-life ~7 days</li>
<li>Dulaglutide (Trulicity) - half-life ~5 days</li>
<li>Exenatide ER (Bydureon) - half-life ~2 days</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Short-acting (twice daily)</strong>:</p>
<ul>
<li>Exenatide (Byetta) - half-life ~2.4 hours</li>
<li>Liraglutide (Victoza, Saxenda) - half-life ~13 hours (once daily)</li>
</ul>
<h3>DPP-4 Inhibitors (Incretin Enhancers)</h3>
<ul>
<li>Sitagliptin, Linagliptin, Saxagliptin - prolong endogenous GLP-1 action</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Applications</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Type 2 diabetes</strong>: First-line after metformin for patients with cardiovascular disease or obesity</li>
<li><strong>Obesity</strong>: Approved for weight management (Wegovy, Saxenda)</li>
<li><strong>Cardiovascular risk reduction</strong>: Proven benefit in secondary prevention (SELECT trial)</li>
<li><strong>NASH/NAFLD</strong>: Emerging evidence for hepatic benefit</li>
<li><strong>Neurodegenerative disease</strong>: Ongoing trials for Alzheimer's and Parkinson's</li>
</ul>
<h2>Therapeutic Benefits</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Glycemic control</strong>: HbA1c reduction 1.0-1.8% (GLP-1 agonists)</li>
<li><strong>Weight loss</strong>: 5-15% body weight reduction depending on dose and agent</li>
<li><strong>Cardiovascular protection</strong>: 14-26% reduction in MACE</li>
<li><strong>Blood pressure</strong>: 2-5 mmHg systolic reduction</li>
<li><strong>Kidney protection</strong>: Slows CKD progression (FLOW trial)</li>
<li><strong>Beta-cell preservation</strong>: Potential disease-modifying effect</li>
</ul>
<h2>Adverse Effects</h2>
<p><strong>GLP-1 Agonist Class Effects</strong>:</p>
<ul>
<li>Gastrointestinal: Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, constipation (30-50%)</li>
<li>Pancreatitis: Rare (&lt;0.2%)</li>
<li>Gallbladder disease: Increased risk</li>
<li>Thyroid C-cell tumors: Rodent signal (unknown human relevance)</li>
<li>Diabetic retinopathy: Rare worsening with rapid glucose improvement</li>
</ul>
