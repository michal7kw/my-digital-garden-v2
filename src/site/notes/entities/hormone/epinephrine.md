<h1>Epinephrine</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Epinephrine (adrenaline) is the final product of the catecholamine biosynthetic cascade: L-Tyrosine → L-DOPA → Dopamine → Norepinephrine → Epinephrine. It is synthesized almost exclusively in the adrenal medulla by PNMT (phenylethanolamine N-methyltransferase), which methylates norepinephrine using SAMe as the methyl donor. PNMT expression is induced by cortisol from the adrenal cortex, creating a functional link between the HPA axis and catecholamine production.</p>
<p>As the primary fight-or-flight hormone, epinephrine orchestrates the body's rapid response to acute stress by simultaneously increasing cardiac output, redirecting blood flow to skeletal muscles, mobilizing glucose from liver glycogen, and dilating airways. It acts on all four major adrenergic receptor subtypes (alpha-1, alpha-2, beta-1, beta-2) with varying affinities, producing tissue-specific responses. Clinically, epinephrine is the first-line treatment for anaphylaxis and cardiac arrest, and plays a central role in understanding stress physiology and the relationship between L-Tyrosine supplementation and stress resilience.</p>
<p><strong>Evidence Level:</strong> 5/5</p>
<h2>Key Functions</h2>
<h3>Biosynthesis</h3>
<ul>
<li>Synthesized from norepinephrine by PNMT in adrenal medulla chromaffin cells</li>
<li>PNMT transfers a methyl group from S-adenosylmethionine (SAMe) to the amine group of norepinephrine</li>
<li>PNMT expression is glucocorticoid-dependent — cortisol from the adrenal cortex induces PNMT transcription</li>
<li>This is why the adrenal medulla is anatomically surrounded by the cortex — ensuring high local cortisol concentrations</li>
</ul>
<h3>Receptor Pharmacology</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Alpha-1</strong>: Vasoconstriction (skin, splanchnic), mydriasis, sphincter contraction — &quot;fight&quot; response</li>
<li><strong>Alpha-2</strong>: Presynaptic feedback inhibition of NE release, platelet aggregation, insulin suppression</li>
<li><strong>Beta-1</strong>: Increased heart rate (chronotropic), contractility (inotropic), and conduction velocity (dromotropic)</li>
<li><strong>Beta-2</strong>: Bronchodilation, vasodilation in skeletal muscle, hepatic glycogenolysis, lipolysis</li>
</ul>
<h3>Metabolic Effects</h3>
<ul>
<li>Hepatic glycogenolysis: rapid glucose mobilization via beta-2/alpha-1 receptor activation of glycogen phosphorylase</li>
<li>Lipolysis: fatty acid release from adipose tissue via beta-3 receptor activation</li>
<li>Insulin suppression: alpha-2 receptor-mediated inhibition of pancreatic beta-cell insulin secretion</li>
<li>Net effect: hyperglycemia and free fatty acid elevation to fuel fight-or-flight response</li>
</ul>
<h3>Metabolism</h3>
<ul>
<li>Extremely short plasma half-life: 2-3 minutes</li>
<li>Metabolized by COMT (catechol-O-methyltransferase) → metanephrine</li>
<li>Metabolized by MAO (monoamine oxidase) → ultimately to vanillylmandelic acid (VMA)</li>
<li>Urinary metanephrines and VMA used as biomarkers for pheochromocytoma</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Relevance</h2>
<h3>Emergency Medicine</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Anaphylaxis</strong>: First-line treatment (0.3-0.5 mg IM via auto-injector); reverses bronchospasm, hypotension, and angioedema</li>
<li><strong>Cardiac arrest</strong>: Used in ACLS protocols (1 mg IV every 3-5 minutes) for its beta-1 effects on cardiac contractility</li>
<li><strong>Severe asthma</strong>: Subcutaneous epinephrine for acute bronchospasm</li>
</ul>
<h3>Stress Physiology</h3>
<ul>
<li>Completes the catecholamine cascade that begins with L-Tyrosine</li>
<li>L-Tyrosine supplementation supports the entire cascade under stress conditions</li>
<li>Creatine supports brain energy needed for processing stress-induced catecholamine surges</li>
</ul>
