<h1>Niacin</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Niacin (Vitamin B3) encompasses two primary forms: nicotinic acid and nicotinamide (niacinamide). Both serve as precursors to NAD+ (nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide), one of the most critical coenzymes in human biology. NAD+ participates in over 500 enzymatic reactions, with central roles in glycolysis, TCA cycle, oxidative phosphorylation, sirtuin-mediated longevity pathways, and PARP-mediated DNA repair.</p>
<p>Unlike NMN and NR (which enter NAD+ synthesis at different points), niacin is the oldest and most established NAD+ precursor with decades of clinical data. Nicotinic acid uniquely activates the GPR109A receptor, producing cardiovascular benefits (raising HDL, lowering Lp(a)) but also causing the characteristic flushing side effect. Nicotinamide avoids flushing but may inhibit sirtuins at high doses.</p>
<p>NAD+ levels decline with age (approximately 50% reduction by age 60), making niacin and other NAD+ precursors increasingly relevant for maintaining cellular energy metabolism and repair capacity.</p>
<p><strong>Evidence Level:</strong> 4/5</p>
<h2>Mechanism of Action</h2>
<h3>NAD+ Synthesis (Preiss-Handler Pathway)</h3>
<ul>
<li>Nicotinic acid → NaMN (via nicotinic acid phosphoribosyltransferase)</li>
<li>NaMN → NaAD (via NMNAT)</li>
<li>NaAD → NAD+ (via NAD+ synthetase)</li>
<li>This pathway effectively replenishes the cellular NAD+ pool</li>
</ul>
<h3>Energy Metabolism via NAD+</h3>
<ul>
<li>NAD+ is the primary electron acceptor in glycolysis (produces NADH)</li>
<li>NAD+ is required at 3 steps of the TCA cycle</li>
<li>NADH donates electrons to Complex I of the ETC</li>
<li>Without adequate NAD+, cellular energy production collapses</li>
</ul>
<h3>Sirtuin Activation</h3>
<ul>
<li>NAD+ is the obligate substrate for all 7 sirtuins (SIRT1-7)</li>
<li>Sirtuins are NAD+-dependent deacylases that regulate metabolism, stress responses, and aging</li>
<li>NAD+ availability is rate-limiting for sirtuin activity</li>
</ul>
<h3>Cardiovascular Effects (Nicotinic Acid Only)</h3>
<ul>
<li>Raises HDL by 15-35% (most effective known agent)</li>
<li>Lowers Lp(a) by 20-30%</li>
<li>Activates GPR109A receptor (causes flushing but provides anti-inflammatory effects)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Dosage</h2>
<h3>Standard Supplementation</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>RDA</strong>: 14-16 mg/day</li>
<li><strong>NAD+ support (nicotinamide)</strong>: 500-1500 mg/day</li>
<li><strong>Cardiovascular (nicotinic acid ER)</strong>: 500-2000 mg/day (monitor liver enzymes)</li>
<li><strong>Form</strong>: Extended-release nicotinic acid minimizes flushing; nicotinamide avoids flushing entirely</li>
<li><strong>Timing</strong>: Evening with food (ER nicotinic acid); any time (nicotinamide)</li>
</ul>
<h3>Important Notes</h3>
<ul>
<li>Nicotinic acid and nicotinamide have different clinical profiles</li>
<li>Monitor liver function at doses &gt;1000 mg/day</li>
<li>Do not use immediate-release nicotinic acid at high doses (hepatotoxicity risk)</li>
</ul>
