<h1>L-Tryptophan</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>L-Tryptophan is one of nine essential amino acids that the body cannot synthesize, making dietary intake obligatory. It occupies a unique position in metabolism as the sole precursor to serotonin (via the 5-HTP pathway) and subsequently melatonin, directly linking dietary protein quality to mood regulation and sleep architecture. Tryptophan depletion studies in humans -- where plasma tryptophan is acutely lowered -- demonstrate rapid onset of depressive symptoms in vulnerable individuals, confirming its critical role in serotonin-dependent brain function.</p>
<p>Compared to 5-HTP, L-tryptophan produces a more gradual and physiological increase in serotonin because it must pass through the rate-limiting tryptophan hydroxylase (TPH) step. This confers a lower risk of serotonin syndrome and makes it more suitable for long-term use. Importantly, approximately 95% of dietary tryptophan is metabolized through the kynurenine pathway (producing NAD+, kynurenic acid, and quinolinic acid) rather than the serotonin pathway, meaning that supplementation can meaningfully shift the balance toward serotonin production. Brain uptake is enhanced by consuming tryptophan with carbohydrates, as the resulting insulin release clears competing large neutral amino acids from the bloodstream.</p>
<h2>Mechanism of Action</h2>
<p>L-Tryptophan is an essential amino acid that enters the serotonin synthesis pathway via tryptophan hydroxylase (TPH), the rate-limiting enzyme, which converts it to 5-HTP (requiring iron and tetrahydrobiopterin as cofactors). 5-HTP is then converted to serotonin by aromatic amino acid decarboxylase (AADC, requiring vitamin B6). Serotonin in the pineal gland is further converted to melatonin by SNAT and HIOMT enzymes. Tryptophan competes with other large neutral amino acids (LNAAs: tyrosine, phenylalanine, BCAAs) for transport across the blood-brain barrier via the LAT1 transporter — carbohydrate consumption triggers insulin release, which clears competing amino acids from the blood and enhances tryptophan brain uptake. Unlike 5-HTP, tryptophan provides a more gradual increase in serotonin because it must pass through the rate-limiting TPH step. Approximately 95% of dietary tryptophan is metabolized via the kynurenine pathway, not the serotonin pathway, highlighting why supplementation can meaningfully shift the balance.</p>
<h2>Dosage</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Mood support</strong>: 500-1000 mg, 1-2 times daily</li>
<li><strong>Sleep</strong>: 500-2000 mg, taken 30-60 minutes before bed</li>
<li><strong>Stress resilience</strong>: 500-1000 mg daily</li>
<li><strong>Timing</strong>: Take with carbohydrates for enhanced brain uptake; away from high-protein meals</li>
<li><strong>Evening dosing</strong>: Preferred for sleep support</li>
<li><strong>Cofactors</strong>: Ensure adequate iron, vitamin B6, and folate for optimal serotonin synthesis</li>
<li><strong>Caution</strong>: Do not combine with SSRIs, SNRIs, or MAOIs (serotonin syndrome risk)</li>
</ul>
