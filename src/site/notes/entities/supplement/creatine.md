<h1>Creatine</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Creatine monohydrate is the most researched and effective sports supplement in history, with over 500 peer-reviewed studies demonstrating safety and efficacy. Beyond athletic performance, creatine is an essential molecule for cellular energy — the phosphocreatine system rapidly regenerates ATP in tissues with high energy demand (skeletal muscle, brain, heart). Emerging evidence supports cognitive benefits (especially under sleep deprivation or stress), neuroprotection, and potential antidepressant effects. The brain uses 20% of the body's ATP, making it responsive to creatine supplementation.</p>
<h2>Mechanism of Action</h2>
<p>Creatine is phosphorylated to phosphocreatine (PCr) by creatine kinase. PCr donates its phosphate group to ADP, rapidly regenerating ATP during high-intensity activity (the phosphagen system). This 5-10 second energy buffer is rate-limiting for explosive movements. In the brain, PCr provides energy for neurotransmission, ion homeostasis, and membrane potential maintenance. Creatine also acts as an intracellular osmolyte (draws water into muscle cells), modulates cellular signaling (AMPK, mTOR), and has direct antioxidant properties.</p>
<h2>Dosage</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Loading phase</strong> (optional): 20g/day (4x5g) for 5-7 days — saturates stores faster</li>
<li><strong>Maintenance</strong>: 3-5g/day (consensus dose for all populations)</li>
<li><strong>No cycling needed</strong>: Continuous supplementation is safe; no evidence of receptor downregulation</li>
<li><strong>Form</strong>: Creatine monohydrate (cheapest, most studied, most effective — no benefit to &quot;fancy&quot; forms like HCl, buffered, etc.)</li>
<li><strong>Timing</strong>: Anytime; post-workout with carbs may marginally improve uptake but timing is not critical</li>
<li><strong>Hydration</strong>: Increase water intake (creatine draws water into muscle cells)</li>
</ul>
