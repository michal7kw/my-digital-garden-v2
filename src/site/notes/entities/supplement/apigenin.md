<h1>Apigenin</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Apigenin is a naturally occurring flavonoid abundant in chamomile tea, parsley, celery, and many herbs. It has gained attention in the longevity and sleep-optimization communities for its dual role as a mild anxiolytic/sedative (via GABA-A receptor modulation) and a CD38 inhibitor that preserves intracellular NAD+ levels. Unlike benzodiazepines, apigenin binds the GABA-A receptor at a subunit-selective site, producing calming effects without significant sedation, tolerance, or dependence.</p>
<p>Research highlights apigenin's broad pharmacological profile: anti-inflammatory activity (COX-2 and NF-kB inhibition), antioxidant capacity, neuroprotective effects in animal models of Alzheimer's and Parkinson's disease, and emerging evidence for anti-cancer properties through cell cycle arrest and apoptosis induction. Its ability to inhibit CD38 -- a major NAD+-consuming enzyme that increases with age -- positions apigenin as a complementary strategy alongside NMN and NR for maintaining NAD+ homeostasis during aging. Dietary intake is typically under 5 mg/day, far below supplemental doses.</p>
<p><strong>Evidence Level:</strong> 3/5</p>
<h2>Mechanism of Action</h2>
<p>Apigenin exerts its effects through multiple molecular targets. It binds the benzodiazepine site on GABA-A receptors (particularly alpha-2 containing subtypes), producing anxiolytic and mildly sedative effects without the amnestic or muscle-relaxant properties of classical benzodiazepines. As a CD38 inhibitor, apigenin reduces NAD+ degradation, supporting sirtuins (SIRT1, SIRT3) and PARP activity for DNA repair and mitochondrial function. Apigenin inhibits COX-2 and NF-kB transcriptional activity, reducing prostaglandin E2 and pro-inflammatory cytokine production (IL-6, TNF-alpha). It scavenges reactive oxygen species directly and upregulates endogenous antioxidant enzymes (SOD, catalase) through Nrf2 pathway activation. In cancer models, apigenin induces G2/M cell cycle arrest via p21 upregulation and promotes intrinsic apoptosis through caspase-3 activation.</p>
<h2>Dosage</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Sleep / relaxation</strong>: 50-100 mg, 30-60 minutes before bed</li>
<li><strong>NAD+ support / longevity</strong>: 250-500 mg daily</li>
<li><strong>Anti-inflammatory</strong>: 100-300 mg daily</li>
<li><strong>Form</strong>: Capsules or chamomile extract standardized to apigenin content</li>
<li><strong>Timing</strong>: Evening for sleep benefits; any time for general health</li>
<li><strong>Note</strong>: Dietary intake from chamomile tea (3-5 mg per cup) is far below supplemental doses</li>
</ul>
