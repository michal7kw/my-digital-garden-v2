<h1>Iron</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Iron is an essential trace mineral required by virtually every cell in the body. As the central atom in hemoglobin (oxygen transport) and myoglobin (muscle oxygen storage), iron is fundamental to aerobic energy production. It is also a critical cofactor for mitochondrial electron transport chain complexes (I, II, III, IV), making it indispensable for ATP generation. Beyond oxygen and energy, iron is required for DNA synthesis (ribonucleotide reductase), neurotransmitter production, and myelination.</p>
<p>Iron deficiency is the most common nutritional deficiency worldwide, affecting an estimated 2 billion people. Crucially, iron deficiency impairs neurotransmitter synthesis (tyrosine hydroxylase for dopamine, tryptophan hydroxylase for serotonin) well before hemoglobin levels drop to anemic ranges, causing fatigue, poor concentration, restless legs, anxiety, and depressed mood at subclinical levels. Supplementation should be guided by ferritin levels, as iron overload (hemochromatosis) is equally dangerous, generating oxidative damage through Fenton chemistry. Iron bisglycinate is the best-tolerated form, and co-administration with vitamin C significantly enhances absorption.</p>
<h2>Mechanism of Action</h2>
<p>Iron is a cofactor for critical enzymes throughout the body. As the central atom in hemoglobin and myoglobin, it enables oxygen transport and storage. In the mitochondrial electron transport chain, iron-sulfur clusters (Complex I, II, III) and cytochrome c (Complex IV) are essential for ATP production. For neurotransmitter synthesis, iron (Fe2+) is a required cofactor for tyrosine hydroxylase (the rate-limiting enzyme converting L-tyrosine to L-DOPA for dopamine synthesis) and tryptophan hydroxylase (converting L-tryptophan to 5-HTP for serotonin synthesis). Iron also supports tetrahydrobiopterin (BH4) recycling, which is needed for both enzymes. Iron is essential for DNA synthesis (ribonucleotide reductase), myelination of neurons, and immune cell proliferation. Hepcidin, the master iron-regulatory hormone, controls intestinal absorption and macrophage release of stored iron.</p>
<h2>Dosage</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>RDA</strong>: 8 mg (men), 18 mg (premenopausal women), 27 mg (pregnancy)</li>
<li><strong>Supplementation (deficiency)</strong>: 18-45 mg elemental iron daily</li>
<li><strong>Therapeutic (anemia)</strong>: 100-200 mg elemental iron daily (divided doses)</li>
<li><strong>Form</strong>: Iron bisglycinate (best tolerated), heme iron (highest absorption), ferrous sulfate (most studied)</li>
<li><strong>Enhancement</strong>: Take with vitamin C (200 mg) to significantly boost absorption</li>
<li><strong>Avoid</strong>: Do not take with calcium, coffee, tea, or high-phytate foods (separate by 2 hours)</li>
<li><strong>Monitoring</strong>: Check ferritin levels; supplement only when deficient to avoid iron overload</li>
</ul>
