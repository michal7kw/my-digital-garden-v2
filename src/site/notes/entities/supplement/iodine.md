<h1>Iodine</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Iodine is an essential trace element that the body cannot synthesize, making dietary or supplemental intake mandatory. It is the key substrate for thyroid hormone production: the thyroid gland actively concentrates iodine to 20-40 times its plasma level using the sodium-iodide symporter (NIS), then incorporates it into T4 (thyroxine) and T3 (triiodothyronine) -- hormones that regulate basal metabolic rate, thermogenesis, growth, and neurodevelopment.</p>
<p>Iodine deficiency remains the leading preventable cause of intellectual disability worldwide and is surprisingly common even in developed countries, particularly among pregnant women, vegans, and those avoiding iodized salt. Deficiency manifests as goiter, hypothyroidism, fatigue, weight gain, and impaired cognition. However, excessive iodine can paradoxically suppress thyroid function (Wolff-Chaikoff effect) or trigger thyroiditis in susceptible individuals, especially those with pre-existing Hashimoto's disease. Optimal thyroid health requires balancing iodine with selenium, as selenoprotein deiodinase enzymes are needed to convert T4 to the active T3 form.</p>
<h2>Mechanism of Action</h2>
<p>Iodine is actively transported into thyroid follicular cells by the sodium-iodide symporter (NIS). Inside the cell, thyroid peroxidase (TPO) oxidizes iodide and catalyzes iodination of tyrosine residues on thyroglobulin, forming monoiodotyrosine (MIT) and diiodotyrosine (DIT). Coupling of MIT and DIT produces T3 (triiodothyronine) and T4 (thyroxine). T4 is the primary secreted form and is converted to the active T3 by selenoprotein deiodinase enzymes (D1, D2) in peripheral tissues. Thyroid hormones regulate basal metabolic rate, thermogenesis, protein synthesis, and neurodevelopment by binding nuclear thyroid hormone receptors (TR-alpha, TR-beta) that modulate gene transcription. Selenium is an essential cofactor for deiodinase enzymes, making iodine-selenium synergy critical for optimal thyroid function.</p>
<h2>Dosage</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>RDA</strong>: 150 mcg daily (adults); 220-290 mcg during pregnancy/lactation</li>
<li><strong>General supplementation</strong>: 150-300 mcg daily</li>
<li><strong>Thyroid support</strong>: 150-500 mcg daily (monitor thyroid function)</li>
<li><strong>Form</strong>: Potassium iodide (most common), nascent iodine, or kelp (natural source)</li>
<li><strong>Timing</strong>: With meals; consistent daily timing</li>
<li><strong>Caution</strong>: Excess iodine (&gt;1100 mcg/day) can cause thyroid dysfunction; balance with selenium (200 mcg)</li>
</ul>
