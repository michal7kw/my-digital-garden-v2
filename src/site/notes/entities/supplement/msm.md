<h1>MSM</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Methylsulfonylmethane (MSM) is a naturally occurring organosulfur compound found in small amounts in fruits, vegetables, grains, and beverages. It is the oxidized form of dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO) and serves as a bioavailable source of sulfur, an essential element for the synthesis of connective tissue components including glycosaminoglycans (GAGs), collagen, and keratin.</p>
<p>MSM's therapeutic effects extend beyond simple sulfur donation. It exerts anti-inflammatory activity through inhibition of NF-kB nuclear translocation, reducing the expression of downstream inflammatory mediators including TNF-alpha, IL-1beta, and IL-6. MSM also supports the body's antioxidant defenses by serving as a sulfur donor for glutathione synthesis, the principal intracellular antioxidant.</p>
<p>Clinical trials in knee osteoarthritis have demonstrated modest but statistically significant improvements in pain and physical function, particularly at doses of 3000-6000mg/day over 12 weeks. MSM is frequently combined with glucosamine sulfate, and combination studies suggest additive benefits. The compound has an excellent safety profile with no serious adverse effects reported at standard doses, and its low molecular weight ensures good oral bioavailability.</p>
<h2>Dosage Guidelines</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Standard dose</strong>: 1000-3000mg/day in 2-3 divided doses</li>
<li><strong>Clinical trial dose</strong>: Up to 6000mg/day for osteoarthritis</li>
<li><strong>Combination</strong>: Often paired with 1500mg/day glucosamine sulfate for synergistic joint benefit</li>
<li><strong>Timing</strong>: Can be taken with or without food; divide doses to maintain steady levels</li>
<li><strong>Onset</strong>: Gradual benefit over 2-4 weeks; studies typically run 12 weeks for full assessment</li>
</ul>
