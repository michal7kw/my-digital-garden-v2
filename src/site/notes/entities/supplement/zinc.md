<h1>Zinc</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Zinc is an essential trace mineral required for the catalytic activity of approximately 300 enzymes and the structural integrity of thousands of proteins (zinc finger domains). It is the second most abundant trace mineral in the body, with the highest concentrations in the prostate, retina, bone, muscle, and brain. Zinc cannot be stored in the body, so daily intake is required. Deficiency is remarkably common — estimated at 17% of the global population — and manifests as impaired immunity, slow wound healing, hair loss, hypogonadism, skin lesions, and impaired taste/smell.</p>
<h2>Forms and Bioavailability</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Zinc Picolinate</strong> — Highest absorption (picolinic acid chelate) — best for deficiency correction</li>
<li><strong>Zinc Glycinate</strong> — Excellent absorption, gentle on stomach</li>
<li><strong>Zinc Citrate</strong> — Good absorption, affordable</li>
<li><strong>Zinc Carnosine</strong> — Specific affinity for gastric mucosa (gut healing)</li>
<li><strong>Zinc Gluconate</strong> — Common in lozenges for cold symptom reduction</li>
<li><strong>Zinc Oxide</strong> — Lowest absorption — avoid for supplementation</li>
</ul>
<h2>Key Functions</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Immune function</strong>: Required for thymulin (T-cell maturation), NK cell activity, and neutrophil function</li>
<li><strong>Testosterone synthesis</strong>: Cofactor for 5-alpha reductase and Leydig cell function</li>
<li><strong>Wound healing</strong>: Required for collagen synthesis, cell division, and immune response at wound sites</li>
<li><strong>Antioxidant defense</strong>: Component of Cu/Zn superoxide dismutase (SOD1)</li>
<li><strong>Thyroid function</strong>: Required for T4 → T3 conversion (deiodinase enzymes)</li>
<li><strong>DNA synthesis and repair</strong>: Required for DNA and RNA polymerase function</li>
</ul>
<h2>Dosage</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>RDA</strong>: 11 mg (men), 8 mg (women)</li>
<li><strong>Optimal supplementation</strong>: 15-30 mg/day</li>
<li><strong>Therapeutic</strong>: 30-50 mg/day (short-term, with copper)</li>
<li><strong>Upper limit</strong>: 40 mg/day without copper co-supplementation</li>
<li><strong>Copper ratio</strong>: 2 mg copper per 30 mg zinc for long-term use</li>
<li><strong>Timing</strong>: Take with food; separate from iron, calcium, and phytate-rich foods by 2 hours</li>
</ul>
