<h1>L-Tyrosine</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>L-Tyrosine is a conditionally essential amino acid that serves as the primary precursor for catecholamine neurotransmitters (dopamine, norepinephrine, epinephrine) and thyroid hormones (T3, T4). While the body can synthesize tyrosine from phenylalanine, supplementation becomes valuable when catecholamine demand exceeds the body's synthetic capacity — particularly under acute stress, sleep deprivation, cold exposure, or high cognitive load.</p>
<p>The key insight about L-tyrosine is that it does not significantly raise catecholamine levels above baseline in rested, non-stressed individuals. The rate-limiting enzyme (tyrosine hydroxylase) is tightly regulated by end-product inhibition. However, under conditions of catecholamine depletion — when neurons are firing rapidly and depleting their neurotransmitter stores — supplemental tyrosine replenishes the precursor pool and maintains cognitive performance that would otherwise degrade. This makes it particularly effective for maintaining performance under stress rather than enhancing it above baseline.</p>
<p>Military research has extensively studied tyrosine for maintaining soldier performance during sustained operations, cold stress, and sleep deprivation, with consistently positive results for attention, working memory, and executive function under these demanding conditions.</p>
<p><strong>Evidence Level:</strong> 3/5</p>
<h2>Mechanism of Action</h2>
<h3>Catecholamine Synthesis Cascade</h3>
<p>L-Tyrosine participates in the following biosynthetic pathway:</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>L-Tyrosine → L-DOPA</strong>: Tyrosine hydroxylase (TH) — rate-limiting step; requires iron (Fe2+) and tetrahydrobiopterin (BH4) as cofactors</li>
<li><strong>L-DOPA → Dopamine</strong>: Aromatic L-amino acid decarboxylase (AADC) — requires pyridoxal phosphate (vitamin B6)</li>
<li><strong>Dopamine → Norepinephrine</strong>: Dopamine beta-hydroxylase (DBH) — requires ascorbic acid (vitamin C) and copper</li>
<li><strong>Norepinephrine → Epinephrine</strong>: Phenylethanolamine N-methyltransferase (PNMT) — requires S-adenosylmethionine (SAMe)</li>
</ol>
<h3>Thyroid Hormone Synthesis</h3>
<ul>
<li>Tyrosine residues on thyroglobulin are iodinated by thyroid peroxidase</li>
<li>Mono- and diiodotyrosine combine to form T3 (triiodothyronine) and T4 (thyroxine)</li>
<li>Adequate tyrosine and iodine are both required for normal thyroid hormone production</li>
</ul>
<h3>Stress-Depletion Model</h3>
<ul>
<li>During acute stress, the locus coeruleus (norepinephrine) and ventral tegmental area (dopamine) fire at high rates</li>
<li>Rapid neurotransmitter release depletes vesicular stores faster than synthesis can replenish them</li>
<li>Supplemental tyrosine provides substrate to maintain synthesis rate under high-demand conditions</li>
<li>This explains why tyrosine is effective under stress but shows minimal effects at rest</li>
</ul>
<h3>Transport Competition</h3>
<ul>
<li>L-Tyrosine competes with other large neutral amino acids (LNAAs: tryptophan, phenylalanine, BCAAs) for transport across the blood-brain barrier via the LAT1 transporter</li>
<li>Taking tyrosine on an empty stomach or with carbohydrates (which trigger insulin, clearing competing amino acids from blood) improves brain uptake</li>
<li>High-protein meals may reduce efficacy due to LNAA competition</li>
</ul>
<h2>Dosage</h2>
<h3>General Cognitive Support</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Standard dose</strong>: 500-2000 mg/day</li>
<li><strong>Form</strong>: Free-form L-Tyrosine preferred over NALT (N-Acetyl L-Tyrosine), which has lower bioavailability due to poor deacetylation in vivo</li>
</ul>
<h3>Acute Stress / Performance Pre-Load</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Research dose</strong>: 100-150 mg/kg body weight, taken 60 minutes before stressor</li>
<li><strong>Example</strong>: 7-10 g for a 70 kg person (military study protocols)</li>
<li>This higher acute dose is used in research settings but may cause GI discomfort</li>
</ul>
<h3>Timing and Administration</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Empty stomach</strong>: Best absorption and brain uptake (reduces LNAA competition)</li>
<li><strong>With carbohydrates</strong>: Insulin clears competing amino acids, enhancing brain entry</li>
<li><strong>Morning/early afternoon</strong>: Preferred timing; dopamine and norepinephrine have stimulant-like effects and may interfere with sleep if taken late</li>
<li><strong>Avoid with high-protein meals</strong>: Competes with dietary amino acids for BBB transport</li>
</ul>
<h3>Interactions</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Cofactor requirements</strong>: Ensure adequate iron, vitamin B6, and vitamin C for full catecholamine synthesis</li>
<li><strong>Serotonin balance</strong>: Large doses of tyrosine may shift neurotransmitter balance away from serotonin (competitive transport with tryptophan); consider alternating with or spacing from 5-HTP or tryptophan</li>
<li><strong>MAO inhibitors</strong>: Contraindicated with MAOIs (risk of hypertensive crisis from catecholamine accumulation)</li>
<li><strong>Thyroid medications</strong>: May potentiate thyroid hormone production; monitor in hypothyroid patients on levothyroxine</li>
</ul>
<h2>Key Research</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Cognitive flexibility</strong>: L-Tyrosine (2g) improved cognitive flexibility (task-switching) in healthy young adults, suggesting enhanced dopaminergic function in prefrontal cortex (PMID:25797188)</li>
<li><strong>Stress-induced cognitive deficits</strong>: Tyrosine (100 mg/kg) reversed cognitive performance decrements caused by acute uncontrollable stress in military personnel, particularly in working memory and vigilance (PMID:10548261)</li>
<li><strong>Cold stress protection</strong>: Tyrosine supplementation (150 mg/kg) maintained cognitive performance and mood during cold exposure (4.5C for 4.5 hours) in military study — prevented the decline seen in placebo group (PMID:15489899)</li>
<li><strong>Multitasking under stress</strong>: Tyrosine (2g) improved performance on a demanding multitasking paradigm in healthy adults, with benefits most pronounced on the most difficult task conditions (PMID:25598314)</li>
<li><strong>Dopamine synthesis and cognition review</strong>: Comprehensive review of how tyrosine availability modulates dopaminergic function and cognition, with emphasis on individual differences in baseline dopamine (PMID:26424423)</li>
</ul>
