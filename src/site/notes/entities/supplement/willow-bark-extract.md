<h1>Willow Bark Extract</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Willow bark extract (Salix alba) is one of the oldest known analgesic and anti-inflammatory remedies, with use dating back to ancient Egypt and Hippocrates. The bark contains salicin, a beta-glucoside that is metabolized in the gut and liver to salicylic acid -- the same active metabolite produced by aspirin (acetylsalicylic acid). This makes willow bark the natural precursor and historical origin of the entire NSAID drug class.</p>
<p>The anti-inflammatory mechanism involves COX-1 and COX-2 inhibition by salicylic acid, reducing prostaglandin synthesis. However, unlike aspirin, salicylic acid does not irreversibly acetylate COX enzymes, resulting in weaker antiplatelet effects. Importantly, willow bark extract contains additional bioactive compounds -- flavonoids, tannins, and polyphenols -- that contribute anti-inflammatory and antioxidant effects beyond simple salicylate activity. This polypharmacological profile may explain clinical observations that willow bark extract has effects disproportionate to its relatively low salicylate content.</p>
<p>Clinical evidence supports modest efficacy for chronic low back pain and osteoarthritis. A Cochrane-quality RCT demonstrated that 240mg salicin/day was superior to placebo for low back pain, with effect sizes comparable to rofecoxib 12.5mg. Evidence in osteoarthritis is more limited but suggestive. The slower onset compared to synthetic NSAIDs (days vs hours) and potentially fewer gastrointestinal side effects make it suitable for chronic use in patients seeking natural alternatives, though it should be avoided in aspirin-allergic individuals and those on anticoagulants.</p>
<h2>Dosage Guidelines</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Standard dose</strong>: 240mg salicin per day (typically as standardized bark extract)</li>
<li><strong>Equivalent potency</strong>: Roughly equivalent to 50mg aspirin for anti-inflammatory effect</li>
<li><strong>Onset</strong>: Slower than synthetic NSAIDs (days rather than hours)</li>
<li><strong>Timing</strong>: Take with meals to reduce GI irritation</li>
<li><strong>Duration</strong>: Suitable for long-term use; reassess after 4 weeks if no benefit</li>
</ul>
