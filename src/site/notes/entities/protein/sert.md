<h1>SERT</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>The serotonin transporter (SERT, also known as 5-HTT) is a 12-transmembrane domain protein (UniProt: P31645) that terminates serotonergic neurotransmission by reuptaking serotonin (5-HT) from the synaptic cleft back into the presynaptic neuron. It is encoded by the SLC6A4 gene on chromosome 17q11.2. SERT uses the sodium/chloride electrochemical gradient to co-transport one serotonin molecule with one Na+ and one Cl- ion, while counter-transporting one K+ ion.</p>
<p>SERT is the primary molecular target of all selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs). These drugs competitively bind to the serotonin binding site on SERT, blocking reuptake and increasing synaptic serotonin availability. SERT is expressed throughout the CNS — particularly in the raphe nuclei, cortex, hippocampus, amygdala, and thalamus — as well as peripherally in platelets and enterochromaffin cells of the gut (where ~95% of body serotonin is found).</p>

<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>SSRI mechanism</strong>: All SSRIs exert their antidepressant effect by inhibiting SERT, increasing synaptic serotonin</li>
<li><strong>Delayed therapeutic onset</strong>: Despite rapid SERT blockade, therapeutic effects take 2-4 weeks due to 5-HT1A autoreceptor desensitization</li>
<li><strong>Peripheral effects</strong>: SERT blockade on platelets reduces serotonin uptake, impairing aggregation (explains SSRI-associated bleeding risk)</li>
<li><strong>Genetic variation</strong>: The 5-HTTLPR polymorphism in the SLC6A4 promoter affects SERT expression levels and influences both baseline serotonergic tone and SSRI treatment response</li>
</ul>
