<h1>Presenilin-1</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Presenilin-1 (PS1, encoded by the PSEN1 gene) is a 467-amino acid, nine-transmembrane domain protein that serves as the catalytic subunit of the gamma-secretase complex. This intramembrane aspartyl protease complex, which also includes nicastrin, APH-1, and PEN-2, cleaves over 150 type I transmembrane protein substrates within their transmembrane domains. PS1 is localized primarily in the endoplasmic reticulum and Golgi apparatus.</p>
<p>Presenilin-1 is the most commonly mutated gene in familial Alzheimer's disease (FAD), with over 300 documented pathogenic mutations accounting for approximately 70% of all FAD cases. These mutations generally shift gamma-secretase cleavage to produce longer, more aggregation-prone Aβ42 and Aβ43 peptides relative to the shorter Aβ40 species.</p>
<h2>Mechanism of Action</h2>
<p>PS1 contains two catalytic aspartate residues (D257 and D385) that form the active site of gamma-secretase. During maturation, PS1 undergoes endoproteolytic cleavage between transmembrane domains 6 and 7, generating N-terminal and C-terminal fragments that remain associated as a heterodimer within the complex. Gamma-secretase performs sequential cleavage of substrates: an initial endopeptidase cut (epsilon-cleavage) followed by progressive carboxypeptidase-like trimming that determines the final length of products. For APP processing, PS1 first cuts at the epsilon-site to generate Aβ48 or Aβ49, then progressively trims to produce Aβ42 or Aβ40. FAD mutations impair the trimming activity, resulting in longer, pathogenic Aβ species. Beyond APP, PS1 cleaves Notch receptors (releasing the Notch intracellular domain for nuclear signaling), ErbB4, E-cadherin, and CD44.</p>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<p>PSEN1 mutations are the most common cause of early-onset familial Alzheimer's disease, typically presenting between ages 30-60 with aggressive cognitive decline. The &quot;amyloid hypothesis&quot; is built substantially on PSEN1 genetics: FAD mutations increase the Aβ42/Aβ40 ratio, promoting amyloid plaque formation. Gamma-secretase inhibitors (e.g., semagacestat) failed in clinical trials due to mechanism-based toxicity from blocking Notch signaling, causing gastrointestinal and immunological adverse effects. Gamma-secretase modulators (GSMs), which shift cleavage toward shorter Aβ species without blocking Notch, represent a more promising therapeutic approach. PS1 also has gamma-secretase-independent functions in calcium homeostasis, autophagy, and beta-catenin signaling.</p>
