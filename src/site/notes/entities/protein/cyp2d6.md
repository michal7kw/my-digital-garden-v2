<h1>CYP2D6</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>CYP2D6 is the second most important cytochrome P450 enzyme in drug metabolism after CYP3A4. Despite representing only ~2-4% of total hepatic CYP content, it metabolizes approximately 25% of all clinically used drugs. Its clinical significance is amplified by extreme genetic polymorphism — over 100 allelic variants have been identified, creating four distinct metabolizer phenotypes that profoundly affect drug response.</p>
<p>CYP2D6 is particularly critical for opioid metabolism (codeine → morphine activation), SSRI clearance (fluoxetine, paroxetine), beta-blocker metabolism (metoprolol), and antipsychotic clearance (haloperidol, risperidone). Poor metabolizers accumulate toxic drug levels, while ultra-rapid metabolizers may fail to achieve therapeutic concentrations.</p>
<h2>Key Substrates</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Opioids</strong>: Codeine (prodrug), tramadol, hydrocodone, oxycodone</li>
<li><strong>Antidepressants</strong>: Fluoxetine, paroxetine, venlafaxine, desipramine</li>
<li><strong>Beta-blockers</strong>: Metoprolol, propranolol, timolol</li>
<li><strong>Antipsychotics</strong>: Haloperidol, risperidone, aripiprazole</li>
<li><strong>Other</strong>: Tamoxifen (prodrug → endoxifen), dextromethorphan, ondansetron</li>
</ul>

<h2>Clinical Interaction Mechanisms</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Inhibitors</strong> (reduce CYP2D6 activity): Fluoxetine, paroxetine, bupropion, quinidine</li>
<li><strong>Substrates competing</strong> for enzyme: Multiple CYP2D6 substrates taken together compete for metabolism</li>
<li><strong>Prodrug risk</strong>: Ultra-rapid metabolizers convert codeine to morphine too quickly → respiratory depression</li>
</ul>
