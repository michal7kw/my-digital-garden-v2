<h1>CYP2C9</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>CYP2C9 is one of the most abundant cytochrome P450 enzymes in the liver, accounting for approximately 20% of hepatic CYP content. It metabolizes about 15% of clinically used drugs, with its most critical role being the metabolism of S-warfarin (the more potent enantiomer of the anticoagulant warfarin). CYP2C9 polymorphisms are a major determinant of warfarin dose requirements — carriers of reduced-function alleles (*2, *3) require significantly lower doses and have higher bleeding risk.</p>
<p>CYP2C9 is also the primary metabolizing enzyme for most NSAIDs, phenytoin, and several angiotensin receptor blockers, making it relevant for a wide range of drug interactions.</p>
<h2>Key Substrates</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Anticoagulants</strong>: Warfarin (S-enantiomer — the active form)</li>
<li><strong>NSAIDs</strong>: Ibuprofen, diclofenac, celecoxib, naproxen, piroxicam</li>
<li><strong>Antiepileptics</strong>: Phenytoin</li>
<li><strong>Antihypertensives</strong>: Losartan (prodrug activation), irbesartan</li>
<li><strong>Hypoglycemics</strong>: Glipizide, tolbutamide</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Warfarin + CYP2C9 PM</strong>: Requires 50-80% lower dose; FDA label recommends pharmacogenomic testing</li>
<li><strong>Combined CYP2C9 + VKORC1 testing</strong>: Together explain ~40% of warfarin dose variability</li>
<li><strong>NSAIDs + CYP2C9 inhibitors</strong>: Increased NSAID exposure → GI bleeding risk</li>
<li><strong>Phenytoin + CYP2C9 PM</strong>: Non-linear accumulation at standard doses → toxicity</li>
</ul>
<h2>Interaction Mechanisms</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Inhibitors</strong>: Fluconazole (potent), amiodarone, sulfamethoxazole, fluvastatin</li>
<li><strong>Inducers</strong>: Rifampicin, carbamazepine</li>
<li><strong>Competition</strong>: Multiple NSAIDs competing for metabolism</li>
</ul>
