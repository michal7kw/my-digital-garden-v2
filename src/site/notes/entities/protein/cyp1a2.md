<h1>CYP1A2</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>CYP1A2 accounts for approximately 13% of total hepatic CYP content and metabolizes about 15% of clinically used drugs. It is notable for being the primary enzyme responsible for caffeine metabolism, making caffeine a practical biomarker for CYP1A2 activity. CYP1A2 is uniquely sensitive to environmental inducers — smoking tobacco increases CYP1A2 activity 1.5-2x, while dietary components like cruciferous vegetables can also modulate its activity.</p>
<p>Clinically, CYP1A2 is most important for the metabolism of clozapine, olanzapine, theophylline, and tizanidine. Interactions involving CYP1A2 inhibitors (especially fluvoxamine) can cause dangerous drug accumulation of these substrates.</p>
<h2>Key Substrates</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Methylxanthines</strong>: Caffeine, theophylline</li>
<li><strong>Antipsychotics</strong>: Clozapine, olanzapine</li>
<li><strong>Other</strong>: Tizanidine, ramelteon, melatonin, duloxetine</li>
</ul>
<h2>Interaction Mechanisms</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Potent inhibitors</strong>: Fluvoxamine (10-15x increase in clozapine levels), ciprofloxacin</li>
<li><strong>Moderate inhibitors</strong>: Oral contraceptives, cimetidine</li>
<li><strong>Inducers</strong>: Smoking (PAH in smoke, not nicotine), cruciferous vegetables, chargrilled meat, omeprazole (at high doses)</li>
<li><strong>Smoking cessation risk</strong>: Stopping smoking rapidly reduces CYP1A2 → clozapine/olanzapine levels spike</li>
</ul>
