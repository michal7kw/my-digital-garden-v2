<h1>ATP Synthase</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>ATP synthase (Complex V) is the enzyme responsible for synthesizing the majority of cellular ATP. It is a remarkable molecular machine that uses the proton gradient generated by the electron transport chain to drive the phosphorylation of ADP to ATP.</p>
<p>The enzyme consists of two main portions:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>F1</strong> (catalytic): Mushroom-shaped head in the matrix</li>
<li><strong>Fo</strong> (proton channel): Membrane-embedded base</li>
</ul>
<p>ATP synthase represents one of the smallest rotary motors in nature, spinning at up to 9,000 rpm and producing approximately 100 ATP molecules per second.</p>
<h2>Structure</h2>
<h3>Fo Portion (Membrane)</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>a subunit</strong>: Contains proton half-channels</li>
<li><strong>c-ring</strong>: 8-15 c subunits forming a rotating ring</li>
<li><strong>b subunit</strong>: Part of peripheral stalk</li>
<li>Function: Converts proton flow to rotation</li>
</ul>
<h3>F1 Portion (Matrix)</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>α3β3 hexamer</strong>: Alternating α and β subunits</li>
<li><strong>β subunits</strong>: Contain catalytic sites</li>
<li><strong>γ subunit</strong>: Central rotating shaft</li>
<li><strong>δ/ε subunits</strong>: Coupling between Fo and F1</li>
<li><strong>OSCP</strong>: Part of peripheral stalk</li>
</ul>
<h3>Peripheral Stalk</h3>
<ul>
<li>Connects Fo and F1 non-rotationally</li>
<li>Prevents co-rotation of α3β3 with γ</li>
<li>Essential for coupling</li>
</ul>
<h2>Rotary Catalysis</h2>
<h3>Proton-Driven Rotation</h3>
<ol>
<li>Protons enter from intermembrane space via a subunit half-channel</li>
<li>Protons bind to c subunit carboxyl groups</li>
<li>c-ring rotates as protons are released to matrix</li>
<li>Rotation transmitted to γ subunit</li>
</ol>
<h3>Binding Change Mechanism (Paul Boyer)</h3>
<p>The three β subunits cycle through three conformational states:</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Open (O)</strong>: Low substrate affinity, releases ATP</li>
<li><strong>Loose (L)</strong>: Binds ADP + Pi loosely</li>
<li><strong>Tight (T)</strong>: Substrates bound tightly, catalysis occurs</li>
</ol>
<p><strong>120° rotation converts each site to next state</strong></p>
<h3>ATP Synthesis</h3>
<ul>
<li>Each 360° rotation: 3 ATP synthesized</li>
<li>H⁺/ATP ratio: ~3-4 protons per ATP</li>
<li>Efficiency: ~40% (comparable to combustion engines)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Energetics</h2>
<h3>Proton Motive Force</h3>
<ul>
<li>ΔG for ATP synthesis: +30.5 kJ/mol</li>
<li>Requires ~3-4 protons (10-13 kJ/mol each)</li>
<li>Proton gradient provides driving force</li>
</ul>
<h3>Reversibility</h3>
<ul>
<li>ATP synthase can run in reverse</li>
<li>Hydrolyzes ATP to pump protons</li>
<li>Occurs during ischemia (wastes ATP)</li>
<li>IF1 protein inhibits reverse mode</li>
</ul>
<h2>Regulation</h2>
<h3>Inhibitory Factor 1 (IF1)</h3>
<ul>
<li>Prevents ATP hydrolysis during hypoxia</li>
<li>Active at low pH (ischemia)</li>
<li>Protects ATP stores</li>
</ul>
<h3>Ca²⁺ Regulation</h3>
<ul>
<li>Ca²⁺ enhances ATP synthase activity</li>
<li>Coordinates with TCA cycle activation</li>
<li>Important in muscle and neurons</li>
</ul>
<h3>Membrane Potential</h3>
<ul>
<li>Activity depends on proton motive force</li>
<li>Uncouplers dissipate gradient, stop synthesis</li>
<li>Critical for metabolic flexibility</li>
</ul>

<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<h3>Mitochondrial Diseases</h3>
<ul>
<li>ATP synthase deficiency (rare but severe)</li>
<li>ATP6 mutations (mtDNA-encoded)</li>
<li>Neuropathy, ataxia, retinitis pigmentosa (NARP)</li>
<li>Leigh syndrome</li>
</ul>
<h3>Ischemia-Reperfusion</h3>
<ul>
<li>Reverse mode wastes ATP during ischemia</li>
<li>IF1 provides some protection</li>
<li>Therapeutic target for cardioprotection</li>
</ul>
<h3>Cancer</h3>
<ul>
<li>Altered ATP synthase in some cancers</li>
<li>IF1 overexpression in some tumors</li>
<li>Metabolic adaptation to hypoxia</li>
</ul>
<h3>Aging</h3>
<ul>
<li>ATP synthase activity declines with age</li>
<li>Contributes to mitochondrial dysfunction</li>
<li>Assembly defects accumulate</li>
</ul>
