<h1>Dopamine Beta-Hydroxylase</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Dopamine beta-hydroxylase (DBH) is the third enzyme in the catecholamine biosynthetic cascade, converting dopamine to norepinephrine. It occupies a unique position in neurotransmitter biochemistry as the only catecholamine synthetic enzyme localized within synaptic vesicles rather than in the cytoplasm. This vesicular localization means that dopamine must first be transported into vesicles by VMAT2 before it can be hydroxylated to norepinephrine.</p>
<p>DBH is a copper-dependent enzyme that requires ascorbic acid (vitamin C) as an electron donor for each catalytic cycle. This cofactor requirement explains why vitamin C deficiency (scurvy) affects noradrenergic function and mood. Serum DBH levels are a clinically useful biomarker of sympathetic nervous system activity, and genetic variants in the DBH gene that affect enzyme activity are associated with various psychiatric and cardiovascular phenotypes.</p>
<p><strong>Evidence Level:</strong> 4/5</p>
<h2>Key Functions</h2>
<h3>Catalytic Mechanism</h3>
<ul>
<li>Hydroxylates dopamine at the beta-carbon to produce norepinephrine</li>
<li>Uses molecular oxygen (O2) as the oxygen source</li>
<li>Copper (Cu2+) at the active site coordinates oxygen activation</li>
<li>Ascorbic acid (vitamin C) donates electrons to reduce Cu2+ back to Cu+ after each cycle</li>
<li>The reaction also requires fumarate as an allosteric activator</li>
</ul>
<h3>Vesicular Localization</h3>
<ul>
<li>Exists as a type II membrane protein with two membrane-bound and two soluble subunits</li>
<li>Only enzyme in catecholamine synthesis that is not cytoplasmic</li>
<li>Dopamine must be transported into vesicles by VMAT2 before conversion</li>
<li>Co-released with norepinephrine upon exocytosis — soluble DBH appears in blood/CSF</li>
</ul>
<h3>Biomarker Utility</h3>
<ul>
<li>Serum DBH levels reflect sympathetic noradrenergic activity</li>
<li>Highly heritable (~90%) — mostly determined by genetics, not acute stress</li>
<li>Low DBH genotype associated with altered dopamine:norepinephrine ratio</li>
<li>Useful in clinical research for assessing autonomic function</li>
</ul>
<h3>Genetic Variation</h3>
<ul>
<li>Common functional polymorphisms (rs1611115, rs77905) dramatically affect serum DBH levels</li>
<li>Low-activity variants associated with increased risk of ADHD, substance use, and orthostatic hypotension</li>
<li>High-activity variants associated with increased cardiovascular reactivity to stress</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Relevance</h2>
<h3>DBH Deficiency</h3>
<p>Rare autosomal recessive disorder causing profound norepinephrine and epinephrine deficiency with preserved dopamine. Presents with severe orthostatic hypotension, ptosis, and nasal congestion. Treated with droxidopa (synthetic norepinephrine precursor).</p>
<h3>Cofactor Dependencies</h3>
<p>Vitamin C and copper deficiency both impair DBH activity. Scurvy-related fatigue and depression may partly reflect reduced norepinephrine synthesis. Copper deficiency from excessive zinc supplementation can also reduce DBH function.</p>
