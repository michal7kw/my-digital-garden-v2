<h1>D-Dimer</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>D-Dimer is a fibrin degradation product, a small protein fragment present in blood after a blood clot is degraded by fibrinolysis. It is named for two crosslinked D fragments of the fibrin protein. The D-Dimer test is primarily used as a screening tool to help rule out thromboembolic events, including deep vein thrombosis (DVT), pulmonary embolism (PE), and disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC).</p>
<p>The test has an extremely high sensitivity (&gt;95%) but relatively low specificity for thromboembolism. A normal D-Dimer result (&lt;500 ng/mL FEU) in a patient with low or moderate clinical probability effectively excludes venous thromboembolism, potentially avoiding the need for imaging studies. However, D-Dimer can be elevated in many non-thrombotic conditions including infection, inflammation, malignancy, pregnancy, recent surgery, trauma, and liver disease.</p>
<p>Age-adjusted D-Dimer cutoffs (age x 10 ng/mL for patients over 50 years) have been validated to improve specificity in elderly patients, reducing unnecessary imaging by 12-30% while maintaining safety. Current guidelines recommend D-Dimer testing in conjunction with validated clinical probability scores such as the Wells score or Geneva score.</p>

<h2>Notes</h2>
<ul>
<li>Results should be interpreted by a qualified healthcare provider</li>
<li>Reference ranges may vary by laboratory and assay method (FEU vs DDU units)</li>
<li>A normal D-Dimer does NOT rule out thrombosis in high clinical probability patients</li>
<li>D-Dimer is elevated in pregnancy, surgery, trauma, infection, malignancy, and aging</li>
<li>Age-adjusted cutoffs (age x 10 ng/mL for &gt;50 years) improve specificity</li>
<li>FEU (Fibrinogen Equivalent Units) values are approximately twice DDU (D-Dimer Units) values</li>
</ul>
