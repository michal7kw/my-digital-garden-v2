<h1>Heart</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>The heart is a hollow, four-chambered muscular organ approximately the size of a closed fist, located in the mediastinum of the thoracic cavity between the lungs. It consists of two atria (upper chambers) that receive blood and two ventricles (lower chambers) that pump blood out. The right side receives deoxygenated blood from the body and pumps it to the lungs, while the left side receives oxygenated blood from the lungs and pumps it to the systemic circulation. The interventricular septum separates the two sides, and four valves (tricuspid, pulmonary, mitral, aortic) ensure unidirectional blood flow.</p>
<p>The heart wall is composed of three layers: the epicardium (outer), myocardium (thick muscular middle layer responsible for contraction), and endocardium (inner lining). The myocardium of the left ventricle is significantly thicker than the right, reflecting the higher pressures needed to perfuse the systemic circulation. Cardiac muscle cells (cardiomyocytes) are uniquely adapted for sustained rhythmic contraction, connected by intercalated discs that allow synchronized electrical conduction.</p>
<p>The heart's own blood supply comes from the coronary arteries, which branch from the aorta just above the aortic valve. The left coronary artery divides into the left anterior descending (LAD) and circumflex arteries, supplying most of the left ventricle and septum. The right coronary artery supplies the right ventricle and inferior wall. Venous drainage occurs primarily through the coronary sinus into the right atrium. Blockage of these vessels is the primary mechanism of myocardial infarction.</p>
<h2>Key Functions</h2>
<ul>
<li>Pumping oxygenated blood to all tissues and organs via the systemic circulation</li>
<li>Directing deoxygenated blood to the lungs for gas exchange via the pulmonary circulation</li>
<li>Generating and conducting electrical impulses through the SA node, AV node, Bundle of His, and Purkinje fibers to maintain rhythmic contraction</li>
<li>Producing atrial natriuretic peptide (ANP) and B-type natriuretic peptide (BNP) to help regulate blood volume and pressure</li>
<li>Maintaining adequate perfusion pressure for organ function, typically generating a cardiac output of 4-8 L/min at rest</li>
</ul>
<h2>Associated Conditions</h2>
<ul>
<li>Coronary artery disease (CAD) — atherosclerotic plaque buildup in coronary arteries leading to angina and myocardial infarction; the leading cause of death globally</li>
<li>Heart failure — inability of the heart to pump sufficient blood to meet metabolic demands; classified as systolic (reduced ejection fraction, HFrEF) or diastolic (preserved ejection fraction, HFpEF)</li>
<li>Atrial fibrillation — the most common sustained cardiac arrhythmia, characterized by chaotic electrical activity in the atria, increasing stroke risk five-fold</li>
<li>Hypertension — chronically elevated blood pressure that forces the heart to work harder, leading to left ventricular hypertrophy and eventual failure</li>
<li>Valvular heart disease — stenosis or regurgitation of one or more heart valves, commonly affecting the aortic and mitral valves</li>
<li>Myocarditis — inflammation of the myocardium, often caused by viral infections, that can lead to acute heart failure or sudden cardiac death</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<p>Cardiovascular disease remains the leading cause of morbidity and mortality worldwide. Key biomarkers for cardiac assessment include troponin I and T (myocardial injury), BNP and NT-proBNP (heart failure), CRP and homocysteine (cardiovascular risk), and lipid panels (atherosclerosis risk). Echocardiography, electrocardiography (ECG), coronary angiography, and cardiac MRI are essential diagnostic tools. Nutritional support through omega-3 fatty acids, CoQ10, and magnesium has demonstrated benefit in specific cardiac conditions, and lifestyle modifications (exercise, dietary changes, smoking cessation) remain foundational to cardiovascular health.</p>
