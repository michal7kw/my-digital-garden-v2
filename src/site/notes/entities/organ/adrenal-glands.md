<h1>Adrenal Glands</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>The adrenal glands are a pair of small, triangular (right) and crescent-shaped (left) endocrine organs, each weighing approximately 4-6 g, situated on the superior poles of the kidneys within the perirenal fascia. Despite their small size, they are among the most highly vascularized organs in the body, receiving blood from three arterial sources: the superior adrenal arteries (branches of the inferior phrenic artery), middle adrenal arteries (branches directly from the aorta), and inferior adrenal arteries (branches of the renal arteries). Venous drainage is asymmetric: the right adrenal vein drains directly into the inferior vena cava, while the left drains into the left renal vein.</p>
<p>Each adrenal gland consists of two functionally distinct regions. The outer adrenal cortex (approximately 80-90% of the gland's mass) is derived from mesoderm and organized into three zones: the zona glomerulosa (outermost, producing aldosterone), zona fasciculata (middle and largest zone, producing cortisol), and zona reticularis (innermost, producing adrenal androgens including DHEA and androstenedione). The inner adrenal medulla (10-20%) is derived from neural crest ectoderm and functions as a specialized sympathetic ganglion, containing chromaffin cells that synthesize and secrete catecholamines — primarily epinephrine (adrenaline, ~80%) and norepinephrine (~20%) — directly into the bloodstream.</p>
<p>Cortisol secretion is regulated by the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis: the hypothalamus releases corticotropin-releasing hormone (CRH), stimulating the anterior pituitary to secrete adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH), which stimulates the adrenal cortex. Cortisol follows a diurnal pattern, peaking in the early morning and reaching its nadir around midnight. Aldosterone secretion is primarily regulated by the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS) and serum potassium levels, rather than ACTH. The medullary catecholamine response is rapid and triggered by sympathetic preganglionic neurons during acute stress (fight-or-flight response).</p>
<h2>Key Functions</h2>
<ul>
<li>Cortisol production (zona fasciculata): regulates glucose metabolism (gluconeogenesis), suppresses inflammatory and immune responses, modulates protein and fat metabolism, and maintains vascular tone</li>
<li>Aldosterone production (zona glomerulosa): promotes sodium reabsorption and potassium excretion in the distal nephron, thereby regulating blood volume and blood pressure</li>
<li>Adrenal androgen production (zona reticularis): DHEA and DHEA-S serve as precursors for peripheral conversion to testosterone and estrogen, particularly important in women</li>
<li>Catecholamine secretion (medulla): epinephrine and norepinephrine mobilize the acute stress response — increasing heart rate, blood pressure, bronchodilation, and glucose availability</li>
<li>Circadian rhythm regulation: cortisol's diurnal pattern helps synchronize the sleep-wake cycle and daily metabolic rhythms</li>
<li>Immune modulation: cortisol exerts broad anti-inflammatory effects by suppressing NF-kB signaling, reducing cytokine production, and inhibiting leukocyte migration</li>
</ul>
<h2>Associated Conditions</h2>
<ul>
<li>Addison's disease (primary adrenal insufficiency) — destruction of the adrenal cortex, most commonly by autoimmune adrenalitis, resulting in deficiency of cortisol, aldosterone, and adrenal androgens; characterized by fatigue, hypotension, hyperpigmentation, and salt craving</li>
<li>Cushing's syndrome — chronic cortisol excess from exogenous glucocorticoids (most common), pituitary adenoma (Cushing's disease), adrenal adenoma, or ectopic ACTH production; manifests as central obesity, moon face, striae, hyperglycemia, and osteoporosis</li>
<li>Pheochromocytoma — catecholamine-secreting tumor of the adrenal medulla causing paroxysmal or sustained hypertension, headache, sweating, and palpitations; follows the &quot;rule of 10s&quot; (10% bilateral, 10% malignant, 10% extra-adrenal)</li>
<li>Adrenal fatigue (HPA axis dysregulation) — a pattern of suboptimal cortisol production and blunted diurnal rhythm associated with chronic stress; not recognized as a formal medical diagnosis but commonly discussed in integrative medicine</li>
<li>Congenital adrenal hyperplasia (CAH) — a group of autosomal recessive disorders (most commonly 21-hydroxylase deficiency) causing impaired cortisol synthesis and compensatory ACTH-driven adrenal androgen excess</li>
<li>Primary hyperaldosteronism (Conn's syndrome) — autonomous aldosterone overproduction, typically from an adrenal adenoma, causing resistant hypertension and hypokalemia</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<p>Adrenal function is evaluated through morning cortisol levels, ACTH stimulation testing (Synacthen test for adrenal insufficiency), 24-hour urinary free cortisol, late-night salivary cortisol, and low-dose dexamethasone suppression test (Cushing's screening). Aldosterone-to-renin ratio (ARR) screens for primary hyperaldosteronism. Plasma and 24-hour urinary metanephrines and normetanephrines screen for pheochromocytoma. CT and MRI of the adrenals evaluate masses (adrenal incidentalomas are found in approximately 4% of abdominal CT scans). Adaptogenic supplements such as ashwagandha, rhodiola rosea, and eleuthero are commonly used to support HPA axis function, while vitamin C is concentrated in the adrenal cortex at levels 50-100 times higher than plasma, supporting steroidogenesis.</p>
