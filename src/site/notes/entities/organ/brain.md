<h1>Brain</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>The brain is the central organ of the nervous system, weighing approximately 1.4 kg in adults, enclosed within the cranial vault and surrounded by three protective meningeal layers (dura mater, arachnoid mater, and pia mater). It is broadly divided into the cerebrum (two hemispheres with frontal, parietal, temporal, and occipital lobes), the cerebellum (motor coordination and balance), and the brainstem (midbrain, pons, medulla oblongata — controlling vital autonomic functions). The cerebral cortex, a 2-4 mm layer of gray matter containing approximately 16 billion neurons, is responsible for higher-order functions including consciousness, language, reasoning, and sensory processing.</p>
<p>The brain contains approximately 86 billion neurons interconnected by an estimated 100 trillion synapses, communicating via electrical impulses and chemical neurotransmitters including glutamate (excitatory), GABA (inhibitory), dopamine, serotonin, norepinephrine, and acetylcholine. Glial cells (astrocytes, oligodendrocytes, microglia) outnumber neurons and perform critical support functions: maintaining the blood-brain barrier, myelinating axons, providing metabolic support, and mediating immune responses. The blood-brain barrier (BBB), formed by tight junctions in cerebral endothelial cells, selectively regulates which substances can enter the brain parenchyma.</p>
<p>Despite comprising only 2% of body weight, the brain consumes approximately 20% of the body's total oxygen and 25% of its glucose at rest. It receives blood via two pairs of arteries: the internal carotid arteries (anterior circulation) and vertebral arteries (posterior circulation), which anastomose at the Circle of Willis at the base of the brain, providing collateral circulation. Venous drainage occurs through cerebral veins into the dural venous sinuses, ultimately flowing into the internal jugular veins. The brain also produces approximately 500 mL of cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) daily, which cushions the brain, removes metabolic waste (via the glymphatic system during sleep), and helps maintain intracranial pressure.</p>
<h2>Key Functions</h2>
<ul>
<li>Higher cognitive functions: consciousness, attention, memory formation and retrieval, language processing, abstract reasoning, planning, and decision-making (primarily cerebral cortex and hippocampus)</li>
<li>Sensory integration: processing and interpreting visual, auditory, somatosensory, olfactory, and gustatory information from the environment</li>
<li>Motor control: planning and executing voluntary movements (motor cortex, basal ganglia, cerebellum), maintaining posture and balance</li>
<li>Autonomic regulation: controlling heart rate, blood pressure, respiration, body temperature, and circadian rhythms via the hypothalamus and brainstem</li>
<li>Endocrine regulation: the hypothalamus-pituitary axis orchestrates hormone release from the thyroid, adrenals, gonads, and other endocrine organs</li>
<li>Emotional processing: the limbic system (amygdala, hippocampus, cingulate cortex) mediates emotional responses, fear conditioning, and reward pathways</li>
</ul>
<h2>Associated Conditions</h2>
<ul>
<li>Alzheimer's disease — the most common neurodegenerative disorder, characterized by progressive accumulation of amyloid-beta plaques and neurofibrillary tau tangles, leading to memory loss and cognitive decline</li>
<li>Stroke (cerebrovascular accident) — ischemic (85%) or hemorrhagic (15%) disruption of cerebral blood flow causing acute neurological deficits; a leading cause of disability</li>
<li>Depression (major depressive disorder) — mood disorder involving dysregulation of serotonergic, noradrenergic, and dopaminergic neurotransmission, with neuroinflammation and hippocampal atrophy in chronic cases</li>
<li>Parkinson's disease — progressive loss of dopaminergic neurons in the substantia nigra, causing tremor, rigidity, bradykinesia, and postural instability</li>
<li>Epilepsy — recurrent unprovoked seizures from abnormal synchronized neuronal activity; affects approximately 50 million people worldwide</li>
<li>Traumatic brain injury (TBI) — physical damage to the brain from external forces, ranging from concussion to severe diffuse axonal injury</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<p>Neurological assessment includes mental status examination, cranial nerve testing, motor and sensory evaluation, and reflex testing. Key diagnostic tools include MRI (gold standard for structural imaging), CT (acute hemorrhage detection), EEG (seizure activity), PET scanning (metabolic activity, amyloid imaging), and lumbar puncture (CSF analysis for infections, autoimmune conditions, and neurodegenerative biomarkers). Blood biomarkers under investigation include neurofilament light chain (NfL) for neurodegeneration and S100B for brain injury. Nutritional support for brain health includes omega-3 fatty acids (DHA for neuronal membrane integrity), B vitamins (B6, B12, folate for homocysteine metabolism and myelination), magnesium (NMDA receptor modulation), creatine (cerebral energy metabolism), and curcumin (anti-neuroinflammatory properties).</p>
