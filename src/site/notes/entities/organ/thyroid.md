<h1>Thyroid</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>The thyroid is a butterfly-shaped endocrine gland weighing 15-25 g, located in the anterior neck at the level of the C5-T1 vertebrae, just inferior to the larynx and anterior to the trachea. It consists of two lateral lobes connected by a thin isthmus, and occasionally a pyramidal lobe extends superiorly from the isthmus. The gland is enclosed in a thin fibrous capsule and is highly vascularized, receiving blood from the superior thyroid arteries (branches of the external carotid) and inferior thyroid arteries (branches of the thyrocervical trunk), making it one of the most vascular organs per unit weight in the body.</p>
<p>Microscopically, the thyroid is composed of spherical follicles lined by a single layer of follicular epithelial cells surrounding a colloid-filled lumen. The colloid contains thyroglobulin, the protein precursor of thyroid hormones. Follicular cells take up iodide from the blood via the sodium-iodide symporter (NIS), oxidize it using thyroid peroxidase (TPO), and incorporate it into thyroglobulin to produce thyroxine (T4, the predominant circulating form) and triiodothyronine (T3, the biologically active form). Approximately 80% of T3 is produced peripherally by deiodination of T4, primarily by type 1 and type 2 deiodinases in the liver, kidneys, and other tissues. Parafollicular C cells, scattered between the follicles, produce calcitonin, which modestly inhibits osteoclast activity to lower blood calcium.</p>
<p>Thyroid function is regulated by the hypothalamic-pituitary-thyroid (HPT) axis: the hypothalamus releases thyrotropin-releasing hormone (TRH), which stimulates the anterior pituitary to secrete thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH), which in turn stimulates thyroid hormone synthesis and secretion. Elevated T3 and T4 exert negative feedback on both the hypothalamus and pituitary. Thyroid hormones affect virtually every cell in the body, increasing basal metabolic rate, oxygen consumption, heat production, and protein synthesis, and are essential for normal growth and neurodevelopment in children.</p>
<h2>Key Functions</h2>
<ul>
<li>Production and secretion of thyroid hormones T4 (thyroxine) and T3 (triiodothyronine), which regulate basal metabolic rate in virtually all tissues</li>
<li>Regulation of thermogenesis and body temperature through increased mitochondrial oxygen consumption and uncoupling protein expression</li>
<li>Essential role in growth and development, particularly central nervous system maturation in fetuses and infants (cretinism from congenital hypothyroidism)</li>
<li>Modulation of cardiac function: T3 increases heart rate, contractility, and cardiac output by upregulating beta-adrenergic receptors</li>
<li>Influence on lipid metabolism: thyroid hormones increase LDL receptor expression, enhancing cholesterol clearance (hypothyroidism causes hyperlipidemia)</li>
<li>Calcitonin production by parafollicular C cells, contributing to calcium homeostasis</li>
</ul>
<h2>Associated Conditions</h2>
<ul>
<li>Hypothyroidism — insufficient thyroid hormone production, most commonly caused by Hashimoto's thyroiditis (autoimmune); symptoms include fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance, constipation, dry skin, and bradycardia</li>
<li>Hyperthyroidism — excess thyroid hormone production, most commonly caused by Graves' disease (autoimmune TSH receptor stimulation); symptoms include weight loss, heat intolerance, tremor, tachycardia, and anxiety</li>
<li>Hashimoto's thyroiditis — autoimmune destruction of thyroid tissue with anti-TPO and anti-thyroglobulin antibodies; the most common cause of hypothyroidism in iodine-sufficient populations</li>
<li>Graves' disease — autoimmune disorder with TSH receptor-stimulating antibodies causing diffuse goiter, hyperthyroidism, and potentially ophthalmopathy (exophthalmos) and dermopathy</li>
<li>Thyroid nodules and cancer — nodules are very common (50% of adults by ultrasound), with approximately 5-15% being malignant; papillary thyroid cancer is the most common type with excellent prognosis</li>
<li>Goiter — enlargement of the thyroid gland, which may be diffuse or nodular, caused by iodine deficiency, autoimmune disease, or autonomous nodules</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<p>Thyroid function is assessed primarily through serum TSH (most sensitive screening test), free T4, and free T3. Anti-TPO and anti-thyroglobulin antibodies identify autoimmune thyroid disease. Thyroid ultrasound evaluates nodule size, characteristics (TI-RADS scoring), and guides fine-needle aspiration (FNA) biopsy for cytological assessment (Bethesda system). Radioactive iodine uptake (RAIU) scan differentiates causes of hyperthyroidism. Key nutrient dependencies include iodine (essential substrate for hormone synthesis; 150 mcg/day recommended for adults), selenium (required for deiodinase enzymes converting T4 to T3 and for glutathione peroxidase protecting against oxidative damage), zinc (involved in T3 binding to nuclear receptors), and L-tyrosine (amino acid backbone of thyroid hormones).</p>
