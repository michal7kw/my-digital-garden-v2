<h1>Joints</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Joints (articulations) are the structural connections between two or more bones in the skeletal system that enable movement, provide mechanical support, and allow force transmission throughout the body. The human body contains over 300 joints, ranging from immovable fibrous joints (sutures of the skull) to freely movable synovial joints (knee, shoulder, hip) that permit complex multi-axial motion.</p>
<p>Synovial joints are the most clinically significant type, featuring a joint capsule lined with synovial membrane that secretes synovial fluid for lubrication and nutrient delivery to avascular articular cartilage. The articular cartilage itself is composed of type II collagen, proteoglycans (primarily aggrecan), and chondrocytes embedded in an extracellular matrix that provides shock absorption and near-frictionless gliding surfaces with a coefficient of friction lower than ice on ice.</p>
<p>Joint health depends on a complex interplay between cartilage integrity, synovial fluid composition, ligament and tendon stability, surrounding muscle strength, and systemic inflammatory status. Joints are metabolically active structures requiring adequate blood supply to periarticular tissues and diffusion-based nutrient delivery to cartilage through cyclic loading and unloading.</p>
<h2>Structure and Function</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Articular cartilage</strong>: Hyaline cartilage covering bone ends; avascular, relies on synovial fluid for nutrients; contains type II collagen and aggrecan for compressive resistance</li>
<li><strong>Synovial fluid</strong>: Viscous dialysate of plasma enriched with hyaluronic acid; provides lubrication, shock absorption, and nutrient transport</li>
<li><strong>Joint capsule</strong>: Fibrous outer layer (mechanical stability) and synovial inner lining (fluid production, debris clearance)</li>
<li><strong>Ligaments</strong>: Dense connective tissue bands connecting bone to bone; provide passive stability and proprioceptive feedback</li>
<li><strong>Menisci and labra</strong>: Fibrocartilaginous structures in specific joints (knee, hip, shoulder) that deepen sockets, distribute load, and enhance stability</li>
<li><strong>Bursae</strong>: Fluid-filled sacs reducing friction between tendons, ligaments, and bone at high-stress points</li>
<li><strong>Proprioceptors</strong>: Mechanoreceptors in joint capsule, ligaments, and periarticular tissues providing position sense and movement awareness</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<p>Joint disorders represent the leading cause of disability worldwide. Osteoarthritis affects over 500 million people globally, driven by cartilage degradation, subchondral bone remodeling, and chronic low-grade inflammation. Rheumatoid arthritis is an autoimmune condition causing synovial inflammation and progressive joint destruction. Gout results from monosodium urate crystal deposition in joint spaces. Joint hypermobility syndromes (Ehlers-Danlos spectrum) involve collagen defects leading to instability and chronic pain. Maintaining joint health requires regular movement (which pumps nutrients into cartilage), adequate collagen precursors (vitamin C, glycine, proline), anti-inflammatory nutrients (omega-3 fatty acids, curcumin), and management of systemic inflammation and body weight.</p>
