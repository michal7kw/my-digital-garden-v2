<h1>Eyes</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>The eyes are paired sensory organs responsible for vision, converting electromagnetic radiation (visible light, 380-700 nm) into neural signals processed by the visual cortex. Each eye contains over 120 million photoreceptor cells (rods and cones) in the retina, making it the most metabolically active tissue per unit weight in the human body, consuming oxygen at a rate comparable to brain tissue.</p>
<p>The eye functions as a sophisticated optical system: the cornea provides approximately two-thirds of the eye's refractive power, the crystalline lens enables accommodation for near and far focus, and the iris regulates light entry through pupillary control. Light reaching the retina is transduced by photoreceptors -- rods for scotopic (low-light) vision and three types of cones for photopic (color) vision -- generating electrical signals transmitted via the optic nerve to the lateral geniculate nucleus and then to the primary visual cortex in the occipital lobe.</p>
<p>The retina has an extremely high concentration of polyunsaturated fatty acids, particularly docosahexaenoic acid (DHA), in photoreceptor outer segment membranes. This lipid composition, combined with constant light exposure and high oxygen consumption, makes the retina particularly vulnerable to oxidative damage. The macular pigments lutein and zeaxanthin serve as critical blue-light filters and antioxidants protecting the fovea.</p>
<h2>Structure and Function</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Cornea</strong>: Transparent avascular tissue providing primary refraction; depends on tear film for oxygen and nutrients</li>
<li><strong>Retina</strong>: Neural tissue containing photoreceptors, bipolar cells, and ganglion cells; the macula provides high-acuity central vision</li>
<li><strong>Photoreceptors</strong>: 120 million rods (peripheral, low-light) and 6 million cones (central, color); require constant DHA membrane renewal</li>
<li><strong>Optic nerve</strong>: ~1.2 million retinal ganglion cell axons transmitting visual information to the brain</li>
<li><strong>Lens</strong>: Transparent crystalline structure enabling accommodation; loses flexibility with age (presbyopia)</li>
<li><strong>Vitreous humor</strong>: Gel-like substance maintaining eye shape; composed of water, collagen, and hyaluronic acid</li>
<li><strong>Retinal pigment epithelium (RPE)</strong>: Supports photoreceptors through phagocytosis of shed outer segments, retinoid recycling, and nutrient transport</li>
<li><strong>Lacrimal system</strong>: Produces tear film providing corneal lubrication, antimicrobial defense, and optical smoothing</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<p>Age-related macular degeneration (AMD) is the leading cause of irreversible vision loss in developed countries, driven by oxidative damage and RPE dysfunction. The AREDS2 study demonstrated that supplementation with lutein, zeaxanthin, vitamin C, vitamin E, zinc, and copper significantly reduces AMD progression risk. Diabetic retinopathy results from microvascular damage and is the primary cause of blindness in working-age adults. Glaucoma involves progressive optic nerve damage from elevated intraocular pressure or impaired blood flow. Dry eye disease affects over 300 million people globally. Blue light exposure from screens may contribute to digital eye strain and potentially accelerate retinal photoreceptor stress. DHA deficiency impairs photoreceptor membrane function and is associated with increased AMD risk.</p>
