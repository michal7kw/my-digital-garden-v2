<h1>Muscles</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Muscles are contractile tissues that enable movement, maintain posture, stabilize joints, and generate heat. Skeletal muscle constitutes approximately 40% of total body mass in healthy adults, making it the largest organ system by weight and the body's primary metabolic tissue. The human body contains over 600 named skeletal muscles, each composed of multinucleated muscle fibers (cells) organized into fascicles.</p>
<p>There are three types of muscle tissue: skeletal (voluntary, striated), cardiac (involuntary, striated), and smooth (involuntary, non-striated). Skeletal muscle fibers are classified by metabolic profile into Type I (slow-twitch, oxidative, fatigue-resistant), Type IIa (fast-twitch, oxidative-glycolytic), and Type IIx (fast-twitch, glycolytic, fatigue-prone). The relative proportion of fiber types varies between muscles and individuals, influenced by genetics and training adaptation.</p>
<p>Skeletal muscle is the primary site of insulin-stimulated glucose uptake, accounting for approximately 80% of postprandial glucose disposal. This makes muscle mass a critical determinant of metabolic health, insulin sensitivity, and glycemic control. Muscle tissue also functions as the body's largest amino acid reservoir, serves as an endocrine organ secreting myokines (IL-6, irisin, myostatin, BDNF) that influence metabolism, inflammation, and brain health, and is a major contributor to resting metabolic rate.</p>
<h2>Structure and Function</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Sarcomere</strong>: The fundamental contractile unit composed of actin (thin) and myosin (thick) filaments; shortening produces force via the sliding filament mechanism</li>
<li><strong>Motor units</strong>: A motor neuron and all muscle fibers it innervates; size determines force precision (small in eye muscles, large in quadriceps)</li>
<li><strong>Sarcoplasmic reticulum</strong>: Intracellular calcium store; releases Ca2+ to trigger contraction and reuptakes it for relaxation</li>
<li><strong>Mitochondria</strong>: Abundant in oxidative fibers; produce ATP via oxidative phosphorylation using fatty acids and glucose</li>
<li><strong>Satellite cells</strong>: Muscle stem cells residing between the sarcolemma and basal lamina; activated by injury or exercise for repair and hypertrophy</li>
<li><strong>Neuromuscular junction</strong>: Synapse where acetylcholine from motor neurons triggers muscle fiber depolarization and contraction</li>
<li><strong>Myokine secretion</strong>: Exercise-induced release of signaling molecules (irisin, IL-6, BDNF) with anti-inflammatory and metabolic effects</li>
<li><strong>Thermogenesis</strong>: Involuntary shivering and non-shivering thermogenesis contribute significantly to body temperature regulation</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<p>Sarcopenia (age-related muscle loss) begins around age 30 with 3-8% muscle mass decline per decade, accelerating after age 60. It is a major contributor to frailty, falls, metabolic syndrome, and mortality in older adults. Resistance training is the most effective intervention for sarcopenia prevention. Muscle insulin resistance is an early event in type 2 diabetes pathogenesis, preceding beta-cell failure by years. Rhabdomyolysis involves acute muscle fiber breakdown with release of myoglobin, creatine kinase, and potassium, potentially causing acute kidney injury. Muscular dystrophies are genetic disorders causing progressive muscle weakness. Adequate protein intake (1.6-2.2 g/kg/day for active individuals), vitamin D (supports muscle fiber contractility and satellite cell function), magnesium (cofactor for over 300 enzymatic reactions including ATP production and muscle relaxation), and creatine (enhances phosphocreatine energy reserves) are key nutritional factors supporting muscle health.</p>
