<h1>Gut</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>The gut encompasses the gastrointestinal (GI) tract, particularly the small and large intestines, which together span approximately 7.5 meters in length and present an absorptive surface area of roughly 32 square meters. The gut is the body's primary interface for nutrient absorption, water and electrolyte balance, and serves as the largest immune organ, housing approximately 70-80% of all immune cells in the gut-associated lymphoid tissue (GALT).</p>
<p>The gut microbiome is a complex ecosystem of 38 trillion microorganisms (bacteria, archaea, fungi, viruses) weighing approximately 1.5-2 kg, with a collective genome (microbiome) containing 150 times more genes than the human genome. Dominant bacterial phyla include Firmicutes and Bacteroidetes, with diversity and composition influencing metabolic health, immune function, mental health, and disease risk. The microbiome produces short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs) -- butyrate, propionate, and acetate -- through fermentation of dietary fiber, which serve as energy sources for colonocytes, regulate immune function, and influence systemic metabolism.</p>
<p>The gut-brain axis represents bidirectional communication between the enteric nervous system (200-600 million neurons) and the central nervous system via the vagus nerve, hormonal signaling, immune mediators, and microbial metabolites. Approximately 95% of the body's serotonin is produced by enterochromaffin cells in the gut, establishing a direct link between gut health and mood regulation. The intestinal epithelial barrier, maintained by tight junction proteins, prevents translocation of bacteria and toxins into the bloodstream; disruption of this barrier (&quot;leaky gut&quot;) is implicated in autoimmune and inflammatory conditions.</p>
<h2>Key Functions</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Nutrient absorption</strong>: Small intestine absorbs macronutrients, vitamins, and minerals through villi and microvilli; site-specific absorption (iron and calcium in duodenum, B12 in terminal ileum)</li>
<li><strong>Immune defense</strong>: GALT includes Peyer's patches, mesenteric lymph nodes, and intraepithelial lymphocytes; IgA secretion neutralizes pathogens</li>
<li><strong>Microbiome metabolism</strong>: Bacterial fermentation of fiber produces SCFAs, synthesizes vitamin K and B vitamins, metabolizes bile acids, and detoxifies xenobiotics</li>
<li><strong>Barrier function</strong>: Single-layer epithelium with tight junctions prevents bacterial translocation while permitting nutrient passage</li>
<li><strong>Enteroendocrine signaling</strong>: Secretes GLP-1, PYY, CCK, ghrelin, and serotonin regulating appetite, insulin secretion, and gastric motility</li>
<li><strong>Water and electrolyte homeostasis</strong>: Large intestine absorbs approximately 1.5 liters of water daily; regulates sodium, potassium, and chloride balance</li>
<li><strong>Gut-brain communication</strong>: Vagal afferents, microbial metabolites, and enteric neurotransmitters influence mood, cognition, and stress responses</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<p>Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), comprising Crohn's disease and ulcerative colitis, affects over 6 million people worldwide and involves chronic immune-mediated destruction of the intestinal lining. Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS) affects 10-15% of the global population. Dysbiosis (microbial imbalance) is associated with obesity, type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, depression, and autoimmune conditions. Small intestinal bacterial overgrowth (SIBO) causes malabsorption, bloating, and nutrient deficiencies. Celiac disease involves gluten-triggered autoimmune damage to intestinal villi. Probiotic supplementation can restore microbial balance, while prebiotic fibers (inulin, FOS, GOS) feed beneficial bacteria. Berberine has demonstrated antimicrobial, anti-inflammatory, and glucose-lowering effects partly mediated through gut microbiome modulation. Glutamine supports intestinal epithelial barrier integrity and enterocyte energy metabolism.</p>
