<h1>Kidneys</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>The kidneys are a pair of bean-shaped organs, each approximately 11 cm long and weighing 150 g, located retroperitoneally on either side of the vertebral column at the T12-L3 level. The right kidney sits slightly lower than the left due to displacement by the liver. Each kidney is encased in a tough fibrous capsule surrounded by perirenal fat. Internally, the kidney is divided into an outer cortex (containing glomeruli and convoluted tubules) and an inner medulla (containing loops of Henle and collecting ducts arranged in renal pyramids), which drain into the renal pelvis and ureter.</p>
<p>Each kidney contains approximately one million nephrons, the functional units responsible for blood filtration. Each nephron consists of a glomerulus (a tuft of capillaries within Bowman's capsule) that filters blood, and a tubular system (proximal convoluted tubule, loop of Henle, distal convoluted tubule, and collecting duct) that selectively reabsorbs water, electrolytes, glucose, and amino acids while secreting waste products. The kidneys filter approximately 180 liters of plasma daily, producing 1-2 liters of urine.</p>
<p>The kidneys receive approximately 20-25% of cardiac output via the renal arteries, which branch from the abdominal aorta. This high blood flow is essential for efficient filtration. Venous drainage occurs through the renal veins into the inferior vena cava. Beyond filtration, the kidneys serve critical endocrine functions: producing erythropoietin (EPO) to stimulate red blood cell production, renin to regulate blood pressure via the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS), and converting 25-hydroxyvitamin D to its active form (1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D / calcitriol) for calcium homeostasis.</p>
<h2>Key Functions</h2>
<ul>
<li>Filtration of blood to remove metabolic waste products (urea, creatinine, uric acid) and toxins</li>
<li>Regulation of fluid balance and blood osmolality through variable water reabsorption mediated by antidiuretic hormone (ADH)</li>
<li>Electrolyte homeostasis including sodium, potassium, calcium, phosphate, and bicarbonate balance</li>
<li>Blood pressure regulation via the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS) and fluid volume control</li>
<li>Acid-base balance through bicarbonate reabsorption and hydrogen ion secretion, maintaining blood pH at 7.35-7.45</li>
<li>Endocrine functions: erythropoietin (EPO) production, active vitamin D synthesis (calcitriol), and prostaglandin production</li>
</ul>
<h2>Associated Conditions</h2>
<ul>
<li>Chronic kidney disease (CKD) — progressive loss of kidney function over months to years, classified in five stages by glomerular filtration rate (GFR); most commonly caused by diabetes and hypertension</li>
<li>Acute kidney injury (AKI) — sudden decline in kidney function over hours to days, caused by prerenal (hypoperfusion), intrinsic (tubular necrosis, glomerulonephritis), or postrenal (obstruction) etiologies</li>
<li>Kidney stones (nephrolithiasis) — crystalline deposits forming in the renal pelvis, most commonly calcium oxalate; cause severe colicky flank pain and can obstruct urine flow</li>
<li>Glomerulonephritis — inflammation of the glomeruli, often immune-mediated, that can cause hematuria, proteinuria, and progressive renal failure</li>
<li>Polycystic kidney disease (PKD) — autosomal dominant or recessive genetic disorder characterized by multiple fluid-filled cysts that progressively destroy renal parenchyma</li>
<li>Diabetic nephropathy — leading cause of end-stage renal disease, driven by glomerular hyperfiltration, mesangial expansion, and basement membrane thickening from chronic hyperglycemia</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<p>Kidney function is primarily assessed by serum creatinine and estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR), with urinalysis and albumin-to-creatinine ratio (ACR) used to detect proteinuria. Blood urea nitrogen (BUN), electrolyte panels, and cystatin C provide additional assessment. Imaging includes renal ultrasound (first-line for structural evaluation), CT urography (stone detection), and renal biopsy for histological diagnosis of glomerular diseases. The kidneys are highly sensitive to nephrotoxic drugs (NSAIDs, aminoglycosides, contrast agents), and dosing adjustments are required in renal impairment. Adequate hydration, blood pressure control, and glycemic management are the cornerstones of kidney preservation.</p>
