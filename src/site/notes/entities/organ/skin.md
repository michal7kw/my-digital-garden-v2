<h1>Skin</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>The skin is the largest organ of the human body, covering approximately 1.5-2.0 square meters of surface area and weighing 3.5-10 kg (including the hypodermis). It is composed of three primary layers: the epidermis (outermost, avascular layer of stratified squamous keratinized epithelium), the dermis (underlying connective tissue layer containing blood vessels, nerves, hair follicles, and glands), and the hypodermis or subcutaneous tissue (loose connective tissue and adipose for insulation and energy storage). The epidermis itself has five strata in thick skin (stratum basale, spinosum, granulosum, lucidum, corneum), with keratinocytes progressively keratinizing as they migrate outward over a 28-day turnover cycle.</p>
<p>The epidermis contains four specialized cell types: keratinocytes (90%, producing keratin for structural integrity and water-proofing), melanocytes (producing melanin pigment for UV protection), Langerhans cells (dendritic antigen-presenting cells forming the cutaneous immune surveillance system), and Merkel cells (mechanoreceptors for light touch). The dermis is divided into the papillary layer (thin, with dermal papillae interlocking with epidermal rete ridges for structural attachment and nutrient diffusion) and the reticular layer (thicker, containing dense irregular connective tissue with collagen type I and elastic fibers providing strength and elasticity). Skin appendages include hair follicles, sebaceous glands (sebum production), eccrine sweat glands (thermoregulation, distributed across the body), and apocrine sweat glands (scent, concentrated in axillae and groin).</p>
<p>The skin's blood supply is organized into two horizontal plexuses: the subpapillary (superficial) plexus at the papillary-reticular dermis junction and the deep (cutaneous) plexus at the dermal-hypodermal junction. These are fed by perforating arteries from underlying muscles and fascia. The cutaneous vasculature plays a critical role in thermoregulation: vasodilation increases heat loss (up to 8 L/min blood flow in extreme heat), while vasoconstriction conserves heat. The skin also serves as the primary site for vitamin D3 (cholecalciferol) synthesis when UVB radiation (290-315 nm) converts 7-dehydrocholesterol in the epidermis.</p>
<h2>Key Functions</h2>
<ul>
<li>Barrier protection: prevents water loss, blocks entry of pathogens, chemicals, and UV radiation through the cornified layer, acid mantle (pH 4.5-5.5), and antimicrobial peptides (defensins, cathelicidins)</li>
<li>Thermoregulation: eccrine sweat glands produce up to 10 liters of sweat per day during extreme exertion; cutaneous vasodilation and vasoconstriction modulate heat exchange</li>
<li>Sensation: dense network of mechanoreceptors (Meissner's corpuscles for light touch, Pacinian corpuscles for pressure/vibration), thermoreceptors, nociceptors (pain), and pruritoceptors (itch)</li>
<li>Vitamin D synthesis: UVB-mediated conversion of 7-dehydrocholesterol to previtamin D3, which is then thermally isomerized to cholecalciferol and hydroxylated in the liver and kidneys to its active form</li>
<li>Immune defense: Langerhans cells and dermal dendritic cells capture and present antigens to T cells; the skin contains resident memory T cells and produces antimicrobial peptides</li>
<li>Wound healing: a complex, overlapping process of hemostasis, inflammation, proliferation (granulation tissue, re-epithelialization), and remodeling that can restore skin integrity</li>
</ul>
<h2>Associated Conditions</h2>
<ul>
<li>Eczema (atopic dermatitis) — chronic inflammatory skin disease characterized by pruritus, erythema, and impaired epidermal barrier (filaggrin mutations); part of the atopic triad with asthma and allergic rhinitis</li>
<li>Psoriasis — immune-mediated chronic inflammatory condition with accelerated keratinocyte proliferation (3-5 day vs. normal 28-day turnover), producing characteristic silvery-scaled plaques; mediated by Th17/IL-17 and IL-23 pathways</li>
<li>Acne vulgaris — chronic inflammatory disease of pilosebaceous units involving excess sebum production, follicular hyperkeratinization, Cutibacterium acnes colonization, and inflammation; affects up to 85% of adolescents</li>
<li>Melanoma — the most dangerous form of skin cancer, arising from malignant transformation of melanocytes; UV exposure is the primary modifiable risk factor, and early detection (ABCDE criteria) is critical for survival</li>
<li>Non-melanoma skin cancers — basal cell carcinoma (most common human cancer, slow-growing, rarely metastasizes) and squamous cell carcinoma (arising from epidermal keratinocytes, 2-5% metastatic potential)</li>
<li>Wound healing disorders — chronic non-healing wounds (diabetic foot ulcers, venous leg ulcers, pressure injuries) affecting approximately 2.5% of the US population, driven by diabetes, vascular insufficiency, and chronic inflammation</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<p>Dermatological assessment begins with visual inspection under good lighting, using dermoscopy (dermatoscope magnification) for pigmented lesion evaluation. Skin biopsy (punch, shave, or excisional) with histopathological examination is the gold standard for definitive diagnosis. Patch testing identifies contact allergens, and skin prick testing evaluates immediate hypersensitivity. Wood's lamp (UV) examination detects fungal infections and pigment disorders. Key nutritional factors for skin health include vitamin D (immune modulation and keratinocyte differentiation), vitamin C (essential cofactor for collagen synthesis by prolyl and lysyl hydroxylases), zinc (wound healing, immune function, and anti-inflammatory effects), vitamin A/retinoids (regulating keratinocyte proliferation and differentiation), and omega-3 fatty acids (anti-inflammatory and photoprotective properties).</p>
