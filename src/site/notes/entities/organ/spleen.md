<h1>Spleen</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>The spleen is the largest lymphoid organ in the body, weighing approximately 150-200 grams in adults and located in the left upper quadrant of the abdomen beneath the diaphragm. It serves as a critical interface between the circulatory and immune systems, filtering approximately 300 mL of blood per minute through its specialized vascular architecture. Unlike lymph nodes, which filter lymph, the spleen is the only lymphoid organ that directly filters blood.</p>
<p>Structurally, the spleen is divided into two functional compartments: the red pulp and the white pulp. The red pulp comprises about 75% of splenic volume and functions as a blood filtration system, removing aged, damaged, or abnormal erythrocytes through a process called culling. Macrophages in the red pulp also recycle iron from hemoglobin, returning it to the circulation bound to transferrin. The white pulp is organized lymphoid tissue containing T-cell zones (periarteriolar lymphoid sheaths) and B-cell follicles, where adaptive immune responses to blood-borne antigens are initiated.</p>
<p>The spleen also serves as a reservoir for monocytes, platelets, and red blood cells, which can be mobilized rapidly during physiological stress such as hemorrhage, hypoxia, or infection. In fetal development, the spleen is a major site of hematopoiesis, and it can resume this role in adults under pathological conditions (extramedullary hematopoiesis).</p>
<h2>Key Functions</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Blood filtration</strong>: Removes senescent, damaged, and antibody-coated red blood cells; clears particulate matter, bacteria, and cellular debris</li>
<li><strong>Iron recycling</strong>: Splenic macrophages phagocytose old erythrocytes, catabolize hemoglobin, and recycle iron via ferroportin and transferrin</li>
<li><strong>Immune surveillance</strong>: White pulp mounts adaptive immune responses to blood-borne pathogens; major site of IgM antibody production</li>
<li><strong>Opsonin production</strong>: Synthesizes properdin (factor P) and tuftsin, opsonins critical for complement activation and phagocytosis of encapsulated bacteria</li>
<li><strong>Blood reservoir</strong>: Stores approximately 30% of circulating platelets and a large pool of monocytes; contracts during sympathetic activation to release cells</li>
<li><strong>Erythrocyte quality control</strong>: Removes Howell-Jolly bodies, siderotic granules, and nuclear remnants from red blood cells (pitting function)</li>
<li><strong>Hematopoiesis</strong>: Primary hematopoietic organ during fetal development; can resume blood cell production in myeloproliferative disorders</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<p>Splenectomy or functional asplenia (as in sickle cell disease) dramatically increases susceptibility to overwhelming post-splenectomy infection (OPSI), particularly from encapsulated bacteria (Streptococcus pneumoniae, Neisseria meningitidis, Haemophilus influenzae). Asplenic patients require lifelong vaccination and prophylactic antibiotics. Splenomegaly can result from portal hypertension (cirrhosis), hematologic malignancies (lymphoma, leukemia), hemolytic anemias, infections (malaria, EBV mononucleosis), and storage diseases (Gaucher disease). Hypersplenism causes cytopenias through excessive sequestration and destruction of blood cells. The spleen's role in iron homeostasis means splenic dysfunction can contribute to iron overload or deficiency states.</p>
