<h1>Liver</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>The liver is the largest solid internal organ, weighing approximately 1.5 kg in adults, located in the right upper quadrant of the abdomen beneath the diaphragm. It is divided into a larger right lobe and smaller left lobe by the falciform ligament, with the caudate and quadrate lobes visible on the inferior surface. Microscopically, the liver is organized into roughly one million hexagonal lobules, each centered on a central vein and bounded by portal triads (containing a portal venule, hepatic arteriole, and bile ductule). Hepatocytes, the primary functional cells, are arranged in plates radiating from the central vein, bathed by sinusoidal blood.</p>
<p>The liver has a unique dual blood supply: approximately 75% arrives via the portal vein carrying nutrient-rich blood from the gastrointestinal tract, and 25% via the hepatic artery carrying oxygenated blood. This portal circulation means the liver is the first organ to process absorbed nutrients, drugs, and toxins. Venous drainage flows through the hepatic veins into the inferior vena cava. The liver also produces 500-1000 mL of bile daily, which is stored in the gallbladder and released into the duodenum to aid fat digestion.</p>
<p>The liver performs over 500 distinct metabolic functions, broadly categorized into metabolism (carbohydrate, lipid, and protein processing), detoxification (phase I oxidation via cytochrome P450 enzymes including CYP3A4, and phase II conjugation reactions), synthesis (albumin, clotting factors, complement proteins, lipoproteins), storage (glycogen, vitamins A, D, B12, iron, and copper), and immune function (Kupffer cells filtering portal blood of pathogens and debris). The liver is also remarkable for its regenerative capacity, able to restore its full mass from as little as 25% of remaining tissue.</p>
<h2>Key Functions</h2>
<ul>
<li>Detoxification of drugs, alcohol, metabolic waste, and environmental toxins through phase I (CYP450 oxidation) and phase II (glucuronidation, sulfation, glutathione conjugation) reactions</li>
<li>Bile production and secretion for emulsification and absorption of dietary fats and fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K)</li>
<li>Glucose homeostasis through glycogenesis, glycogenolysis, and gluconeogenesis</li>
<li>Synthesis of plasma proteins including albumin (oncotic pressure), clotting factors (I, II, V, VII, IX, X, XI), and acute-phase reactants</li>
<li>Lipid metabolism including cholesterol synthesis, lipoprotein production (VLDL), and fatty acid oxidation</li>
<li>Storage of glycogen (~100g), vitamin A (1-2 year supply), vitamin B12, iron (as ferritin), and copper</li>
</ul>
<h2>Associated Conditions</h2>
<ul>
<li>Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) — hepatic steatosis in the absence of significant alcohol use, affecting approximately 25% of the global population; can progress to non-alcoholic steatohepatitis (NASH) with inflammation and fibrosis</li>
<li>Cirrhosis — end-stage liver fibrosis with loss of normal architecture, caused by chronic alcohol use, viral hepatitis, or NASH; complications include portal hypertension, ascites, and hepatic encephalopathy</li>
<li>Hepatitis B and C — viral infections causing acute and chronic liver inflammation; hepatitis C is curable with direct-acting antivirals, while hepatitis B is managed with nucleoside analogues</li>
<li>Drug-induced liver injury (DILI) — hepatotoxicity from medications (acetaminophen being the most common cause) or supplements; a leading cause of acute liver failure</li>
<li>Hepatocellular carcinoma — primary liver cancer, typically arising in the setting of cirrhosis or chronic hepatitis B infection</li>
<li>Hemochromatosis — hereditary iron overload disorder causing progressive hepatic fibrosis if untreated</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<p>Liver function is assessed through a panel of blood tests: alanine aminotransferase (ALT) and aspartate aminotransferase (AST) for hepatocellular injury, alkaline phosphatase (ALP) and gamma-glutamyl transferase (GGT) for cholestatic patterns, albumin and prothrombin time (PT/INR) for synthetic function, and bilirubin for conjugation and excretion capacity. Imaging modalities include ultrasound (first-line for fatty liver and masses), FibroScan (transient elastography for fibrosis staging), CT, and MRI. The liver's central role in drug metabolism makes it critical to assess hepatic function before prescribing medications, and supplements such as NAC, milk thistle (silymarin), alpha-lipoic acid, and curcumin are commonly investigated for hepatoprotective properties.</p>
