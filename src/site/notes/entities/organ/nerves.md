<h1>Nerves</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>Nerves are bundled collections of axons in the peripheral nervous system (PNS) that transmit electrochemical signals between the central nervous system (brain and spinal cord) and every tissue in the body. The PNS comprises 12 pairs of cranial nerves, 31 pairs of spinal nerves, and extensive autonomic nerve plexuses, collectively containing billions of individual nerve fibers that enable sensation, voluntary movement, and autonomic regulation of organ function.</p>
<p>Each nerve is organized in a hierarchical connective tissue framework: individual axons are wrapped in endoneurium, grouped into fascicles by perineurium, and bundled into whole nerves by epineurium. Many peripheral axons are myelinated by Schwann cells, which form insulating sheaths that enable saltatory conduction at speeds up to 120 m/s along large myelinated fibers. Unmyelinated C-fibers conduct more slowly (0.5-2 m/s) and transmit pain, temperature, and autonomic signals.</p>
<p>The peripheral nervous system is divided functionally into the somatic nervous system (voluntary motor and conscious sensory) and the autonomic nervous system (sympathetic, parasympathetic, and enteric divisions). The enteric nervous system alone contains 200-600 million neurons governing gastrointestinal motility, secretion, and blood flow, earning it the designation &quot;second brain.&quot; Peripheral nerves possess significant regenerative capacity compared to the CNS, with axon regrowth rates of approximately 1-3 mm per day following injury.</p>
<h2>Structure and Function</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Sensory (afferent) neurons</strong>: Transmit information from receptors (mechanoreceptors, nociceptors, thermoreceptors, proprioceptors) to the CNS for processing</li>
<li><strong>Motor (efferent) neurons</strong>: Carry commands from the CNS to skeletal muscles (somatic) or smooth muscle, cardiac muscle, and glands (autonomic)</li>
<li><strong>Myelin sheaths</strong>: Schwann cell-derived insulation enabling rapid saltatory conduction; composed of lipid-rich membranes requiring adequate fatty acids and B vitamins</li>
<li><strong>Nodes of Ranvier</strong>: Gaps between myelin segments where voltage-gated sodium channels cluster, enabling action potential regeneration</li>
<li><strong>Neuromuscular junction</strong>: Specialized synapse where motor neurons release acetylcholine to trigger muscle contraction</li>
<li><strong>Autonomic ganglia</strong>: Relay stations where preganglionic neurons synapse with postganglionic neurons controlling involuntary functions</li>
<li><strong>Enteric nervous system</strong>: Semi-autonomous network in the gut wall regulating digestion, absorption, and gut-brain communication via the vagus nerve</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<p>Peripheral neuropathy affects an estimated 20 million people in the United States alone, with diabetes being the most common cause (diabetic polyneuropathy). Vitamin B12 deficiency causes demyelination and axonal degeneration, producing numbness, paresthesias, and potentially irreversible nerve damage if untreated. Carpal tunnel syndrome is the most common entrapment neuropathy. Guillain-Barre syndrome involves acute autoimmune demyelination of peripheral nerves. Chemotherapy-induced peripheral neuropathy (CIPN) affects up to 70% of patients receiving neurotoxic agents. Alpha-lipoic acid has demonstrated neuroprotective effects in diabetic neuropathy through antioxidant mechanisms. Maintaining nerve health requires adequate B vitamins (B1, B6, B12), essential fatty acids for myelin membrane integrity, and management of metabolic risk factors including glucose control and inflammation.</p>
