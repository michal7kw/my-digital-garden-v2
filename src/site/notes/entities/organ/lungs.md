<h1>Lungs</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>The lungs are a pair of spongy, cone-shaped organs occupying most of the thoracic cavity, separated by the mediastinum containing the heart. The right lung is divided into three lobes (upper, middle, lower) by the oblique and horizontal fissures, while the left lung has two lobes (upper and lower) separated by the oblique fissure, with a cardiac notch accommodating the heart. Each lung is enclosed in a double-layered pleural membrane: the visceral pleura adhering to the lung surface and the parietal pleura lining the thoracic wall, with a thin layer of pleural fluid between them reducing friction during breathing.</p>
<p>The airway system begins at the trachea, which bifurcates into the right and left main bronchi at the carina (approximately T4-T5 level). The bronchi subdivide progressively into lobar bronchi, segmental bronchi, bronchioles, terminal bronchioles, and finally respiratory bronchioles that open into alveolar ducts and alveoli. The lungs contain approximately 300 million alveoli, providing a total gas exchange surface area of 50-75 square meters. Each alveolus is surrounded by a dense network of pulmonary capillaries, and the extremely thin alveolar-capillary membrane (0.2-0.5 micrometers) enables rapid diffusion of oxygen and carbon dioxide.</p>
<p>The lungs receive deoxygenated blood from the right ventricle via the pulmonary arteries (the only arteries carrying deoxygenated blood). After gas exchange in the alveolar capillaries, oxygenated blood returns to the left atrium via the pulmonary veins. The bronchial arteries, branching from the thoracic aorta, supply oxygenated blood to the lung tissue itself (airways, pleura, supporting structures). Ventilation is driven by the diaphragm (primary muscle) and intercostal muscles, with accessory muscles recruited during increased respiratory demand.</p>
<h2>Key Functions</h2>
<ul>
<li>Gas exchange: delivering oxygen to pulmonary capillary blood and removing carbon dioxide via diffusion across the alveolar-capillary membrane</li>
<li>Ventilation: moving air in and out of the lungs through the coordinated action of the diaphragm and intercostal muscles</li>
<li>Acid-base regulation: adjusting CO2 elimination to help maintain blood pH (respiratory compensation for metabolic disturbances)</li>
<li>Immune defense: mucociliary clearance, secretory IgA, alveolar macrophages, and surfactant proteins (SP-A, SP-D) that opsonize pathogens</li>
<li>Surfactant production: type II alveolar cells produce surfactant to reduce surface tension and prevent alveolar collapse</li>
<li>Metabolic functions: conversion of angiotensin I to angiotensin II by pulmonary endothelial angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE), and inactivation of bradykinin, serotonin, and prostaglandins</li>
</ul>
<h2>Associated Conditions</h2>
<ul>
<li>Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) — progressive, largely irreversible airflow limitation caused primarily by smoking; encompasses chronic bronchitis and emphysema</li>
<li>Asthma — chronic inflammatory airway disease characterized by reversible bronchoconstriction, mucus hypersecretion, and airway hyperresponsiveness</li>
<li>Pneumonia — infection of the lung parenchyma by bacteria (commonly Streptococcus pneumoniae), viruses, or fungi, causing alveolar consolidation</li>
<li>Lung cancer — leading cause of cancer death worldwide; non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) accounts for approximately 85% of cases, with small cell lung cancer (SCLC) comprising the remainder</li>
<li>Pulmonary embolism — obstruction of pulmonary arteries by blood clots, most commonly originating from deep vein thrombosis; can be life-threatening</li>
<li>Pulmonary fibrosis — progressive scarring of lung tissue from idiopathic, autoimmune, or environmental causes, leading to restrictive lung disease and impaired gas exchange</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<p>Pulmonary function is assessed with spirometry (measuring FEV1, FVC, and their ratio for obstructive vs. restrictive patterns), arterial blood gases (PaO2, PaCO2, pH), pulse oximetry, and diffusion capacity (DLCO). Imaging includes chest X-ray (first-line), high-resolution CT (interstitial lung disease, nodules), and CT pulmonary angiography (pulmonary embolism). Bronchoscopy allows direct airway visualization and sampling. Key nutritional and supplemental considerations include NAC (mucolytic properties and glutathione precursor), vitamin D (immune modulation and respiratory infection risk), and omega-3 fatty acids (anti-inflammatory effects in COPD and asthma).</p>
