<h1>Pancreas</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>The pancreas is a retroperitoneal, elongated organ approximately 15-25 cm long, located posterior to the stomach and extending from the concavity of the duodenum (head) to the hilum of the spleen (tail). It is anatomically divided into the head (nestled within the C-loop of the duodenum), uncinate process (projecting posteriorly), neck, body, and tail. The pancreatic duct (duct of Wirsung) runs the length of the organ, joining the common bile duct at the ampulla of Vater before emptying into the duodenum. An accessory duct (duct of Santorini) may drain the upper head separately.</p>
<p>The pancreas has two distinct functional components. The exocrine portion, comprising approximately 95% of the organ's mass, consists of acinar cells organized into lobules that produce digestive enzymes (trypsinogen, chymotrypsinogen, pancreatic lipase, amylase, elastase) secreted as inactive zymogens to prevent autodigestion. Ductal cells secrete bicarbonate-rich fluid that neutralizes acidic gastric chyme entering the duodenum. The endocrine portion consists of the islets of Langerhans (approximately 1-2 million clusters), containing alpha cells (glucagon), beta cells (insulin), delta cells (somatostatin), PP cells (pancreatic polypeptide), and epsilon cells (ghrelin).</p>
<p>The pancreas receives its blood supply from branches of the celiac trunk and superior mesenteric artery: the superior and inferior pancreaticoduodenal arteries supply the head, while the splenic artery gives off branches (dorsal pancreatic, greater pancreatic, and caudal pancreatic arteries) to supply the body and tail. Venous drainage follows corresponding veins into the portal venous system, ensuring that pancreatic hormones pass first through the liver.</p>
<h2>Key Functions</h2>
<ul>
<li>Exocrine secretion of digestive enzymes (proteases, lipase, amylase, nucleases) essential for the breakdown of proteins, fats, carbohydrates, and nucleic acids in the small intestine</li>
<li>Bicarbonate secretion to neutralize gastric acid and create optimal pH (approximately 7-8) for pancreatic enzyme activity in the duodenum</li>
<li>Insulin secretion by beta cells in response to rising blood glucose, promoting cellular glucose uptake, glycogen synthesis, and lipogenesis</li>
<li>Glucagon secretion by alpha cells during hypoglycemia, stimulating hepatic glycogenolysis and gluconeogenesis to raise blood glucose</li>
<li>Somatostatin secretion by delta cells, acting as a paracrine inhibitor of both insulin and glucagon release to fine-tune glucose regulation</li>
<li>Regulation of digestive processes via secretin and cholecystokinin (CCK) signaling from the duodenum</li>
</ul>
<h2>Associated Conditions</h2>
<ul>
<li>Type 2 diabetes mellitus — progressive beta cell dysfunction and insulin resistance leading to chronic hyperglycemia; the most common metabolic disorder globally, strongly linked to obesity and sedentary lifestyle</li>
<li>Type 1 diabetes mellitus — autoimmune destruction of beta cells resulting in absolute insulin deficiency; typically presents in childhood or adolescence</li>
<li>Acute pancreatitis — sudden inflammation of the pancreas, most commonly caused by gallstones or alcohol, characterized by severe epigastric pain radiating to the back and elevated serum lipase/amylase</li>
<li>Chronic pancreatitis — progressive inflammatory disease leading to irreversible structural damage, exocrine insufficiency (maldigestion), and endocrine insufficiency (diabetes)</li>
<li>Pancreatic cancer — one of the most lethal malignancies, with adenocarcinoma of the pancreatic head being the most common type; often diagnosed late due to lack of early symptoms</li>
<li>Pancreatic exocrine insufficiency (PEI) — inadequate enzyme production causing maldigestion, steatorrhea, and nutritional deficiencies; managed with pancreatic enzyme replacement therapy</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<p>Pancreatic function is assessed through fasting glucose, HbA1c, and oral glucose tolerance testing (endocrine function), as well as fecal elastase-1 and 72-hour fecal fat collection (exocrine function). Serum lipase is the preferred marker for acute pancreatitis (more specific than amylase). Imaging modalities include abdominal ultrasound and CT for pancreatitis staging, endoscopic ultrasound (EUS) for small lesions and tissue sampling, and MRCP for ductal anatomy. Berberine has demonstrated hypoglycemic effects comparable to metformin in some studies, and chromium, alpha-lipoic acid, and cinnamon extract are commonly investigated for glycemic support.</p>
