<h1>Intestines</h1>
<h2>Overview</h2>
<p>The intestines are a long, continuous muscular tube extending from the pyloric sphincter of the stomach to the anus, divided into the small intestine (approximately 6 meters) and the large intestine (approximately 1.5 meters). The small intestine consists of three segments: the duodenum (25 cm, receiving bile and pancreatic secretions), the jejunum (approximately 2.5 m, the primary site of nutrient absorption), and the ileum (approximately 3.5 m, absorbing bile salts, vitamin B12, and remaining nutrients). The large intestine comprises the cecum (with the appendix), ascending colon, transverse colon, descending colon, sigmoid colon, rectum, and anal canal.</p>
<p>The small intestinal mucosa is highly specialized for absorption, featuring circular folds (plicae circulares), villi (finger-like projections 0.5-1.5 mm tall), and microvilli (the brush border) that together increase the absorptive surface area to approximately 200 square meters. Each villus contains a central lacteal (lymphatic capillary for fat absorption) and a capillary network (for amino acid and carbohydrate absorption). Specialized cells include enterocytes (absorption), goblet cells (mucus secretion), Paneth cells (antimicrobial defensins and lysozyme), and enteroendocrine cells (producing hormones such as secretin, CCK, GLP-1, and serotonin — the gut produces approximately 95% of the body's serotonin). The large intestine lacks villi but has abundant goblet cells for mucus production and is the primary site of water and electrolyte reabsorption.</p>
<p>The intestines receive blood from the superior mesenteric artery (small intestine, cecum, ascending and transverse colon) and inferior mesenteric artery (descending colon, sigmoid, and rectum). Venous drainage flows into the portal system via the superior and inferior mesenteric veins for hepatic processing of absorbed nutrients. The intestines house the gut microbiome — approximately 38 trillion microorganisms (predominantly bacteria from phyla Bacteroidetes and Firmicutes) — which plays essential roles in vitamin synthesis (K, B vitamins), short-chain fatty acid (SCFA) production, immune development, pathogen colonization resistance, and metabolism of bile acids and xenobiotics. The gut-associated lymphoid tissue (GALT), including Peyer's patches, constitutes approximately 70% of the body's immune tissue.</p>
<h2>Key Functions</h2>
<ul>
<li>Digestion and absorption of macronutrients: carbohydrates (brush border disaccharidases), proteins (peptidases), and fats (bile salt-mediated emulsification and lipase activity)</li>
<li>Absorption of micronutrients: iron and calcium primarily in the duodenum; water-soluble vitamins in the jejunum; vitamin B12 and bile salts in the terminal ileum</li>
<li>Water and electrolyte homeostasis: the small intestine absorbs approximately 8-9 liters/day, the large intestine absorbs an additional 1-1.5 liters</li>
<li>Immune defense: GALT (Peyer's patches, isolated lymphoid follicles, intraepithelial lymphocytes) monitors luminal antigens and maintains oral tolerance to food antigens while mounting responses to pathogens</li>
<li>Microbiome support: providing habitat and substrates for commensal bacteria that produce short-chain fatty acids (butyrate, propionate, acetate), which nourish colonocytes and modulate systemic inflammation</li>
<li>Enteroendocrine signaling: secretion of GLP-1 (incretin effect on insulin), PYY (satiety), serotonin (motility), and motilin (migrating motor complex)</li>
</ul>
<h2>Associated Conditions</h2>
<ul>
<li>Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS) — functional gastrointestinal disorder characterized by chronic abdominal pain and altered bowel habits without structural abnormality; affects 10-15% of the population globally</li>
<li>Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) — chronic inflammatory conditions comprising Crohn's disease (can affect any GI segment, transmural inflammation) and ulcerative colitis (confined to the colon, mucosal inflammation)</li>
<li>Celiac disease — autoimmune enteropathy triggered by gluten (gliadin) in genetically susceptible individuals (HLA-DQ2/DQ8), causing villous atrophy and malabsorption</li>
<li>Colorectal cancer — the third most common cancer worldwide, arising from adenomatous polyps through the adenoma-carcinoma sequence; screening colonoscopy is recommended beginning at age 45</li>
<li>Small intestinal bacterial overgrowth (SIBO) — excessive bacterial colonization of the small intestine causing bloating, diarrhea, and malabsorption; diagnosed by glucose or lactulose breath testing</li>
<li>Intestinal dysbiosis — imbalance in the gut microbiome composition associated with obesity, metabolic syndrome, autoimmune conditions, and neuropsychiatric disorders via the gut-brain axis</li>
</ul>
<h2>Clinical Significance</h2>
<p>Intestinal assessment includes stool analysis (calprotectin for inflammation, elastase for pancreatic sufficiency, occult blood for bleeding), serologic testing (anti-tTG and anti-endomysial antibodies for celiac disease), and breath tests (hydrogen/methane for SIBO and carbohydrate malabsorption). Endoscopy (upper GI endoscopy and colonoscopy) allows direct visualization, biopsy, and therapeutic interventions such as polypectomy. Capsule endoscopy images the small bowel segments not accessible by standard endoscopy. Probiotics (particularly Lactobacillus and Bifidobacterium strains) are widely used to support microbiome health, while L-glutamine supports enterocyte integrity, and butyrate (from fiber fermentation or supplementation) nourishes colonocytes and reduces intestinal inflammation.</p>
